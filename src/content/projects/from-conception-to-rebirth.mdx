<p>As I described in the backgrounder on Mothers and Children in Prison BC Corrections in 2006 cancelled the Mother Child program that had been operating successfully at Alouette Correctional Centre for Women. The cancellation was successfully challenged in BC Supreme Court as a violation of the Charter rights of both mothers and children, Justice Ross’s decision in <em>Inglis v British Columbia (Ministry of Public Safety)</em> came down in December 2013. In the spring of 2014 three students in the 2014 Penal Policy Seminar jointly produced a 47 minute documentary film about the decision, “The Mother Baby Program and the Inglis Decision: From Conception to Rebirth.” For this ambitious completion of a documentary film, to my knowledge the first by law students at UBC, Robin Phillips, Mike Jones, and Jamie Hansen interviewed lawyers, health and corrections professionals about their involvement in the Inglis case, their personal reflections on the Mother Baby Program, the constitutional arguments put forward in the case, and their hopes for the future. In their production journal Robin, Mike and Jamie describe the inspiration for the project:</p>

<MediaEmbed url="https://videopress.com/v/nXC8peIi?resizeToParent=true&amp;cover=true&amp;preloadContent=metadata&amp;useAverageColor=true" />

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This idea for this project came about midway through the fall 2013 term. Jamie, Mike and Robin were all taking Penal Policy together. They wanted to create something that could have the potential to extend beyond the law school-to design a project that may make a positive impact on society at large. We had heard that Michael Jackson was a professor who encouraged creativity and fostered student innovation when it came to final projects. It seemed like Penal Policy would be the ideal class to apply what we had learned in law school to something other than a research paper. Robin approached Mike and Jamie with the idea of making a documentary on the cancelled mother baby program. Jamie was working for the Indigenous Community Legal Clinic and knew about the clinic's work in the court case. Mike had film editing experience and was excited to work on his film-making skills while promoting a worthy issue. We had all been disappointed that the mother baby program in prison had been cut and felt like this was a topical issue that would make for a good documentary.</p>

<p>Soon after we made the decision to make a documentary (if possible) on the topic of the mother baby program, the Inglis decision was released. This made the direction of the film much clearer, since we now had a court case to investigate. As a group, we met and decided that the main purpose of our documentary would be to humanize the issue of babies in prison. Many online comments on news stories about the mother baby program were extremely negative and seemed based on ignorance about babies in prison. We hoped to make a documentary that could communicate the issue in an accessible way that would be interesting to both law students and lay people. We also wanted to also look behind the scenes of the Inglis decision.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>For anyone who might think electing an alternative medium for thinking about the law is a ‘soft touch’ compared to writing a paper, reading the Production Journal quickly demonstrates both the conceptual and logistical work involved in making this documentary. Over and above conceptualizing the ideas behind the film, it describes the challenges in finding the necessary audio-visual equipment, scheduling interviewees who had busy professional careers, finding appropriate locations for those interviews, editing video and mixing the sound, all the while maintaining full course loads. Here are some passages from the Journal.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>We met to come up with a loose "story board" of what the documentary should look like and identified several themes: the history of the program, what the program was like while it was in existence, the cancellation of the program, the court case, any hopes for the future of the program. We turned to our "wish list" of speakers and divided it up into a contact list. Mike had found the contact information for many of the people involved in Inglis and we took this as a starting point. Each of us contacted approximately 3 potential subjects,</p>

<p>Scheduling with each interview subject was rather complex. All of our interviewees where very busy and the three of us had full-time course loads. We also had different schedules from each other. Our first interview was with Ruth Martin. We emailed her and agreed to meet at her office. At this point, we did not have our video camera, so we filmed Dr. Martin using two camera phones. Dr Martin was, in many ways, an ideal first subjects since she was connected with a lot of other people involved in the program. She was helpful throughout the project, for example she gave us filmed footage from a panel discussion on the future of the mother baby program.</p>

<p>Our next interview was with Sarah Rauch who Jamie knew through the Indigenous Community Legal Clinic. Sarah was willing to participate and we filmed her at Allard Hall. The main challenge with Sarah was finding a good location to film where we would not be disturbed. Professor Benedet gave us permission to use the Centre for feminist Legal studies and to put a 'do not disturb' note on the door. Sarah also has a soft speaking voice. We found we had to be careful when filming to not distract from her information. Jamie conducted this interview. Sarah was very useful at providing information on the origins of the case, since the clinic had originally had the file. She also provided quite a personal view of the case, having been intimately involved.</p>

<p>Mike had arranged to interview Brenda Tole, the former warden of ACCW. She lived outside the greater Vancouver area but was coming into Surrey. We arranged to meet her at a community centre. She surprised us all with her kind manner - not what we were expecting in a prison warden. Our first problem was finding a suitable location to film. We ran about the large community centre no looking for a quiet corner; we found nothing. We asked the staff about using a room but they told us rooms had to be booked in advance. We managed to borrow some chairs and set up in the far corner of the main forum, choosing a location with good light. We hoped that no one would come our way as we did the interview. Unfortunately, it seemed that as soon as we began, hoards of children began walking by us in field trip formation-hand-in-hand and talking loudly amongst themselves. The odd child's shriek - which we initially thought might have actually gone well with the subject of the documentary - was replaced by a cacophony of children's voices that drowned out Brenda Tole. Mike and Jamie continued to film, since we had limited time and we hoped we could adjust sound levels during editing, while Robin ran to try to find another place to film. There was a computer room in the library and after assuring the librarian than no patrons would be filmed and we would only be about 20mins, Robin obtained permission to use the space. The group moved to the room in the library. Moving turned out to be the right decision, since this was the only footage we shot which was useable - the other footage had too much background noise. The room was almost empty, so aside from the odd cough from one patron, all the footage we shot there was useable. The filming turned out to be a great success and provided us an adventure out to Surrey. She was also the first subject to benefit from the new camera (or more appropriate we benefitted from filming her with the new camera.)</p>

<p>Michael Jackson's contribution to the documentary ended up being the cornerstone of our film. Many speakers had mentioned seeing him testify in court. Being in his class, we had seen footage of him before the Supreme Court of Canada, so we expected him to be a confident speaker. We filmed him in a room with lots of natural light and with our new camera. Michael Jackson is clearly a well-polished speaker and his footage ended up being the introduction to many of our sections, since his points are communicated so well.</p>

<p>Kasari Govinder was another excellent speaker. Since Mike was going to be doing the lions' share of the editing, Jamie and Robin interviewed Kasari. They headed down to the Westcoast LEAF office and met Kasari there. She was an engaging speaker who was able to explain the legal arguments in a very straightforward manner.</p>

<p>After completing the majority of our filming, we decided it would be great to have someone from Fir Square talk to round out the medical information. The Inglis decision cites Sarah Payne, a midwife from Fir Square and former director of the program, so we reached out to her to see if she would like to be involved in our project. Lucky for us, Sarah Payne was available and interested and we set up an interview. She nicely rounded out the information regarding the medical attention that was given to women in the mother baby program. She also supplied a perspective on the transformative power of birth that fit very well with our documentary.</p>

<p>We were unable to obtain permission to film at ACCW. To compensate, we found some footage of ACCW that had been produced by the BC government. Fittingly, this footage is all about how 'unique' ACCW is and how the facility was designed to be un-prison-like. We use this footage as background while the speakers are describing the grounds. We also looked for images that we could use to keep the audience captivated while people are talking-we felt more cuts lends itself to a more interesting documentary. These images also masked shaky camera work. Since some of the footage was spliced together, images also hides some of the rougher cuts. Since Mike was handling the edits at this point, Robin and Jamie trolled the internet for appropriate images. Jamie then loaded these pictures into imovie to make them workable in the film.</p>

<p>We then spent several more viewing sessions switching out photos for ones we felt were<br />Better suited to the material. A limiting factor was that we needed photos of a certain level of pixilation, lest the slides be blurry. We had quite a task of searching for images of the proper resolution. Each time we changed images (which we did frequently) Jamie had to re-animate them using imovie on her computer and Mike had to re-render the film.</p>
</blockquote>
