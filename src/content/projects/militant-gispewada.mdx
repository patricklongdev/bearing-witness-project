<p>Christina Gray’s blanket, titled “Militant Gispewada”, completed for the 2013 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar, symbolically places her family crest on a military camouflage background representing “the tension that exists for First Nations people and the Canadian government.” Christina explains that as a member of the House of Wasiibax, of the Ginaxangiik tribe, part of the Lax Kw ‘alaams Nation, “since we are a matrilineal people… my mother's traditions are passed to me, which includes the ability to use this crest and to create regalia with that symbol on it”.</p>

<p>Christina describes way in which artistic expression is an important part of the articulation and vindication of the rights of Indigenous Peoples.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>It remains that Tsimshian people are able to create art today and draw upon that adaawk, which imbues with it a spiritual power to continue our own cultural and artistic traditions. This blanket serves to symbolize that ability to transcend the colonial history but also to represent it.</p>

<p>Following the military motif of my project, it has become a requirement to continue on many warrior-like paths by written word, artistic or cultural works, oral arguments in Courts, and through the physical labour of hunting and fishing. The goal of this artistic work is to strive towards a goal in order to gain the right to self-determination on a nation-to-nation level and the right to a level of autonomy between Canada and First Nations. As self-determination by the Canadian state would be the substantive force that ensures both Indigenous peoples and Canadians could co-exist together.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her essay Christina relates her project to aspects of Tsimshian cultural ways of being and identity as well as relevant Canadian jurisprudence on Aboriginal rights, including an analysis of the Supreme Court of Canada decision in <em>Lax Kw 'a laams Indian Band v. Canada (Attorney General )</em> 201 I SCC 56.<sup data-fn="ab3fa145-daf1-4d0a-97d7-428b36a8a220" class="fn"><a href="#ab3fa145-daf1-4d0a-97d7-428b36a8a220" id="ab3fa145-daf1-4d0a-97d7-428b36a8a220-link">1</a></sup> In that case the Supreme Court upheld the decisions of the lower courts rejecting the claim by Lax Kw ‘alaams to a commercial right to the fishery. In her essay Christina poignantly describes her mother’s reaction to the blanket:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I showed my mother my blanket at an East Vancouver cafe that we frequent on a regular basis. We were talking about how the frayed edges of the cotton were purposefully done like that because it represented the tenuous relationship that exists between First Nations and the Canadian government. Not all parts of the blanket edges are frayed, the top part of the blanket with the red and white polka dots have a clean edge, which represents the apology made by Prime Minister Harper who acted on behalf of the government of Canada in apologizing for residential school policies. My mom relayed the frayed edges back to those children who went to residential school, and said that they would likely feel happy to receive a blanket like this one. Even if the edges were frayed and it is not finely crafted like some of the blankets that are worn today. Those children would be happy because they got to continue as aspect of their culture. This struck a chord with me as I remembered all those children who live with the intergenerational effects of colonial policies like residential school.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Christina, as an Indigenous women who no longer lives in her traditional territory, describes the impact of the restrictions that have been cumulatively placed on Indigenous peoples’ ability to maintain important cultural and economic activities:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The continuation of culture may also be put in jeopardy as a result of the limited way that Aboriginal people are permitted to hunt and fish. My ancestors, and only a few generations ago, were fisherman or worked in canneries and now I am far removed from the sustenance- based culture where I type instead of fish. Although it remains that many people still fish, but on a limited and restricted basis at allotted times of the year. First Nations people are forced to exercise their cultural Aboriginal rights under a regulatory regime.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The importance of Christina’s crested regalia was reflected when she wore a ceremonial button blanket with the Killer-Whale crest along with a traditional cedar-bark hat at the UBC Convocation Ceremony in 2013 which she received her J.D. Prior to her admission to the bar of Ontario in 2015 she requested permission to wear her ceremonial regalia rather than the traditional black barristers gown. She pointed out that the crested blanket represents Tsimpshian traditional laws, precedents and ways of being.</p>

<p>The Law Society initially rejected Christina’s request, saying that the blanket could only be worn after the ceremony, as it interfered with the long-standing tradition of wearing barristers robes. According to a spokesperson for the Law Society, “candidates are traditionally required to wear robes because the ceremony is officially a session of the court. It mirrors what barristers wear in court, and the idea is to make everyone equal, so nobody stands out.”</p>

<p>After the initial rejection, the Lax Kw ‘alaams First Nation and Aboriginal Legal Services Toronto, where Christine articled, made submissions to the Law Society. Christa Big Canoe and Jonathan Rudin of Aboriginal Legal Services wrote:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Surely there must be space in our legal pluralistic society and specifically within the realm of our profession, to celebrate and embrace Indigenous legal traditions within those traditions of common law.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>After reconsideration the Law Society agreed to honour Christina’s request to wear the button blanket, saying it appreciated the dialogue.<sup data-fn="75d6654e-ba15-4eb6-8cb4-7621534efebb" class="fn"><a href="#75d6654e-ba15-4eb6-8cb4-7621534efebb" id="75d6654e-ba15-4eb6-8cb4-7621534efebb-link">2</a></sup><sup data-fn="f8156486-4807-4359-9844-9ea8dffc1d7c" class="fn"><a href="#f8156486-4807-4359-9844-9ea8dffc1d7c" id="f8156486-4807-4359-9844-9ea8dffc1d7c-link">3</a></sup></p>
