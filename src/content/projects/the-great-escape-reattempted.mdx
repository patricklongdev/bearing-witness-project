Escape from prison, in the popular imagination and the movies, is seen primarily as a well-planned physical event, whether by building tunnels, scaling high walls or even climbing onto helicopters hovering over the prison yard. Marya Achtar for her project in the 2009 Prisoners' Rights seminar, titled “The Great Escape -Reattempted”, reflected on the multi- dimensional concept of escape, by weaving together her own prose and poetry and that of other authors, some well known, some not.

> This is an attempt to look deeper into the notion of escape and the many ways in which the stage of escape is accomplished by prisoners. Escape is more than merely running away from prison. It is the use of drugs, it is making a parallel world inside the walls, it is expressing yourself through art, it is hurting yourself, it is improving yourself, getting yourself ready for the outside and it is breaking yourself down, reaching a point where you no longer feel pain, it is reached through spirituality and healing sessions and through blindness and suicide. This paper is a compilation of various poems and excerpt of prose from writers, artists, prisoners – all whom I have listened to or read and who influence me. Belonging to a family who have fought, struggled and been imprisoned in the name of justice during the many turbulent times of Pakistan’s history – and eventually reached the status of political refugees -, escape, I find is more than merely running away.
>
> Anything which is not credited to someone is my own writing.

Here are parts of Marya’s word tapestry:

> ### On human interaction/solidarity (and the art of prison poetry)
> These poor men only needed to be allowed to live a little in their own way, to enjoy themselves like human beings, to escape the prison-camp routine just for an hour, - and they would be morally transformed, even if only for a few minutes.
>
> (Dostoevsky – The House of the Dead, my emphasis)
>
> I am here to serve my time. My punishment is confinement.
>
> What confinement? The one of the walls or the one building up inside me? Do I not show sufficient remorse if I try to escape? Physically? Mentally?
>
> Or if I attempt to escape knowing that I will be caught again; in the knowledge that these seconds (minutes? hours? days? months? years?) that I get will be the only ones. That freedom is a glimpse outside the walls. Am I sufficiently incarcerated if I escape by drugs? If I cut? If I write? If I sing or dance? Or cry or laugh?
>
> Inspired by:
>
> Every time I see a crack in the door I try to get a look on the other side. I figure I’m never going to get out of prison and so the days I take are the only days I’m ever going to get.
>
> (Dennis Smith’s evidence as recounted in Justice Behind the Walls by Michael Jackson)
>
> This prose is inspired by a BBC documentary titled “Stolen Dreams” made by my mother in the early 80’s on child prisons in Pakistan. She bribed the police, smuggled a camera in and started interviewing the kids there ---
>
> A street kid once, now a prisoner; when asked his name responds with his prison number. He proudly shows his battle wounds and scars, he smiles and laughs when asked what he’s in for. What did the judge sentence him? He’s never been tried. He is here because the streets needed to be cleaned up. Does he miss the world outside? Sure he does. But he is glad he gets food, and he’s made good friends. They are like brothers. Where is his family? He falls silent.
>
> ### On pain, hate and anger (and the art of prison poetry)
> I hurt myself today  \
> To see if I still feel  \
> I focus on the pain  \
> The only thing that's real  \
> The needle tears a hole \
> The old familiar sting  \
> Try to kill it all away  \
> But I remember everything\
> (Trent Reznor – Hurt)
>
> ### On beauty
> Each star a rung,\
> night comes down the spiral\
> staircase of the evening.\
> The breeze passes by so very close\
> as if someone just happened to speak of love.\
> In the courtyard,\
> the trees are absorbed refugees\
> embroidering maps of return on the sky.\
> On the roof,\
> the moon - lovingly, generously -\
> is turning the stars\
> into a dust of sheen.\
> From every corner, dark-green shadows,\
> in ripples, come towards me.\
> At any moment they may break over me,\
> like the waves of pain each time I remember\
> this separation from my lover.\
> This thought keeps consoling me:\
> though tyrants may command that lamps be smashed\
> in rooms where lovers are destined to meet,\
> they cannot snuff out the moon, so today,\
> nor tomorrow, no tyranny will succeed,\
> no poison of torture make me bitter,\
> if just one evening in prison\
> can be so strangely sweet,\
> if just one moment anywhere on this earth.
>
> (A Prison Evening - Faiz Ahmed Ahmed [1911-1984] acknowledged as one of the greatest Urdu poets in Pakistan, imprisoned and exiled for his political views, nominated shortly after his death for the Nobel Prize)
>
> ### On treatment
> “The Government cannot be concerned any longer with outmoded penological theories. Cram criminals together and see what happens. You get concentrated criminality, crime in the midst of punishment. Soon we may be needing all our prison space for political offenders” […] “You’re not cured yet. There’s still a lot to be done. Only when your body reacts promptly and violently to violence, as to a snake, without further help from us, without medication, only then – ”
>
> (Anthony Burgess – A Clockwork Orange)
>
> ### On restoration
> Suppose that confinement is not human. That it is not within human nature. Hence, the normal reaction to confinement is an attempt to escape.
>
> Suppose that what is natural is when a crime has been committed things return to status quo ante. This is done through redemption, through restoration.
>
> Greenlandic inuit societies call for a community meeting where the victim or relatives speak things through – an adequate punishment is decided upon in agreement with the violator or his representatives.\
> Tribal clans of Pakistan and Afghanistan apply the practice of blood money.
>
> Yet others practice magical healing.
>
> These are all customs from societies where the community is valued higher than individual losses.[^1] Here, confinement has little or no place. It serves neither victim nor violator. These are societies that have developed through hundreds of years. Confinement was introduced in the end of the 18th century. It should not be hard to suppose that confinement is not human.
>
> Of course, you cannot simply close down prisons on that rationale. Our society is literally built upon confinement. You only need your very basic Foucault to know that, or a look at a blueprint of the panopticon to see how it has influenced us.
>
> Suppose then, that confinement is here to stay. How many types of escapes shall we prohibit, how many shall we allow?
>
> A secret meeting is being held.\
> They sit in a corner in the common area and talk in low voices.\
> They appear the same way they would any day\
> But today is different\
> Today they are no longer confined\
> Today they set each other and themselves free\
> They are sisters in here\
> They are criminals seeking forgiveness and forgiving each other today.
