<p>Heather Burley’s collage, “Freedom Flaunted” was created for the 2016 Penal Policy seminar, and like Kirsten Barnes’, is one of a series in the archive that were inspired by visits students made to Matsqui Institution in Abbotsford BC. Matsqui is one of the sites of my research in several of my studies including <em>Justice behind the Walls: Human Rights in Canadian Prisons</em>. For over twenty years an integral part of my Penal Policy seminar was taking students to either Matsqui or Kent Maximum-Security institution to visit with prisoners. This was not like a prison tour where visitors are given limited access to some of the physical facilities; rather the visit is a meeting with a group of prisoners, many serving life sentences, where prisoners are given the opportunity to address the students with issues regarding the conditions of their confinement. In my meetings with prisoners beforehand I encouraged them to explain to the students why they, as law students, future lawyers and judges, should be concerned about the conditions of confinement and why, given the claims of other marginalized populations in Canada who have little access to legal services, the representation of prisoners should be given any priority. After the prisoners’ presentations, which are made in the absence of any correctional staff, the students and the prisoners break into small groups where they talk and listen over he course of several hours. Over the many years I have facilitated these meetings students have told me that the visit to Matsqui or Kent was the most enduring memory of their three years of law school and it is not surprising therefore that it has inspired so many projects. Students told me that being able to reflect on their meetings with the prisoners across a broad spectrum of media enabled them to process the experience and the lessons learned in ways that were both personal and profound.</p>

<p>Heather in her essay “Freedom Flaunted” describes how she experienced her visit to Matsqui in March 2016.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>On Tuesday March 1, 2016 we participated as seminar students in Professor Jackson's annual Penal Policy class trip to Matsqui Institution in Abbotsford, BC. I carpooled from the law school on UBC's Point Grey campus to the prison with four other students from the seminar class, leaving around 3pm to avoid traffic and arrive well before our 6pm scheduled visit. That Tuesday was a typically horrible March day in Vancouver with howling winds, black skies, and torrential downpours. In fact, a rainfall warning was in place. Needless to say this made for a less than pleasant drive through rush hour traffic on the highway. Towards the end of our journey several students were starting to remark on how tired they were already feeling and daunted at the long evening ahead of us, particularly given the ominous weather and the arduous commute. To be honest, we were not in the best of spirits and not nearly as excited as we should have been for the unique learning experience ahead of us.</p>

<p>However, as we pulled off the highway and enjoyed a surprisingly good meal at a local coffee shop before our visit, the rain began to die down . With food and coffee in our bellies and an increasingly brightening sky, we drove onto the prison grounds around 5:45pm, which, for that time of year in Vancouver, was around dusk. As we pulled up the driveway between the fences of Fraser Valley Institution and Matsqui Institution, I remarked on my sudden inclination as to how surreal it would be to be confined to the same physical space for a period of years, decades, or even the rest of one's life. Despite having resented the rain and traffic on the trek from Point Grey to Abbotsford, I suddenly felt a wave of gratitude for the often taken for granted ability to move freely from one place to another in daily life.</p>

<p>As we approached the parking lot, a flock of geese was gathered on the lawn just outside of Matsqui's fence. As we passed, several of them took flight. Already pondering the physical confinement of prison, I remarked to my colleagues that the geese almost seemed to be "flaunting their freedom" to those behind the walls by taking flight. My comment contained a note of melancholy, knowing that indeed the sweet simplicity of (physical) freedom was something that many of the inmates I was about to meet had not felt in years, probably longed for, and that some would never know again.</p>

<p>We proceeded through the sign in and security procedures and eventually settled in a room we were told was called "Social D." There were approximately equal numbers of inmates and students, roughly 15 of each. As we began our evening of conversation the first few minutes were conducted to the background sound of the honking of the geese. The door of our meeting room was open, leading down a corridor to the outside yard, and so we could hear clearly the birds as they flew over the social room. I remarked to myself again the juxtaposition of the lives of the birds to those of the inmates. I thought how cruel I might find it if I were an inmate, to see birds land and graze on the grounds of the prison only to fly off again to wherever they wanted whenever they wanted. To the birds, the prison grounds were just another gathering place to rest and peck for food but to the inmates it was a place of segregation and isolation, punishment and, supposedly though not realistically from what we heard, rehabilitation.</p>

<p>As the birds flew off overhead, the honking subsided and our conversations continued uninterrupted. I was surprised to hear, in addition to some tales of violence, abuse, sadness, and anger that I had anticipated, a series of extremely optimistic, insightful, and poignant tales of the inmates ' experiences of learning, personal growth, and healing behind the walls. Although the inmates I spoke to personally admitted that there were significant problems with the institution and its staff as well as the broader carceral system, I was humbled by the positive attitude and even gratitude they expressed for some of the things they had experienced or learned while confined in institutions.</p>

<p>This evoked for me the distinction between physical freedom and emotional and personal freedom. Although confined to a penitentiary, and despite difficult experiences behind and ahead of them, these men had found ways of building relationships, making connections, and bettering their own lives and selves, and attaining new and more profound levels of personal freedom.</p>

<p>Nevertheless, upon leaving the prison I once again remarked at my own ability to pick up and return to my regular life in Vancouver while these men returned to their cells on a patch of land where some of them would live out the rest of their days. When we exit ed the prison grounds, the flock of geese we had seen upon entry was gone.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This account, already evocative with geese flaunting their freedom, is transformed by Heather into a visual feast which in her accompanying essay explores many of the themes of the seminar including the physical, personal, and psychological nature of both freedom and imprisonment.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For my final project I decided to draw on the above experiences to create a visual piece that was reflective of my own personal insights from our visit to Matsqui and the symbolism and meaning that I drew from it. Two of the main components of the piece are the two opposing viewpoints illustrated on either side of the canvas. </p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Heather_Burley_01-1-1024x843.jpg" alt="Heather Burley's 2016 Collage titled &quot;Freedom Flaunted&quot;" class="wp-image-1822"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>The aerial view of Matsqui on the left is representative of the view that the geese, or "freedom flaunters" as I imagine them, would have when they take flight over the institution. It is at this point, when they have this view, that they are taking advantage of the physical freedom to leave the prison grounds, a liberty of which the inmates do not have the luxury. </p>

<p>The opposing view on the right is meant to reflect the visual perspective of the inmates on the ground, behind the walls of Matsqui, and is represented by the sky view of the geese flying in a V formation overhead. </p>

<p>I imagine the feeling of being confined behind the walls, watching these free creatures arrive and depart of their own free will, to be a frustrating but also potentially inspiring experience for inmates. While a reminder of their own confinement, the repeated arrival and departure of these migratory birds might also serve as a connection to nature, to the outside world, and to a society to which they hope one day to return. Corresponding to the image of the prison on the left is a series of other images, words, symbols and artifacts within the literal chains of the border that are meant to be symbolic of life and experiences within the correctional system and behind the walls of the prison. In contrast, the images on the right bordered by white feathers are representative of the physical, personal, and psychological freedom outside the walls.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Heather_Burley_03-1024x683.jpg" alt="Detail from Heather Burley's 2016 Collage titled &quot;Freedom Flaunted&quot;" class="wp-image-1817"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>The piece also contains a second V formation constituted entirely of words beginning with V. I decided to construct the V symbol using words beginning with V that I understood as significant to the experience of incarceration. A legend of those words and their ascribed meanings is located on the backside of the art piece itself During our early conversation in Social D, although I could not see the flock of geese, I could hear them collectively honking, communicating, and I pictured them travelling together in the traditional V formation. This led me to ponder the purpose and effect of their evolutionary decision to travel in such a way and to analogize it with the lives and experiences of the prisoners.</p>

<p>I recalled from basic high school science courses that geese are migratory birds, flying south each year for the winter but travelling in flocks regularly during all seasons. The V formation is a more efficient form of group travel because the shape and physics of it allow them to fly over 70% farther than by exerting the same efforts alone.1 As the geese flap their wings , this creates an " uplift" and by flying together they increase the impact and effectiveness of this draft. Each goose provides additional lift and reduces air resistance for the goose flying behind it. The geese take turns being at the front of the formation where there is no updraft, thereby rotating leadership . If one goose falls out of the formation, he or she will suddenly feel the drag and ineffectiveness of flying alone. As a result, the bird will quickly move back into the formation to take advantage again of the lifting power of the birds immediately in front of it. If one goose gets sick, a couple other birds will stay with it until it is able to rejoin the flock or has died. During flight, geese honk at each other to communicate.</p>

<p>While the image of geese flying together in a V formation and the underlying scientific rationale have obvious links to general concepts important to team work, the connection to prisoners and imprisonment is less clear. However, during my time at Matsqui and in my subsequent reflections, I personally saw the inmate community as its own sort of team or community. The stories of support and brotherhood that we heard from some of the inmates at Matsqui truly did reveal that they had built strong relationships with one another and frequently relied on each other for assistance or support, particularly in the face of corrupt, abusive or oppressive correctional staff One inmate had been incarcerated for over 30 years and had taken on a clear leadership role (the head of the V, if you will) assisting prisoners with grievances and the complaints procedure, and becoming proficient in prison law and policy in the process. His expertise seemed to " lift " other prisoners and ease the process of filing complaints and holding staff and management accountable, just as the goose at the head of the V provides much needed uplift to his fellow flyers. Further, much like the geese that drop out to help a sick of ailing flock mate, this prisoner came to the support of fellow inmates who had encountered issues or mistreatment in their experience of incar ceration This demonstrates the continued importance of building and maintaining relationships while in prison, both to improve the experience of incarceration but also to improve one's own chances of rehabilitation and of successful re-entry into one's community upon release.</p>

<p>I also drew a parallel between the geese who come and go from the prison grounds and the visitors who come see their incarcerated friends and family members, only to return to their outside daily lives afterwards. Much like these visitors, the geese have the freedom to come and go from the prison grounds at their own choosing whereas the inmates have lost that physical liberty. However, even the visitors are not as free as the geese since their arrival and departure from the institution is ultimately governed by the prison's visiting schedule. In this way, the flight of the geese also inspired me to think about the ripple effects of incarceration. Not only does incarceration imprison and limit the lives of inmates , but it also has a restricting ripple effect on the prisoners ' loved ones. This ripple effect contributes to cycles of trauma, violence, and incarceration that are so often seen in vulnerable communities .</p>

<p>I also included the poem " Wild Geese" by Mary Oliver on the artistic canvas.</p>

</figure>

<p>Although I do not claim to be a literary critic I nonetheless found relevance in the words of the poem to my understanding of the experiences of imprisonment and prisoners. The poem alludes to the importance of love, and the need only to share the stories of one' s own despair with others rather than to "walk on your knees for a hundred miles through the desert, repenting." This recalled for me the futility of the entire project of incarceration and the importance and potential of both rehabilitation and restorative justice. Rather than making prisoners suffer and be punished for their crimes, which are so often crimes of circumstance, public policy might be better aimed at improving the social conditions of those who go on to become criminals, and to providing genuine and viable opportunities for rehabilitation and reconciliation with victims. The poem also notes that " [m]eanwhile the world goes on… Meanwhile the wild geese, high in the clean blue air, are heading home again." This made me think again of the prisoners confined to the physical space of the prison while the world and the lives of everyone else continues on as usual, symbolized yet again by the flight of the geese overhead. The lines evoked the freedom of the geese just like those I saw at Matsqui, and reminded me of the very difficult transition many prisoners must eventually face if and when they are released after many years of incarceration. The world has gone on while they were inside and will have undoubtedly changed drastically, presenting a whole new set of challenges on top of the usual personal, financial, and emotional adjustments to life post-incarceration.</p>

<p>Oliver closes the poem by saying "[w]hoever you are, no matter how lonely, the world offers itself to your imagination, calls to you like the wild geese, harsh and exciting- over and over announcing your place in the family of things." This made me think of a few different things. Firstly, for me it speaks to the loneliness that prisoners must undoubtedly feel at times, but also the opportunity that such solitude presents for reflection and imagination. It also seems to imply that no matter where someone is situated in the world, no matter their circumstances, or any loneliness they may feel, they have a place in " the family of things." So, despite incarceration, segregation from society, and all the things that go along with that, an effective and humane penal policy should strive to recognize and emphasize that even inmates sentenced to incarceration for committing crimes remain members of humanity and should be treated as such no matter what, for they are always members of that broader family, community , and society.</p>

<p>The other overlapping collage imagery layers not only allowed me to fit in more creative aspects of my project but also represent the complexities of the prison experience. Some positive, some negative, some violent, some insightful , but all piling up on top of each other over time to ultimately re-create the prisoner, transforming him from who he was the day he was originally incarcerated into who he is ultimately upon his release. After hearing the stories of the Matsqui men , I understand the experience of incarceration to be a multi-faceted one where horrible, violent, and traumatizing things can happen, but also that the prison, and in particular a lower security level institution, can be a place of great potential for growth and healing . However, it also became clear that this growth and healing was usually as a result of the efforts and work of the prisoners alone rather than as a result of any institutional mandate, programming, or assistance. Most of the other imagery and words on the artistic piece speak for themselves but include images that evoke the racial and historical injustices of the Canadian carceral system, the juxtaposition of life inside behind the walls versus outside in the free world, and contrasting words and phrases that underline this distinction.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her conclusion to her essay Heather reflects on the value of both her visit to Matsqui and her creative work:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I found my time at Matsqui eye opening and transformative. It was a privilege to meet and hear from the inmates who surprised and humbled me with their insights, intelligence, and sincerity. The images and notions of freedom and belonging that I was inspired to ponder were valuable and I hope, well captured in this, my final project, "Freedom Flaunted." It was a unique and reflective experience to have the opportunity to channel my learning experience at Matsqui into a creative art piece rather than a traditional paper and for that, as well as all the learning I did in both the seminar and at the institution, I am also very grateful.</p>
</blockquote>
