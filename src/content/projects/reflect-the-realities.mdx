<p>I have earlier discussed <a href="/projects/militant-gispewada/">Christina Gray</a>’s project and her successful initiative in persuading the Law Society of Ontario that she could wear her indigenous ceremonial regalia at a bar admission ceremony. In my classes in the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar, while discussing the <em>Delgamuukw</em> case, I show a series of photographs, one with the Gitxsan and Wetsuwet’in Hereditary Chiefs outside the Vancouver Courthouse in their ceremonial regalia, and another with the legal team in their black barristers robes. I commented that the Hereditary Chiefs looked far more impressive than their lawyers.</p>

<p>Pavanpreet Sran, a student in the 2021 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation seminar, not only agreed with my assessment, but for her seminar project set about combining her legal studies with her expertise as a seamstress, in creating a barrister’s robe that represents the pluralistic legal nature of Canadian society and reflected both indigenous and colonial legal traditions. Knowing that I was a member of the legal team representing the Haida Nation in their Aboriginal title case, she asked me whether it would be possible for her to craft a robe incorporating indigenous Haida images. Understandably she was concerned about issues of cultural appropriation. Based upon my consultation with Haida artists I advised her that since this was a not a commercial project but one designed to be used only for the purposes of legal education, it would be permissible to make such a robe provided that proper attribution was given to the artists’ whose designs she drew upon. In her essay describing both the analytic framework and the laborious process of creating the robe, Pavan incorporated the lessons learned from Christina Grey’s initiative in 2015 and Chief Justice Finch’s challenge to the legal profession in his 2012 CLE paper “The Duty to Learn: Taking Account of Indigenous Legal Orders in Practice ”.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I appreciate the value in requiring lawyers to wear identical black robes. Robes not only signify respect for the judicial system, but also reduce prejudice arising from appearance. By obscuring differences in appearance, robes make lawyers appear more “equal” to court participants (e.g., clients, juries, and other lawyers). In addition to helping decision makers focus on laws, facts, and submissions, a reduction in potential distractions also helps protect lawyers’ against their skills and knowledge being superseded by irrelevant factors. However, I recognize that a robe’s ability to reduce prejudice arising from appearance is limited to what it can conceal; unfortunately, prejudice continues to arise from factors that remain unconcealed, such as age, gender, and ethnicity.</p>

<p>Through a video project, But I Was Wearing a Suit, Indigenous lawyers recently shared their experiences with racism and stereotyping in the practice of law. The project hoped to reveal and encourage discussion about how Indigenous people are being treated within the legal profession. Most stories involved Indigenous lawyers being regularly mistaken for victims or clients and not being recognized as lawyers by others in the courthouse. One could argue that wearing a robe could help mitigate these issues by making these individuals identifiable as lawyers. However, if wearing a robe that symbolizes the colonial legal system is necessary to make Indigenous lawyers recognizable as legal professionals, it does not create equality but rather forces upon the Indigenous lawyer a colonial identity in order to make them “more acceptable” to the colonial legal system.</p>

<p>Gowning is a practice steeped in traditions; the robes and practice have changed little since their inception. For court apparel to remain a “positive symbol of the legal profession,” it should “reflect the realities” of the legal professionals wearing it. Unfortunately, the unchanging design of Canadian court apparel does not reflect the realities of all its wearers. In 2012, Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg revealed that she felt that judicial robes did not represent women and were “made with men’s ties and trouser pockets in mind… [so she and a colleague] broke the plain black monotony by wearing a variety of lace collars, and [they] also added sewn-in pockets.” During my Judicial Externship at the BC Provincial Court, I saw judges following Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg’s lead by wearing a variety of tabs (e.g., pleated satin and lace) to express their identities. I also followed her lead and made non-traditional tabs; the design that I put on the tabs is a replication of the ring of The Council of the Haida Nation’s insignia I believe it is time to reconsider the design of robes in relation to Indigenous peoples. There are multiple legal orders co-existing in Canada. From the perspective of Indigenous people, “…Indigenous legal traditions stand alongside the civil and common law. While reflecting on a statement of the Court in <em>R v Delgamuukw</em>, the former Chief Justice of British Columbia, Lance Finch, said that: “[if]… we are to find space for multiple legal orders to co-exist, and if we are ultimately to achieve an equal reconciliation, we must recognize that to stay must also be to learn.” </p>

<p>I believe that space already exists; one underutilized area is on a robe. In their current form, robes remain a relic of colonial clothing, representative of a system that has “routinely diminished” and “often unacknowledged” the “fundamental contribution” of Indigenous people and their legal traditions, in spite of the fact that “Indigenous legal traditions form part of the basis upon which the current system stands.” Indigenous people were likely left out of the conversation when the policies around gowning were created; it is unfair for them to be bound by these policies. We need to learn about and embrace what Indigenous people would wear to ceremonies of this nature and understand that to do so would not detract from colonial legal traditions. If the system cannot permit them to wear the regalia of their choice, at the very least their traditions should be integrated into colonial court apparel. If the same system that has oppressed and discriminated against Indigenous people were to overtly recognize their identities and contributions to the justice system through integration of their traditions on robes, it would be a step towards reconciliation.</p>

<p>For this project, I tried to design a robe that represents the pluralistic legal nature of Canadian society. My goal was to design a robe that honoured both Indigenous and colonial legal traditions. From my Judicial Externship experience, I learned that lawyers have two roles in the courtroom: they are both officers of the court and representatives of their clients. I wanted to incorporate both of these responsibilities into the design. I tried to create a fusion between a robe and a blanket; my goal was to strike a balance between a robe and a blanket that would be difficult for a court or Law Society to reject.</p>

<p>In recent years, the Law Society of BC and BC Courts have allowed items of religious and cultural significance to be worn during the Call to the Bar ceremonies and sessions of court. For instance, in 2013, courts began allowing Kirpans to be worn to court. My parents, who are Sikh immigrants, have been allowed to wear items that identify them as Sikh (e.g., Kirpan or Turban) to court since at least 2013. On the other hand, Indigenous people pre-date the system of laws in Canada, their “legal traditions were the first laws of [this] land…[and] continue to form part of the legal fabric of Canada,” and yet, they were not allowed to wear items that signify their identity to the Call to the Bar ceremonies until at least 2015. In 2015, the Law Society of Upper Canada initially rejected Christina Gray’s request to wear her Tsimshian button blanket and cedar bark hat to her Call to the Bar Ceremony. For Christina Gray, a member of the Gispewada clan, it was an important matter of cultural and legal identity. Her feelings at the time were justified - being forced to wear a lawyer’s robe without her regalia “was an impediment to recognizing Aboriginal peoples and [their] laws.” In her response to the Law Society, Christina Gray described that “[the] button blanket truly signifies to others [their] identity. It represents [their] inherited rights, laws, culture, family, and customs.” The button blanket is a “coded legal document” which “proclaims heredity rights, obligations and powers.” Christina Gray says “the blanket represents ayaawx, which are Tsimshian traditional laws, precedents and ways of being.”</p>

<p>In their initial rejection of Christina Gray’s request, the Law Society stated that the “ceremony is officially a session of the court” and that “candidates are traditionally required to wear robes” to make “everyone equal, so nobody stands out.” Upon receiving letters from Christina Gray and the community, it appears as if the Law Society of Upper Canada put former Chief Justice Lance Finch’s words into action by “learn[ing] about the importance of the regalia, and its relevance to legal and cultural traditions.” The Law Society of Upper Canada eventually permitting Christina Gray to wear her regalia in the ceremony, what they view as an official session of court, suggests that they may be open to engage in meaningful dialogue about the much needed change to court apparel. While giving her permission, they also confirmed their “commitment to diversity and reconciliation.” Given these indicators of flexibility, I believe now is the time to “[break] the plain black monotony” and start integrating Indigenous traditions into robes.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Pawan’s essay concludes with her reflections on her robe project and how it relates to her understanding of her future role in the legal profession.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I am grateful to have had the opportunity to reflect on what I learned this semester through an apparel design project; it brought my passions for the law and designing clothes together. I am also grateful to have had an opportunity to learn how button blankets are made from Indigenous artists.</p>

<p>This robe is a representation of the type of lawyer I want to become. Across Canada, “there is a growing recognition…of the need for a deeper understanding and more meaningful inclusion of the Indigenous Peoples of Canada.” For the reason that “our legal orders and law are entirely created within our cultures, it is difficult to see and understand law in other cultures.” I grew up in an education system that did not present an opportunity to learn about Indigenous people. It was in my first couple days of law school that I realized how much I do not know. Since then I have been working on becoming more culturally competent (e.g., I completed the Indigenous Cultural Competency Certificate program). To improve my cultural competency in Indigenous cultures, I plan to read the Guide for Lawyers Working with Indigenous Peoples this summer. I will use this guide as a starting point to “learn about Indigenouscultures and understand the interplay between Indigenous legal orders and the Canadian legal system.” I recognize, however, that this is only one step toward preparing me to become the best version of myself for my clients.</p>

<p>I will strive to become a truly competent lawyer. I will continue to develop my skills and expand my knowledge. When engaging in legal analysis, I will strive to not only understand the facts and the law,but also the social context from which the problems arise. I will strive to build relationships of respect and trust with my clients and others around me. I will work on my ability to read verbal and nonverbal cues. I will try to notice and address gaps in communication. I want to be sensitive to different cultural understandings and how these cultural differences can affect others’ expectations of the legal process and their interactions. I will continue to self-reflect and self-monitor in both my personal and professional life.I will improve my awareness about how stereotypes and assumptions affect my thoughts and perceptions and work to reduce their impact. I grew up in and have advocated for people in diverse and marginalized communities. I am aware of the challenges that diverse and marginalized communities face, and I want tobring that awareness into my research forever.</p>
</blockquote>
