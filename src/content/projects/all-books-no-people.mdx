<p>Robyn Gervais, as a companion to her storybook Trail of Tears, in a Directed Research project in 2007, created a painting reflecting on her law school experience and the state of legal pedagogy. </p>

</figure>

<p>In her accompanying essay Robyn explained:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Given that I am nearing the end of my law degree, I wanted to provide some sort of visual representation of my law school experience. Law school was a struggle for me on many levels, and I grappled with what theme to present. I began with two ideas. One was to provide a visual representation of all the confusion I felt (and still feel) about what career path to follow. In the end I decided that there is too much confusion to come up with a satisfactory visual. The other idea grew out of two words that continually swirl around in my head when I think about my law school experience - books and people. These two words form the title of my painting, All Books No People.</p>

<p>Before exploring this theme, I would like to say that this is in no way meant to derogate the education I have received at UBC Law School. All of my professors have been helpful, and kind. Nor is this meant to be a bitter rant from a student who never achieved top grades, despite valiant efforts. This is meant only to be a critique of the pedagogy surrounding law school, which is inevitably carried forward into the profession, mindset, and lifestyle of many lawyers. The painting represents the inordinate emphasis placed on academia in law school. I chose the colors in the painting to represent the predominant style of teaching and evaluation in law school. The grey in the painting represents the cold, monotonous, analytical style of teaching that prevails throughout law school. The blue represents a safe, numbing, hospital type feeling that is associated with stagnancy.</p>

<p>In a perfect world, the law would be taught in a storytelling fashion, with the inclusion of people who certain laws have affected. Grades as a result of rote memorization, handwriting/typewriting speed, and one's capability to think quickly and act quicker, would be a thing of the past. Although there are clear exceptions to this rule, in general law school is culturally blind and ignorant. At this time, I am in the midst of writing a paper on the topic of whether child protection mediation is flexible enough to meet the needs of First Nations people. I have interviewed nine First Nations people from across BC who are currently training as mediators. The one common critique that all of the trainees identified is that the current model of mediation does not allow participants enough time to think and reflect on decisions being made. In the words of one participant "a lot of First Nations people believe that the more serious the matter, the more slowly you should go in terms of discussing and offering solutions to resolve it." Clearly the law school examination process offers little if any time for reflection. What could be more important than taking time to reflect when dealing with such serious matters? So why are law students rewarded for making quick, snappy judgments?</p>

<p>There are strong indications that legal pedagogy is evolving, but I look forward to further development. I hope in twenty years the typical law school exam no longer exists. I hope that students will be valued and rewarded for a diversity of skills. I hope that first year students can finish the year with their self-esteem intact.<sup data-fn="02e8d9af-8508-4895-b184-e8fc0ac1401d" class="fn"><a href="#02e8d9af-8508-4895-b184-e8fc0ac1401d" id="02e8d9af-8508-4895-b184-e8fc0ac1401d-link">1</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
