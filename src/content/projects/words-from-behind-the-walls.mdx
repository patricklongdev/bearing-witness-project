<p>Ashley Syer’s work for the 2009 Penal Policy seminar, “Words from behind the Walls” was the final project in her academic career and in offering some of her own poetry she candidly explained how the seminar, and particularly the visit with prisoners at Matsqui, was an important part of her change in opinion about criminals and imprisonment:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>When I signed up to take your class, it was really just so I could see what the inside of a prison looks like. I do not intend to go into criminal law, and I thought that this would likely be my only chance to see what things looked like from the other side of the bars. I did not have any great interest in the plight of prisoners in Canadian institutions. If I am to be perfectly honest, I did not care. Part of me believed that if a person did a terrible thing, they deserved to live in terrible conditions and suffer as punishment for the suffering they had caused others to endure. I was one of the people who believed that simply being incarcerated was not necessarily sufficient punishment, but that having to live in hellish conditions was part of the punishment. I was never one who advocated or believed in poor treatment of inmates by prison guards, nor did I believe in deprivation of basic human rights, but I did believe that prison life should be a thoroughly unpleasant experience.</p>

<p>I had always empathized with victims of crime, and had tried to imagine how I would feel about a person who had committed a horrible wrong against me or someone I loved. I always imagined that I would want that person to rot in a tiny, miserable cell for as long as they could be kept there. I made no distinction between the person who committed the crime and the crime itself. In my mind, the criminal was his crime.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Ashley explained in her essay that her conversations with a Norwegian student who was a prison guard at Stavanger Prison in Norway when they were both on a student exchange in Scotland initiated her change in attitude:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I asked if she_was every afraid to be in such close proximity to people who had proven themselves to be violent criminals. She told me that, while they had all done very bad things, they were not necessarily bad people, and that she just tried to put aside what they had done and treat them like she would treat anyone else. It was after this conversation that I began to think that maybe my perspective on prisons and prisoners was a bit skewed.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>A second encounter with the physical space and violent history of Kilmainham Gaol in Dublin deepened Ashley’s reappraisal:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I was not sure what to expect when I arrived, but what I saw was certainly more than I ever could have imagined. It was one of the most haunting (and probably haunted) places I have ever visited. It was originally built to house 120 prisoners, but at its peak held around nine thousand men, women, and children. It was built with the goal of keeping prisoners separate from each other, where each would have his own cell with one tiny glassless, barred window to the outside world. The newer wing was built in the style of Foucault's panopticon, so every cell could be viewed from a single point in the room, and inmates would never know when they were being watched. I was told that when the prison was still operational, there was carpeting along the main hall so that inmates would not be able to hear the footsteps of the guards, so would never know if a guard was coming to look through the peephole in the cell door. This was a place of public hangings, firing squads, and people living in degrading, squalid conditions. We were told stories about some of the prisoners and were given the opportunity to read some of the letters they had written to their families while they were jailed in Kilmainham. This was the first time I had really thought about what prison life might be like for the prisoners who had to live there. It was terrifying.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Penal Policy seminar meeting prisoners at Matsqui was the final part of Ashley’s reassessment:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>When I arrived in your class, I was still unsure of where I stood in relation to my feelings about prisons and the people who live in them. As appalled as I had been with what I had seen at Kilmainham, I kept telling myself that it was not the same because that was how things were in troubled times in Ireland, not how things were today in Canada. We were not locking people up because of their political beliefs or because they stole food to feed their families, we were locking them up because they had done horrible things. And, while I kept in mind the conversation I had had with my friend Karen, I still found myself unable to separate the criminal from the crime. I think the most accurate way to describe my feelings about the situations of prisoners in Canadian prisons was confusion.</p>

<p>As I began to hear your stories about inmates, their experiences, and the conditions that they lived in, my confusion deepened. I was still torn between embracing the logic that I saw in your book and heard in your words and the feelings that I had held so long about prisoners being deserving of whatever faced them in prison. I think the shift in my feelings came when you showed us the video from Kwikwexwelhp. The men in that video did not look the way I had always imagined criminals to look. By this I mean that they did not appear to be the monsters I had always imagined when I had pictured what a murderer or an armed robber would appear to be. They were just like anyone else.</p>

<p>The visit to Matsqui was the real turning point for me. I was not sure what to expect when we arrived, but I am not sure that I was prepared for the reality of it. That visit had a profound effect on me. I kept finding myself wondering how I could have failed to separate these men from their crimes for so long. After all, I have now had two classmates in my lifetime (that I know of) who have killed, and I know that they are not monsters. I was shocked. And I was so moved by what some of these men had to say. Some of them have been in prison as long as I have been alive, yet if I had met any one of them on the street, I would never have thought, now there is a hardened criminal, or a bank robber, or a killer.</p>

<p>After that visit I have had many conversations with classmates, friends, and family about crime, prisons, and prisoners. I have challenged those who hold the beliefs that I used to cling to so strongly that there must be a better way.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The meeting with prisoners at Matsqui also became the inspiration for Ashley’s poems.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For my final project, I did not want to give you just another research paper. I thought something more reflective would be appropriate as this truly is my final project of my academic career. I am a hobby poet, and I found inspiration in some of the words that I read and heard from the inmates you introduced us to. I would like to share some of those words with you here.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-left">Choice</h3>

<p class="has-text-align-left">One bad decision One wrong turn<br />That is why l am here That is all that separates us<br />We started out the same<br />With hopes and dreams and endless possibilities Then I said yes and you said no<br />That is all that separates us<br />We both know the law<br />I have been beaten by it, you have used it to beat me Now I stare out at you through these bars<br />That is all that separates us</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-left">Respect</h3>

<p>Respect does not exist for us<br />Everyone out there sees only the bad<br />Someday they will see that what we are<br />People first, prisoners second<br />Every day they talk about<br />Correcting us, but<br />Time is all we have to do</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-left">Home</h3>

<p>I've been in 23 years<br />I have no family<br />I have no friends<br />I have no community<br />I have no hope<br />They open my door and tell me that I am free<br />Free to do what<br />This place is all I know<br />This is my home<br />This is my world<br />I step out into the rain<br />I walk toward the gate<br />There is a plastic water bottle impaled on the razor wire<br />I always thought that would be the only way out<br />I am more scared to leave<br />Than I was when I got here<br />That was another lifetime<br />Back then I had hope<br />Now I have nothing but eighty dollars in a savings account<br />That doesn't get you much these days<br />I am a free man<br />But I have never felt less free<br />I have nowhere to go<br />I am shut out of my home<br />I turn back around and start banging on the doors<br />Let me back in<br />This is all wrong<br />I just want to come home</p>
</blockquote>
