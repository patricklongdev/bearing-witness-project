<p>The Numbered Treaties, beginning twenty one years after the Robinson Huron and Robinson Superior Treaties, are the subject of a broad spectrum of projects in the Archive. Before introducing them it will be helpful to give visitors an overview drawn from the Canadian Encyclopedia:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Numbered Treaties were a series of 11&nbsp;treaties&nbsp;made between the&nbsp;Crown&nbsp;and First Nations from 1871 to 1921. The Numbered Treaties cover the area between the&nbsp;Lake of the Woods&nbsp;(northern&nbsp;Ontario, southern&nbsp;Manitoba) to the&nbsp;Rocky&nbsp;Mountains (northeastern&nbsp;British ColumbiaMountains&nbsp;(northeastern&nbsp;British Columbia&nbsp;and interior Plains of&nbsp;Alberta) to the&nbsp;Beaufort Sea&nbsp;(north of&nbsp;Yukon&nbsp;and the&nbsp;Northwest Territories).</p>

<p>The years immediately following Confederation were characterized by the Canadian government’s desire to expand westward as a means of securing the nation’s economic future. The lands of the&nbsp;Hudson Bay Company, namely&nbsp;Rupert’s Land&nbsp;and the&nbsp;North-Western Territory, were of particular interest to expansionists because they were sources of valuable natural resources and vast space, which could be used for European immigration and industrial and commercial developments. With the Americans expanding westward, many Canadian politicians feared their neighbours to the south would annex these lands (see&nbsp;Manifest Destiny). In order to extend its&nbsp;sovereignty&nbsp;over portions of northern and western Canada, the federal government acquired Rupert’s Land and the North-Western Territory in 1870.</p>

<p>As part of the Hudson Bay Company’s obligations for the transfer of Rupert’s Land and the North-Western Territory to the federal government, Canada had to address all&nbsp;Indigenous&nbsp;claims to those lands. Based on the model of the 1850 Robinson Treaties the Crown signed 11 treaties with various First Nations between 1871 and 1921 that would allow the&nbsp;Crown&nbsp;access to, and jurisdiction over, traditional territories in exchange for certain promises and goods, such as&nbsp;reserve&nbsp;lands, annual payments and hunting and fishing rights to unoccupied crown lands.</p>

<p>The treaties provided the Crown with land for industrial development and White settlement. In exchange for their traditional territory, government negotiators made various promises to First Nations — both orally and in the written texts of the treaties — including special rights to treaty lands and the distribution of cash payments, hunting and fishing tools, farming supplies, and the like. These terms of agreement are controversial and contested. To this day, the Numbered Treaties have ongoing legal and socioeconomic impacts on Indigenous communities.</p>

<p>The written record of Treaty 3 contain parts of the speech of Chief Mawedopenais:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This is what we think, that the Great Spirit has planted us on this ground where we are, as you were where you came from. We think where we are is our property. I will tell you what he said to us when he planted us here; the rules that we should follow—us Indians—He has given us rules that we should follow to govern us rightly.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This statement by Anishinaabe chief of ownership and jurisdiction in 1873 echoes the speech of the Huadenoshaunee Chief Canassatego in 1744:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>You came out of the Ground in a Country that lies beyond the Seas, there you may have a just Claim, but here you must allow us to be your elder Brethren, and the Lands to belong to us long before you knew anything of them.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>As exemplified in Treaty 3 the English text of the Numbered Treaties was drafted by government lawyers and is transactional in terms of stipulating that the Indian treaty partners surrender all of their title and rights to the traditional territory in exchange for what are said to be reciprocal benefits of reserves, hunting fishing and trapping rights, assistance in moving to an agricultural economy, and annuities in the form of annual individual monetary payments. A common theme of the projects in the Archive is the exploration of the indigenous understanding of the treaties and their very different and deeper conception of treaty-making that goes beyond the text of the document.</p>
