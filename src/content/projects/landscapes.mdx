Hasna Syed, outside of law school is an accomplished musical performer whose band DEYS writes music about global problems, human rights, and social justice. Their song "Raise Our Voices," is featured with the United Nations and Jeunesses Musicales International, for the International Labour Organization (ILO)'s Music Against Child Labour campaign. For her 2019 project for Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Hasna composed a song "Landscapes" with an accompanying essay that explores the issues associated with crating a legal landscape that includes Indigenous legal orders. This is part of that exploration:

> The overarching theme I tried to convey through this project is tied to what we learned in the course on "Making Space for Indigenous Legal Orders" and the "Duty to Learn," and what I interpret the course overall tried to instill in students. My goal for this project was to have the song convey and have the effect of illustrating that my generation or this "next" generation of lawyers and judges have a responsibility and duty to learn about different Indigenous legal orders and recognize that these should be balanced and included with our current Common Law. My written analysis of this project is focused more on the 'legal perspective' and in recognition that reconciliation is a complex matter. The song, "Landscapes", is meant to emphasize and narrate for law students and legal practitioners the approach we should be taking when practicing the law, as well as how our legal system in Canada should be applied broadly. It is about transcending our presupposed notions on what constitutes "Aboriginal law" from a Eurocentric lens, and reformatting our preconceptions to determine ways to question our approaches to improve and integrate a multifaceted "Indigenous lens."
>
> Canada has always been and continues to be a "multi-jural" nation. This is reflected in our historical and contemporary cultural and territorial landscape. Upon interviewing Terri-Lyn Williams about her legal work and musical career, I was inspired to pick a word that would form the foundation of the lyrical component of my song. After doing several read-throughs of readings assigned for this course, words that I contemplated using as a basis for the song included: nation, "multi-jural," making space, duty, challenge, supernatural, sacred, teachings, respect, and understanding. While all of these words and phrases do play a role in the song and lyrics to some extent, the vigor of the word "landscape" I found to best encompass the message, tone, and effect of the theme and concept of 'duty to learn', 'mutual respect', and 'creating space' that I was trying to convey both metaphorically and practically/legally.
>
> As Finch CJ articulates, "from the outset of our education (as lawyers) we are immersed in a particular context and point of view. This saturation far transcends our legal training…by our understanding of place, kinship, and ideas about personhood." If our own educational legal foundation is not strong enough, that is, has not given a solid basis to critically think about our position as future legal practitioners and the role of our profession as it ought to intersect with Indigenous legal orders and society, then we cannot move forward with reconciliation or improving our domestic judicial system. The song, "Landscapes" attempts to question this and make listeners think about 'unlearning' and also encouraging aspirations to sharpen the tools and avenues used to create a lasting change in our legal practices through recognizing our history of colonial enterprise that still exists today. This allows us to look and analyze the legal system as it exists currently and think more deeply and critically about what it should look like – the first step towards creating positive change.
>
> Additionally, one of the crucial lines in the song is the lyric in the chorus, "to move forward we need to look back." This lyric has a multitude of meanings behind it. It starts from a place of acknowledging that we, as both current and incoming legal practitioners, must be communicating with Indigenous communities or at the very least trying to understand our positionality in relation to the history of Indigenous peoples in Canada. It is important for practitioners to be learning from our past, particularly Canada's colonial history and how that history continues to play a role in our legal system, academic, and landscape today. The lyrics are also meant to be interpreted broadly and were written in a way to communicate with different parties and generations on how they can come to an understanding through their own lens but also in a way that is communicative and respectful of the other "side" – Indigenous communities and practices.

As with other projects in the Archive that use media in which as a law professor I have no expertise Hasna provides insights into the process and architecture of musical composition. Significantly, the structure of her composition is linked to the restructuring of legal relationships with Indigenous peoples:

> Musically, the song uses a mix of minor and major chords, which I intentionally used to illustrate this balance that is needed in our legal system in order to make it "sound." The major chords give a hopeful and positive undertone, to show that that there remains work to be done by legal practitioners to include Indigenous ideas and orders but that collectively we can move forward in an effective way. The minor chords are there to acknowledge our country's colonial history and the lasting ramifications that has had on Indigenous communities. This is why mixing both is important because not only does it instill the importance of relationship-building, but also emphasizing that we cannot mix and balance ideas and practices and move forward without truly acknowledging our past and peoples.
>
> The process of finalizing the piece of music was also important to this project. As the music software used requires mixing both the instrument and vocal sound. Again, this act of physically combining variant aspects enabled me to de-tach from one "way" of doing things, and instead enter a space that allowed me to blend and form something special built on an end-goal. Applying this artistic concept to the institutionalized legal system, we can come to an understanding that there is no certainty on one "right" way towards reconciliation, but that a blending of perspectives is necessary.
>
> Overall, this project has been the most meaningful to me throughout my time in law school. While on an academic level, it enabled me to read through and think critically about case law and papers that touch the hallmarks of Indigenous legal practices, issues, and experiences, it also highlighted where the Canadian common law has the potential to be moving to. On a personal level, the process of creating this song was a reminder to recognize there is much work to be done and that I have a responsibility as a future legal practitioner to include Indigenous narratives while recognizing where my positionality fits into that, and that the legal system needs my generation to collectively advocate for understanding, justice, context, and respect for Indigenous peoples. Sharing understanding and knowledge, and respecting a kind of reciprocate principle between a multi-principled system, can be a strong start to move forward for "making space" for Indigenous legal orders in the Canadian legal system and practice as it currently stands.
