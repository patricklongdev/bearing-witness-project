Nigel Genier-Baker, for his project in the 2018 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation seminar set an ambitious precedent in staging his  dance performance before the entire law school community in the Franklin Forum. Nigel belongs to the Gisgahaast clan from the Gitxsan Nation and is also Swampy Cree from Churchill, Manitoba. In addition to being a law student Nigel was and is  a lead dancer for Dancers of Damelahamid, an Indigenous dance company based in Vancouver. His 2018 dance performance Titled ‘Talking Past Each Other’ addressed one of the most important challenges in the legal field of understanding and respecting Indigenous peoples relationships to their territory and  the differences between Indigenous and settler perspectives, conceptually, spiritually and spacially. A regular feature of my seminars is having students share/audition their projects with other students in the last class. Nigel took the leap in sharing his performance with all his peers and professors.

In this narrative Nigel takes you into the world view, oral histories and laws of the Gitksan Nation, integral parts of which were  also placed in evidence by the Gitksan and Wet’suwet’en Hereditary Chiefs in BC Supreme Court in the Delgamuukw trial but were in equal measure  misunderstood, or dismissed by the trial judge, Chief Justice McEachern,  reflecting the importance of rising to  Nigel’s challenge of not ‘Talking Past Each Other’ if reconciliation is ever to be achieved. Following Nigel’s explanatory essay I have provided some additional context from my
experience in *Delgamuukw*.

> ## Part One: Relationship with Land 
>
> ### Bathing and Hunting 
>
> The first main theme of Talking Past Each Other is, ‘What is our relationship with the Land?’ The piece begins with a scene where the protagonist undergoes a cleansing ceremony which lasts for four days. Each morning at dawn, the hunter bathes in the icy cold waters of a mountain creek. He chews devils club, swallowing its juices. He stays in isolation to pray and reflect. He abstains from engaging in sexual activity during the cleansing ceremony. Gitxsan people believe that animals can perceive more than just the physical aspects of human beings, they can also see our souls. Animals will only allow a hunter to catch them if his soul and intentions are pure. The cleansing ceremony purifies the soul of the hunter and improves the chances of a successful hunt. Following the ceremony, the protagonist goes on a hunt and travels through the forest.
>
> The connection between people and the natural world is one of the most fundamental relationships within Ayuuk (Gitxsan law). In our origin story, Gilouli (creator) placed the first Gitxsan ancestors in a place we call Damelahamid (paradise), founding our relationship with the land. Gitxsan Ayook (law) reflects an underlying ethic of reciprocal relationships with non-human life forms. The relationship between Gitxsan people and the land is formalized through our Ayook (laws) regarding land tenure. In this complex legal system, territories are collectively held by the Wilp (house). The Wilp is the fundamental unit of social organization in Gitxsan society. It means ‘house,’ referring to the longhouses that Gitxsan people historically lived in. The Wilp does not own the land in a western sense, it acts as a steward of the territory. This relationship is reciprocal for the land provides everything required to ensure our survival. Therefore, Gitxsan people often refer to land as ha’ni t’ookxw, a table or an luu to'os't, a storage area to acknowledge the abundance which it provides.
>
> The relationship between human beings and the natural world is also governed by Ayuuk Niiye’e. Ayuuk Niiye’e translates as ‘laws of grandfather,’ referring to the laws that stem from Gilouli. The central characteristic of the relationship between Gitxsan and Gilouli has been a pattern of divine punishment and reward in response to human behaviour. When the people of Damelahamid live in accordance with Ayook Niiye’e they flourish. When they breach these laws, punishment follows swift and severe. Over the course of history, Gitxsan people have engaged in an intergenerational learning process where we have identified the obligations which we hold to Gilouli through trial and error. Some of the key principles include; that we must take only what we need, that we do not hunt for sport, that we give thanks for what we take, and that we share what we take with those in need, to name a few. Ayook Niiye’e is an articulation of the moral concepts of right and wrong. What is good does not harm the people. What is wrong leads to sheer destruction. Thus, Ayuuk Niiye’e provides both a legal and moral basis for the principles governing the relationship between the human and natural world. 
>
> ### Forest Fire and Lament 
>
> Following the cleansing ceremony and hunting, the protagonist in Talking Past Each Other hears a forest fire in the distance. He becomes alert and concerned. He frantically travels across the stage until he arrives at his village, which has been destroyed by the fire. He looks across the ashen remnants of the village and sings a lament. The lament is presented in the style of a limk oo’y, a mourning song derived from a tragic event in Adaawk (oral history).
>
> The symbolism of the fire is two-fold. First, it is an illusion to a historical event which took place in 1872, when prospectors from the Omineca gold rush burned the village of Gitsegukla to the ground. While no lives were lost, twelve longhouses and the belongings of their inhabitants were destroyed. Following the fire, the Simgeeget (Chiefs) from Gitsegukla met with representatives from the Province of British Columbia on board a British naval vessel situated near Metlakatla, Canada to negotiate the appropriate compensation for the loss sustained by the people of Gitsegukla. During the negotiations, the two sides exchanged gifts and formed a peace pact between the Gisgahaast and the Province. This is the only event since contact that has been significant enough to be formally incorporated into the Adaawk of my Wilp. We call this event ‘Stem Botan Boston’ which is a Gitxsan pronunciation of ‘Steamboat from Boston.’ The significance of Stem Botan Boston derives from the solemn pact that was formed between the Gisgahaast and the Province, which was the first time that the two entities had entered a legal relationship. This story is also significant today because of the legal norms and principles that it contains. I chose to incorporate the fire from Stem Botan Boston into Talking Past Each Other as a reflection of the colonial relationship with land that was established during the gold rush. It was a time when prospectors had no concept of Aboriginal rights or title. Their actions were driven by greed and their relationship with Indigenous people was permeated with racism. The fire is a symbol of the harm that extractive industries can cause to Indigenous people and the history of colonization that Indigenous people have endured. 
>
> The second meaning behind the fire is to act as a metaphor for capitalism. By capitalism, I am referring to the belief system that sees the natural world only as a collection of resources which can be exploited and turned into profit. The process of exploitation is seen by the colonizer as both an inevitable outcome of progress and an inherent good for the prosperity of society. Capitalism is intertwined with settler colonialism, in which the displacement of Indigenous peoples allows for foreign populations to simultaneously settle the land and exploit its resources. Both capitalism and settler colonialism harm Indigenous peoples through the processes of physical dispossession of land and the ideological displacement of Indigenous laws concerning relationship to land.

> ## Part Two: Sovereignty
>
> ### Blanketing and Limk Tsim Halyte 
>
> Following the lament, the piece transitions into the second main theme, ‘What is sovereignty?’ Members of the protagonist’s community walk on to the stage bringing a gwis halyte (blanket), am halyte (headdress), and haseex (rattles). They uplift him by standing him up and dressing him. The protagonist dances a limk tsim halyte (chief’s headdress song). This style of song was traditionally danced by a young Simoigit (chief) in Wilp Li’ligit (the Feast Hall) when he received his title. In Gitxsan society, names are passed along the matrilineal line from mother to daughter and uncle to nephew. The young Simoigit would dance in front with a line of uncles dancing behind him in a supporting role. His am halyte contains miix k’aax (eagle down feathers), which spread throughout the room as he shakes his head. The miix k’aax is a symbol of gawa gyani (peace) which derives from the story of the Waydetai. In this story, a flood covered the earth until one day, the waters were covered in miix k’aax and two rainbows touched the surface of the waters and they started to recede. The Simoigit spreads miix k’aax to symbolize that they intend to fulfill their responsibilities which accompany their new title in a manner that will bring peace to the people.
>
> Limk tsim halyte represents the young Simoigit (Chief) receiving the daxgyet of their Wilp as well as the corresponding responsibilities. Daxgyet is a legal term that means ‘the chief’s power and authority, strength of a people’ or ‘sovereign authority.’ The role of the Simoigit is to act as a steward of the people, land, water, tangible property, and intangible property belonging to the Wilp. This role requires him to conduct acts of service to take care of all these things which he is responsible for. Accordingly, while all members of a Wilp have the right to access property belonging to the Wilp, the Simoigit has the sole authority to manage the property. The Simoigit also has sole authority to host a Li’ligit (feast) on behalf of his Wilp, however in reality the entire Pdeek will support him in hosting the feast. The Simoigit also has a responsibility to represent the Wilp at Li’ligit that are hosted by other Pdeek (clan). The Simoigit has the authority to serve their people. I chose to incorporate this dance to represent the Gitxsan conception of sovereignty.
>
> Following the limk tsim halyte, the protagonist in Talking Past Each Other takes off his gwis halyte, am halyte, and haseex. He dresses himself in a suit, a portrait mask, and a bow/arrow. The mask has three lines painted on it, which is the signature painting style worn by the warrior naxnox from my Wilp. In the background, a narration reads the statements of claim from R v Calder and R v Delgamuukw, as well as the defendant’s response. The narration highlights the fundamentally different perspectives on sovereignty held by First Nations and the Crown. The Gitxsan assert that we are sovereign based on our deep relationship with the land, as recorded in Adaawk, and the daxgyet held by our Simgeeget. The Crown asserts that Aboriginal title was extinguished and complete dominion was taken by the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland through discovery and the enactment of legislation. The two arguments are based in fundamentally different epistemologies.
>
> The protagonist strips away his regalia and dresses in a suit to represent the attempts made by Canada to assimilate First Nations peoples. Indian Residential Schools, the Potlatch Ban, and missionary work are just a few examples of forces which aimed to assimilate First Nations Peoples. The ideology behind these policies was that First Nations peoples and cultures was inferior to European society, and that government ought to play a paternalistic role in assimilating the Indians. The colonizers believed this was a benevolent process which bestowed First Nations with the gifts of civilization, progress, and Christianity. The same ideology that promoted the assimilation of First Nations peoples also promulgated the belief that First Nations people were lawless and lacked the political and legal institutions required to achieve sovereignty. This ideology, called terra nullius, attempted to justify the erasure of Indigenous legal orders and the assertion of Crown sovereignty. We still see terra nullius in Aboriginal rights and title litigation. The protagonist places his regalia on a display table and mannequin to represent the collection of First Nations belongings by museums around the world. The display of First Nations ceremonial objects in this piece also represents the use of First Nations laws and oral histories as evidence in Aboriginal rights and title cases.
>
> In the next scene, the protagonist does a warrior dance. This dance is performed in Wilp Li’ligit (Feast Hall) to represent the strength and military power of the host. Today, the warrior represents resistance to colonization. Resistance goes beyond enduring, it involves thinking and acting in a way that disrupts the process of colonization. The purpose of resistance is to diminish the repression and harm that colonization continues to enact on Indigenous people and legal systems. Dancing the warrior dance in a suit is also a symbol of transforming our suffering into strength. The protagonist takes the tools that were meant to assimilate him (the suit) and uses them to assert his daxgyet. It is an act of claiming space which parallels the significance of doing this performance in a law school. The same building where students are taught to believe and operate within Canadian conceptions of law and sovereignty is being claimed through this performance as a space to assert Gitxsan Ayook and daxgyet. Doing the warrior dance in a suit is also a metaphor for the role of Indigenous lawyers, and our allies. Lawyers play a crucial role in using the courts as a place for resistance and the assertion of Indigenous laws. In this way, lawyers fulfill the responsibilities of the warrior to defend Indigenous peoples, lands, waters, etc.
>
> At the end of the warrior dance, the protagonist lays down his bow and pulls apart his mask for one final transformation. The mask is comprised of three pieces held together by magnets. The protagonist goes through an act of transforming into himself. This represents the resurgence of Indigenous law. Resurgence is a process of strengthening Indigenous laws as grounded in our epistemologies. Resurgence requires us to live life in way that upholds Indigenous laws and to engage them with practice. Resurgence is an ongoing process, the impacts of which we are just beginning to witness.[^1]
>

In writing the opening statement at trial, as  counsel to the Hereditary Chiefs, we had made clear that one of the central issues in *Delgamuukw* was the admissability of the oral histories, the adaawk of the Gitksan and kungax - the trails of song - of the Wet’suwet’en.These histories not only evidenced the millennia long presence of the ancestors of the Gitksan and Wet’suwe’tin but carried the freight of Indigenous laws. These histories are transmitted across generations through oral retelling by guardians of the history in both informal settings but also validated at ceremonial feasts. At the trial Gitksan Elder Antgulilibix, Mary Johnson, narrated a Gitksan adaawk - oral history - of the Medeek which describes the destruction of the ancient village of Temlaxam by a terrible force which ripped through the forest, bringing down trees in its wake and causing a rapid rise in the waters of a nearby lake. This destructive force is attributed in the adaawk to a giant grizzly bear- the Medeek. Using the detail of the account in terms of the nature of the event and its physical location, "scientific" evidence would be presented relating this event to a landslide or debris flow that occurred in what is now known as the Chicago Creek drainage, accompanied by the damming of and rise in the waters of Seeley Lake. This event occurred, according to the scientific evidence, some three thousand five hundred years ago.  After narrating the adaawk, Antgulilbix was asked to complete her account by singing the accompanying song that memorialized this event. Chief Justice McEachern was extremely discomfited by this approach to evidence commenting that he was engaged in a trial not a performance. Nevertheless, he allowed Antgulilbix to proceed to sing her song but advised her that he had ‘a tin ear’. 

There are two iconic portrayals of the this encounter evoking the challenges facing the judiciary in understanding Indigenous world views that were captured by artist Don Monet during the *Delgamuukw* trial. In the first one Don illustrates the contrast between Eurocentric reliance upon written documentation to record history and Indigenous forms of oral traditions.[^2]

Don, with an illustrator’s poetic license, also produced a second  drawing which both captured the Chief Justice’s discomfort and inability to appreciate the song as evidence of Gitksan performative law and Antgulilbix ability, as a Gitxsan historian, to open his ears.
