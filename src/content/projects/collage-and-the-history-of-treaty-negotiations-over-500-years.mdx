<p>One of the unanticipated pleasures of receiving the creative perspectives of students is an enhanced education in art history. Margot McMillan in her 2004 project “Collage and the History of Treaty Negotiations over 500 years” explains why her choice of Collage was an appropriate way to represent volumes of history.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The art of collage began in 1912 when Picasso pasted a section of commercially printed oilcloth to his cubist painting, Still Life with Chair Caning. Since then, it has become a form of basic Dada and surrealist art that has been used in various political movements throughout the 20th century as a means of expressing discontent and as a tool for destabilizingthe status quo. Indeed, in discussing the collage, Picasso clearly defined its purpose: "No, painting is not made to decorate apartments. It's an offensive and defensive weapon against the enemy.<br />The revolutionary quality at the heart of collage stems from the unorthodox and often subversive way in which images are combined. The unusual combination of images and material raises questions about the nature of existing reality and the possibility of new realitiesas created by the artist. As Katherine Hoffman has suggested, "Collage may be seen as a quintessential twentieth-century art form with multiple layers and signposts pointing to a variety of forms and realities, and to the possibility or suggestion of countless new realities.” Artists use the juxtaposed images in collage to reflect the changing realities around them.</p>

<p>The collage became popular with the situationalist movement in the late 1950s as a means of revolt against the "picturesque and sublime" paintings which depicted pastoral scenes of people at ease in an idyllic landscape. These paintings gave the impression of the city as "an ideal urban landscape where the struggle with nature, with the body, with space and with class had inexplicably come to an end". By contrast, the collage portrayed the city as a place where the struggles remained severe and often incoherent.</p>

<p>The idea of collage as a means to alter a preconceived reality and suggest alternate realities is what makes it so useful in tracking the portrayal of the evolution of treaty negotiations in North America over the last 500 years. By including images, maps, articles, newspaper clippings and some objects, I have attempted to create a feeling of incoherence that to me is characteristic of the progression of treaties from the time of contact to the present day.</p>

<p>The use of the words evolution and progression themselves do not accurately reflect the nature of the treaty process. There is generally a positive connotation ascribed to concepts like evolve and progress; however, history tells a different story when the earliest treaties, such as the Treaty of Lancaster in 1744, where both Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal parties were treated with equal diplomacy and respect, are compared with the later numbered treaties and some of the early modern agreements where the same respect and courtesy were not extended to the Aboriginal parties.</p>

<p>Widespread use of words like evolution and progression in describing historical events is illustrative of how the printed version of history is often narrow and misleading. By displaying certain images and excerpts from various treaty-making events of the last 500 years in fragments and layers, I hope to give the viewer the opportunity to develop new perspectives and realities about treaty-making and its goals and outcomes</p>

<p>While the goal of the collage is to stimulate the development of new realities, there remain 3 underlying themes that l endeavour to represent: one, the relationship between Aboriginal title and treaty-making; two, the dual nature of the notion of Aboriginal title (i.e. property and jurisdiction or self government); and three, the relationship between litigation ,judicial decisions and politics.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Margot’s two metre wide collage is vast in scope both historically and geographically. It has hung in my office at the Law School these many years and every time I look at it I discover something new. I encourage visitors to the Archive to spend some time with this remarkable document.</p>

<p>I have included here some of the close-ups but I expect, as Margot anticipated, every visitor will focus on different aspects of the collage.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Juxtaposing layered and fragmented images also makes it difficult to form a simple reality. On one glance, the various materials can appear overwhelmingly chaotic. This is in direct opposition to the traditional map that lies underneath, which lays out the boundaries in astatic, seemingly settled manner. The chaotic nature of the collage is deliberate deviation from the character of the map and is intended illustrate that for a deeper understanding of history, and ultimately of the present day conditions, it is necessary to look beyond the accepted status quo. Only by scrutinizing the information we are presented with and digging deeper than the surface, will we begin to understand the true history and effects of treaty making in North America. The images in the collage often extend beyond the map underneath. Again, this is deliberate and is designed to show that history cannot be easilycontained and does not fit into a neat box.</p>

<p>I have attempted to portray some of the missing elements in the collage . While I have loosely kept to some of the geographic areas where different incidents occurred, the way the images are set up is intended to portray incoherence and falsity in the Canadian interpretation of history. Additionally, the images are fragmented and layered to represent the equally fragmented and layered nature of history. By juxtaposing material and images from different cultures and often different centuries, I have tried to show that there are several different ways in which history can be interpreted and each can yield different realities. For example, I have included two opposing advertisements in the New York Times with respect to the Hydro-Quebec hydro-electricity project on the La Grande River. The advertisement opposing the project ran the week before the Hydro-Quebec ad defending the project.</p>

<p>ln order to avoid further subverting the goal of collage to allow the viewer to create his or her own realities, I did not include a detailed list of each image, article, quote or other material used in the collage. It seemed to me that by identifying each component, I would be interfering further with the viewer developing his or her own realities in that the viewer would not necessarily discover the content through the collage itself but by looking at the list of material. This could allow the viewer to selectively view only those components that he or she deemed important as a result of preconceived ideas of history. By forcing the viewer to access the content only through the collage itself hopefully the original intent of the collage is maintained. I have included a Works Cited section to give credit to the people whose work I have relied on to make the collage.</p>
</blockquote>
