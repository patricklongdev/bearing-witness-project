<p>Tamara Olding in her 2007 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights project, “Ravenous Equality and Reconcilable Differences”, using Northwest Coast Indigenous Raven crest figures, critically examines recent developments in British Columbia in achieving reconciliation between “existing Aboriginal sovereignty assumed Crown sovereignty”. Translating the language of the Supreme Court into the cultural language of Northwest Coast Indigenous Nations Tamara represents symbolically the First Nations and the Crown’s titles and sovereignties as Ravens.</p>

<p>Tamara begins her essay with a quotation from Chief Justice McLachlan in the 2004 <em>Haida Nation</em> case:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Where treaties remain to be concluded, the honour of the Crown requires negotiations leading to a just settlement of Aboriginal claims… Treaties serve to reconcile pre-existing Aboriginal sovereignty with assumed Crown sovereignty, and to define Aboriginal rights guaranteed by s. 35 of the Constitution Ac4 1982. Section 35 represents a promise of rights recognition, and "[i]t is always assumed that the Crown intends to fulfil its promises"… This promise is realized and sovereignty claims reconciled through the process of honourable negotiation. It is a corollary of s. 35 that the Crown act honourably in defining the rights it guarantees and in reconciling them with other rights and interests. This, in turn, implies a duty to consult and, if appropriate, accommodate.<sup data-fn="97f99519-05c7-4f1b-96c4-0c749efb2c10" class="fn"><a href="#97f99519-05c7-4f1b-96c4-0c749efb2c10" id="97f99519-05c7-4f1b-96c4-0c749efb2c10-link">1</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Tamara’s essay explains the legal and political dynamics of reconciliation revealed in the Raven Vest:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Ravenous equality and reconcilable differences. What's in a title? How does the title vest in the vest? The vest shows two Ravens, equal in stature, strength and enveloped in honourable garm. Their eyes fixed upon on each other. There is a struggle at hand (or talon), for the Raven representing the Crown attempts to assert its sovereignty by placing its flag on the traditional territory of the other. Clearly, the First Nations' Raven will not allow its pre-existing rights, titles and interests to be subsumed by such an attempted assertion and is pulling the flag pole upwards. There is something of a disadvantage, as the Crown has its weight leveraged downwards. It will take all of the First Nations Raven's wing strength to overcome, but her wings have the strength of a millennia of flight in these lands.<br /><br />There is an impasse and they now commence a process of reconciliation. In short, the narrative in the vest illustrates this struggle to reconcile Aboriginal and Crown sovereignty, the struggle to find true rights recognition, to fulfill section 35's noble purpose.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The essay explores how reconciliation is playing itself out in number of contexts in British Columbia and how we remain far from achieving nobility”. In the context of the 2005 First Nations Leadership Accord, the 2005 New Relationship political accord and the BC Treaty Negotiation process, Tamara writes:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>With regard to our embattled Ravens, the First Nations Leadership Accord represents the realization that reconciliation cannot be done alone or divided. First Nations acting outside their own Nation, First Nations organizations in opposition with one another, Aboriginal peoples turning upon themselves, will only undermine true reconciliation. First Nations mutually recognizing and affirming each other's rights, titles and interests in a respectful manner provides the necessary strength that this struggle necessitates. Our First Nations' Raven has just caught some sweeter sea air that carries the potency of our own united Nations. She may have the upper hand.<br /><br />For our Ravens, this promise of reconciling discussions is not a new one, Aboriginal peoples have always been in a process of negotiation with the Crown or settlers. Whether it be barter of goods or sitting arms crossed at negotiation tables, there is always time for a handshake and snickering smile. But, in this struggle that they are currently embroiled, the Crown Raven will have to cede his efforts to plant this flag, otherwise there will be no handshake. Neither one of them has the strength to wrestle and shake hands at the same time. This New Relationship. is the agreement to set down this flag, stop the squawking and pecking and finish this life long discussion. The words are not new.<br /><br />Our embattled Ravens, have rested their wings for a moment of reflection. Perhaps, to look upon their own image in something reflective. It may take just that introspection that is required to get beyond this impasse. It may take the Crown and First Nation Ravens examination of each other's motives to find some peace, some equality, some equity. For the Crown, a reconsideration of the weight placed on First Nations rights, title and interests is absolutely necessary. It may be fair to say that we are at a time where the Provincial Crown acknowledges but not truly recognizes our rights. Recognition requires more than just a nod in speeches and lip service at the politically correct opportunity. Recognition requires integrating rights recognition into all policy, manual, guideline that directs the Crown. It will take some time.<br /><br />For the First Nations' Raven, reconciliation is the promise, the hope of this challenging old relationship. Reconciliation is that utopia where oral histories are on equal footing, where everything is legally and morally defensible, where our laws can be read together. Getting to that utopia will very likely require all of her strength, patience and unity. With initiatives such as the Unity Protocol, First Nations Leadership Council and the New Relationship, there may be reason for hope.</p>
</blockquote>
