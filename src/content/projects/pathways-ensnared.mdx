<p>Elspeth Kaiser Derrick, as a Directed Research in her LL.M program created a set of lithographs to accompany her research paper on the different ways in which indigenous women experience imprisonment. With my encouragement Elspeth included her lithographs as part of her LLM thesis, which to my knowledge was the first time that the UBC Graduate Studies had accepted artwork as part of a master’s thesis in law. Her thesis was subsequently published by the University of Manitoba Press.<sup data-fn="bc82a574-6fe6-4a14-853f-5fa6f1ba2999" class="fn"><a href="#bc82a574-6fe6-4a14-853f-5fa6f1ba2999" id="bc82a574-6fe6-4a14-853f-5fa6f1ba2999-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Elspeth is now working on her doctorate and I am a member of her supervising committee. When I asked Elspeth’s permission to use her original lithographs for the Archive she told me that she had reworked several of them, including one that was used as the cover page for her book and had created a new set. She suggested that for the purpose of the Archive, to both reflect her developing research in the issues of women’s imprisonment and continuing artistic work, I include her new set of lithographs.</p>

<p>In her Artist Statement she explains this new series:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The overarching theme of this series of linocut prints is entanglements of trauma, where that trauma is both generated and exacerbated by the correctional system. “Pathways, Ensnared” is the original linocut print appearing on the cover of my book, Implicating the System: Judicial Discourses in the Sentencing of Indigenous Women, pictured here:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>https://uofmpress.ca/books/detail/implicating-the-system. The imagery of this linocut print represents how I seek to understand ways in which women’s experiences of victimization can constrain their available options and resources such that they become vulnerable to criminalization, even becoming ensnared by and within the criminal justice system. I created it as an abstract design to evoke pathways that tighten and trap.</p>

<p>My original inspiration was based on a photo I had taken of branches, intersecting and closing in, which I recreated in more literal terms in the linocut print that recurs across “System Health, Rising,” “System Health, Falling,” and “Trauma, Roots.” These branches emerge from clusters of tree trunks on each side of the linocut print, symbolizing, respectively, the traditionally punitive manifestation of the correctional system and its enmeshment with the ostensibly healing force of mental health services administered within that system, where each dimension of the system becomes mutually reinforcing, often deepening harms for those within.</p>

<p>Such harms can include isolation, where women who are struggling are constructed as threats to themselves or the institution, and placed in segregation. In “Abandonment, Overfocus,” women’s trauma and needs are decontextualized, such that systemic harms are not visible within the frame, and there are just women isolated in boxes, under the intense scrutiny of the system. The teal colour here, as elsewhere in this series, relates to vulnerability, oppression, and loss, engendered by the system.</p>

<p>Referring back to “Pathways, Ensnared,” in terms of interventions and accelerations of the criminal justice system where experiences of victimization leave women with limited supports, “Landscape, Violence” and “Mothers, Extracted” are structured to evoke the land. These linocut prints refer to systemic, intergenerational, state-perpetuated traumas through ongoing processes of colonization, which include the overincarceration of Indigenous women, and thereby the continued state separation of Indigenous mothers from their children and communities.</p>

<p>In “Trauma, Roots,” the treeline has suffered the trauma of a forest fire, and the recurrence of the linocut print of branches of the correctional system here becomes integrated into the root system of the scorched trees. Here, trauma is not simply a precursor to entry into the system, but an outgrowth of the system itself, trauma produced by women’s experiences of and within the system. In this and other prints, orange represents systemic trauma, the unsettling colour the sky can turn during forest fires.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2019/06/Elspeth_Derrick_07-770x1024.jpg" alt="Painting #7 in Elspeth Kaiser Derrick's 2019 project, &quot;Pathways, Ensnared&quot;" class="wp-image-4438"/></figure>

<p>The burnt trees reappear in “Fireweed, Remains,” against this sky. Below the treeline are representations of fireweed, a vibrant pink wildflower that is among the first regrowth after forest fire devastation, here symbolizing the agency and resilience of women in the system through trauma. I reprinted “Pathways, Ensnared” in magenta to reference fireweed and emphasize women’s agency and resilience in the context of their victimization and criminalization, and this colour functions similarly elsewhere in this series, too.</p>

<p>The final linocut print, “Mirror, Paralleled,” depicts the only face in the entire series, my own: the women here could be any of us, given experiences of trauma, poverty, oppression, and other inequalities and structural forms of vulnerability, if we lack or are restricted from access to various needed resources. We are all part of this community, whether inside or beyond prison walls, and we all share a need for connection, community, support, and solidarity. Further, I created this doubled self-portrait as a signifier of accountability, making myself visible as the artist here, responsible for this work – not an unseen and unidentified force, but someone who must engage with potential impacts and harms of her own work, in her own decisions about representation here. Similarly, the correctional system, and the criminal justice system writ large, must contend with its own role in the production of harms, the reach and entanglement of its own scorched branches in the lives of the women within its surveillance and control.</p>
