<p>Claire Stilwell’s 2008 project “Treaty Identity: Moving beyond Legal Definitions” A Symbolic Representation of Prairie Treaties in the Style of a Nuuchaanulth Ceremonial Curtain” is a remarkable synthesis, in canvas and text, spanning the long history of treaty-making in North America.</p>

<p>Claire describes the inspiration for the project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><em>" What 's so special about Treaties?"</em></p>

<p>This simple question, coming from a friend over dinner in response to our discussion about my Aboriginal Rights Moot and Aboriginal and Treaty Rights class, was unexpectedly difficult to answer. As I stumbled through references to ancient ideas of contract, colonialism, terra nullius, doctrines of discovery, land claims, sui generis and modern case law, I came to realise that my understanding of Treaties was rooted in exceptionally strict legal ideas. The idea, the identity, of ' Treaty' , for me, was still an amorphous and uncertain one.</p>

<p><em>" They Are Who We Are"</em></p>

<p>At the beginning of this, my last semester of law school, I was fortunate enough to be present at a premier screening of a good friend' s documentary on the work of Ron Hamilton (Ki ke-in) and the Nuuchaanulth Ceremonial Curtains he paints. This screening was attended by many of the families whose histories had been featured on the curtains, and by Ron Hamilton himself. As I had started to research Indigenous legal traditions for my Aboriginal Rights Moot paper I could not help but wonder about the legal status of the curtains in the community. Ron Hamilton seemed taken aback by the question, and found me after the audience had dispersed to talk to me more about it. As I learned later, he typically faced questions on whether or not the curtains were used as ' decoration' in family households, and he wanted to know why I had thought to look at them at legal documents. Our discussion was a long one, and through it I came to understand the symbolic nature of the curtains, and their importance as a legal and historical record for the Nuuchaanulth Community. As Ron Hamilton writes in the pamphlet accompanying the display of his works at UBC, 'They are who we are'. The symbols on the curtains represent family identity, and they are as unique as each of the family lineages they trace. Indeed, one of the most beautiful aspects of these curtains is that they follow no conventional, or 'traditional' format; there is no 'Ron Hamilton style'.</p>

<p>Growing up on the Prairies: the Numbered Treaties of Alberta<br />Drawing inspiration from Ron Hamilton's work, from what I have learned in my law school classes, and from my youth growing up on the Prairies, I have decided to investigate the identity of the Numbered Treaties that cover land in Alberta. The concept of 'treaty' is one defined by history, by law, and by society and for this project I hope go beyond the simple legal terms in which treaties have been described throughout my law school experience by constructing a curtain of my own which symbolically represents my knowledge and conception of the Numbered Treaties of Alberta.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Claire explains the “Symbols of Identity” represented in her Curtain.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">This is Treaty 7 Land</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2025/03/Claire_Stilwell_03.jpg" alt="Detail of Claire Stilwell’s 2008 project, &quot;Treaty Identity: Moving beyond Legal Definitions&quot;" class="wp-image-4015"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Only looking back now do I realise the subtle, yet omnipresent, role Treaties in Alberta had in my life growing up. Living next to the T' suu Tina reserve in Calgary I would walk by their 'Treaty 7 Lands' sign several times a week. Driving Highway 1 to Banff from Calgary I came to know the stretch that ran through the Chiniki Nation in Morley as the 'Morley Express', a term given to it by Albertans because members of the Chiniki community would often be trying hitchhike to different points in their Treaty land, as they were too poor to afford transportation of their own. When Calgary, a city preoccupied with building faster and wider transportation corridors, began negotiations with the T'suu Tina to build a 'ring-road' the phrase 'Treaty lands' was heard frequently, and most often in highly negative terms. The frustration of Calgarians with the inability of the city to expropriate this highly valuable land was palpable; the fact our major roads (Crowchild, Sarcee, Deerfoot, and Blackfoot Trail) already stood as testament to land ceded by the Prairie Aboriginal tribes had clearly been lost to history.</p>

<p>In school, like most Canadian students, I learned about how our country was settled bit by bit, in small little portions of confederation. I learned that we sometimes treated the Indians unfairly, and that when Treaties were made their written terms often did not reflect the oral negotiations. Trips to Head Smashed in Buffalo for class fieldtrips were often prefaced with tales of the 'noble savages' that traveled the plains before we came and wiped out the buffalo. But outside of the classroom the rhetoric was far different, with Aboriginal communities often described as lazy or corrupt. This was despite the fact that the vast majority of Calgarians had interacted with our local Aboriginal communities in exactly two ways: utilizing their golf courses and buying their cheap gas. Growing up within this dichotomy of attitude towards Aboriginal peoples in Alberta, and with my parents' exasperation with the simplification of Aboriginal issues both in schools and in the community, prompted me to pursue classes on Aboriginal and Treaty rights in law school. This class was the culmination of this pursuit, and this 'curtain' the manifestation of what I have discovered on this journey.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Railway Tracks</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2025/03/Claire_Stilwell_04.jpg" alt="Detail of Claire Stilwell’s 2008 project, &quot;Treaty Identity: Moving beyond Legal Definitions&quot;" class="wp-image-4018"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Beyond Peace and Friendship: Treaties as tools of economic expansion.<br />The 1978 curriculum guide prepared for the Washington public education system states, as part of their answer to the question "Why were treaties made?" that "Tribes were frequently able to defend against encroachments on their lands, forcing American settlers to pay for what they needed." This stark (and oddly sensible) statement speaks to the equality of bargaining position between Aboriginal groups and British colonizers that characterized the early treaties. Most notably, the Covenant Chain treaties, "encompassed the spectrum of political and economic concerns", including reciprocal recognition of jurisdiction and transfer of land rights. These treaties were heavily influenced by the population differences in the two communities, with local Aboriginal peoples far outnumbering settler communities, and by the intra-community wars in which the settlers were heavily engaged. This situation required them to form strategic military and economic alliances with First Nations.</p>

<p>After the wars between the United States and the British ceased, the rapid expansion of settler communities and the change in the colonial economic base from fur-trade to farming caused a considerable change in the attitude of the British towards treaty making. Instead of important, and equal, allies, Aboriginal communities were viewed as an impediment to progress, and as something to be displaced when required.</p>

<p>The prairie treaties were motivated by the early Canadian government's desire to expand, and to feed that expansion with land and minerals. Federal authorities feared the expansion of the United States, and wanted to secure the Western frontier. Around the same time gold, oil, and gas were discovered in the northern plains, causing the Crown to declare that "no time should be lost by the Government in making a treaty with these Indians for their rights over this territory." Indeed, the Crown saw Treaty 8 (and later, Treaty 11) as a business agreement designed to give them free, and peaceful, control and ownership over the mineral resources beneath the land.</p>

<p>The railway has had an enormously important role in the formation and identity of Canada. Credited as the driving force behind British Columbia ' s acceptance to enter Confederation, for decades the railway was an unstoppable iron force, representing progress, expansion, and economic growth. For these reasons I have chosen to include railway tracks as part of the curtain to represent the colonial motivation behind the treaties signed on the Prairies, and the economic aspect of historical treaty identity.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">River</h3>

<p>The only way forward: Settling terms.<br />Of course, the irony of the later numbered treaties is that the railway of progress had affected the signatory Aboriginal communities long before it brought them any treaty ' benefits'. The buffalo had been severely depleted, and the populations of animals that had been traditionally hunted and trapped were slowly vanishing. But the Cree, Sarcee, Blackfoot, Blood and Peigan lived on lands that were rich in agricultural potential and the Blackfoot Confederacy posed a serious military threat to the settlements in the south of the Prairies. The southern Prairie First Nations had a great deal to offer in the Treaty process, and looked to it to help them adapt to a new way of life, as theirs had been affected so radically they could no longer rely on their land to sustain them. However, government policy did not call for treaties to be made on the Prairies, and thus the Prairie First Nations were left to call for a treaty on their own. Chief Sweet Grass of the Cree wrote to the legislature;</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>We heard our lands were sold and we did not like it; we don't want to sell our land s; it is our property, and no one has a right to sell them. Our country is getting ruined of fur-bearing animals, hitherto our sole support, and now we are poor and want help - we want you to pity us. We want cattle, tools … and assistance in everything when we come to settle - our country is no longer able to support us.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Chief Sweet-Grass' request is a powerful reminder of how valuable some First Nations groups viewed treaties to be. It is difficult, viewing this attitude from the modern-day, to understand why such faith was placed in treaties. According to the Supreme Court, the sui generis nature of treaties does not make them sacred, it makes them more easily infringed; allowing governments to diminish rights they once solemnly declared sacred. But in going beyond the strict legal definition, it is necessary to look at the language used during treaty negotiations, such as the introductory speech given by Alexander Morris at the beginning of Treaty 6 meetings.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I come to you, children of the Queen, to try to help you; when I say yes I meant it, and when I say no, I mean it too. I want you to think of my words, I want to tell you that what we talk about is very important. What I trust and hope we will do is not for to-day or to-morrow only; what I will promise, and what I believe and hope you will take, is to last as long as that sun shines and yonder river flows.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Rivers are a life-giving, forever-changing, landscape-altering force that course throughout history and geography. They are a symbol of renewal and destruction and, like the railway, they are always moving forward. As treaties were signed across the prairies they brought a series of life-giving promises, of a renewed relationship between First Nations and the Queen. And, despite the wariness evidenced by the Treaty 8 groups, this river of treaty would move forward despite suspicion, despite the knowledge of broken promises, despite the certainty that life would forever be changed by its terms. When asked as part of the Treaty and Aboriginal Rights Research (T.A.R.R) interview program at the University of Alberta why his ancestors signed treaties, one Elder responded, " … because the promises were so good."</p>

<p><strong>Dreamcatcher</strong><br />Treaty Promises</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2025/03/Claire_Stilwell_05.jpg" alt="Detail of Claire Stilwell’s 2008 project, &quot;Treaty Identity: Moving beyond Legal Definitions&quot;" class="wp-image-4023"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>When signing Treaty 8 the elders were promised a lifestyle in which they would remain "free and independent , but with government assistance to fall back on and the prospect of greater opportunities in the fur trade." Putting the technicalities of questioning whether what was promised orally was accurately or faithfully recorded as the text of the treaties, the fact remains today that the promises made by the Crown were strictly interpreted while the concessions made by First Nations have been broadly taken advantage of.</p>

<p>The violent reaction to the Marshall decision on the East Coast of Canada, learning the true story behind Johnson v. M'Intosh and the Campbell case in British Columbia confirmed to me that our present-day attitudes towards Aboriginal Treaty rights is one that is formulated on inaccurate assumptions and historical wrongs.</p>

<p>A dreamcatcher is well known and successfully commercialized Ojibwa cultural object. Designed to hang above a sleeping child, their spider-web center traps the nightmares and only allows the good dreams through. Legend has it that the rising of the morning sun destroys the nightmares tangled in the web during the night. I chose this to represent the modern identity of Canadians' attitudes towards treaties for several reasons. Firstly, the adoption (co-option?) of First Nations cultural objects for commercial purposes echoes the commercialization of their land that occurred as a result of the numbered treaties in the Prairies. Secondly, to this day Canadians view treaties through a dreamcatcher lens, forgetting the nightmares and unfairness of the modern treaty arrangements; allowing them to perish in the modern light. For instance, Canadians are quick to point to the fairness of making treaties with Aboriginal communities (as opposed to their outright destruction) but their characterization of treaties as "sacred promises that cannot be altered but can be interpreted in a modern context" hints at the reluctance in honouring the sharing spirit upon which these treaties were founded. The negotiations surrounding whether or not First Nations could control the mineral resources on their land also reflects the reluctance with which 'equal sharing' and control of their land was viewed.</p>

<p>Similarly, with the landmark decisions of <em>R. v. Badger</em> the Supreme Court of Canada indicated that interpreting treaties in accordance with the 'honour of the Crown' simply meant that infringements on long-held treaty-rights required certain justifiable reasons. It would seem the fact that these rights could be infringed at all did not give many pause for thought. It is also telling that in the Samson case the approach to the elder evidence was one where the starting point came from an understanding of treaty agreements as full documents of surrender without any allowance for the possibility these documents were intended to share. Harold Johnson eloquently captures the be discrepancy between the haves and have-nots in his book Two Families: Treaties and Government "If we were to be free to live after the treaty as we had lived before it, then we would have access to the resources of our territory … It is a wealthy territory, Kiciwamanawak; it has fed, clothed, and housed my family since we were put here. It is only by your regulation of resources that we are denied the self-sufficiency that we were assured at treaty."</p>

<p>Whether Canadians' blindness to the plight of the Treaty people is willful or not, the dreamcatcher that has hung in between the reality and the perception of treaty rights in Canada must be examined carefully to see what has been caught on the web; and the poverty and inequality that face the Aboriginal people on the Prairies because the promises of the Treaties have not been kept must be addressed.</p>

<p><strong>Crocus</strong><br />The promise of a new beginning</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2025/03/Claire_Stilwell_06.jpg" alt="Detail of Claire Stilwell’s 2008 project, &quot;Treaty Identity: Moving beyond Legal Definitions&quot;" class="wp-image-4027"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Frank Calder said his forward-looking attitude came from his residential school's motto "there is no backward step." And it is this attitude that must characterize Canadians' motivation to improve the relationship between Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal peoples in Canada, in the 21st century.</p>

<p>The treaty process has been given new life in British Columbia, a land virtually untouched by treaty for the majority of the 20th century. We have seen new language be introduced to the treaty process in the Nisga'a Final Agreement, where Aboriginal title and rights are 'modified' and the ancient language "cede, release, surrender and yield up" is put to rest.</p>

<p>The granting of Aboriginal title no longer allows the Crown to take rights in all the land such that they can then parcel out postage-stamp portions to Aboriginal communities. Section 35 of the Constitution has allowed landmark litigation to fundamentally alter the idea of Aboriginal title and the honour the Crown as it relates to the duty to consult.At the conclusion of the Kawaskimon Moot one of our guiding Elders, Elder Decontie stood to tell the story of his interaction with the law as a defendant in <em>R. v. Cote</em>. The fact that his refusal to pay fees to enter the park that had been established on his traditional territory was had made him guilty of a criminal act troubled him deeply, especially as his motivation for entering the land had been to teach traditional methods of fishing and camping to young students at his school. But, as he spoke of his time in court, he became passionate about the role lawyers could play in reshaping the relationship between Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal Canadians. He looked at us all, sitting in a talking circle, and declared that perhaps our generation would be the one to finally live in a decolonized country, and acknowledged with humour the heavy burden he had placed on our shoulders. We took his words with us when we left.</p>

<p>On the prairies, the crocus is the flower that heralds the first sign of spring. It signals renewal, beauty from the frozen land, and is strong enough to withstand the last few lingering frosts. I hope that the ability to withstand setbacks and bring vibrant renewal to a once barren landscape will represent future of Treaty and Aboriginal Rights in Canada, and for these reasons I have chosen to put the crocus on the curtain. As Harold Johnson writes "I am offering a solution to the very serious problems that I see in this territory. If we return to the original intention of treaty and recognize that we are relatives, Kiciwamanawak, we should be able to walk into the future in a good way."</p>

<p>The language of ' reconciliation' has dominated the Aboriginal and Treaty rights discourse in legal, political, and social spheres, but this word must make way for a language of renewal. Reconciliation of parties that are defined by ancient and colonial attitudes may never be achievable, but a renewal of the original spirit of friendship, cooperation, and desire to move successfully and peacefully forward can be achieved. The crocus can spring from the winter ground.</p>

<p>The T'suu Tina define themselves as proud Treaty 7 people. The symbols on this curtain speak to a history and identity of treaties that is not always proud. But I hope that, in the future, I too can speak of being a proud Treaty 7 person, abiding by the spirit of the sacred promises the treaties of Canada contain.</p>
</blockquote>
