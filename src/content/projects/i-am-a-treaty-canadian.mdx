<p>In addition to ceremonial curtains and paintings students have created poetry to explore themes arising from the history of treaty making. Jenny Biem’s collection of poems, written for the 2007 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar, does so through the lens of a non-Indigenous Canadian. She writes, “before taking this course I never understood myself as a party to the treaties and as having individual responsibility to ensure they are respected”. Her poem “I am a Treaty Canadian’ offers an alternative rendition of the national anthem’.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Oh Canada<br />Our home on native land!<br />True blinding love the deed is in my hands.</p>

<p>With opened eyes I see thee writhe,<br />Is it pain or ecstasy?</p>

<p>Justice must be done,<br />Oh Canada, we choose who we will be.</p>

<p>God hold our hand to the spirit of the treaties!<br />Honourable Crown, I stand on guard for thee</p>

<p>Oh honoured Crown, I stand on guard for thee</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Another poem, “Taken Up”, reflecting on a Buffy Saint Marie song, addresses the clause in the Numbered Treaties, that while on the one hand guaranteeing the Treaty Nations ‘the right to pursue their usual invocations of hunting and trapping and fishing throughout the track surrendered’, on the other hand empowered the Crown “to take up those lands” for the all-encompassing purposes of “settlement, mining, lumbering trading or other purposes”. This clause has enabled the Crown to privilege the interests of a settler society and systematically undermine the treaty promises as they were understood by the Treaty Nations. As we have seen the failure of the Crown to live up to promises in the Numbered Treaties as they were understood by the treaty nations is a theme explored across many forms of media in the archive.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Taken up<br />Cash raked up<br />until there is no choice left.</p>

<p>Chop it down<br />Dig further down<br />Until there is no choice left</p>

<p>Colonize</p>

<p>Economize<br />Until there is no choice left</p>

<p>Corporate greed<br />Ruins with speed<br />Food water medicine air</p>

<p>Fur and food?<br />It’s over dude!<br />Instead take profit-shar</p>

<p>Live on the land?<br />Quit dreaming man!<br />Time to time’s fragmented that</p>

<p>Work for us<br />Buy from us<br />You take a cut of the fat</p>

<p>There’s only room<br />For one way<br />To earn a living now</p>

<p>Make a deal<br />You have to now<br />You know there’s no choice left</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Jenny’s “Treaty 8 Haiku”, in less words than a tweet, expresses the impact of Alberta’s oil sands development on Treaty 8 lands:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Rivers run poisoned<br />Prairie grasslands almost gone<br />Sunshine’s melts brings death</p>
</blockquote>
