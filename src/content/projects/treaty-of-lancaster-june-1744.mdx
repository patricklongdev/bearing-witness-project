<p>The historical record that we have of Covenant Chain treaty councils is quite remarkable and has no parallel in later eras. The treaty councils with the Six Nations were viewed as matters of state and because their public significance to the security of the colonies the meetings were often held in places like the courthouses and town halls where important public business was conducted. For example, in Philadelphia the courthouse and Quaker Meeting House where the site of many treaties during the first half of the 18th century before being replaced by the even more impressive Statehouse. The Council meetings were observed by a large audience of colonial dignitaries and the occasion of the Six Nations coming to town for the purpose of transacting treaty business was a major event. From contemporary accounts the effect was the 18th century version of a major political convention. The proceedings were not only carefully transcribed but were also published. Starting in 1736 many of the proceedings were published under the auspices of the printing firm of Benjamin Franklin who in his later career was to become one of the Fathers of Confederation and drafters of the American Declaration of Independence.<sup data-fn="0c610dc2-e9e2-4d83-8689-5f9f49119d32" class="fn"><a href="#0c610dc2-e9e2-4d83-8689-5f9f49119d32" id="0c610dc2-e9e2-4d83-8689-5f9f49119d32-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Visitors to the archive will have the benefit of delving into this history, including the text of one of the Covenant Chain council meetings. Krista Johanson, in the 2009 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Seminar prepared an annotated and illustrated copy of the Treaty of Lancaster, held in 1744 in the town of Lancaster, Pennsylvania. In her annotations and illustrations Krista provides a guide to the actors in the Treaty council, including the great Onandaga statesman Canassatego, the language of Haudenosaunee diplomacy with its references to “Clearing the Road”, “enlarging the Fire” and “brightening the Chain” that were all recurring Haudenosaunee metaphors related to the themes of connection and communication associated with Covenant Chain diplomacy which were adopted as part of the diplomatic discourse by the colonial authorities.</p>

<p>Krista describes the genesis of her project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>At the beginning of this term, I set myself two criteria for the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>I wanted to embrace the opportunity to do something that was not traditionally academic; and</li>

<li>I wanted to be respectful of Aboriginal culture. In particular, I did not feel comfortable about making use of another society’s cultural and artistic tradition. I think it’s wonderful to take a traditional art form and transform it into a cross-cultural exploration (as several of my fellow students have done successfully), but I worried that my lack of creativity and feeble artistic skills would result not in a moving tribute like the medicine bag Christina presented in class, but in something more akin to the experience she had when a well-meaning friend took her to the Tomahawk Restaurant.</li>
</ul>
</blockquote>

<p>Eventually, I began researching the Treaty of Lancaster, without any clear idea of what I would do with the information I found. The first time I read this treaty, I was fascinated. I was fascinated by the fact the treaty contained a record of the treaty council, not simply the signed agreement to transfer land. I was fascinated by the way in which colonial governments participated in what was largely an aboriginal process, and a very formal one at that. I was moved by the arguments made, and the beautiful, metaphorical way in which they were expressed. Like Richard Peters, the Pennsylvania clerk who was present at the treaty council, I immediately formed the impression that “the Indians really appear superior to the Commissioners in point of sense and argument” (see below, page 60).</p>

<p>I had many questions. What was the nature of the relationship between the Six Nations? Why were only representatives of five of them present? What was the history of the Covenant Chain? Why were the Iroquois ceding more territory, when they clearly didn’t have enough as it was? Was wampum passed back and forth, or was a new belt or string used each time? Where did the Colonial governments obtain their wampum? Was this treaty unique, or similar to others? How had treaty-making evolved since the 17th century? How was this treaty regarded in the 19th century? How is it regarded today? Why was the treaty council held at Lancaster? Why did everybody sit around doing nothing for two days once the Iroquois delegates arrived? I had vague recollections of the Iroquois fighting for the French against the English. Was I wrong, or did that happen later? When did respect for the Iroquois way of doing things erode? And many more questions besides.</p>

<p>The January 21st lecture provided context. It answered several questions, and raised others. Some of these questions could be classified as historical. Some could be called legal. Some might be considered mere curiosity. But the more research I did, the more I realized that in the field of “Aboriginal Law”, legal and historical questions can’t easily be separated – and nor should they be. Unfortunately, many law students have no previous experience in dealing with history. Some, like me, approach the subject with a history background, but fewer still have experience in dealing with Aboriginal history. Yet an inquiry into any Aboriginal legal issue requires knowledge of histories that are lengthy, complex, often overlooked and always controversial.</p>

<p>I learned a lot from the four corners of the treaty, but I couldn’t hope to accurately interpret it from its text alone. I needed to take advantage of the considerable research that others have done in order to even begin to get an idea of its many meanings. I saw how hopelessly inadequate the strict rules of evidence must have been, historically, for Courts attempting to determine issues of Aboriginal law, and wondered whether today’s procedures are adequate.</p>

<p>I decided that the Treaty of Lancaster could serve as a symbol, or touchstone, for the intersectionality of history and law and culture. By annotating the treaty to exhibit aspects of its history and its legacy, I could not only make the document more accessible, but I could invite readers to draw their own conclusions about these issues. To that end, I have not included my own legal or historical conclusions.</p>

<p>Much has been written about the Treaty of Lancaster and Six Nations treaties, generally. The information I have selected is not intended to be comprehensive; it is intended as a springboard for discussion and research. One major weakness in the information presented is that little of it comes from contemporary Iroquois sources. That issue (along with the accompanying issue of the unreliability of historical and anthropological research) is also worthy of discussion.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>It was during my research as Counsel in the <em>Delgamuukw</em> case that I learnt that at the Council at Lancaster the principal Iroquois speaker, Canassatego delivered what is the most eloquent statement of the evolution and nature of the Covenant Chain. (You can also read the text in context at p.17 of Krysta’s annotated copy).</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Brother, the Governor of Maryland,</h3>

<p>When you mentioned the Affair of the Land Yesterday, you went back to old Times, and told us, you had been in Possession of the Province of Maryland above One Hundred Years; but what is One Hundred Years in Comparison of the Length of Time since our Claim began? since we came out of this Ground? For we must tell you, that long before One Hundred Years our Ancestors came out of this very Ground, and their Children have remained here ever since. You came out of the Ground in a Country that lies beyond the Seas, there you may have a just Claim, but here you must allow us to be your elder Brethren, and the Lands to belong to us long before you knew anything of them. It is true, that above One Hundred Years ago the Dutch came here in a Ship, and brought with them several Goods; such as Awls, Knives, Hatchets, guns, and many other Particulars, which they gave us; and when they had taught us how to use their Things, and we saw what sort of People they were, we were so well pleased with them, that we tied their Ship to the Bushes on the Shore; and afterwards, liking them still better the longer they staid with us, and thinking the Bushes too slender, we removed the rope, and tied it to the Trees; and as the Trees were liable to be blown down by high winds, or to decay of themselves, we, from the Affection we bore them, again removed the Rope, and tied it to a strong and big Rock (here the Interpreter says they mean the Oneido Country) and not content with this, for its further Security we removed the Rope to the big Mountain (here the Interpreter says they mean the Onandago Country) and there we tied it very fast, and rowlled Wampum about it; and, to make it still more secure, we stood upon the Wampum, and sat down upon it, to defend it, and to prevent any Hurt coming to it, and did our best Endeavours that it might remain uninjured for ever.</p>

<p>During all this Time the New-comers, the Dutch acknowledged our Right to the Lands, and sollicited us, from Time to Time, to grant them Parts of our country, and enter into League and Covenant with us, and to become one People with us.</p>

<p>After this the English came into the Country, and, as we were told, became one People with the Dutch. About two Years after Arrival of the English, an English Governor came to Albany and finding what great Friendship subsisted between us and the Dutch, he approved it mightily, and desired to make as strong a League, and to be upon as good Terms with us as the Dutch were, with whom he was united, and to become one People with us: And by his further Care in looking into what had passed between us, he found that the Rope which tied the Ship to the great Mountain was only fastened with Wampum, which was liable to break and rot, and to perish in a Course of Years; he therefore told us, he would give us a Silver chain, which would be much stronger, and would last for ever. This we accepted, and fastened the Ship with it, and it has lasted ever since.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>You will see in the Treaty of Lancaster repeated references to the presentation of wampum belts. Reading the published transcripts of the Treaty of Lancaster and the dozens of other treaty councils exposes a rich vein of intercultural communication and one that Canadian lawyers and judges have only now begun to discover.</p>

<p>Krista’s annotations also allows us to listen in on the ceremonial sounds of treaty making. After every major speech, along with the presentation of wampum, the Iroquois would chant the Yo Hah. For example, Canassatego, the principal spokesman for the Iroquois at the Lancaster Council, in response to the opening response to “Brother Onas” - the Governor of Pennsylvania stated:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>You spoke in the Presence of Assaragoa and the Governor of Maryland to us, advising us to receive them as our Brethren, and to unite with them in the Covenant Chain as one Body, and one Soul. We have always considered them as our Brethren, and, as such, shall be willing to brighten the Chain of Friendship with them; but since there are some Disputes between us respecting the Lands possessed by them, which formerly belonged to us, we, according to our Custom, propose to have those Differences first adjusted, and then we shall proceed to confirm the Friendship subsisting between us, which will meet with no obstruction after these matters are settled. Here they presented the GOVERNOR with a Belt of Wampum, in return for the Belt given them in the Morning by the GOVERNOR; and the Interpreter was ordered to return the Yo-ha.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">What did it sound like?</h3>

<p>Marshe describes the “Yo-Hah”, or chant of approbation, in his journal (see sidebar, right). Conrad Weiser, who had heard it far more often throughout his many treaty councils, described it in his own journal as follows, also in reference to the Treaty of Lancaster:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>When they make treaties with whites, the wholle council and all the warriors perform the “shout of approbation,” the “Io—hau.” It is performed in the following manner: The Speaker, after a Pause, in a slow tone pronounces the U—huy; all the other Sachems in perfect Silence; So soon as he s tops, they all with one Voice, in exact Time, begin one general Io’ raising and falling their Voices as the Arch of a Circle, and then raise it as high as the first, and stop at the Height at once, in exact time; and if it is of great Consequence, the Speaker gives the U—huy thrice, and they make the Shout as often. It is usual, when the white People speak to them, as they give a belt or string of Wampum, for the Interpreter to begin the U—huy, and the Indians to make the shout.</p>
</blockquote>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Marshe Journal: The “jo-hah!”</h3>

<p>In the court-house, Monday [22 June], P.M.<br />Whilst Mr. Jenings delivered his speech, he gave the interpreter a string and two belts of wampum, which were by him presented to the sachem Cannasateego; and the Indians thereupon gave the cry of approbation; by this we were sure the speech was well approved by the Indians. This cry is usually made on presenting wampum to the Indians in a treaty, and is performed thus: The grand chief and speaker amongst them pronounces the word jo-hah! with a loud voice, singly; then all the others join in this sound, woh! dwelling some little while upon it, and keeping exact time with each other, and immediately, with a sharp noise and force, utter this sound, wugh! This is performed in great order, and with the utmost ceremony and decorum; and with the Indians is like our English huzza! (p. 120)<br />According to William Fenton, “the literature contains no better description of cries heard in the modern condolence ceremony: yoh-henh, and the antiphonal waʔ waʔ: , or hi:yenh (Great Law at 427).</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">What did it signify?</h3>

<p>According to one Richard Cullen Rath, “Sounds sealed treaties and made them real, whether between Indian Nations or between Indians and whites. First Nations people structured their public spaces through nonverbal response to speech.” Franklin, on the facing page, compares the Yo-hah to an English “huzza”, but it has a more precise meaning. Made after a speech had been interpreted, it signified that the entire community understood and assented to whatever had just been said. As the explorer Jonathan Carver understood, the Yo-hah “served to bind the agreement as signatures would have on a written document.” Franklin’s use of the word “approbation” is appropriate: it is defined as “the action of formally or authoritatively declaring good or true; sanction”.</p>

<p>The use of the Yo-hah in combination with wampum further served to emphasize and give meaning to whatever had just been said in treaty council. Wampum not only made the spoken words effective, but, Rath argues, “the speech entered into the wampum, leaving traces of its meaning.” He suggests that to the early Iroquois, texts, reading, and currency would be understood as like wampum.</p>
</blockquote>
