<p>Karri-Lynn Hennebury’s Behind the Walls: Citizen Lawbreaker (Life Edition) created for the 2014 Penal Policy seminar is an imaginative combination of some popular games that provide players with an understanding of the challenges facing a prisoner sentenced to life for second-degree murder as they try and negotiate and earn their way from maximum to minimum security and hopefully back to the community on parole.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2025/02/Karri-Lynn_Hennebury_02-1024x707.jpg" alt="The box from Karri-Lynn Hennebury’s 2014 project, Behind the Walls: Citizen Lawbreaker (Life Edition), game. " class="wp-image-3700"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>Karri-Lynn in her detailed Rules and Manual Guide provides this overview of the game:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>You have just received a life sentence for second-degree murder and were given a parole ineligibility period of ten years. A life sentence means that you could have to spend the rest of your life in prison. You could also one day be granted parole. A lot of your future depends on your behaviour and actions while in the correctional facility.</p>

<p>Welcome to life behind bars. You have been slotted to spend your sentence in the Pacific Region. You will serve your sentence starting at Kent, and then will be cascaded down to Matsqui when you have reached your eligibility to do so, and then you will continue your time at Ferndale minimum-security, before you may apply for parole.</p>

<p>As a new prisoner in the Canadian correctional system, you have met with a staff member who has created a Correctional Plan for you. The Correctional Plan is a document that outlines a risk management strategy for each person, which specifies intervention techniques to address areas associated with your risk to re-offend.</p>

<p>You are expected to achieve the goals set out for you in your Correctional Plan at each level of security. If you manage to follow the plan and meet all the criteria,you will be eligible to apply for parole. The quicker you manage to meet your objectives, the quicker you can return to your family or at the very least, return to the outside world.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Introduction</h3>

<p>This game is meant to be a fun, educational tool for people to gain a little knowledge on the penal system in Canada, discuss its elements, and engage with critical ideas surrounding the entire way that we as a society handle some of our most vulnerable members. The following information is fore a more in-depth understanding about this game and the correctional system in Canada.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Boards</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2025/02/Karri-Lynn_Hennebury_03-1024x682.jpg" alt="Karri-Lynn Hennebury’s 2014 project, Behind the Walls: Citizen Lawbreaker (Life Edition), game. " class="wp-image-3703"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The correctional facility boards are designed to inform, educate, and give players a (muted) glimpse into the life of a person sentenced to life imprisonment. The boards obviously cannot convey the true experience of life behind bars, but there are specific differences that are meant to inform players about the various types of institutions in Canada. To optimize the experience, only institutions in British Columbia were selected as representations for the locations that players may be placed. Given that players will likely play the game in this province, this choice gives players the opportunity to connect what they learn from the game, to real life institutions where people spend their days.</p>

<p>At Kent (the maximum-security board), a player has the most amount of tiles to traverse but the least ability to actually move forward in their correctional plan. This choice was intentional. Prisoners in Kent experience limited mobility because of their purported security risk. This makes moving more difficult. With raised security concerns, this also makes moving forward in your correctional plan more difficult.</p>

<p>Players will notice that Matsqui (the medium-security board) is somewhat similar to the Kent board. These types of institutions are similar in that they are gated, that they have guards posted, there are segregation cells that prisoners are put into, and there is very little autonomy in these facilities. Life can be dependent on forces outside of the prisoners' control ( ex: if a prisoner rats them out for something they did not do, the innocent party may spend time in administrative segregation having done nothing wrong). The maximum and medium-security institutions inhibit a person's right to liberty in the most severe ways and can do so with this kind arbitrary exertion of power. Therefore, the boards are painted in the shape of prison cells, to signify the detention, and infringement on freedom experienced in these cells. Black and silver are the predominant shades on the board to signify the darkness that exists behind the walls of institutions in Canada. The paint colours used on these boards are primary colours to represent a return to the basics, a life without much liberty, and a life with limited privilege.</p>

<p>In Ferndale (the minimum security board), players will notice that the board is significantly different than the other two boards. A minimum-security institution in Canada does not need fences. Instead, these facilities are usually just a bunch of small dwelling places, and prisoners usually go to work or exist in the community during the day. The board for Ferndale signifies the increased liberty and autonomy a prisoner experiences at this level. The Parole Board square is yellow to represent the small ray of light at the end of the long journey ahead.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Transferring/Parole</h3>

<p>Transferring through the Correctional system in Canada is not a given. Lowering from a high security to lower security facility is a process that can frustrate many prisoners. It can sometimes be political and may mean that a prisoner cannot transfer down because of there is no room at the lower facility, other inmates cannot co-exist with him, or the other warden does not desire the transfer to move forward for a different reason. In order to capture this frustrating reality in a game, players need to roll the month die and rely on luck to get from one place to another.</p>

<p>Certain players have more months on their Correctional Plans when it comes to applying to transfer or when applying for parole at the end of the game. This decision is to recognize that players with certain traits will have a better chance at obtaining parole than others with differing backgrounds. The rest of the cards are meant to be realistic in regard to their presence on a correctional plan. If a prisoner had educational gaps or needed vocational skills, they may be designated to go to school or to go to obtain a job in the prison ( or if in minimum-security, a job in the community). The programming cards specifically address the types of programs offered through CSC (Correctional Service of Canada).</p>

<p>The crimes committed by the prisoners are a combination of imagination and real-life Canadian murders. The names are purely fictional. In order to not trivialize or cartoonize real men or their experiences behind bars, there are not real prisoners depicted in the game as characters. In addition, there are no real gang names used either. The names used are similar to real-life gangs such as the UN gang and the Red Scorpions.</p>

<p>Each prisoner has ten things in total on their correctional plan to represent the decision in R v Shropshire, that the baseline parole ineligibility period should be ten and then factors can bring it up. Rather than make one player's experience way easier (even if that may be more realistic), it was decided to make all players need to accomplish the same amount of things but to make certain players have easier tasks and increased chances at transfers and parole.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Canteen</h3>

<p>Canteen cards feature a basket on the front of it. A typical unit of measurement to determine purcahases at prison canteens in Canada is called a "canteen basket". Politicans use this to compare the price that prisoners have to pay over time. Thus, a picture of a basket is on each of these cards. The restriction on the number of canteen cards that a prisoner can hold is representative of the fact that inmates may only have a maximum dollar amount worth of items from the canteen in their cell per pay period. For maximum accuracy, the game creator spoke with an actual person who works in a prison and is responsible for canteen items. Therefore, the information and items available in the canteen are representative of items available to prisoners. Commissioner's Directive 890 was also consulted.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Work</h3>

<p>A hammer is on the card to depict the type of work a prisoner may do while incarcerated. However, the word cards themselves are not meant to be a literal representation of the prison working experience. It is not possible to have players produce CORCAN products. This element was the most altered from reality to make the experience the most enjoyable for players. These cards are meant to foster critical thinking, creativity, and communication (teaching and listening). The purpose of the game is to educate and create a dialogue with people not necessarily 100% immersed in the criminal justice or penal system in Canada. Including a fun element that still incorporates ideas or items associated with prison was key to the overall gaming experience.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Education</h3>

<p>The education cards have a diploma and award a player credits to attempt to provide the imagery and conceptual framework for schooling. This card is one of the most important to the game. Most cards pose questions to the player and challenge their knowledge. Some of the cards seem intuitive, others will challenge players to question what they know about the system entirely. Most cards also contain a link so that curious players can learn more to further their education outside of the game or to read more during gameplay if they do not believe the answer.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Life</h3>

<p>The image on the life cards is designed to give players a glimpse into daily lives: simply put, it is life behind bars. The majority of days, nothing happens but when something happens it can have dire results. Players may not have anything happen for almost the entire game before one card can send them to segregation or have them lose everything. Segregation time periods in this game were not given for extended time. In reality, a prisoner could be in segregation for months. This reality is a huge problem but was not one that would adequately fit within a board game experience. This board game merely raises the issue.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The evocative title of Karri-Lynn’s board game “Citizen Lawbreaker” comes from a passage in the judgement of Chief Justice Beverly McLachlan in the <em>Sauve</em> case where the Supreme Court of Canada ruled unconstitutional the provision in the Canada Elections Act that prohibited prisoners serving sentences of two years or more from voting in federal elections.<sup data-fn="b9d39369-8100-4410-b264-20560f7131a1" class="fn"><a href="#b9d39369-8100-4410-b264-20560f7131a1" id="b9d39369-8100-4410-b264-20560f7131a1-link">1</a></sup> Chief Justice McLachlin uses the phrase “citizen-lawbreakers” to describe prisoners and their relationship to Charter rights. In <em>A Flawed Compass </em>I described the significance of the Chief Justice’s language.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The significance of this description is that it emphasizes that in any analysis concerning prisoners’ rights, prisoners are to be primarily understood as citizens and as members of society, and only secondarily as law breakers. Chief Justice McLachlin describes the importance of the “citizen-lawbreaker” description as follows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The social compact requires the citizen to obey the laws created by the democratic process. But it does not follow that failure to do so nullifies the citizen’s continued membership in the self-governing polity. Indeed, the remedy of imprisonment for a term rather than permanent exile implies our acceptance of continued membership in the social order. Certain rights are justifiably limited for penal reasons, including aspects of the rights to liberty, security of the person, mobility, and security against search and seizure. But whether a right is justifiably limited cannot be determined by observing that an offender has, by his or her actions, withdrawn from the social compact. Indeed, the right of the state to punish and the obligation of the criminal to accept punishment are tied to society’s acceptance of the criminal as a person with rights and responsibilities. Other Charter provisions make this clear. Thus s. 11 protects convicted offenders from unfair trials, and s. 12 from “cruel and unusual treatment or punishment”.</p>

<p>Chief Justice McLachlin in <em>Sauve</em> subscribes to a vision of prisoners’ rights that sees human rights as inherent to every individual, both those that abide by the laws and those that do not. Not only is denying prisoners their rights suspect because it effectively removes them from Charter protection, it is also suspect because such a blanket denial of rights “runs counter to our constitutional commitment to the inherent worth and dignity of every individual”. Therefore, while a prisoner may be legitimately deprived of some Charter rights for the purposes of imprisonment it is another thing to say that a particular class of people for a particular time will completely lose a particular constitutional right. This is tantamount to saying that the affected class is outside the full protection of the Charter. It is doubtful that such an unmodulated deprivation,…is capable of justification…</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The second concern that the court in <em>Sauvé</em> raises with regard to depriving prisoners of their rights it that it is “bad pedagogy”. The Federal Government in seeking to justify denying federal prisoners the right to vote had argued that it sent an “educative message” about the importance of respect for the law to inmates and to the citizenry at large. In the case of the Panel’s suggestions, the rationale offered for depriving prisoners of all but their basic rights is that this approach will teach offenders to be “responsible for their actions and [that they] are obligated to respect the rights and freedoms of others in society.” However, Chief Justice McLachlin in <em>Sauvé</em> held that denying prisoners rights is bad pedagogy. It misrepresents the nature of our rights and obligations under the law, and it communicates a message more likely to harm than to help respect for the law. .. [It]is more likely to become a self-fulfilling prophecy than a spur to reintegration. Depriving at-risk individuals of their sense of collective identity and membership in the community is unlikely to instill a sense of respon-sibility and community identity.</p>

<p>Furthermore, the Chief Justice McLachlin goes on to hold that “more profoundly, it sends the unacceptable message that democratic values are less important than punitive measures ostensibly de-signed to promote order.” Through these statements, we once again see the Chief Justice returning to the idea that prisoners, regardless of what crimes they have committed, retain their membership in the citizen-community. Charter rights are meant to embody democratic values and recognize the importance of the dignity of every human being.<sup data-fn="6e962db5-18f9-4e65-aa9d-1b65a9f01081" class="fn"><a href="#6e962db5-18f9-4e65-aa9d-1b65a9f01081" id="6e962db5-18f9-4e65-aa9d-1b65a9f01081-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
