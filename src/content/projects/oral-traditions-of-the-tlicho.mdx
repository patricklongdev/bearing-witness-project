<p>Giselle Marion<strong>,</strong> writing seven years after the <em>Delgamuukw</em> judgment, for her 2004 project in the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar presented an adapted story from the Tlitcho oral tradition. titled, “Oral Traditions of the Tlicho." The story is beautifully illustrated with Giselle’s own drawings and an accompanying essay on the issue emerging from <em>Delgamuukw</em> of how the courts should treat evidence of oral histories. </p>

<p>Giselle, a member of the Tlicho Nation, born in Rae/ Edzo in the NWT, explains in her essay the inspiration for her project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The purpose of this paper is to touch on the importance of oral history to the Tlicho Nation, and to assess the place of culture in the courts. To this end I have adapted a key story from the oral tradition, the story of Wezitsatla (Went-Inside-Mountain) into a storybook : How the Hozt ( Barrenlands) came to be and how the Wezitsatla (Went- Inside-Mountain) got its name. Accompanying Story Book of Wezitsatla is a paper which puts the story in its appropriate cultural context and explains the continuing importance oral traditions to the Tlicho today.</p>

<p>There are three parts to this paper. The first section consists of a brief overview of the Wezitsatla as told to anthropologist Jun Helm in 1962. This leads into a brief history of the Tlicho people focusing on the importance of the oral tradition to their cultural and social self identification. Finally, given the centrality of the oral tradition to Native life, we will outline its increasing legal significance – particularly in the areas of land-claims – over the past decade.</p>

<p>The oral tradition, as it evolves and is augmented over time, serves as a tangible connection to the past, and a record of the evolution of native cultures and peoples, it memorializes sites, events and people who are central to group identity and it often re- cost them – subtly or significantly – over time. This exposition of the story of Wezitsatla when underline the challenges that face the efforts of Canadian courts to grapple with this and in the process’ fix’ the oral tradition into legal evidence.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her essay Giselle explains that there are more than one version of the story of Wezitsatla:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The story varies over time, and there are variations from the main story of the jealous husband and unfaithful wife recorded by Helm in 1962. One version of the story of Wezitsatla has only heroes and none of the moral ambiguity of the dominant narrative. In the alternative version of the story, a powerful medicine man uses the mountain to protect a group of Tlicho who are “hiding from a raiding party of.. Yellowknife warriors”.</p>

<p>The medicine man lifts the mountain up and covers the Tlicho fugitives with it, providing them shelter from their enemies. According to this version, the whispering heard on the mountain today are the voices of the medicine man’s wife and the young man she ran away with.</p>

<p>Although radically different, the two stories have marked similarities: the role of the powerful medicine man in ceiling or placing people within the mountain; the role of the mountain as a place of sanctuary in a time of danger; the melding of people with the land (in both versions the people who go into the mountain literally become a part of it); and the lingering evidence that something important happened in that place (the whispering). Despite the moral ambiguity of the first version of the story, it is one that is more widely known: it is the version that my uncle told me when I was a young girl.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Giselle’s essay makes the point that the oral tradition of the Tlitcho is not static but an important part of building a strong contemporary Tlicho Nation. At the time of her creating her project the Tlicho had only the year before (2003) signed the Tłı̨ chǫ Land Claims and Self-Government Agreement with Canada and the Government of the Northwest Territories. The Tlitcho Agreement was one of a series of regional land-claims and self-government agreements in the NWT, that in effect replaced the colonial regimes established under Treaties 8 and 11. Significantly the Tłı̨ chǫ Land Claims and Self-government Agreement was the first NWT Agreement to abandon the language of extinguishment of Aboriginal title and to explicitly recognize the right tself-government.<sup data-fn="1341f9c5-7302-4b83-9dc0-60bdcbedf515" class="fn"><a href="#1341f9c5-7302-4b83-9dc0-60bdcbedf515" id="1341f9c5-7302-4b83-9dc0-60bdcbedf515-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Prior to the negotiation of the final agreement, Giselle explains that the Tlicho had already taken important steps to take back control over their communities and invigorate “their cultural pride and cohesive community cohesiveness” and that the Tlicho Final Agreement represents a new stage in Tlicho self-determination:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Tlicho have developed many programs and initiatives to foster cultural pride and community cohesiveness. Recognizing that language is one of the most important signifiers of community, the Dogrib Divisional Board Of Education has produced Tlicho Tatii Enihtle-a Dogrib dictionary: Tlicho community and regional organizations have committed to conducting business and community affairs and teacher. All of this has fostered a sort of renaissance and treat our culture and spurred a regionwide interest in conducting traditional knowledge research. The recovery of the oral tradition is one very significant part of that effort.</p>

<p>The oral tradition is central to the Tlicho identity and self understanding. Mostly unrecorded, except for rare exceptions like Helms Tells of the Dogrib’s, the oral tradition is free to grow, change and evolve over time. it is responsive to historical changes and historical needs (witness the two different versions of the Wezitsatla story). And it provides a glimpse of the inner workings of both culture and society at a particular moment in time.</p>

<p>The oral tradition is a powerful signifier of identity for the Tlicho Nation and for Aboriginal peoples in general. it provides them with a sense of connection to both the land in the past. It provides them with a sense of order and a shared history as an organized people and society around which both community and culture coalesce. Generations of forced acculturation have damaged Native cultures and Native peoples profoundly and the recovery of their past and traditions has been a slow, but ultimately empowering process. The fact that the Tlicho are e highly organized society has sustained them and strengthened them to adapt and to set goals that include self-government which they have always had . Education and research initiatives such as those launched by the Elders of the Tlicho Nation show great promise for creating an even more vibrant cultural communities, for recovering language and cultural artifacts that were on the verge of extinction less than thirty years ago.</p>

<p>The next step is already underway. The conclusion of the Final Agreement in 2003, and the slow legislative process that will eventually create a sovereign Tlicho Nation represents a great step forward in the recovery of native rights and titles in Canada. This would not have been possible had oral history and the oral tradition not found their place in the evidentiary process.</p>

<p>It will be interesting to see what extent the advisory committee that is a key part of the Tlicho Nation’s Final Agreement will be allowed to make government responsive to tradition and vice versa. As with the courts, the Tlicho will develop criteria to be culturally relevant, Tlicho lawmaking will transfer stories like Wezitsatla into a codified language. The danger is losing fluidity. The advantage of oral transmission is that each generation that “hears the stories” have it reinterpreted to them in ways that the new generation can understand. The elders play a vital role in this re-transmission and so their participation in the lawmaking projects of the community through self-government is very important. They are clearly on the ground creators of new relationships not only to the land but to each other.</p>

<p>The courts need to re-examine how to build in space for new legal precedent and oral traditions in defining these relationships and inevitable tensions that result from the creation.</p>

<p>As the Tlicho prepare for the future, they continually integrate their heritage culture and way of life into the complexities of life in the twenty-first century. The great chief Jimmy Bruneau of the Tlicho spoke of the future of a strong culture, mixing traditions in a modern government structure, bringing together two worlds to the benefit of both.</p>

<p>I have the benefit of both worlds in my life and by my writing a version of The Storybook of Wezitsatla is my sincerest hope of demonstrating the fusion of two worlds one spoken by chief Jimmy Bruneau.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Gisele Marion is now Director of Client Services in the Behchoko office of the Tlicho Government.</p>

The Agreement provides the Tłı̨ chǫ with ownership of a single block of 39,000 square kilometres of land, including subsurface resources, centred on the four Tłı̨ chǫ communities. The Government of Canada provides the Tłı̨ chǫ Government with capital transfer payments totalling $152 million over 14 years and the Tłı̨ chǫGovernment will receive a share of resource royalties collected by government from resource development in the Mackenzie Valley. The Agreement also provides for self-government. The Tłı̨ chǫ Government was created with law-making authority over Tłı̨ chǫ Citizens in Tłı̨ chǫ communities and on Tłı̨ chǫ lands. This law-making authority includes aspects of education, adoption, child and family services, training, income support, social housing, and Tłı̨ chǫ language and culture. Consistent with the Agreement, the Tłı̨ chǫ Community Governments (TCGs) of Behchoko, Whatì, Gamètì, and Wekweètì were established pursuant to territorial legislation on August 4, 2005. The TCGs replaced the Indian Act Bands and NWT municipal corporations that were in place prior to the effective date of the Agreement. The TCGs are responsible for municipal services and represent and serve all residents in the communities. The Tłı̨ chǫ Agreement is accompanied by an Intergovernmental Agreement that establishes the Tłı̨ chǫ Community Services Agency, pursuant to territorial legislation. The Agency is responsible for the management, administration and delivery of health, education and other social programs and services to all residents in the four Tłı̨ chǫ communities and on Tłı̨ chǫ lands, based on Tłı̨ chǫ Laws and NWT Laws. <a href="#1341f9c5-7302-4b83-9dc0-60bdcbedf515-link" aria-label="Back to content">↩</a></li>
