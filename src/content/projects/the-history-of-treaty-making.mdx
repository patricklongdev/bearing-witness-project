<p>This second part of the Archive addresses the history, significance and interpretation of treaty-making. Several projects physically reproduce the ceremonial and diplomatic instruments that were an integral part of the early treaty-making; others provide annotated and illustrated commentaries on historical treaties concluded in the 18th and 19th centuries and modern treaties concluded in British Columbia in the 20th and 21st centuries. There are projects by students who are themselves citizens of Indigenous treaty nations that incorporate family and community histories; others by non-indigenous students that reflect on their experience as non-indigenous persons growing up in parts of Canada where treaties have been signed.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The History of Treaty Making The Covenant Chain</h3>

<p>Before introducing the projects it may be helpful for visitors to have some historical context. For many Canadians, including lawyers and judges, the popular image of an Indian Treaty is a stereotype in which unsophisticated Aboriginal peoples were duped by colonial governments into giving up their land for beads and blankets. The historical reality demonstrates that treaties and agreements were entered into with Indian nations for the acquisition of territory and jurisdiction and that treaties played a vital part in the accommodation of Crown-Indian relationships from the earliest stages of colonial settlement in North America. In stark contrast to the stereotype, Indian treaty-making was not the importation of a European model of law and diplomacy into a North American landscape but built upon existing Indigenous treaty traditions remarkable for their sophistication and elegance. Several projects highlight the particular importance of the Covenant Chain treaty making between the Haudenasaunee -The Six Nations of the Iroquois Confederacy- and the British colonial authorities.</p>

<p>In the context of 17th and 18th century North America the Iroquois Confederacy came to occupy an increasingly significant role, one explained partly by their strategic geographical location and military strength but even more so by their authority and influence forged through treaty diplomacy. As described by Robert Williams:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The league… was one of the largest tribal groups in eastern North America. The league nations possessed an ability to muster up to as many as 2000 warriors during the colonial period, making their confederacy one of the most significant military powers on the continent. Geographically, the Iroquois controlled one of the most strategically vital regions in colonial North America. The league's traditional territories were situated in what is now upper New York State along the southern side of the St. Lawrence River and Lake Ontario. To their north and west on the opposite side of the St. Lawrence was French Canada; to their immediate south and east were Great Britain's Atlantic seaboard colonies. Their advantageous position on the major transmontane and riverine highways of the greater Appalachian frontier provided them easy access to the widely dispersed supplies of valuable beaver furs that dominated the North American colonial trade. Situated squarely between the competing imperial ambitions of France and England on the continent and rivaling both countries militarily by sheer force of numbers, the Iroquois tribes were valued and courted as allies by these two European colonial rivals throughout the Encounter era. Of all of the Indian tribes of North America, the Iroquois were listened to most seriously by these Europeans.<sup data-fn="610b0a74-4293-4006-a636-4ed2d8b5190a" class="fn"><a href="#610b0a74-4293-4006-a636-4ed2d8b5190a" id="610b0a74-4293-4006-a636-4ed2d8b5190a-link">1</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>As the struggle for colonial ascendancy in North America intensified it became increasingly clear to the French and English that the Iroquois Confederacy held the balance of power and therefore maintaining an enduring alliance with the Confederacy and those Indian nations within its sphere of influence was an essential element of colonial policy. Felix Colin in his Handbook of Federal Indian Law, speaking of the role of the Iroquois, had this to say:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The friendship of these Indians was a highly important, if not the decisive factor, in the struggle of France and England for this continent. The history of the struggle, as enacted in America, is largely the history of these Indians, who in defending their own lands, played an international role which brought them recognition in treaties between France and England. It is no wonder that the Iroquois were “courted and conciliated” by England and that their national character was scrupulously observed and recognized.<sup data-fn="5e5697d1-4a7f-4ddc-a4bf-ade8e602200d" class="fn"><a href="#5e5697d1-4a7f-4ddc-a4bf-ade8e602200d" id="5e5697d1-4a7f-4ddc-a4bf-ade8e602200d-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Iroquois Confederacy, originally comprised the Mohawks, Oneidas, Onandagas, Cayugas and Senecas, and later, in the eighteenth century the Tuscaroras. The Confederacy’s origins are contained within the epic of Deganawidah where after generations of warfare and feuding the Creator sends a prophet, Deganawidah, with a message of peace and power. According to the epic Deganawidah tells the message of peace first to the Iroquois chief, Hiawatha, accompanied by the rituals of condolence. As described by Robert Williams:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Offering strings of wampum, Deganawidah spoke several words of condolence: the first dried Hiawatha's weeping eyes, the second opened his ears, the third unstopped his throat, and so on until his sorrow was relieved and his reason restored. These condolence ceremonies were at the core of a new gospel, the Good News of Peace and Power, that would make mourning wars unnecessary. "When men accept it," Deganawidah said of his message, "they will stop killing, and bloodshed will cease from the land."</p>

<p>Hiawatha accepted Deganawidah's message of peace, and the Condolence Ceremonies, which embodied and made effective the Good News of Peace, became, at least in one very strong sense, the reason for the existence of the League of the Iroquois. Together, Deganawidah and Hiawatha carried the "Great Law" to the local chiefs of the Iroquois settlements and urged them to "abandon their feuds, reform their minds, and unite”. A "Tree of Peace" was to be planted among the Iroquois, with its "Four White Roots of Peace" extending in cardinal directions and reaching all peoples with the "Good News of Peace and Power."</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I, Deganawidah, and the confederate lords now uproot the tallest pine tree and into the cavity thereby made we cast all weapons of war. . . . We bury them from sight forever and plant again the tree. Thus shall all Great Peace be established and hostilities shall no longer be known between the Five Nations but only peace to a United people.According to the epic, it was Deganawidah, with Hiawatha as his agent, who thus formed the Iroquois confederacy out of this time of intense chaos and crises and endowed it with its central rites and symbols Contained in the Condolence Council ceremonies.</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>Although the Iroquois had been referred to as the “Romans of the Western world” Prof. Williams explains that it was more than their numerical strength and strategic location that explained their great influence. Williams traces their "dominant influence on the intercultural diplomacy of the Encounter era” and the “multicultural constitutional tradition” they introduced derived from “the Great Peace”.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>We therefore bind ourselves together by taking hold of each other's hands firmly and forming a circle so strong that if a tree shall fall prostrate upon it, it could neither shake nor break it, and thus our people and our grandchildren shall remain in the circle in security, peace and happiness.</p>

<p>One of the most significant efforts by the Iroquois to perpetuate this constitutional tradition occurred at the Three Rivers Treaty Council of 1645. There, Kiotseaeton, the great Iroquois diplomat/storyteller, sought to form an alliance with the French and their allied tribes by presenting a treaty belt that would, in his words, "bind the two sides very closely together. In explaining the "words" of this wampum belt, described "as extraordinarily beautiful" by Father Vimont, Kiotseaeton took hold of a Frenchman, placed his arm within his, and with his other arm he clasped that of an Algonquin. Having thus joined himself to them, "Here," he said, "is the knot that binds us. . . . Even if the lighting were to fall upon us, it could not separate us, for, if it cuts off the arm that holds you to us, we will at once seize each other by the other arm.</p>

<p>Kiotseaeton's linking arms together with the French and the Algonquian Indians was an imaginative way of retelling the same basic story of Deganawidah's founding act of Iroquois multitribal unity. By joining the Iroquois with the French and their allied tribes of Canada, Kiotseaeton perpetuated an ancient constitutional traditional of human solidarity achieved between different peoples on the multicultural frontiers of the Encounter era.<sup data-fn="ddd6eca7-a69b-46b1-9a96-80ca4b9e4ba5" class="fn"><a href="#ddd6eca7-a69b-46b1-9a96-80ca4b9e4ba5" id="ddd6eca7-a69b-46b1-9a96-80ca4b9e4ba5-link">3</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>This concept of linking arms together received its fullest expression in the most famous multicultural treaty system known as the "Covenant Chain" which reflected in metaphorical terms how the Indian Nations saw their relationship with the Dutch and later the English.</p>

<p>Robert Williams has counterpoised the significance of the Covenant Chain from both colonial and Iroquois perspectives:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>English colonial officials universally regarded the Iroquois Covenant Chain as the single most important indigenous political, military, and economic institution on the continent. Through it, as the English clearly recognized, the Five Nations of the Iroquois confederacy maintained an effective capability of tipping the balance of power in North America. Only the Iroquois "empire" stood between England's weakly defended colonies on the Atlantic seaboard and France's desires to assert its hegemony over the North American continent….</p>

<p>According to their [Iroquois] American Indian vision of law and peace, the Covenant Chain told an important story of how two different peoples had established a valuable treaty relationship that both could rely on to protect their interests in survival in a multicultural North America.<sup data-fn="26deec05-c4de-44fa-88d3-0f775542a165" class="fn"><a href="#26deec05-c4de-44fa-88d3-0f775542a165" id="26deec05-c4de-44fa-88d3-0f775542a165-link">4</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The first projects in this section of the Archive looks at Indigenous memorials of the Covenant Chain - Wampum Belts- that reveal a world of meaning in the history of treaty-making. Fashioned from conch and clam shells in the shape of beads, wampum served as a medium of exchange. It not only had a definite value in relation to deerskins, beaver pelts, and later British coins but of even greater significance, the exchange of wampum was the lingua franca in treaty diplomacy.</p>

<p>In the context of 17th and 18th-century North American diplomacy, an understanding of “wampum” was as indispensable to success as the European derived conventions and formalities that took place at the courts in London, Paris, Vienna and St Petersburg. “So vital was wampum as a medium of communication that no frontier negotiator could hope to succeed unless he knew the language of beads.” The form of that language ranged from single strands of beads to elaborately woven belts often six feet long and up to a foot wide, reserved for memorializing the most important events. There is nothing in our contemporary international diplomacy, with its prepared texts and communiqués, typically drafted by panels of speech writers/officials, delivered with the aid of telepropters, that matches the language of beads.</p>

<p>This account reveals some of that power of the language of beads:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In diplomacy, wampum worked its magic in various ways. Indian messengers would say that it served to "confirm" or "enforce" their words, it guaranteed that "we speak truth" and ensured that a speech would "have Credit with you," would "have its full Effect on" the listener's "Mind.' Natives might even have thought strings and belts more powerful still, so powerful that the shells themselves held the message. Thus an Iroquois or Delaware council spoke words directly into a string or belt as we would into a tape recorder; then a messenger, reaching his destination, merely turned the beads on and became their mouthpiece. So animated, a belt took on a mind and life of its own. The wampum, Indian envoys might say, "has been leading us by the arm." One Iroquois go-between even talked of how "this day a Belt of Wampum (black) came to Shamokin from Oneida from the Six Nations," as if it had floated down the Susquehanna on its own.</p>

<p>The delicate beads, then, carried heavy freight in formal conversation. "Without Wampum," one colonist observed, "Nothing is to be done Amongst the Indians."' The number of beads needed to conduct diplomacy was staggering. Some messengers had just a string or two in a pocket or pouch, but others crammed a bag or "casket" with ten, twenty, even thirty or more belts, each bearing part of a talk, each containing anywhere from several hundred to ten thousand beads that, woven into a belt, might be a foot wide and six feet long.</p>

<p>Wampum's value as a communications medium stemmed in part from its versatility. Just as it had many uses within a town, so between peoples one string might mark a messenger's status while another sanctioned part of a speech and—carefully stored in a "Counsel Bagg," then regularly pulled out for rehearsal of its message—kept words alive far into the future. Better still, wampum could take many forms. A large belt or long string meant something important. White shells denoted peace, "black" (actually purple) ones war, though words of war also came in as scalps tied to a white belt, red paint splashed across it, or a hatchet woven into its face.</p>

<p>Other patterns, their message less self-evident, nonetheless helped wampum be read and reread. Friendship might appear as people holding hands or as a row of dark beads running down a white belt to connect nations, themselves depicted as squares or diamonds, hands or human figures. Some belts told a more specific tale. One, sent from Iroquoia to Susquehanna Indians in 1722 to urge that they dump rather than drink rum, bore a small circle for the keg and a hatchet denoting its destruction. Senecas wove one that had six human figures, five of them branded with two hearts, to say that Senecas alone, with but one heart, were true to the English. A belt Teedyuscung spoke on in 1756 was equally elaborate: an armed man posted at each end, with lines leading to an empty square in the middle, told of natives' determination to keep their country free of both British and French armies pressing upon it.</p>

<p>However clear the message, making belts talk was an art. Pulling them out, a messenger would set them "in order on the Table," then pick up each one in turn and hold it while speaking its words. Wampum virtuosos had a certain flair. During the war an Onondaga messenger named Ogaghradarisha, to mark how far up the Susquehanna his people wanted Pennsylvania to build a fort, left a wampum belt folded in half to designate the road to Shamokin; then, at the right moment, he opened it full length to stretch, metaphorically, all the way upriver past Wyoming.<sup data-fn="eaf1bd84-0f7e-4f63-9736-055ef565a07b" class="fn"><a href="#eaf1bd84-0f7e-4f63-9736-055ef565a07b" id="eaf1bd84-0f7e-4f63-9736-055ef565a07b-link">5</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The first covenant chain treaty concluded between English colonists and the Iroquois was made shortly after the English conquest of New Netherlands in 1664. In September of that year, Colonel George Cartwright met in Albany with the Sachems of the Mohawk and the Seneca Nations. The treaty was designed to ensure the continuation of the friendly relations established by the Dutch with the Iroquois.</p>

<p>The principal subject matter of the treaty was the punishment of trans-national crimes and the treaty expressly recognizes the mutual independence and juridical equality of the contracting parties. Articles I and II refer to the “Indian princes … and their subjects”, distinguishing them from “any English, Dutch or Indian (under the protection of the English)”. In Articles II and III, each contracting party recognizes the jurisdiction of the other over its subjects or peoples under its protection for transnational crimes. Accordingly, crimes committed by English subjects or those under English protection against the Iroquois were to be punished by the English authorities, while those committed by the Iroquois against English subjects or Indians subject to English protection were to be punished by the Iroquois.</p>

<p>The historical record of this treaty is not however limited to in the archival text. It is also memorialized in the Iroquoian archive of wampum belts in a belt known as Gus-Wen-Qah or Two Row Wampum.</p>
