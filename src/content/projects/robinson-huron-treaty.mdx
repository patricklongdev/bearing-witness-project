<p>The “hope that we will establish a better future” is a theme explored in an imaginative project by Katie McGreer in the 2010 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Seminar. Written in the form of an investigative news report Katie writes about a third year law student, Acta Visim, who time travels back to 1850 to Sault Ste. Marie to a time just prior to the negotiation of the Robinson Huron treaty. This and the companion Robinson Superior treaty dramatically changed the scale of the Indigenous territory subject to treaty-making and contained important provisions regarding harvesting rights that were carried over into the Numbered Treaties later negotiated between 1870 and 1921. Under the Vancouver Sun byline “Investigative Report: UBC Law Student Gives Legal Advice to the Ojibwa People by Using Grandparents’ Time Machine, Katie writes:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>TWO MONTHS ago, in early March 2010, Acta Visim, a third year law student at UBC, found a time machine in her grandparent’s attic. Since then, she has been using the contraption to travel back to the mid 19th century and advise the Ojibwa People of Lake Huron of their legal rights. The time machine travels through space, so Ms. Visim is able to meet with the Ojibwa in Sault Ste. Marie, Ontario.</p>

<p>No one has been in contact with Ms. Visim for the last three days. Last reported sighting of the law student was Friday April 23rd.</p>

<p>Friends say that Ms. Visim has been traveling back to the past since mid-March. She goes a few times a week to collect information, learn the language, and gain the Ojibwa nations’ trust. In an attempt to explain her actions, Ms. Visim’s family reports that she has always been troubled by what she perceives as the lack of integrity of the treaty making process. Visim is reportedly passionate about constitutional law issues and in particular aboriginal rights in Canada.</p>

<p>The police uncovered notes from Ms. Visim’s class on Aboriginal and Treaty Rights. They found the following extracts from the Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples 1996 report underlined and highlighted in her binder:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“While the wording in both treaties provided that Ojibwa hunting and fishing would be undisturbed, the written treaty describes the agreement as a total surrender of territory, terminology that had not been agreed to in negotiations […] Ojibwa understood that the treaties involved only a limited use of their land…”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Authorities speculate that Ms. Visim is trying to clarify the terms of the agreement with the Ojibwa people before they sign the treaty. If she does indeed seek to lend a “helping” hand, it would come as no surprise seeing as Ms. Visim also underlined the following in her text:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“negotiation and dialogue did not, and could not venture into the meaning of specific terminology, legal or otherwise, and remained at a broad general level, owing to time and language barriers […] under these circumstances, conceptual and language barriers would have been difficult to overcome”.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>No one knows how much contact Acta Visim has already had with either party to the agreement or what legal advice, if any, she has provided. Professor Doom, from the University of Cynicism’s Faculty of Law is overcome with anger at Visim’s endeavor:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“I have little confidence in the ability of a 3rd year law student to provide meaningful advice to parties striking a momentous deal. Her impulsiveness is clear indication that she is incapable of rational, sound thinking. I am baffled that someone would be presumptuous enough to meddle with history. Ms. Visim is facing a quagmire and she already has one foot in the bog.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>While the consequences of any actions Ms. Visim may take are unknown, the risks are high. Any intervention could change the course of history. Professor Hope, from Utopia University applauds Visim for her sense of initiative:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“I am happy to know that today’s youth are not complacent. We need more people like Ms. Visim who are willing to take a stand for what they think is right and who aren’t afraid to get their hands dirty.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Dr. Hope maintains that Visim’s intercourse with history could be a good thing. It may, according to Hope, lead to more a more fair and equitable outcome for the Ojibwa people. The spillover effect, she projects, would be fantastic. Meanwhile, the government has put together an intervention group and they are devising plans – and contingency plans – to deal with the consequences of Visim’s actions. Thus far, no one else has been able to use the time machine; Visim secured the primary transmitter and thrown away the deactivator.</p>

<p>No one has figured out the passcode.</p>

<p>In the meantime, while we wait with baited breath for Ms. Visim to return, we can take a look at the history, which may be changing, before our eyes.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Katie’s essay reviews the change in the pattern of treaty-making in Canada in the 19th century, in large measure due to the shift in the balance of power between Indigenous people and the rapidly expanding settler population; the colonial interests in opening up the Ojibway territories for mining exploration and the Ojibway protests and resistance to those activities without the recognition of their rights that provided the context for the Robinson Huron and Robinson Superior treaties. The essay also questions the integrity of the treaty-making process in the different conceptions of what the treaty was intended to accomplish. She cites the Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples Report:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples asserts that aboriginal people were routinely misled in their dealings with the Crown:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“In some, and perhaps many, of these transactions, the Indian nations thought they were conveying their land to the Crown for the limited purpose of authorizing the Crown to ‘protect’ their lands from incoming settlement”</p>

<p>It is likely that the Ojibwa people knew they were permitting the Crown to use their land -especially in relation to the exploitation of subsurface minerals. But it is uncertain whether they knew the treaty was an unconditional surrender of land.</p>

<p>The Commission further asserts that a total agreed to during the negotiations between the Ojibwa and Robinson.</p>

<p>Although the Robinson- Huron Treaty guarantees that the Crown will not interfere with the Ojibwa’s ability to hunt and fish, and despite the fact that they knew the British Crown would benefit from the treaty, the Commission thinks it is unlikely the Ojibwa people intended to transfer their land to Crown indefinitely. </p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>Katie’s essay concludes with an intriguing question of how history might have changed if the Indigenous peoples had known of the dark shadow that colonial expansionist and assimilationist policies would cast over there societies and cultures and economies.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Implications of Acta’s Interference with History</h3>

<p>The Robinson treaties are unique and Visim’s decision to intervene in September 1850 may have been strategic. The Robinson Treaties mark the start of Britain’s “cash for- land” policies on treaty making. The BC government recognizes the impact the Robinson treaties had on subsequent land treaties:</p>

<p>“Much of the groundwork, moreover, for future treaty-making procedures in Canada was being set by the Robinson-Superior and Robinson-Huron Treaties of 1850. The Robinson Treaties […] did not establish a formula for future treaty activities but provided a tidier method”</p>

<p>No one knows what will happen when Ms. Visim returns from 1850. Her actions may have a profound effect on the past, the present and the future. It may be good news for aboriginal groups in Canada, but it will also change the political and legal landscape of Canada. The results of her foray into the past will be contingent on the advice she gives the Ojibwa people. As such we will have to see what the future has in store.</p>
</blockquote>
