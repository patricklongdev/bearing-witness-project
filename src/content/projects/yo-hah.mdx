In both the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights and Prisoners' Rights seminars students responded to the mixed-media challenge of reflecting their understanding of the law by designing board games. Julia Hincks, for her project in the 2012 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation seminar created "Yo-Hah" "as a fun and educational game designed to test your creativity and knowledge about Indigenous people in North America. The name Yo -Hah comes from the Haudenosaunee chant of approval given to show approbation for a speech delivered at a treaty council.[^1]

> ### Yo-Hah- Instructions and Notes
>
> Creators Note
>
> Yo-hah is designed to be informational and enjoyable for friends and families including children 12 and older. The game will encourage you to challenge your mind will provide an opportunity to learn every time you play! The game attempts to recognize the varied cultures and norms of First Nations and settlers in North America, yet does not attempt to address each cultural group. The object is to illustrate how First Nations issues, treaty rights specifically, can fit into the realm of trivia and entertainment in Canadian households. I hope you enjoy playing it as much as I enjoyed putting it together!
>
> ### Object
>
> The object of the game is to be the first team to move around the board in a clock wise fashion into the centre representation of the 7 Cherokee Clans, and complete a final activity for the win!
>
> ### Setup:
>
> 1. Divide the group into teams of two or more. Each team chooses a game piece and places it on the dwelling structure labelled "Start"
> 2. Set out the four boxes of questions, and the die, timer, and tub of clay.
> 3. Give each team a pad and pencil.
> 4. You're ready to go!
>
> ### How to Play:
>
> 1. Select which team will go first.
> 2. On your turn, the team to your right draws your card and reads it aloud to you. Your card is drawn from the character card box that matches the coloured space your team's game piece is on.
>
>    **\*Note**: On your first turn, and every time you reach a dwelling structure, you get to choose which box your card will be drawn from.
>
> 3. Your team will then need to attempt to do the activities on the card.
> 4. If you're successful- roll the die and move to the next colour indicated on the die. You must stop on every dwelling structure, even if your roll would otherwise take you past it. If you roll a purple, go on to the next dwelling house. Your turn is now over and play passes to your left.
> 5. If you are unsuccessful, don't roll or move, you must wait until your next turn and try again.
>
>    **How to dart as fast as an arrow:** At each dwelling structure you have the chance to get your team to zip ahead like an arrow. If your team is successful on the first activity you do at the dwelling structure, you get to follow the coloured arrows running diagonally across the board. Otherwise, meander along the footpath on the outer scenic route.
>
> ### Winning:
>
> 1. Enter the 7-sided representation of the Cherokee Clans on a roll at the end of a turn. Move to the name of the character card box that matches the colour on the die. If you roll purple, you can choose your starting point on the circle. Now wait for your next turn.
> 2. Each time you successfully complete an activity in the Cranium Circle, keep the card, move clockwise to the next character name and wait until your next turn to do an activity from that box. If you are not successful, stay where you are and try again on your next turn.
> 3. Once your team holds one card from each character card box, move into the centre of the clan circle. On your next turn, the other teams collectively choose from the character card box for your final activity. If you are unsuccessful, you must try again on your next turn. If your team is the first to complete an activity in the centre of the clan circle, congratulations! You've won!
>
> ### A Note on Sources:
>
> During the creation of this game, from the conception to completion, I borrowed images, and ideas from other games and artists. In creating the questions, I turned largely to the course material and lecture notes from the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights and Aboriginal Peoples Course at the University of British Columbia Faculty of Law. I have cited the creative works I borrowed, as well as included a short note on the artists I borrowed from. Because this work is subject to copyright, the game is not intended for any commercial distribution.

> ### Artists:
>
> For each of the cards and their boxes, I sought out images that I felt illustrate some of the variety of contemporary First Nations art. Please see a short description of the artists below, as well as a sentence on why I selected the piece for each category.
>
> **Sue Coleman:** Owl on the Lore Category Cards\
> Sue Coleman is an artist from Vancouver Island, who does watercolour work that transcends cultural boundaries. I selected this piece for the Lore cards as it shows the wisdom of the owl and pushes boundaries between art forms.\
> Online: [Sue Coleman Website](https://www.suecoleman.ca/artcards.htm)
>
> **Andy Everson:** Warrior (or Harbinger of the Treaty Empire?) on the Talking Stick Category Cards\
> Andy Everson is an artist from Comox, British Columbia, who is influenced by the artistic traditions of both the K'omoks and Kwakwakawakw First Nations. The piece that I selected for the Talking Stick questions because it is a cheeky nod to a famous movie, but also speaks to the treaty making process and asks if it is really black and white, or is it shades of grey? "Warrior (or Harbinger of the Treaty Empire?)", online: [Andy Everson website](http://www.andyeverson.com/2011/warrior_or_harbinger_of_the_treaty_empire.html)
>
> **Aaron Paquet:** The Night I was Healed I Flew to the Stars on the Visionary Category Cards. Aaron Paquet is a Cree and Cherokee artist based out of Edmonton, Alberta. I selected this image for the Visionary category because it spoke to me as illustrative of the key to success in this category- creativity and an open mind.\
> "The Night I was Healed I Flew to the Stars" online: [Aaron Paquette](http://www.aaronpaquette.net)
>
> **Bill Reid:** Grizzly Bear on the Puzzled Category Cards, Sockeye Salmon Serigraph on background to board game and box cover.\
> Bill Reid was one of Canada's foremost First Nation's artists, whose works included jewellery, sculpture, screen-printing and painting. He was born in British Columbia and had an American father, and a mother from the Haida nation. I selected the Grizzly Bear image for the Puzzled category because it shows the beauty that can arise from a methodical, yet creative approach. The bear symbolizes strength and a fierce character.
>
> **Cherokee 7 Clan Image**: In the center of the board.\
> I selected this image because it is illustrative of the diversity that can exist even within a nation, and illustrate the rules and regulations that guide a community.

[^1]: According to Richard Cullen Rath, "Sounds sealed treaties and made them real, whether between Indian Nations or between Indians and whites. First Nations people structured their public spaces through nonverbal response to speech." Richard Cullen Rath, How Early America Sounded (Ithaca, N.Y.Cornell University Press, 2005) at 160. Made after a speech had been interpreted, it signified that the entire community understood and assented to whatever had just been said. As the explorer Jonathan Carver understood, the Yo-hah "served to bind the agreement as signatures would have on a written document." The use of the Yo-hah in combination with wampum further served to emphasize and give meaning to whatever had just been said in treaty council. Wampum not only made the spoken words effective, but, Rath argues, "the speech entered into the wampum, leaving traces of its meaning." He suggests that to the early Iroquois, texts, reading, and currency would be understood as like wampum. You can learn more about the Yo-Hah as part of Indigenous diplomacy in [Krista Johanson's project](/projects/treaty-of-lancaster-june-1744/) in the Treaty Rights section of the website.
