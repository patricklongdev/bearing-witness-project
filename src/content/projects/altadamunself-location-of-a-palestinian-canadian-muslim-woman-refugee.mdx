<p>Dalal Tubeishat for the 2023 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation seminar prepared an illustrated essay titled Altadamun: Self-Location of a Palestinian-Canadian Muslim Woman Refugee. Re-reading her essay in 2026 in light of the most recent war in Gaza and the deaths of thousands of Palestinian civilians and the destruction of Palestinian homes has only heightened the gravitas of her important contribution to the archive. Dalal locates herself and her family as Palestinian refugees and immigrants to Canada and explores the relationship between race, gender, immigration, and settler colonialism and conclude by putting forward an anti-colonial conceptualization of settler that unveils the responsibilities she sees for herself as a racialized settler towards Indigenous peoples, responsibilities that will carry with her as a lawyer. To illuminate her analysis Dalal created a three piece print series titled Altadamun, which means solidarity in Arabic. The three prints are in conversation with the themes of the seminar</p>

<p>Dalal introduces her project in this way:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This paper is inspired by the Allard Law School seminar, Aboriginal and Treaty Rights, created and taught by Michael Jackson KC. This seminar takes a different approach to other Allard Law classes, in that foremost lawyers practicing Indigenous law attend as guest lecturers to speak to students about their current and most impactful cases. At the end of the semester, students are invited to submit a piece of art work as their mode of assessment, rather than the traditional exam or research paper. I drew inspiration for this reflection paper and the accompanying art series from the guest lecturers. Despite practicing in the same field, the guest lecturers were diverse in their years of practice, their backgrounds coming into the law, and their approaches and motives to practicing in Indigenous law. Like many of the guest lecturers, I aspire to practice Indigenous law as a non-Indigenous lawyer. In doing so, like them, I am placing myself in the contradictory relationship of being a settler who inherently benefits from the displacement of indigenous peoples while dedicating my career to combatting the ongoing colonization of indigenous peoples and their land. Many of the guest lecturers named this contradictory relationship, and shared their experiences reconciling their role in settler colonialism with their inherently decolonial work.</p>

<p>In this vein, in this paper, I aim to unpack my own relationship to this contradiction by examining my identities as a Palestinian-Canadian Muslim refugee woman and my relationship with colonization. Through self-locating and unpacking my own identities, I attempt to demonstrate that self-locating as a non-Indigenous lawyer unveils ways in which we can become more effective and informed advocates. To do this, I first explain that as a Palestinian Muslim refugee woman, though I have Canadian citizenship, I do not fit the mold of 'Canadian' and am cast outside the understanding of Canadian citizenship. In connecting race and gender to settler colonialism, I explore how Canadian nation building is directly tied to also casting Indigenous peoples outside the sphere of Canadian civility and citizenship. As a result, I argue that the commonality of these experiences is a profound site for adocacy in which solidarity can flourish.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Print 1: White Washing of Canadian History</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2026/01/Dalal_Tubeishat_02-1024x1008.jpg" alt="Dalal Tubeishat 2023 project titled, &quot;Print 1: White Washing of Canadian History.&quot;" class="wp-image-4706"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In rejecting the identity of settler, my mother contributes to the erasure of the colonial history of Canada and the ongoing displacement of Indigenous peoples. The first print is an attempt to visualize that erasure of history. Though there are many figures, events, and images that are significant in the history of Indigenous peoples in Canada, this print is a small project that aims to depict aspects of Canadian history that have been deliberately concealed in an attempt to whitewash some of the most disturbing chapters of Canadian history.</p>

<p>In the lower left comer, the print depicts the hand of Adam from the fresco painting "The Creation of Adam" by Michelangelo. This hand represents the West and the Church and, in the print, it is painting over the history of Canada in white, which represents the act of  whitewashing. The writing on the white paint is a passage from the 1455 papal bull Romanus Pontifex issued by Pope Nicholas V. The passage describes the colonizing powers issued by the Church, which granted European powers the right</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><br />... to invade, search out, capture, vanquish, and subdue all Saracens and pagans whatsoever, and other enemies of Christ whatsoever placed, and the kingdoms, dukedoms, principalities, dominions, possessions, and all movable and immovable goods whatsoever held and posses by them and to reduce their persons to perpetual slavery, and to apply and appropriate to himself and his successors the kingdoms, dukedoms, counties, principalities, dominions, possessions, and goods, and to convert them to his and their use and profit ... “</p>
</blockquote>

<p>By being embedded in the white paint, it depicts how these racist, colonial words fonn part of the origin of European colonization of Canada and are ingrained in the fabric of Canadian society itself. Instead of a signature of the artist, as is common in paintings, there is the papal bull stamp in the lower right corner. Emerging from the paint are two hands cupped together. Together with the smaller red hand prints, this image represents the countless Indigenous children whose deaths in residential schools were hidden by unmarked graves, some of which have been recently rediscovered. To the left of the hands is Louis Riel, a Metis resistance leader. Though he has been framed as a criminal, he was a land defender and an Indigenous rights activist known for leading the Red River Resistance and fighting against unfair encroachment by the federal government. In the background of the photo is a forest ravaged by deforestation. As discussed in this lecture, the overexploitation of the forests on Haida Gwaii have resulted in the loss of traditional practices, knowledge, and culture. Beyond the forest is a residential school at the base of the mountain. The remote location of the residential school in the print reflects the government's practice of encouraging residential schools to be as distant from each other and Indigenous communities as possible for the "success" of the students' assimilation. To do this, the government required there be as few residential schools as possible by maximizing their capacity for students, despite the underfunding of the schools. Holistically, the images represent small parts of the incriminating and devastating chapters in Canadian history that have been deliberate}y concealed.</p>

</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This print is instead meant to demonstrate that, when inspected more closely, Canada's formation as a state is built on the appropriation of Indigenous peoples and their lands.</p>

<p>Complicating Canada's claim to sovereignty are the First Nations whose rival land claims derive from their occupation on this land since time immemorial. Without their displacement, the Canadian settler state would not exist. To represent this, I spent hours hand-writing the names of all the First Nations, Metis, and Inuit groups in Canada until, when zoomed out, those names depict the Canadian flag. In Appendix C, below the print is a zoomed in section of the print which shows my handwriting. This print is not meant to conflate Indigenous peoples with Canadians generally or with Canada specifically.</p>

<p>al-ta-da-mun: unity or agreement of feeling or action, especially among individuals with a common interest.</p>

<p>The third print honors the theme of this paper. Altadamun is the closest word to solidarity in Arabic. The style of this print is inspired by the graffiti art on the wall in the West Bank that separates the Gaza strip from what is now known as Israel. The wall is adorned with fists, symbols, flags, and other art that is meant to serve as a visual testimony of the suffering of Palestinians due to the Israeli occupation. The print depicts two hands reaching out and clasped together from between two ban-ed windows. The first sleeve is an ode to Truth and Reconciliation Day, a day in which Indigenous peoples and settlers are encouraged to wear an orange shirt to engage with and remember the legacy of residential schools in Canada. The second sleeve is the colors and shapes of the Palestinian flag. Below the hands is altadamun written in Arabic and phonetically in English, and the definition written just below that.</p>
</blockquote>
