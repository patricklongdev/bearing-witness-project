<p>An important part of the Archive features projects in which students have reflected on their personal, family and community histories and their intersection with themes in the seminars. Across a broad spectrum of media, both Indigenous and non-Indigenous students have explored the personal, political and legal dimensions of the history of Indigenous- Settler relationships in Canada</p>

<p>Halie (Kwanxwa’logwa) Bruce, a member of the Namgis First Nation, for her project in the 2006 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Seminar in presenting a PowerPoint photo collage combined Indigenous origin and oral histories, her personal and family history, the impacts of colonization on the Kwakwa̱ ka̱ ʼwakw people and stories of resistance and renewal. Viewing Haile’s project “Where we Come from is Wealth” in 2006 had a particular resonance with my own more recent history in British Columbia. In 1972 it was through the Namgis First Nation that I was first introduced into the Indigenous world and it was that introduction that led directly to my recommending to UBC Faculty of Law that the law school teach a course on Indigenous peoples and the law.</p>

<p>Halie’s photo collage begins with the origin story of the Gwani (Nimpkish ) River:</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/10/Halie_Bruce_02-1024x508.jpg" alt="Wood carving from Halie Bruce's 2006 narrative project titled, &quot;Where we come from is Wealth&quot; " class="wp-image-2306"/></figure>
</figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>When the Transformer (Creator), Kaniki'lakw, travelled around the world, he was eventually returned to the place where Gwa'nalalis lived. In an earlier encounter, the Transformer had beaten Gwa'nalalis, who was ready for his return. Kaniki'lakw asked," Would you like to become a cedar tree?" Gwa'nalalis replied, "No, cedar trees, when struck by lightning, split and fall. Then they rot away for as long as the days dawn in the world." Kaniki'lakw asked again, "Would you like to become a mountain?" "No," Gwa'nalalis answered, "For mountains have slides and crumble away for as long as the days dawn in the world." The Transformer asked a third question. "Would you like to become a large boulder?" Again, Gwa'nalalis answered, "No. Do not let me become a boulder, for I may crack in half and crumble away as long as the days dawn in the world."</p>

<p>Finally, Kaniki'lakw asked," Would you like to become a river?" "Yes, let me become a river that I may flow for as long as the days shall dawn in the world," Gwa'nalalis replied. Putting his hand on Gwa'nalalis' forehead and pushing him down prone, Kaniki'lakw said, "There, friend, you will be a river and many kinds of salmon will come to you to provide food for your descendants for as long as the days shall dawn in the world.”</p>

<p>And so the man Gwa'nalalis became the river, Gwa'ni.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Using a collage of family photos Halie traces the history of two memorial poles. The first was stolen in 1899 by Seattle businessmen from the Tlingit village of Tongass; the second was carved in 1870 by Charlie James in the Kwakwaka'wakw style, with the same figures that were on the Tongass Tlingit pole. The poles were made in honour of Anisalaga, of the Ga’nax’ádi Raven clan. Anisalaga was a Tlingit Noble woman who was one of the original owners of the pole in Tongass. She married Robert Hunt (a Hudson's Bay Company trader) at Fort Simpson, who later was factor then owner of the HBC at Tsaxis (Fort Rupert). Anisalaga and Robert Hunt are Haile’s great, great, great grandparents.</p>

</figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Pictures: (From Top left) My great, great, great grandmother, Anisalaga (Mary Ebbets) &amp; Robert Hunt. The pole is from David Hunt’s Big House in Tsaxis (Fort Rupert) and is a replica of Anisilaga’s grandmother’s memorial pole which originally stood in a Tlingit village at Tsongass (Alaska), where Anisalaga was born. In 1899, the pole was stolen by a group of Seattle businessmen who had stopped in at the village of Tongass. Assuming the village was abandoned, they removed the large pole and took it to Seattle where it was erected in Pioneer Square. In fact, the Tongass were just away at their fishing camps. When they returned, they discovered the pole gone and reported it to Alaskan Governor Brady. The Tongass people wanted the return and payment for of the pole. Eventually, payment was made, but the pole remained in Seattle. It was damaged by a fire in 1938, and a replica was carved by Ketchikan Tlingit carvers as part of a Civilian Conservation Corps project. The replica still stands in Pioneer Square in Seattle today.</p>

<p>This pole, however, was carved for Mary’s grandson, David, and stood at Tsaxis (Fort Rupert). It was a an 1870 Kwakitul version of Anisalaga’s Tlingit pole which David had made in honour of Anisalaga, of the Ga’nax’ádi Raven clan. Anisalaga was a Tlingit Noble woman who was one of the original owners of the pole in Tongass. She married Robert Hunt [a Hudson's Bay Company trader] at Fort Simpson, who later was factor then owner of the HBC at Tsaxis (Fort Rupert). The original pole was raised around 1870 in Tongass as a memorial to Anisalaga's mother. It was carved by Charlie James in the Kwakwaka'wakw style, with the same figures that were on the Tongass Tlingit pole. Anisalaga explained the meaning of the figures on this pole to my great, great Grandfather, George. She said, at the top is the Raven with the moon in his mouth. Which means we are Ravens and relates how Raven stole the sun, moon and stars from a stingy Chief who kept them hidden in a box, and, after being chased and swooped on by Eagles, had dropped a piece which became the stars, another which became the Sun, and another the moon. Below the raven is a woman with a frog. They represent the story of a Ga’nax’ádi woman who teased a frog and was carried away to the frog village where she married and had two frog children. She escaped by sending her two sons back to the house of her father to fetch a bone for the piercing of skin. They had her father break a dam to drain the lake where the frog village was located, killing all of the frogs except her two sons. The figures at the bottom of the pole all relate to another Raven story. Below the frog is Mink, Raven, then a whale with its head pointing downward. At the bottom is the Chief of all Birds. Raven took Mink along as his companion, and they were swallowed by a whale who took them across the water as a favor. The whale invited them to cut pieces from his blubber for food, asking only that they avoid his heart. The whale drifted ashore at Rose Spit on Haida Gwaii. The people outside cut a hole for them to escape, and Mink jumped out all covered with oil. He rolled in rotten wood, giving his fur the appearance it has today, and Raven did the same thing. [There are different versions of this story].</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Haile’s great, great grandfather, George Hunt who was born in 1854 and worked with German anthropologist Franz Boas for 45 years as a translator. He also worked with photographer and filmmaker Edward S. Curtis in the making of his movie “In the Land of the War Canoes.”</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Anisalaga and Robert had many children, one of whom was my Great, great grandfather, George Hunt born February 14, 1854. Although all of their children were raised among and intermarried with the Kwakiutl, Anisalaga retained some of her Tlingit traditions as a Chilkat blanket weaver. She refused to teach the local Kwakiutl women how to make the blankets, some of which were photographed for posterity and remain in museums. It is said she would weave late at night when everyone was asleep, and taught her daughters to weave but no others.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>George Hunt [1854-1933] is most widely known for his work with German anthropologist Franz Boas, who he worked with for 45 years as a translator. See for example: “Kwakiutl Texts, 1905 &amp; 1906). George also worked with Edward S. Curtis in the making of his movie “In the Land of the War Canoes.” George also married into the Kwakwaka’wakw and had many children, including David, Sam, and my great grandfather “Odie” [Jonathan]. Odie was an accomplished Kwakwaka’wakw singer and songwriter, who married my great grandmother “Abusa” and had 16 children. My grandmother Jane “Anitsa” was one them. She married into the ‘Namgis, marrying Stephen and Jane Cook’s youngest Son, Christopher. They had 5 children, including my mother, Tłakwagila (Jall), the eldest. I am the 7th child of Elizabeth from her 3rd husband. My father was a Scot-Canadian fisherman, whose family I never met. Thus I was raised completely within the Kwakwaka’wakw and know little, if anything about my Scottish lineage.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>More recent photos depict Haile’s great grandmother, Ga’axsta’las (Jane (Gilbert) Cook and her grandfather Chris Cook Sr, her family’s logo reflecting her crests and lineage, and images of the commercial fishery that for many years was the lifeblood of the Namgis economy. Hailie’s commentary also reflects the influence of the Anglican Church, the impact of the banning of the potlatch by the Indian Act and how the Kwakwakawak maintained their sense of community in the face of attempts by both governments and churches to suppress their culture.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the top left is a picture of my great grandmother, Ga’axsta’las [Jane (Gilbert) Cook]. The dapper young man in the picture is my grandfather, Chris Cook Sr. He was the youngest of Jane and Stephen’s 16 children and was a commercial fisherman his whole working life. The logo in the upper right corner was created by my cousin William Wasden Jr. The thunderbird represents our family crest and the mountain with the face and a red line on it represents our lineage, with ties to the Nuu-chah-nulth. The church represents Stephen and Jane’s close association with the Anglican church. Jane was a translator for the church and the Indian Agent at the time of the Potlatch trials. It is said that she disapproved of the potlatch initially, influenced by the missionaries, but after she saw the destruction the loss of the potlatch caused the people, she reversed her role in this sad chapter of Namgis and Canadian history and strove to help the Kwakwaka’wakw regain their pride. The boat (the Cape Cook) represents our family’s long history in the commercial fishing industry. All of Jane and Stephen’s sons had boats, mostly named after their sisters. Cook’s are still primarily fishers to this day, but fewer and fewer have pursued this life as the industry has drastically declined. The bottom two picture show what ‘Yalis (Alert Bay) looked like at that time. ‘Yalis was not the traditional home of the ‘Namgis. This was Gwani (the Nimpkish River). However, when two entrpreneurs opened a saltery and a cannery, the ‘Namgis were induced to move to Alert Bay and have lived on the reserve since. The bottom right picture is of Cook’s Store – the store Stephen and Jane owned in Alert Bay.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>There is one set of images in Haile’s photomontage showing the St Michael’s Residential School in Alert Bay that Canadians have only recently acknowledged as a shameful part of the country’s colonial legacy.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/10/Halie_Bruce_11-1024x763.jpg" alt="Archival residential school photos from Halie Bruce's 2006 narrative project titled, &quot;Where we come from is Wealth&quot; " class="wp-image-2322"/></figure>

<p>The title of the montage comes from the statement made in 1920 by Deputy Superintendent of Indian Affairs, Duncan Campbell Scott, that articulated the colonial assumptions underpinning the laws prohibiting the potlatch and the establishment of the residential school system:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><em>I want to get rid of the Indian problem. I do not think as a matter of fact, that the country ought to continuously protect a class of people who are able to stand alone. . . . Our objective is to continue until there is not a single Indian in Canada that has not been absorbed into the body politic and there is no Indian question, and no Indian Department, that is the whole object of this Bill.</em></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The final set of photos chronicles and heralds the continuing journey of Indigenous renewal of Namgis and Kwakwaka’wakw cultural and legal traditions to be recognized and respected as an integral part of a pluralistic Canadian body politic.</p>

<p>After graduating from law school Haile went to practice principally in the area of child welfare addressing the continuing effects of the “Sixties Scoop” “ of which she herself was a victim. Halie has also devoted part of her practice to Indigenous restorative justice initiatives, including writing Gladue Reports for Indigenous people at bail, sentencing and appeal courts in BC. In 2016 she was one of the collaborators in <a href="https://www.cle.bc.ca/testify-a-project-of-the-indigenous-laws-the-arts-collective/" data-type="link" data-id="https://www.cle.bc.ca/testify-a-project-of-the-indigenous-laws-the-arts-collective/">Testify: A Project of the Indigenous Laws + the Arts Collective</a>. Testify is a performance and art show in which eleven pairs of artists and legal thinkers work in conversation with each other to create pieces that explore Indigenous laws. A project in partial response to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC), Testify sought to change the way we think about law and challenges the absence of Indigenous law in the legal landscape.</p>

<p>When I first went to Alert Bay and had my introduction to the Namgis First Nation community in 1972 Hailie was 7 years old. 53 years later she has joined me on the Board of the West Coast Prison Justice Society, a non-profit society of which I am President, one of whose objectives is to research and respond to another of the continuing legacies of colonialism, the mass incarceration of Indigenous people, an issue that is explored in projects in the Prisoners Rights section of the Archive.</p>
