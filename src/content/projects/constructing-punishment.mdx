<p>The Prison historically and in contemporary society occupies a forbidding physical, legal and moral space. Fifty years ago, at the beginning of my legal research on the prison, I entered the BC Penitentiary in New Westminster. Built in the 1878 on a high point overlooking the Fraser River and the city, then the capital of British Columbia, for most of the twentieth century its 40 foot high concrete walls and guard towers were an indelible architectural reminder of the power of the law and the wages of crime.</p>

<p>In my research for Prisoners of Isolation I visited, through the work of historians and sociologists, the prisons that were built in the eighteenth and nineteenth century at the birth and rise of the modern institution of imprisonment. In the 2007 Penal Policy Seminar, J.L (Jody) Barber in her photo essay “Constructing Punishment” explored this history.</p>

<p>As Jody explains “this book uses photographs to trace the history of prison architecture in Western Europe and the United States from the Middle Ages to the Modern Era. It examines how prison architecture is changed over time in accordance with the theories regarding crime, punishment and the purpose of imprisonment.”</p>
