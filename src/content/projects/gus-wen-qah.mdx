<p>Rhaea’s essay provides the historical context for her work:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Canada's nationhood began with the treaties that they signed with First Nations. As these treaties were in English, First Nations offered settlers their understanding of the agreement. The two-row-wampum belt was a tool for conveying the Haudenosaunee or "Iroquois" interpretation of the treaties that were concluded with them. The Iroquois are some of the first peoples to engage in the treaty process with outsiders as well as to contribute to the understanding of treaty terms. The specific contribution made by the Iroquois was though the two-row-wampum belt. The two-row-wampum belt is a depiction of the intended relationship it was designed to convey at the treaties it was presented at. This paper will briefly look at two of the treaties in which a two-row wampum belt was presented, discuss the meaning of the wampum belt and state my experience in re-creating this particular wampum belt.</p>

<p>The belt showed two parallel rows of purple wampum on a background of white wampum. The white wampum symbolized the purity of the agreement. The two purple rows denoted the spirit of the nations' ancestors and the separate, but parallel paths which they would each take in their respective vessels. One vessel, a birch-bark canoe, was for the Iroquois people, their laws, customs and way of life; the other, a ship, was for the British, their laws customs, and way of life. Three beads of wampum-symbolizing peace, friendship, and respect-separated the two row. The three beads were the link between the nations, but just as their paths never cross on the wampum belt.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Rhaea describes and documents in a series of photos how she approached the weaving of the wampum belt:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In learning to weave this wampum belt I experienced various emotions. Just researching what a two-row-wampum belt symbolized made me have such a reverence for my creation. The belt stands for a relationship that was to be built on mutual respect and understanding. It stands for peace and friendship, but most importantly, it stands for the recognition of sovereignty. Each row of beads contributes to this understanding and with each bead I wove I tried to remember this.</p>

<p>The wampum belt was a big project, as I wanted to make it as authentic as possible. By authentic I mean, as close to the way that someone from the Iroquois Nation would have woven it. In creating the loom I chose a branch that had a cure in it so that it looked like a bow. I took two separate pieces of wood and cut fourteen notches into them for the fourteen pieces of hemp that I would place inside in order to create the loom. I chose hemp, as it is the most natural fiber available, as I could not obtain sinew. Once the fourteen pieces of hemp were in place I began to string thirteen beads onto the loom and weave them. This process was very time consuming, but as it came to an end it was worth it for the journey of creating it alone. I have never been able to connect to a project so intimately as this one because it has allowed me to join in the creation of a legal translation of a First Nation's cultural perspective something that does not fit into the typical western notion of education, especially in a legal education. It is with great respect that I completed this project and had an opportunity to experience something different.<sup data-fn="82675432-7d48-4878-88ec-8a98fbea1a97" class="fn"><a href="#82675432-7d48-4878-88ec-8a98fbea1a97" id="82675432-7d48-4878-88ec-8a98fbea1a97-link">1</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
