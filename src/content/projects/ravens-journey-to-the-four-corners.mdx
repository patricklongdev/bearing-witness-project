<p>Maya Stano, for her 2010 project in Aboriginal and Treaty Rights, adapted the Raven Travelling sequence of Indigenous storytelling to reflect on the importance of language and place names in understanding the different relationships that Indigenous peoples and settler populations have towards land, resources and development:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This is a story about Raven, who one day decided to travel across British Columbia and visit the four corners of the province. On his trip, Raven sought to hear the languages of his early sisters and brothers and compare it with that of the white man. Raven wondered if these languages, with their unique words and expressions, would have any impact on the way his brothers and sisters, both new and old, used the land and its non-renewable resources.</p>

<p>Starting near the outskirts of the busy Lower Mainland, Raven's travels brought him eastwards to the vast coal mines of the Kootenays, then northwards to the lands under Treaty 8, after which he flew west to visit the Tahltan Traditional Territories, and finally the islands of Haida Gwaii before turning south and back to his starting point . Raven's story begins near his home on the coastal mountains overlooking Vancouver.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In the course of her imaginary journey around the province Maya introduces us not only to Raven but Owl, Wolf, Eagle, Beaver, Elk, Hawk, Grizzly, Crow and Black Bear, as they discuss the history of colonialism, the Docrine of Discovery, the symbolism on the British Columbia flag, viewed through the lens of Indigenous peoples, the meaning of sustainable development the arbitrary lines drawn on the maps of Canada that fail to recognize Indigenous space.</p>

<p>One calm spring evening, Raven sat on a tree branch and listened to white man speaking in his strange voice. He remembered how Owl liked to sit and listen to the song of voices, and decided to go speak with him about these sounds. Owl began explaining how Aboriginal people once spoke many different languages, many of which had been lost since the arrival of white man.</p>

<p>"Our elder brothers and sisters believe that retaining languages is vital for the survival of their nations" explained Owl. "It is through languages that cultures areaccumulated and shared amongst generations. Many people believe that where a language is allowed to die, the people will most likely die as well . Do you think you would survive if you lost your song?"</p>

<p>After pausing briefly, Owl continued "You know Raven, I think languages even express the uniqueness of a particular society's world view" .</p>

<p>…Raven was pleased he had spoken with Owl. All this talk of languages made him wonder how it really made people different. Could it affect how people interacted with the land? With animals? With each other? This was an exciting thought that Raven had never considered. He had to fly into the wind and cry out his excitementbefore deciding what to do next!</p>

<p>Like so many times before, the flight inspired him, and gave him an idea.</p>

<p>"I should fly around this land I hear the white man call British Columbia, and see what is happening - see how the land is being used! Maybe if I pay attention to how the white man speaks about the land, I will also understand his relationship with the land and how he lives on it. I can also listen to the language that our original brothers and sisters speak and see what differences there are in the way they approach the land."</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Place Names</h3>

<p>Raven had been flying for some time, and decided to rest a while by some rapids. As he sat and watched the great white foam forming from the rushing rapids, he thought back to a story he had once heard about a northern town called Whitehorse. He had heard that name came from the great white rapids that early explorers had to pass through on their way to the Klondike Gold Rush. The white man had named the city Whitehorse because of the rapids that looked like the mane of a great white horse! That thought made him laugh, but then Raven started thinking how before the white man arrived, his original sisters and brothers already had names for special places. What had happened to these traditional names? Why did the white man believe he could simply arrive to new lands and rename everything?</p>

<p>Kicking around some old shells on a sandy bank near the rapids, Raven discovered a tasty treat - "Look what I've discovered!" he called out and then exclaimed! "Discovery - That's it!"</p>

<p>Raven had suddenly remembered an old story he had heard many years back that explained the white man's strange behaviour. At the time, Raven had thought this Doctrine of Discovery was simply made up; a tale his grandfather told him to amuse him. Now he began to understand the truth in the story and how the white man had essentially created a legal fiction to legalize his claims of ownership over these lands that did not belong to him. Raven laughed - what a trickster the white man could be! Able to turn things around to his advantage - Raven was impressed by the white man's skillful trickery.</p>

<p>Returning to the thought of naming places, Raven considered the name that the white man had given this land where he and his ancestors had lived for so long. British Columbia.</p>

<p>From what Raven could understand, this land was not British territory, and the name Columbia, which originated from Christopher Columbus, the first white man to arrive and bring settlers to America, was by far not the first man to walk onthis land. Why should these names be used to describe this land?</p>

<p>As Raven sat pondering these questions, Eagle flew by and perched on a nearby branch.</p>

<p>"Raven, you seem lost, can I help?"</p>

<p>"Eagle, I am only lost in my own thoughts … perhaps you have some wisdom you can share to help me understand some things that are on my mind?" Eagle listened closely as Raven explained, and, after considering the matter carefully he replied.</p>

<p>"Raven, I have seen where our original brothers and sisters live, and their way of life and social structures are much different now from what they were before the white man arrived. After the white man arrived, he imposed his way of life and his laws on our original brothers and sisters. I hear the people call this attitude colonialism, and it seems to me that the name 'British Columbia' symbolizes that history of colonialism."</p>

<p>"Eagle, can't man just go back to using traditional names?"</p>

<p>"Well, it's not that easy Raven. You see, this land was once occupied by a number of different tribes, each with their own languages and territories. But maybe, just maybe, some other name could be adopted … something that represents the collective history of all the peoples that live on this land."</p>

<p>"I know - how about Raven's land!" joked Raven! "It has a nice ring to it!"</p>

<p>"Yes Raven, very nice indeed!" laughed Eagle. "You know, all this talk about British Columbia makes me think of the province's flag. Our brothers and sisters have recognized this flag as being similar in some ways to the traditional wampum belt. Elders have described how the four seasons are represented by the diagonal barson the flag. The half sun describes how the relationship between our brothers andsisters and the white man will continue for as long as the sun shines, goes around the earth, is followed by the moon and the cycle is repeated. The blue wavy lines speak of the two peoples agreeing to exist in mutual respect, not interfering with each other, for as long as the rivers fl ow. Finally, the Crown placed over top of the entire scene is intended to depict the responsibility of the Crown to protect and preserve the agreement made: namely the Honour of the Crown."</p>

<p>"Well Eagle, thanks for explain that to me - it leaves me much to ponder on. You have spoken some very wise words."</p>

<p>And on that note, Eagle swept down and grabbed a large salmon from the river before flying up the valley to enjoy his dinner.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Eastern Coal Mines</h3>

<p>Raven had heard that the Ktunaza language is unique and does not share roots with any other. In the Ktunaxa-Kinbasket Traditional Territories, Raven saw huge holes in the ground, with big trucks loaded with large piles of black rock (coal). He came across Elk, bellowing in an open field near the coal mines, and approached thegreat animal.</p>

<p>"Hello Raven, my good friend. You are as black as the coal the white man cherishes so!"</p>

<p>"Hello Elk - I'm happy to hear you speak of coal - I've come to see how language effects the way our brothers and sisters care for the land."</p>

<p>"Well Raven, out here the white man lives in a town he calls Fernie. And that town gets its name from the first white man to find coal here. The other main town near the coal fields is called Sparwood, and that name comes from the use of the trees near the original settlement, which the white man found were suitable to use as spars for ships."</p>

<p>Moving to the side of the field so he could overlook the big active coal mines, Elk continued. "You know Raven, I guess these place names show how the white man looks at the land and seems to only see its value in how it can benefit him.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Grease of Great Animals</h3>

<p>Arriving to the north-eastern corner of BC, Raven came across big tepees erected by his brothers and sisters. He heard the cry of a great hawk, and flew over to its perch on a nearby tree.</p>

<p>"Well hello Raven!"</p>

<p>"Hello Hawk, I have come to see how our brothers and sisters use the land in the north."</p>

<p>"Well Raven, these lands are covered under Treaty 8, a historic treaty that our original brothers and sisters signed with the white man many seasons ago to protect their hunting grounds from the influx of gold miner s. Today many of our brothers and sisters still live in communities on these lands."</p>

<p>"And has the white man upheld his promise to our brothers and sisters?" asked Raven.</p>

<p>"Not really Raven. In the 1920s and 1930s, the white man imposed trap-line regulations. Then in the 1940s he came and built the Alaska Highway which allowedhim to increase agricultural and forestry activities. But the biggest impact that white man has had on the land is through his thirst for oil and coal."</p>

<p>After pausing briefly, Hawk continued.</p>

<p>"Have you heard the story of the Saya? Oral histories of our Dana-zaa brothers and sisters speak of Soya, the Transformer, who watched and learnt how to hunt the big animals. He then taught our brothers and sisters how to hunt so that they would not become prey, and that's how they became the powerful hunters they are today. The story also speaks of how Soya took the great animals he had hunted, and buried then deep in the earth. According to the Elders, it is the grease from the bodies of these animals that the white man now comes and opens the earth up to take out.”</p>

<p>"Well Hawk, it seems to me that if the white man listened to this story he may see the connection of the oil and coal to life and value life within land more.”</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Agreements for Development</h3>

<p>Raven decided to fly over to the Tahltan Traditional Territories, where he had heard that great discoveries of coal and metals had been stirring up the dust.</p>

<p>Raven wanted to take a look and see what the fuss was about.</p>

<p>Approaching Tahltan lands, Raven came across Grizzly who was enjoying the fine weather.</p>

<p>"Hello Grizzly, I have come to look at the mines I've heard so much about in the Tahltan Territory."</p>

<p>"Ah yes, Raven, there is a lot of activity here! And both our original and our new brothers and sisters are working hard together . Our old siblings control some of the project, and many seem to be relatively content with what's happening on the land ."</p>

<p>After a lengthy stretch, Grizzly continued. "Before all these projects began, Elders were very concerned about the impact that many projects occurring at once would have on the Sacred Headwaters. However, talks with the white man have led to the signing of agreements, and these give our original sisters and brothers more say into the development of the projects."</p>

<p>"Is it in some way like the traditional wampum belt, which speaks of two peoples travelling the river of life together within their own cultures and yet dependent on one another?"</p>

<p>"Well Raven, there are some similarities, but although the Tahltans represent a peoples, the mine developers are not a distinct people, but rather a collective of private interests. Time will only tell how their travel down the river will be together. Speaking of rivers, Raven do you know what the name Tahltan means?"</p>

<p>Raven shook his head to indicate no.</p>

<p>"Well, the traditional story tells of two sisters meeting one another on opposite banks of the River. As they watched the water, one asked the other what she saw. She replied 'something heavy is going up the little water' referring to the ascending salmon making their way up through the rapids . And this is what Tahltan means: 'something heavy in the water '."</p>

<p>"That fits with what I've seen so far Grizzly" replied Raven. "Whenever I look to the meaning of the names that our original brothers and sisters give the land, there is reference to the life within it."</p>

<p>"You know Raven, with respect to rivers, you may find this interesting. A while backI met up with my brother who had travelled across the territory of the Nisga'a people. He told me how the Nisga'a had different names for the Nass River, depending on the season. In the spring, when the river was heavy with sediment from the winter thaw, the Nisga'a called it Ayns lisism, which means the river of milk. This shows how our brothers and sisters really saw the river as a living being, changing over the seasons."</p>

<p>Raven thanked Grizzly for the chat, and after flying over the Galore Creek project he followed the River west until he reached the coast.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Old forests</h3>

<p>Having reached the ocean, Raven decided to travel south and visit the islands of Haida Gwaii where he had heard of great forests filled with very old trees. Trees so old, that one could also consider them a non-renewable resource.</p>

<p>Black Bear was walking through the forest, and Raven flew up to join him. </p>

<p>"Welcome Raven! I am happy to see you on Haida Gwaii, or as the white man calls 'Queen Charlotte Island"'.</p>

<p>Hello Black Bear.. I have been thinking of names in different languages, and the effects on land use and so-called ‘development’”.</p>

<p>Well Raven, the name Haida Gwaii means “Islands of the People” and that is quite different from how the white man describes these islands, namely as land that belongs to a Queen from a far-away count ry. And I think this has had an effect on the way the land is used."</p>

<p>"How do you mean Black Bear" asked Raven.</p>

<p>"Well, the white man has come here for many years and cut down these old trees to use elsewhere. He does not understand that once they are gone, they will not come back for many generations. But our Haida brothers and sisters feel a strong connection to these ancient cedars, and have fought the white man long and hard to protect them. Today, more and more of our white brothers and sisters are referring to these islands by their historic name, and I think is also helping to change their attitude towards the land and how they use it ."</p>

<p>"Raven, do you know what the Haida call their white brothers" Raven signalled that he did not. "He calls them Yaats Haade, or the 'metal people'."</p>

<p>Raven laughed "I guess that makes sense. They do seem to go a little crazy over metal!"</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Returning Home</h3>

<p>On his way home, Raven followed the large new asphalt river that the white man had built between the cities he called Vancouver and Squamish. Raven noticed something was different; new signs were posted by the river crossings and these were not only in the white man's language, but also had the names that his original brothers and sisters had given these waters.</p>

<p>"Isn't that wonderful!" Raven exclaimed. "Maybe white man will begin to understandour brothers' and sisters' traditional languages. And maybe that will have an impact on the white man's relationship with the land!"</p>

<p>Through his travels to the four corners, Raven had seen how language clearly reflected the way his brothers and sisters used and took care of the land. Language really seemed to show the relationship man held with the land. It seemed that the languages of his original brothers and sisters generally described the life within the land. But the white man had a very different approach. He would name theland based on his own achievements or a specific use of the land that benefited him.</p>

<p>As Raven approached his favourite perch on the mountains overlooking the big city of Vancouver, he wondered aloud "Perhaps one day the white man will start seeing the life in the land, and begin to understand how important it is for his own survival that this life be protected. I sure hope he realizes it soon!"</p>

<p>And so ends this tale of Raven and his journey across British Columbia.</p>
