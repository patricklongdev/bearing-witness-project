<p>Krysta Cochrane’s Album, “Nisga’a Self-Government: an Ongoing Journey” through a textual narrative, family photographs and her own art work (painted while living in New Aiyash when she was 8) describes the Nisga’a journey towards self-governance in health, child welfare and criminal justice, and her family’s experiences as part of that journey.</p>

<p>This is my family's story of the struggles that the Nisga'a people faced while obtaining greater self-government over their community health care, criminal justice, and child welfare systems before the Nisga'a Final Agreement in 2000. Although there were costs and struggles associated with assuming responsibility over the delivery of health care and criminal justice in the community, these experiences paved the way for future self-government initiatives in other areas, such as education, social services, economic development and resource management. Through the agency and self- determination that arose from gaining more control over their health care and criminal justice systems, the Nisga'a people were better able to address problems of abuse in their community, ultimately improving the health, well-being, safety, and security of children and families.</p>

<p>In 1988, my mother became the first child and family psychologist for the Nisga'a Valley Health Board in the Nass Valley. Being a Cree/Ojibway woman, she had worked with several other B.C. First Nations in the Queen Charlotte Islands, Dease Lake, Telegraph Creek, lskut, and Bella Coola. She was hired after the formation of the Nisga'a Valley Health Board to address the child and family psychological needs that were prevalent in the community.</p>

<p>My mother's role was provision of psychological services to children and families on reserve, in liaison and consultant with the Band Council, the Nisga'a Tribal Council, external Social Workers, Victim Assistance workers, RCMP, Crown Counsel, community health representatives (CHR's), Provincial Service Agencies, and the Health Canada. She reported her activities to the Nisga'a Valley Health Board once a month. My mother was allocated a house in the village of New Aiyansh where she lived with my brother and I, but she also provided psychological services to other Nisga'a villages in Greenville and Canyon City.</p>

<p>My brother and I were forever impacted by our experience living in the Nass Valley. Once I saw that a community could change for the better through self- government, I studied the impact of community capacity on youth suicide and Aboriginal access to health care as an undergraduate research assistant. Later, I engaged in graduate research on Aboriginal school engagement. Meanwhile, my brother worked as a research assistant studying Aboriginal health. Finally, my brother and I both went to law school to study environmental and Aboriginal law, respectively. Living in the Nass Valley forever changed the way that my brother and I viewed the world, and relationships between the people in it.</p>
