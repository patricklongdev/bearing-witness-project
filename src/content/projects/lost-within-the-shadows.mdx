<p>Colleen Spier, for the 2005 Penal Policy seminar combined poetry with photography in her reflections on populist mythology that became part of a political platform during the Conservative Harper government’s tough on crime years, that equates minimum-security prisons in Canada with Club Fed. In my book <em>Justice behind the Walls: Human Rights in Canadian Prisons</em> I have described the physical setting at one of these so called ‘Club Feds’, William Head Institution on the west coast of Vancouver Island, in this way</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>It had originally been a quarantine camp for new immigrants to Canada, and until 1992, the housing for prisoners consisted of dormitory-style huts with prisoners eating their meals in a common dining room. Since then, prisoners have lived in small duplexes where they make their own meals, do their own laundry and take on the everyday living responsibilities which traditional prisons strip away. It is not the duplexes, however, that have given William Head its "Club Fed" designation. That is largely attributable to the site on which the institution is located: a rocky promontory with magnificent vistas of water, wooded coves, and coastal mountains. Once through the main gate and a security fence topped with razor wire, visitors could easily be led to believe they had arrived at an oceanfront retreat with modest but comfortable housing. Within the eighty acres, there are lofty Douglas fir, western red cedar, and Garry oak trees. A meandering road follows the contours of the coast. The duplexes, which each house six to eight prisoners, are organized in cluster formation, with each cluster sharing a common community building with laundry room and offices for staff and programs.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Despite the beautiful physical surroundings, in <em>Justice behind the Walls</em> I had described the experiences of Gary Weaver and the ways in which some of the worst systemic injustices of the Canadian prison system persisted within its fences.<sup data-fn="bb8b437d-e3b0-45eb-abd7-5202d83b7723" class="fn"><a href="#bb8b437d-e3b0-45eb-abd7-5202d83b7723" id="bb8b437d-e3b0-45eb-abd7-5202d83b7723-link">1</a></sup> Colleen’s poem is inscribed on a backdrop of a photograph of a Garry Oak and explores the illusion of ‘Club Fed’ and the realities of imprisonment. The poem is best read in its original format in the Archive but you will have to zoom in as you go through the verses. In this commentary I have reproduced here the original together with close- up photographs of the verses.</p>
