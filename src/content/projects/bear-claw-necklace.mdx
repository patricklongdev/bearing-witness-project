<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This is a unique and personal piece. I have created a bear claw necklace as my multidisciplinary project for LAW 353D. The word 'bear' in Mi'kmaq is "Mui'n".</p>

<p>The word 'claw' in Mi'kmaq is ugqosi'l. I have named this piece "Mui'n ugqosi'l Necklace". I was inspired to make a mui'n ugqosi'l necklace for a number of reasons. I wanted to do something hands-on and creative that was significant to my culture as a Mi'kmaq person. I took this as an opportunity to learn more about my culture, express my creative side, and learn from others. I also plan on wearing this necklace to the UBC First Nations House of Learning Graduation Ceremony as part of my regalia. I have attached photos to this written piece to demonstrate the creation process of the necklace. This was the first mui'n ugqosi'l necklace and piece of jewellery that I have ever created.</p>

<p>In this written piece, I will use the original name of my people, L'nu, unless directly quoting another source. The Micmac Grammar of Father Pacifique Memoir 7, translated by John Hewson and Bernard Francis, states that L'nu means "The People of the Same Tongue" referring to those who share the same cognitive solidarity and have experienced the same forces of ecology. L'nu are also known as the Micmac or Mi'kmaq, which are derived from the word Ni'kmaq, meaning "My Kjn-Friends".I am Mi'kmaq. I am L'nu.</p>

<p>The L'nu speak an Algonquian dialect and are a member of the Wabanaki Confederacy.Mi'kmak'ik, the ancestral territory of the L'nu, is located on the eastern coast of Turtle Island and has been home to the L'nu since time immemorial. In "Resource Management and the Mi'kmaq Nation" (1997), Suzanne Berneshawi states that the L'nu have developed an intimate and ancient relationship with the land and sea. Dr. L. Jane McMillan emphasized this point and made an important acknowledgement in her article "Colonial Traditions, Co-optations, and Mi'kmaq Legal Consciousness" ("Colonial Traditions") (2011). Dr. McMillan states that while the L'nu's relationship with Mi'kmak'ik is ancient and continues today, European efforts to colonize and displace has impacted this relationship for the last four-hundred years. And that although the L'nu have been resilient in maintaining their traditions, ceremonies, and culture, not all aspects of the culture has survived settler state colonialism.</p>

<p>Knowing that the mui 'n ugqosi'l necklace would have been something the Indian agents would have historically tried preventing me from doing motivated me to complete this project. As Dr. McMillan acknowledged, the settler colonial state has tried to displace my people since first contact. Displacement from the territory was not their only objective. They sought displacement from our culture and language as well. This is what they viewed as necessary for us to assimilate into settler colonial culture. I created this mui'n ugqosi'l necklace not only as an ode to my ancestors, but also as a symbol ofresilience and L'nu renaissance in the 21'' century.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Harvest</h3>

<p>I harvested a black mui'n in May 2022 for food. After gutting and skinning the mui'n, I harvested as much meat as possible, and the paws which I would declaw later on.</p>

<p>Harvesting the mui'n relates to Professor Jackson's teachings on Aboriginal rights. For instance, the White and Bob case confirmed the existence of the treaty right to hunt for food for the Saalequun tribe, and laid down the legal framework for building legal theory for the recognition of Aboriginal rights. As was the case in White and Bob, I was hunting for the purpose of food. I could make the argument that I harvested the mui'n for ceremonial purposes because the necklace is regalia and culturally significant to ceremony. Alternatively, I could argue that while I did not hunt the mui'n on my community's treaty territory, harvesting the mui'n symbolizes my attempts to continue and uphold L'nu traditional teachings and practices.</p>

<p>Ultimately, this harvest represents my commitment to exercising my Aboriginal right to hunt and continue building a deep and ancestral relationship with the land.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Processing the Claws</h3>

<p>The process of removing the claws was long and tedious. After declawing all four paws, I had to salt the claws to harden and remove the cartilage surrounding the knuckle. I underestimated how hard this part of the process was going to be. After some further research, I learned that I would have to separate the claws from the actual bones that were inside the claw (you can see them separated in the photo archive below). In order to separate the two parts I would have to place the claws in simmering water with dish soap. Then I would have to scrape away the excess flesh and then try and pry them apart. This was very hard. I worked on separating the claws from the bone for over 6 hours. They were so stubborn and tough.</p>

<p>Once I had the claws and bones separated I had to let them dry and harden again. After they hardened, I purchased and used a Dremel tool to grind off any remaining hardened flesh from the bone to prevent odor and rot. I used the stone Dremel attachment to do this. I then purchased an accessory for the Dremel called an abrasive buffing head that would be used to help buffer the nails and remove any excess skin membrane on the nails.</p>

<p>Once buffed, I then had to whiten the bones and then glue the bones back into the nails. I then used the Dremel tool to grind off any excess glue. I then drilled a hole through the bone part of the claw for the necklace string to go through. Finally before the string would go through, I would apply three coats of a gloss lacquer to really bring out the beautiful color and grain of the nails.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Significance of Mui'n (Bear)</h3>

<p>Mui'n is very significant to who we are as L'nu people. There are many stories about mui'n. Generally, mui'n is portrayed as a strong and honourable supernatural being with special powers.</p>

<p>For example, Mui 'n and the Seven Bird Hunters is a traditional L'nu sky story which has been orally passed down from generation to generation.6 The story links the cycle of seasons with the movement of stars across Mi'kmak'ik.7 The seven bird hunters hunt mui'n across the seasons.<br />They eventually kill and eat mui'n's physical body in the Fall season. After this, it is Winter, and mui'n's life spirit transforms into a star. It is custom, for the L'nu to hold a mid-Winter feast after the new moon in January to commemorate this spiritual transformation. At this time, the skeleton ofMui'n sits high in the sky over Mi'kmak'ik in a form commonly known as Ursa major.</p>

<p>Mui'n's life spirit then finds their way back to the den and enters as a new physical body hiding all winter long. Mui'n sleeps on their back and remains invisible. When Spring arrives, mui'n awakes and comes out of their den. Again, mui'n will be hunted, killed, and turned into a star by the seven bird hunters. The L'nu are taught to look to the skies to Ursa Major to find mui'n. In each season, mui'n may be found in the sky at different positions. Consequently, the L'nu believe mui'n is the keeper oftime ('Mui'n and the Seven Bird Hunters, narrated in English and Mi'kmaq by L'nu Elder Lillian Marshall of Potlotek First Nation and L'nu Elder Murdena Marshall of Eskasoni First Nation).</p>

<p>This oral history shows the significance of patterns in sky and how they reflect patterns on earth. The story demonstrates how L'nu people understood meaning from the stars and how they interpreted the stars' movement through the story of Mui 'n and the Seven Bird Hunters. As this story illustrates, mui'n is a very significant supernatural being to our culture as L'nu people.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Looking Forward</h3>

<p>I would like to suggest that my mui 'n ugqosi'l necklace is a sign of respect to the supernatural being, mui'n. In creating this necklace, I would like to emphasize mui'n's role in my ancestors' lives, our oral histories, and the legacies of future L'nu generations to come.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Mui'n has had a significant role in my life. When I visited my own First Nation for the first time, my aunty hosted a sweat lodge ceremony to welcome me home. In the sweat lodge, a relative passed over ajar ofmui'n grease and instructed me to apply it to my skin and hair. I was taught of the medicine's healing properties. Mui'n was also the first large animal that I hunted. I have used the mui'n's meat to feed many family and friends. The teachings I have received through mui’n have been both direct and indirect and have made me a better hunter, provider, and relation.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Just as mui'n returns to its den and re-emerges every year, I plan to wear the mui'n ugqosi'l necklace to graduation to symbolically represent how Indigenous students are capable of graduating law school despite all the colonial obstacles facing us.</p>

<p>I would like to think that the necklace is a symbol of resiliency. Despite everything the settler government has done to oppress us, with hard work we are able to emerge from our metaphorical dens to finish our education. To me, wearing the mui'n ugqosi'l necklace at the graduation ceremony, is a reminder that graduating law school will remain a possibility for future Indigenous students for generations to come. It is wearing an ancient L'nu form, a way of looking through an ancestral landscape, in a contemporary setting - a graduation ceremony.</p>

</blockquote>
