<p>Karon Blandino, in her project for the 2012 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Seminar, inspired by Krista Johanson’s annotation of the Treaty of Lancaster, completed an annotation of Treaty 8, both to further her own education and also as an educational guide for others:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Before taking Law 353 - Aboriginal Treaty Rights, my knowledge of First Nation treaties was very limited and, in hindsight, also, inaccurate. I thought that treaties were just historical documents through which the Crown unilaterally usurped aboriginal lands. The image of First Nations as active participants in the treaty process never crossed my mind . However, in the last few months, I have come to understand that Treaties are ongoing relationships between First Nations and the Crown. Although the official text of most treaties is not reflective of the actual promises and obligations that the parties agreed to orally, this does not mean that the Treaties are worthless.</p>

<p>Despite receiving my entire education in Canada, I did not really learn much about First Nations (other than their role in the Fur Trade) until my first year of law school. Many other students never will learn more about First Nations - this is unacceptable. As a result (and inspired by Krista Johanson's project) I chose to do an annotation of Treaty 8. The aim is to provide background information to aid in the reading and interpretation of the Treaty. Ideally, it could be used as part of a lesson plan to teach younger people about the treaty making process and the significance of treaties today.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Karon’s annotation of the 1899 Treaty contains the text of the document, together with its historical context, analysis of the divergence between the legal text and the indigenous understanding of the treaty negotiations, and the issues arising from implementation of the treaty promises. What follows are highlights of Karon’s narrative and some of my own commentary.</p>

<p>Karon’s annotation addresses the major issue of whether, notwithstanding the explicit language of Treaty 8 that</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“the Cree, Beaver, Chipewyan and other Indians, inhabiting the district… DO HEREBY CEDE,RELEASE, SURRENDER AND YIELD UP to the Government of the Dominion of Canada, for Her Majesty the Queen and Her successors for ever, all their rights, titles and privileges whatsoever, to the lands”,</p>
</blockquote>

<p>the Indigenous understanding of the treaty negotiations, and particularly the assurances by the treaty commissioners that there would be no interference with their hunting, fishing and trapping, were an affirmation not renunciation of their Aboriginal rights. This same issue of the dissonance between the text of the treaty and the oral histories of the negotiations also arose in relation to Treaty 11 signed in 1921, shortly after the discovery of oil at Norman Wells.</p>

<p>The issue of whether Treaties 8 and 11 extinguish Aboriginal title has been the subject of litigation. In 1973 when eleven Dene Chiefs commenced legal proceedings claiming Aboriginal title to 450,000 miles of the Northwest Territories and that based on the oral history of the negotiations and the promises made by the treaty commissioners, Treaties 8 and 11, contrary to their written text, did not extinguish that title. Justice Morrow in determining whether there was a prima facie case, traveled to Dene communities and heard evidence from elders on the oral history, including those who had been present at the signing of Treaty 11. Relying upon the judgments of the Supreme Court of Canada in <em>Calder</em>, which had been handed down just months before the Dene Chiefs began their action, Justice Morrow in <em>Re Paulette </em>ruled that there was a sufficient case in support of the Dene claims to justify the issuance of a caveat in the land title registry, such that future purchasers of unpatented land would have legal notice of the Dene claims.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Throughout the hearings before me there was a common thread in the testimony -- that the Indians were repeatedly assured they were not to be deprived of their hunting, fishing and trapping rights. To me, hearing the witnesses at first hand as I did, many of whom were there at the signing, some of them having been directly involved in the treaty-making, it is almost unbelievable that the Government party could have ever returned from their efforts with any impression but that they had given an assurance in perpetuity to the Indians in the Territories that their traditional use of the lands was not affected.<sup data-fn="a9a39b59-a8a0-4f3d-bf2c-61f1801ff709" class="fn"><a href="#a9a39b59-a8a0-4f3d-bf2c-61f1801ff709" id="a9a39b59-a8a0-4f3d-bf2c-61f1801ff709-link">1</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Although in Treaty 8, following the precedent in the earlier numbered treaties, there was a requirement that reserves be established on a formula of one square mile per family of five, based on an anticipated farming economy that was unsuitable for the North, even these limited reserves came under pressure from government officials to be surrendered and issues of treaty land entitlement remain unresolved.</p>
