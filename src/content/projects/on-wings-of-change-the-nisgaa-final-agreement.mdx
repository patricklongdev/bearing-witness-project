Lying at the other end of the poetic spectrum, as well as the historical arc of treaty-making, is the epic poem of Tlell Raffard, “On Wings of Change”. Tlell’s poem composed for the 2011 Aboriginal and Treaty tights seminar addresses the first of the modern BC treaties, the 2000 Nisga’a Final Agreement. As Tlell explains this poem is constructed along the lines of Samuel Taylor Coleridge’s The Rime of the Ancient Mariner.

> **PROLOGUE**
>
> Present-day issues surrounding Aboriginal title and rights invariably include histories pregnant with broken promises, social and cultural degradation, and the misapplication of sovereign authority. The story below unfolds in a framework intended to roughly approximate that of the Rime of the Ancient Mariner, by Samuel Taylor Coleridge, and attempts to capture the essence of events leading up to the Nisga’a Final Agreement in 2000. My intention is not to perpetuate cultural hegemony, a method too often implemented in colonial relationships, but rather to reverse this practice – with all due respect to Mr. Coleridge’s epic and enduring poem – by taking a classically English form of art, and rendering it an instrument for acknowledgment and even celebration of an Aboriginal story. I undertake to do this by narrating through the eyes and voice of a fictional treaty commissioner, sent to Nisga’a territory by the British Columbian government in a bid to facilitate treaty negotiations between the Nisga’a, Canada and BC. Whilst there, he is introduced to a culture far more complex than he had previously appreciated, and is left to contemplate his purpose there in the quiet woods along the Nass River. Feeling abandoned, he prepares to march indignantly back to the village when he is waylaid by the Nisga’a spirit bird, a sacred animal in Nisga’a lore, which guides him on an historical journey through the past, revealing a record of betrayal and loss. While he had been instructed by the province to negotiate terms favourable to its own interests, he cannot help but see the people at the negotiation table through new eyes, and so endeavour to assist an equitable agreement in which the Nisga’a desire for acknowledgment and entitlement to self-determination are fulfilled with dignity and fairness.
>
> While not a perfect treaty – if such a thing exists – the Nisga’a Final Agreement arose out of resilience, enduring hope, monumental efforts to rebuild trust, and a willingness to compromise amongst all parties. I write this poem as a celebration of this achievement, and apologize for omitting countless other noteworthy individuals, events, and efforts, in the interest of limiting length. Indeed, a project such as this one could go on and on, and so I have endeavoured to keep it close to the Nisga’a experience, so much as I might presume to apprehend it.

In “Wings of Change”, in rhyming couplets, you will be taken back in history with references to the Royal Proclamation of 1763, the 1823 decision of the Supreme Court of the United States in *Johnson v McIntosh*, which introduced the Doctrine of Discovery, the enactment of the Indian Act, the 1927 amendment to the Indian Act that prohibited the raising money for the purpose of pursuing any Indian land claim, the assimilationist policies of the federal government, the Nisga’a petitions seeking recognition of their rights to their traditional territory, the 1973 *Calder* case which ushered in the modern era of recognition in Canadian law of Indigenous rights, and the culmination of negotiations between federal and provincial governments in the Nisga’a Final Agreement. The sweep of history is remarkable. In submitting her project Tlell offered this suggestion for visitors to the Archive:

> The thing to bear in mind with a project such as this one, is that poetry is meant to be heard, not merely read, so if you get the opportunity with the final product to read it aloud, or perhaps to have it read to you, the effect would be rather different and, I tend to believe, somewhat richer.

Here are just some of the stanzas:

> **PART II: HEAR YE, HEAR YE**
>
> The Royal we of sixty-three\Attempting to inspire\Did little more than nudge the door\To feed the colonial fire.
>
> In they poured a motley hoard,\Both French and Anglophone,\Declaring theirs all lands and wares\Belonging to the Throne.
>
> The naughty boys of Illinois,\Whose greed would not relent\Did covet lands for veterans\And thus wrought precedent.
>
> **PART III: CHANGING HANDS**
>
> Westminster came and changed the game,\Thus Canada was sworn;\A proper state it could negate\The rights of its firstborn.
>
> And so it did and promptly rid\Its promise to the Indian.\Assimilate and subjugate:\Doctrine of the Christian.
>
> Ensuing Acts did thus detract\Hunting rights and title,\Reserving lands for Indian Bands\But these a meager trifle.
>
> Frank Calder brought an action wrought\To see their claim distinguished.\The lower courts, without remorse,\Found title was extinguished.
>
> The S.C.C., in seventy-three\Considered foreign judgments,\Including back to Royal pacts\And Proclamatory parchments.
>
> Thus the Court found just support,\O’erruled those harsh decisions.\Instead it found the claim was sound\Thus mending past divisions.
>
> **PART IV: WINDS OF CHANGE**
>
> The Gitxsan claim had garnered fame,\The province bade to listen.\It would cater two years later\And launch a new Commission.
>
> They’d started late out of the gate,\Were far behind the race.\Few agreements flowed from treatments\Based on past disgrace.
>
> Not meant to press for past redress,\A rule not meant for brooking,\The B.C.T.C. held succinctly:\‘Twas strictly forward-looking.
>
> The role it plays is to liaise,\According to its mandate.\So perforce it launched its course,\No treaty would it backdate.
>
> Five per cent was all it meant\To grant as territory,\And in exchange it would estrange\Bands’ prior tax-free glory.
>
> **PART V: BALANCING ACT**
>
> The Nisga’a Chief began in brief\By setting out their goals:\An equal chance to help advance\Their economic growth.
>
> He stated clearly they held dearly\Self-determination;\To realize their family ties,\Develop as a nation;\A special clause for making laws\In line with Nisga’a values;\Language rights and burial sites\Were all within their purview.
>
> INAC grappled with the passel\Mainly over lands:\Minerals and fossil fuels\Belong in the Crown’s hands.
>
> The Council balked and almost walked –\A nearly tragic move –\But then I heard the spirit bird,\Its tone a soft reprove.
>
> “Now is the time,” it kindly chimed,\“To step into your role.\Douse the fire of rising ire\And aide the parties’ goal.”
>
> At once I knew its words rang true,\We must find ground to draft it;\Though long inured to harm endured\A text was finally crafted.
>
> Nisga’a Lands in Nisga’a hands –\The rest they had to cede –\Appendices would render these\Inalienable deed.
>
> Their laws would stand across their land,\Along with B.C. statutes;\This new franchise would harmonize,\But for the rare dispute –
>
> To the extent the conflict went\The province would prevail.\Or alternate to this upset\Was Parliament’s detail.
>
> I saw the Chiefs in their beliefs\Rebuild a shattered trust,\And in that light dispel the fight,\Pursuing what was just.
>
> My wonder grew as Nisga’a threw\Their hearts into the fray;\No pity begged for deals reneged,\Instead they offered leeway.
>
> Through it all I heard the call\To see this mission through;\The spirit bird, in quiet words\Had told me what to do.
>
> The final terms contained the germ\Of freedom to self-govern,\And in the passage of the package\Laid a novel pattern.
>
> **PART VI: FLY AWAY HOME**
>
> The agents sought to stir the pot\And compromise demands;\The Nisga’a urged that trust emerge\Between the parties’ hands.
>
> In their dictum, Nisga’a Lisims\Revealed their stance with tact.\In due course all did endorse\The signing of the pact.
>
> Talks wrapped up and all did sup\Within the cedar hall;\We celebrated new conflated\Interests one and all.
>
> Conclusions found on common ground,\A potlatch did ensue;\To celebrate this landmark date\Of forming bonds anew.
>
> In the end the Band would send\The government away,\With gratitude for hope accrued\Through mutual essay.\Was it the best that they could get? Perhaps it was that day.\Not every clause had met applause\But others held cachet.
>
> Future Bands could set their hands\To drafting like instruments –\Negotiate to generate\Their own in-house governments.
>
> In the end it would depend\Upon the Nisga’a story:\Would they thrive and thus revive\Their Nation’s prior glory?
