<p>Although far removed from BC in geography the historical record on rights of Indigenous Peoples begins with the period of Spanish colonization consequent on the first voyage of Christopher Columbus in 1492. Although the Spanish ‘discoveries’ centered on the Caribbean and then Central and South America, this encounter had great relevance because it was in the context of Spanish colonization that the issue of the rights of Indigenous peoples was first debated in legal terms.</p>

<p>The assertion of Spanish rights to Columbus's first landfall, the island of Hispaniola (the site of modern-day Haiti and the Dominican Republic) was steeped in the context of 15th century European power politics where the Papacy claimed not only a spiritual authority but a divinely ordained temporal jurisdiction over its Catholic subjects, including Kings and Emperors. Pursuant to this assumed jurisdiction the Pope in a papal bull issued in 1455 recognized and affirmed Portuguese title to discoveries made on the African Gold Coast. Following Christopher Columbus's first voyage, and with the benefit of the happy coincidence that a newly elected pope, Alexander VI, a Spaniard owed both his election to the papacy and also his family’s fortune to the favours of the Spanish crown, issued in 1493 a series of other bulls, Inter Caetera Divinai. The first of these declared that whereas Columbus had come upon lands and people "undiscovered by others… well disposed to embrace the Christian faith” all the lands discovered or to be discovered in the name of the Spanish crown legally belonged to King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella. Portugal contested any broad reading of this bull and a second version established the famous North to South papal line of demarcation for the entire Western Hemisphere - a line that ran from pole to pole, one hundred leagues west of the Azores. The Inter Caetera Divinae endowed Portugal with dominion over all of the undiscovered lands east of the imaginary line and gave Spain title to the lands west of the boundary, reserving nearly all of North and South America to Spain, and reserving for Portugal a fraction of the South American continent that now constitutes Brazil. Investing Ferdinand and Isabella with "full, free and integral power, authority and jurisdiction" the bull forbade any other person from interfering with Spain's newly recognized possessions or further possessions under the penalty of excommunication.<sup data-fn="58e01300-8a1d-4927-830b-d6a55fbf4ab4" class="fn"><a href="#58e01300-8a1d-4927-830b-d6a55fbf4ab4" id="58e01300-8a1d-4927-830b-d6a55fbf4ab4-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Pursuant to this papal grant the first colonizing expedition to the New World was led by Christopher Columbus on his second voyage in November 1493. Under the terms of his contract with the Spanish Crown Columbus was entitled to keep 1/10th of the wealth generated. The original estimates of mineral wealth failed to materialize and Columbus sought to export the indigenous inhabitants as slaves, an initiative which met with fierce resistance. When significant amounts of gold were finally discovered on Hispaniola in 1499 the large-scale pit mining created a demand for manual labor which Columbus met by instituting a forced labor system, backed by Spanish cavalry and dogs, in which squads of Indians were consigned (in Spanish “commended”) to the Spanish mine owners. The debilitating effect of this on the indigenous peoples of Hispaniola led to the expansion of the search for slave labor to other Caribbean Islands.<sup data-fn="8604778e-3271-405a-8565-de9d501140d3" class="fn"><a href="#8604778e-3271-405a-8565-de9d501140d3" id="8604778e-3271-405a-8565-de9d501140d3-link">2</a></sup></p>

<p>The cruelty of Spanish rule did not go unchallenged. A group of Friars who arrived in Hispaniola in 1510 reported the affront to Christian values that the regime instituted by Columbus represented. One of the Friars in a sermon thundered:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>You are in mortal sin… you live and die in it for the cruelty and tyranny you use in dealing with these innocent people. Tell me, by what right or justice do you keep these Indians in such a cruel and horrible servitude? By what authority had you waged detestable war against these people? Are these not men? Have they not rational souls? Are you not bound to love them as you love yourselves?<sup data-fn="152c89d9-0ea1-41da-be72-123a4c4be14d" class="fn"><a href="#152c89d9-0ea1-41da-be72-123a4c4be14d" id="152c89d9-0ea1-41da-be72-123a4c4be14d-link">3</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The legitimacy of Spanish title, based upon papal grant, remained the subject of dispute. The Dominicans war of words reached Spain and in 1512 the Spanish crown promulgated the Laws of Burgos, the first detailed legislation dealing with Indian affairs based upon a colonial policy of assimilation.<sup data-fn="30edd96d-9cd0-45a9-8fef-20d124091221" class="fn"><a href="#30edd96d-9cd0-45a9-8fef-20d124091221" id="30edd96d-9cd0-45a9-8fef-20d124091221-link">4</a></sup> Perversely, given the condemnation of cruelty of the colonial regime carried to Spain by the Dominicans, two years later royal lawyers prepared the text of a proclamation -the infamous Requerimiento - which Ferdinand instituted in 1514 that was to be announced to the Indians of the New World before Spanish hostilities could be legally pursued.<sup data-fn="bf97a6ec-23dc-42cf-b060-4a68541470a3" class="fn"><a href="#bf97a6ec-23dc-42cf-b060-4a68541470a3" id="bf97a6ec-23dc-42cf-b060-4a68541470a3-link">5</a></sup> Designed to legitimate a just war against the Indian, the Requerimiento, after tracing the origins of the Pope's jurisdiction to make grants and the donation of the New World to the Spanish crown, provided the Indians to whom it was proclaimed with this choice:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Wherefore, as best we can, we ask and require that you consider what we have said to you, and that you take the time that shall be necessary to understand and deliberate upon it, and acknowledge the church as the ruler and superior of the whole world, and the high priest called Pope, and in his name, the king and queen, our lords, in his place as superiors and lords and kings of these islands and this mainland by virtue of the said donation, and that you can send and permit that these religious fathers declare and preach to you the aforesaid…</p>

<p>But if you do not do this or if maliciously delay in doing it, I certify it to you that with the help of God we shall forcefully entered into your country and shall make war against you… and shall subject you to the yoke and obedience of the church and of their highnesses; we shall take you and your wives and your children and shall make slaves of them, and as such shall sell and dispose of them as the highnesses make command; and we shall take away your goods and shall do to you all the harm and damage that we can, as to vassels who do not obey and refuse to receive their lord and resist and contradict him; and we protest that the deaths and losses which accrue from this are your fault, and not that of their Highnesses, or ours, or of the soldiers who come with us.<sup data-fn="770c259b-404b-4a36-b051-5d99daf5a36d" class="fn"><a href="#770c259b-404b-4a36-b051-5d99daf5a36d" id="770c259b-404b-4a36-b051-5d99daf5a36d-link">6</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Hanke, in his history of the Requerimiento, provides this description of the emptiness of this legal appeal to godly authority:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>A complete list of the events that occurred when the Requerimiento formalities ordered by King Ferdinand were carried out in America, more or less according to the law, might tax the reader's patience and credulity, for the Requerimiento was read to trees and empty huts when no Indians were to be found. Captains muttered its theological phrases into their beard on the edge of sleeping Indian settlements, or even a league way before starting their formal attack and at times some …Spanish notary hurled its sonorous phrases after the Indians as they fled into the mountains. .. Ship captains would sometimes have a document read from the deck as they approached an island, and at night would send out enslaving expeditions.<sup data-fn="a9665593-6775-4f05-bf68-5dfaaf0d9142" class="fn"><a href="#a9665593-6775-4f05-bf68-5dfaaf0d9142" id="a9665593-6775-4f05-bf68-5dfaaf0d9142-link">7</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Spanish colonial regime was an affront to the emerging Spanish Renaissance consciousness that emerged in the early 16th century. Increasingly natural law ideas originating from the teachings of Thomas Aquinas were cited by Spanish humanist thinkers to attack the foundation of Spain's title based upon papal donation. These ideas received their fullest expression in the writings of Francisco de Vitoria, a Dominican scholar who first articulated a set of principles for the regulation of relationships between states that are regarded as part of the foundations of modern international law. An integral part of Vitoria’s work related to the nature of the rights of indigenous peoples.</p>

<p>Vitoria's most important work on Indian rights is contained in a lecture he delivered at the University of Salamanca in 1532 entitled "On the Indians Lately Discovered".<sup data-fn="5ac1449a-7949-48c4-9e12-28eb630cb2bf" class="fn"><a href="#5ac1449a-7949-48c4-9e12-28eb630cb2bf" id="5ac1449a-7949-48c4-9e12-28eb630cb2bf-link">8</a></sup> His first two arguments were that (1) the inhabitants of the Indies possess natural legal rights as free and rational men; (2) the Pope's grant to Spain of title to the Indians was baseless and could not effect the inherent rights of the Indian inhabitants.</p>

<p>Vitoria's first argument was based on the premise that certain basic rights inherent in people, not by reason of their race, creed or colour, but by reason of their humanity. This principle is now well established and is enshrined in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, and in Canada in the Charter of Rights and Freedoms and in UNDRIP but in 1550 it was to many a heretical proposition. According to Vitoria, by virtue of their natural rights indigenous peoples "were true owners, before the Spaniards came among them, both from the public and the private point of view."…. and undoubtedly had true dominion in both public and private matters, just like Christians, and that neither their princes nor private persons could be despoiled of their property on the ground of their not being true owners” without just cause.<sup data-fn="3353d70a-c4bc-4d48-b451-5c82fc53bf58" class="fn"><a href="#3353d70a-c4bc-4d48-b451-5c82fc53bf58" id="3353d70a-c4bc-4d48-b451-5c82fc53bf58-link">9</a></sup> Vitoria's own words left little doubt about how he would have viewed the dispossession of Aboriginal peoples by British Columbia 500 years later:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>it is manifest that it is not justifiable to take anything they possess from …unbelievers as such, that is, because they are unbelievers; but the act would be theft or robbery no less than if it were done to Christians.<sup data-fn="3dc51c84-7129-4914-bb19-d34ca197038e" class="fn"><a href="#3dc51c84-7129-4914-bb19-d34ca197038e" id="3dc51c84-7129-4914-bb19-d34ca197038e-link">10</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>In addition to rejecting Spain's claim to title of the New World by virtue of the papal donation, Vitoria also refuted any title derived by virtue of first discovery. Vitoria argued:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Now the rule of the Law of Nations is that what belongs to nobody is granted to the first occupant, as is expressly laid down in the…Institutes. And so, as the object in question was not without an owner, it does not fall under the title which we are discussing….<strong>By itself it gives no support to a seizure of the aboriginies any more than if it had been they who had discovered us.</strong><sup data-fn="7f7b843d-14f7-49e9-8b95-5432a064bed1" class="fn"><a href="#7f7b843d-14f7-49e9-8b95-5432a064bed1" id="7f7b843d-14f7-49e9-8b95-5432a064bed1-link">11</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The publication of Vitoria's lecture did not, however, bring to an end the debate about the legal and moral legitimacy of Spanish colonization. Indeed, the vitality of the debate culminated in one of the most remarkable, but largely forgotten, event in the history of European colonization, the meeting of the Junta or Council summoned by the Emperor Charles V, in Valladolid in 1550-51, to consider the justice of the wars being waged against the Indians by Spain in America. The Valladolid Council took the form of a legal disputation between, on the one hand, Juan Gines de Sepulveda, one of Spain's leading philosophers and theologians and confessor of the Emperor and on the other side Bartolome de Las Casas, the former conquistadore turned Dominican friar and later Bishop, who was appointed Defender of the Indians by official decree of the Emperor. The central issue debated before the Council at Valladolid was the legality and justice of waging war to propagate Christianity amongst the indigenous people.</p>

<p>Underlying Sepulveda's views were his views on the inherent inferiority of indigenous people. Sepulveda described them thus:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>How compare [the Spanish] gifts of prudence, talent, magnaniminity, temperance, humanity and religion with those little men (homunculos) in whom you will scarcely find traces of humanity; who not only lack culture but do not even know how to write, who keep no records of their history except certain obscure and vague reminiscences of some things put down in certain pictures, and who do not have written laws but only barbarous institutions and customs.<sup data-fn="237a66e1-79bf-4bc1-b789-01d5564ec8bf" class="fn"><a href="#237a66e1-79bf-4bc1-b789-01d5564ec8bf" id="237a66e1-79bf-4bc1-b789-01d5564ec8bf-link">12</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Las Casas, in seeking to refute Sepulveda's views that the Indians in some Aristotelian view were fit only for slavery and subjection to a more civilized order, called upon his extensive experience of the Indians and stated:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Even though they lack the art and use of writing, they are not wanting in the capacity and skill to rule and govern themselves, both publicly and privately. Thus they have kingdoms, communities and cities that they govern wisely according to their laws and customs…therefore, not all barbarians are irrational or natural slaves or unfit for government. Some barbarians, then, in accord with justice and nature, have kingdoms, royal dignities, jurisdiction, and good laws, and there is among them lawful government…</p>

<p>They are not ignorant, inhuman, or bestial, rather, long before they had heard the word Spaniard they had properly organized states, wisely ordered by excellent laws, religion and custom. They cultivated friendship and, bound together in common fellowship, lived in populous cities in which they wisely administered the affairs of both peace and war justly and equitably, truly governed by laws that in very many points surpass ours, and could have won the admiration of the sages of Athens.<sup data-fn="e3d04053-f2d0-498a-9d34-6161519ac21e" class="fn"><a href="#e3d04053-f2d0-498a-9d34-6161519ac21e" id="e3d04053-f2d0-498a-9d34-6161519ac21e-link">13</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The relevance of these remarkable events in Spain over 470 years ago to the resolution of the issues of Aboriginal title before the Courts in Calder, Delgamuukw and Tsilchotin it that legal theories regarding the rights of Indigenous people are intimately connected with assumptions and assertions about the nature of Indigenous peoples and societies. To the extent that advocates such as Sepulveda could assert that Indigenous people were outside the pale of civilization so it followed as a matter of law that they were without rights and their territories were subject to lawful invasion. To those like Vitoria and Las Casas who respected and recognized Indigenous civilizations as entitled to the fundamental rights enjoyed by European nations, it followed as a matter of natural and international law that their rights to territorial integrity be respected.<sup data-fn="1b291ea5-452f-43bf-9187-4462f06fb994" class="fn"><a href="#1b291ea5-452f-43bf-9187-4462f06fb994" id="1b291ea5-452f-43bf-9187-4462f06fb994-link">14</a></sup> In the specific context of the colonial history of British Columbia the statements of Joseph Trutch, the Colony’s Commissioner of Lands and Works and the Province’s first Lieutenant Governor, describing Indians as bestial rather than human, were mirror images of those of Sepulveda and were reflected in land policies that denied the existence of Aboriginal title.<sup data-fn="8092877c-b27d-4e4c-b92c-5eafcb72b2fe" class="fn"><a href="#8092877c-b27d-4e4c-b92c-5eafcb72b2fe" id="8092877c-b27d-4e4c-b92c-5eafcb72b2fe-link">15</a></sup> </p>

<p>Over the course of the last 470 years, this concept has re-asserted itself in a number of the cases which have been argued in both U.S. and Canadian courts. It is an attitude which anthropologist Hugh Brody, in his opinion evidence in Delgamuukw, referred to as part of the superiorist assumptions which white Europeans have brought to the frontiers of North America. In Delgumuukw in 1987 Chief Justice McEachern was unable to overcome this inheritance of colonial history in concluding that the Gitksan and Hereditary Chiefs’ ancestors before contact were “a primitive people” living in a “primitive condition”, “eking out an aboriginal life” which was “at best nasty brutish and short”, lived in a “legal and jurisdictional vacuum”, acted on “survival instincts” and were “hardly amenable to obedience to anything but the most rudimentary forms of custom”.</p>
