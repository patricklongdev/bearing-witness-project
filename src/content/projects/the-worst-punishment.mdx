<p>Carolynn’s project is one of three projects in the Archive that address the challenges facing women who are sentenced to imprisonment and have infant children. As an introduction to this issue and the history of programs that have been introduced both federally and provincially in Canada to allow for mothers to have their young infants with them you can <a href="/projects/mothers-and-children-in-prison/">read my backgrounder</a> which includes an important case in BC Supreme Court that successfully challenged the cancellation of the BC provincial program.</p>

<p>Carolynn Conron, in her 2009 project for the Penal Policy Seminar created a diary titled “The Worst Punishment – a Diary from Mommy to Baby”. The diary tells a compelling story, written from the imagined perspective of a young Indigenous expectant mother who is sentenced to a provincial term of imprisonment at Alouette Correctional Centre. Based on a composite of the lives of many young Indigenous women who experience intergenerational family disruption and multiple foster care placements, the diary describes the chaotic life that led her to the prison gates, That history is now officially referred to as the “Aboriginal Social History” that in accordance with the 1999 decision of the Supreme Court of Canada in <em>Gladue</em> must be considered by courts in sentencing Indigenous offenders. The diary is handwritten, and stapled to many of the pages are documents relevant to the evidence considered in the <em>Inglis</em> case by Justice Ross, including press releases and news reports regarding the litigation. The diary is one of a number of Archive projects that demand to be hand held to fully appreciate their gravitas, but from the pages profiled in the Archive you will get a sense of how the closure of the mother child program was experienced by prisoners and why it was challenged in the courts. The Archive file contains the cover page and sixteen photos of diary entries.</p>

<p>In giving her consent to the inclusion of the diary in the Archive &nbsp;Carolynn writes:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>As an established criminal defence lawyer, I hear firsthand about the impact that parenthood has on individuals involved in the criminal justice system.</p>

<p>The bonds we all share with our family has a strong influence on our connection with the world and can be a powerful motivator for change. The desire to be released from custody to be with one’s children and the desire to be better for them is strong. Perhaps it is the way children represent our early lives, before the weight of the world impressed its gravitational pull upon our shoulders, or the way children admire their parents despite our flaws, but the connection and love between a parent and child is felt deeply. When parents lose that connection to their children, they often lose their connection with and hope for a brighter future. &nbsp;</p>
</blockquote>
