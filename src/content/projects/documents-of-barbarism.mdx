<p>In my teaching of Aboriginal and Treaty Rights I have taken a long view of history and invite students to review the very earliest days of the colonial project. That historical record begins over 500 years ago with the period of Spanish colonization consequent on the first voyage of Christopher Columbus in 1492. Although the Spanish ‘discoveries’ centered on the Caribbean and then Central and South America, this encounter had great relevance because it was in the context of Spanish colonization that the issue of the rights of Indigenous peoples rights was first debated in legal terms. The relevance of these remarkable events in Spain half a millenium ago to the resolution of contemporary issues in Canada is that it clearly demonstrates that legal theories regarding the rights of Indigenous people are intimately connected with assumptions and assertions about the nature of Indigenous peoples and societies.</p>

<p>As a law professor and lawyer my stock in trade is the written and spoken word. It is my great fortune and privilege to have had law students who are not so limited. Dana-Lynn McKenzie, a member of the Hwlitsum First Nation, in her 2010 project in a painted wood panel/collage reflected on the legacy of a legal debate almost 500 years ago. Until recently largely forgotten, the debate took place at the meeting of the Junta or Council summoned by the Spanish Emperor Charles V, in Valladolid in 1550-51 to consider the justice of the wars being waged against the Indians by Spain in America. The Valladolid Council took the form of a legal disputation between, on the one hand, Juan Gines de Sepulveda, one of Spain's leading philosophers and theologians and confessor of the Emperor and on the other side Bartolome de Las Casas, the former conquistadore turned Dominican friar and later Bishop, who was appointed Defender of the Indians by official decree of the Emperor. The central issue debated before the Council at Valladolid was the legality and justice of waging war to propagate Christianity amongst the Indigenous people. Dana Lynn’s panel contains extracts from this debate:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>One of the principal arguments of Sepulveda for justifying war against the Indians in order to convert them was that the Indians of the New World were in such a state of barbarism that force was required to liberate them from this condition. Underlying Sepulveda's views were his views on the inherent inferiority of Indigenous people. Sepulveda described them thus:</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>How compare [the Spanish] gifts of prudence, talent, magnaniminity, temperance, humanity and religion with those little men (homunculos) in whom you will scarcely find traces of humanity; who not only lack culture but do not even know how to write, who keep no records of their history except certain obscure and vague reminiscences of some things put down in certain pictures, and who do not have written laws but only barbarous institutions and customs.</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>Las Casas, in seeking to refute Sepulveda's views that the Indians in some Aristotelian view were fit only for slavery and subjection to a more civilized order, called upon his extensive experience of the Indians and stated:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Even though they lack the art and use of writing, they are not wanting in the capacity and skill to rule and govern themselves, both publicly and privately. Thus they have kingdoms, communities and cities that they govern wisely according to their laws and customs…therefore, not all barbarians are irrational or natural slaves or unfit for government. Some barbarians, then, in accord with justice and nature, have kingdoms, royal dignities, jurisdiction, and good laws, and there is among them lawful government…</p>

<p>They are not ignorant, inhuman, or bestial, rather, long before they had heard the word Spaniard they had properly organized states, wisely ordered by excellent laws, religion and custom. They cultivated friendship and, bound together in common fellowship, lived in populous cities in which they wisely administered the affairs of both peace and war justly and equitably, truly governed by laws that in very many points surpass ours, and could have won the admiration of the sages of Athens.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The debate 470 years ago at Valladolid is not a historical footnote. Lewis Hanke in his scholarly historical analysis of the debate at Valladolid has written:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>[T]he historian, looking back on the conflict over the nature of the American Indians, can see why the late José Almoina characterized it as “perhaps the most transcendental controversy that took place at the threshold of the modern age.” This controversy raised permanent problems for the modern age.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>To the extent that advocates such as Sepulveda could assert that Indians were little more than animals outside the pale of civilization so it followed as a matter of law that they were without rights and their territories were subject to lawful invasion. To those like Vitoria and Las Casas who respected Indian peoples and recognized their civilizations as entitled to the fundamental rights enjoyed by European Christian nations, it followed as a matter of natural and international law that their rights to territorial integrity be respected. In the specific context of the colonial history of British Columbia the statements of Joseph Trutch, the Colony’s Commissioner of Lands and Works and the Province’s first Lieutenant Governor, describing Indians as bestial rather than human, were mirror images of those of Sepulveda and were reflected in land policies that denied the existence of Aboriginal title. Over the course of the last 470 years, this concept has re-asserted itself in a number of the cases which have been argued in both U.S. and Canadian courts. It is an attitude which anthropologist Hugh Brody, in his opinion evidence in Delgamuukw, referred to as part of the superiorist assumptions which white Europeans have brought to the frontiers of North America. In <em>Delgumuukw</em> in 1987 Chief Justice McEachern was unable to overcome this inheritance of colonial history in concluding that the Gitksan and Hereditary Chiefs’s ancestors before contact were "a primitive people" living in a "primitive condition", "eking out an aboriginal life" which was "at best nasty brutish and short", lived in a "legal and jurisdictional vacuum", acted on "survival instincts" and were "hardly amenable to obedience to anything but the most rudimentary forms of custom".<sup data-fn="665aba6a-c9f1-4717-8bb8-35baff93c562" class="fn"><a href="#665aba6a-c9f1-4717-8bb8-35baff93c562" id="665aba6a-c9f1-4717-8bb8-35baff93c562-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Dana-Lynn’s painted wood panel harks back to an art medium that was common in Renaissance Europe but Dana-Lynn’s panel resonates both with the words of history, what Robert Williams has described as the “Documents of Barbarism” and her own and her family’s contemporary experiences of colonialism:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>They say that those who do not learn from the past are destined to repeat it. The echoes ofthe past resonate deeply when learning about Aboriginal and Treaty Rights. It was a great privilege to listen to Professor Jackson and how he constructed the lesson of colonialism and the law of treaties. In my project I have attempted to weave in the words of Bartolome de las Casas and the early contact of the Spanish and the Native people of North and Central America. I found the justification of the Requimento very striking. The Requimento encapsulates what I imagine British settlers must have thought to use Christianity to validate displacing the First Nations in what would become Canada.</p>

<p>I wrote down quotes in class of Bartolome's protests. It is amazing that 500 years ago thesame arguments occurred: whether Aboriginals have souls, whether Natives are "men". Indians have only been allowed to vote Federally in Canada for 50 years. As a status person, I am a ward of the state. The Federal government has a power over me that non-Natives in Canada are free from. Viewing the comments on CBC's news website there are so many people who post things that illustrate discrimination against Native people. Often, an equal amount of people viewing discriminatory comments approved of them compared to the number that disapproved of those comments. Non- Native Canadians resent that we are wards of the state. They say they don't want their tax dollars spent to " baby-sit' Native people. There is no equality here. Whether or not we are "men" or have souls is a live issue.</p>

<p>The Discovery Doctrine and the holding of Johnson v. M'Intosh is also a viewpoint that is alive and well. It is another tool that has been used to displace Aboriginal people. I felt it was akin to a rubber-stamping of the plan to forget about the Covenant Chain and the early treaties, and is emblematic of the viewpoint of non-Native Canadians. Many non-native Canadians don' t even see us; they walk right by an Aboriginal person sleeping rough in a doorway. They have low expectations of Aboriginals and have forgotten that this was our land, and they displaced us.</p>

<p>The history of injustice that permeates my family's past, the Hwlitsum First Nation, runs through many Aboriginal communities. The most difficult thing is that there are not many of us able to stand up against it. In my own family the demons of alcohol and violence run rampant, and judging from the statistics of over- incarceration of Aboriginals my family is not alone in this struggle.</p>

<p>In order for a community to even begin to be in a place where treaty making and the case law can be a tool for regaining our land and self-governance, Aboriginal people have to break free of the chains of addiction and crime. Then, as a community, we need to break the perceptions of non-Native Canadians and make them see us. We are more than just wards of the state requiring the taxpayer to "baby-sit". We have souls, and we are "men", like everyone else. Then, as Bartolome stated, we should not be deprived of our liberty, either as a ward of the state, or a guest of Corrections Canada, or displaced people. We will have our treaties and our land, and will be self-sufficient. And the justification that Canada uses, the spectre of addictions and crime, should fade away as we reclaim our culture and honour our ancestors.</p>
</blockquote>
