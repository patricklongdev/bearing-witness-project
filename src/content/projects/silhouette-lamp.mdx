<p>Brock’s essay explains why he chose this distinctive art form:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Over the course of this semester, we have explored the history of the BC Federal Correctional System from the BC Pen in New Westminster to Kent and Matsqui in the Fraser Valley. We have discussed many issues such as administrative segregation, disciplinary and parole hearings, the relationship between the keepers and the kept, and the evolution and de-evolution of correctional policy over the last 50 years. The world behind the razor wire fence has changed considerably since the publication of <em>Justice Behind the Walls</em>, and unfortunately not for the better. Having the opportunity to hear from Deputy Warden Mark Noon-Ward, IPO Ms. Garrison, my brother Beau Bellrichard, the inmates we visited at Matsqui, and Professor Jackson, I got to learned how our correctional institutions impact both inmates and employees.</p>

<p>Based on what I've seen, heard and learned from this class, I found a number of themes that, despite the passage of time and reform, continue to persist in the Canadian Correctional System. To express these themes, I have constructed a lamp that highlights the issues inmates and staff encounter within CSC. The inspiration for this project came from Hughie MacDonald's Tiffany lamp that he had in his cell. According to my brother, Hughie no longer has the lamp, and he suspects it may have been broken by CSC guards. Even though this is not a Tiffany lamp, I believe it represents much of what I have learned and will take away from this course.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The lamp Brock refers to features in a photo taken by my son Shane Jackson for the online gallery associated with <em>Justice behind the Walls</em>. A selection of Shane’s photographs are the subject of a separate entry in the Archive.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Administrative Segregation</h3>

<p>Administrative Segregation was deemed cruel and unusual punishment in 1975 in the case of <em>McCann v The Queen</em>. The <em>McCann</em> decision brought about physical changes to segregation units as well procedural changes to the use of administrative segregation. Nevertheless, even after these changes were made, the haunting screams and stories recorded by Michael Jackson in <em>Justice Behind the Walls</em> shows that administrative segregation continues to deeply impact the well-being of inmates. As Winston Churchill said, "the mood and temper of the public in regard to the treatment of crime and criminals is one of the most unfailing tests of the civilization of any country" (U.K., H.C., Parliamentary Debates, 5th series, vol. 19 col. 1354(20 July 1910)). It is shocking to know that forty years after <em>McCann</em>, cases like Ashley Smith continue to occur and inmates are still subject to indeterminate time in segregation. Fortunately, with the BC Civil Liberties Association having started a lawsuit claiming that the use of administrative segregation is unconstitutional in that is constitutes cruel and unusual punishment, there is hope that the treatment of inmates will improve.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">On the Surface</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2025/02/Brock_Bellrichard_06-617x1024.jpg" alt="Brock Bellrichard's 2015 sculpture project, &quot;Silhouette Lamp,&quot; image 5." class="wp-image-3562"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>One of the troubling things that we have learned from this course is the amount of disrespect and abuse inmates have received from CSC staff towards themselves and their property. The abuse inmates have received from the system they are in has had a major impact on the relationship between the keepers and the kept, on inmate morale, and has left psychological and physical scars. To express this abuse, I have used the lamp as a whole to demonstrate this abuse and disrespect. The cigarette bums, and the blood stains represent the abuse and disrespect inmates receive on a routine basis from some of the staff from CSC. This is not to suggest that all CSC staff are abusive and disrespectful to the inmates under their supervision, but the abuse is present, persistent and growing, as we have heard from both inmates and even Deputy Warden Noon-Ward.</p>

<p>Like physical scars, this only represents the surface of this project. Once illuminated, this lamp highlight s even more issues within Canadian Corrections.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The first image illuminated is of Lady Justice, but like the photo of Professor Jackson at the BC Pen in the 1970's, there is a cigarette burn on her face. I used this reference to represent the lack of respect CSC has for justice, fairness and the justice system. Lady Justice is a symbol that is entrenched in the Canadian judicial system and can be seen at every courthouse in the country. It symbolizes a fair and just legal system to those who wish to benefit from our system and to those who must face the potential penalties of our system. As we have heard and seen, justice and fairness is frequently absent in our correctional institutions. A great example of the lack of respect CSC has for the justice system is what happened to Hughie MacDonald. After being acquitted of the murder of Gary Allen on the grounds of self-defense, CSC still viewed him as guilty and a sufficiently dangerous prisoner to justify a transfer to the Special Handling Unit in Quebec and it required judicial intervention to prevent this. It is difficult to describe how this disrespect of the law impacts inmates from my perspective but hearing from inmates, it is clear that it troubles them. As Beau Bellrichard put it, people are sent to prison to learn that breaking the law is bad and has consequences. But when people are sent to a place where laws are broken on a daily basis by those who are supposed to control and rehabilitate you and there are no consequences to their actions, it makes it difficult for inmates to internalize the lessons they are expected to learn.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Which is the Bigger Number, Five or One?</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2025/02/Brock_Bellrichard_10.jpg" alt="Brock Bellrichard's 2015 sculpture project, &quot;Silhouette Lamp,&quot; image 9." class="wp-image-3569"/></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2025/02/Brock_Bellrichard_11-553x1024.jpg" alt="Brock Bellrichard's 2015 sculpture project, &quot;Silhouette Lamp,&quot; image 10." class="wp-image-3570"/></figure>
</figure>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Another theme that I found in this course is how inmates are no longer supporting each other the same way they did a few decades ago. During the 1970's and 80's, inmates were actively rioting and speaking out against the conditions of the penitentiaries and the treatment they were receiving from staff. These riots and hostage takings had a major impact on the way Corrections Canada operated, most notably the enactment of the CCRA. But what is important to take away from these actions was that inmates fought together to make these changes occur and would not stand idle when another inmate was harassed or abused by staff.</p>

<p>The inmate community today is no longer the collective force they once were. With protective custody inmates out-numbering general populations, integrated populations in many federal institutions, the requirement that an inmate needs to provide staff with information before checking into segregation are just a few factors that have led to a breakdown of inmate morale. As a result, inmates are no longer voicing their opinion as a collective group, and are not banding together when things go bad. We are seeing major changes in CSC that are negatively impacting inmates such as changes to the food, increases in controlled movements, less respectful staff, yet there is no action being taken by inmates. Even Deputy Warden Noon-Ward is surprised that there hasn't been much of a reaction from inmates.</p>

<p>To symbolize this change, I have used a fist and the open palm. The fist represents one, as in inmates banding together as one to try and create change within CSC. The open hand represents five, in that inmates are distant from one another, are unwilling to work together, and are submitting to the recent changes that CSC has made. The open palm is higher than the fist as it represents the current attitude of inmates.</p>

</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Over the course of the semester, we learned about many of the issues with the way the Canadian correctional system operates, and how it has frustrated both inmates and employees of CSC. This silhouette expresses the mental struggle inmates and staff must deal with on a daily basis.</p>

<p>In terms of inmates, these frustrations are often the result of arbitrary changes in CSC, inaccessibility to programs, constantly changing IPO's, arbitrary disciplinary action, and a lack of recognition of positive changes made by inmates. One of the issues that inmates are frustrated the most about is how staff and IPO's focus on the negative actions of an inmate and tend to ignore the positives. Furthermore, blemishes on an inmate's record can follow them for years and be used against them despite it not reflecting the person they are now.</p>

<p>CSC staff are also frustrated by the system due to budget cuts, union requirements, larger caseloads and limited time to conduct their work. Ms. Garrison, an IPO at Kent, explained that due to budget cuts and more intense security measures, it has become nearly impossible for an IPO to do their job properly. Fortunately for staff, they are able to escape these frustrations at the end of each shift, while inmates are entrenched in it.</p>

<p>In all, what is most alarming about the frustration that inmates face is how it has impacted their mental and physical health. For some inmates, they are able to remain positive despite the frustrations, while others react by yelling and fighting with staff or even worse, harming themselves or committing suicide. This sad reality is not sign of a system rehabilitating people, rather it is a sign of a system that is breaking people.</p>
</blockquote>
