<p>Marina la Salle In her 2009 project for the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar presented “A Visual History of Indigenous- Colonial Relations in Point Grey, British Columbia”. Using historical and contemporary photographs, maps, illustrations, archival documents, newspaper articles and official correspondence, Marina provided this overview of her ambitious project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Since Europeans first arrived off the shores of what is now called Vancouver, the relationships formed between them and the Indigenous peoples of this area have been myriad and complex. Indigenous groups occupying the area that Captain Vancouver designated as Point Grey have played all kinds of roles on the stage of European study. Viewed by some initially as curiosities, these groups came to be seen as valuable sources of local knowledge and trade opportunities for those who would eventually establish Fort Langley. Declining numbers and an increasing settler population threatened Indigenous sovereignty, and groups such as the Musqueam were subjected to Crown rule, administration and subjugation. Once close groups were divided by distant reserves and communities fragmented through the residential school system. These once politically, socially and economically independent groups came to rely on wage-labour, albeit offset by continued hunting, fishing and gathering of local resources. Despite rampant racism built into imperialistic government policy, the relationships formed on an individual scale between members of the Musqueam Band and new settlers were heterogeneous and complicated, some formed on an equal basis of power as “workers” and some placed Musqueam in the position of landlord. The question of Indigenous sovereignty, however, never went away despite government attempts to quash the spirit behind it and rights and title claims have loomed large in the forefront of Indigenous-Colonial Relations since at least the 1970s. In the Point Grey area, such land-claims have significantly affected the relationship formed between Musqueam, the government, local residents, and quite often have the principles espoused by believers in social justice been challenged when its resolution came to their backyards. The ongoing effects of colonialism continue to be felt by Indigenous communities, yet there are real strides being made to rebuild all relationships and form new ones between the settler society and Indigenous peoples, relationships based on mutual respect and trust. Only time will tell how these will play out.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>As the scope of the project Marina explains:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This project attempts to illustrate the above history through the use of printed materials, photographs, documents, facts and figures. Its temporal scope is from European contact the present, roughly chronologically, while its spatial focus is on Point Grey, BC, acting as a microcosm for Indigenous-colonial relations nationally. Three things should be said about the materials presented., First, as I stress in several of the page summaries, relationships of all kinds existed between Indigenous individuals and settlers, and so the examples presented here are by no means representative of this entire spread of diversity. Instead, the dynamics focused on are ones that are highlighted in publicly available materials, which limits discussion since what makes its way into print is inherently filtered. Thus, in a sense the discussion ensuing is one of relationships “portrayed”. Finally, what photographs and text I have included here has been strategic itself, and there is meaning both in content and their arrangement. A thesis could be written explaining the complexity of the connections, and it would still not grasp the subtleties of even one paragraph. Thus, the summary is provided a meant only to express some of the explanation and analysis of each page; leave the rest up to the reader.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>With reference to Marina’s comment about “the complexity of the connections”, because of the multilayered content of every folio, documenting her project has been challenging and it is one where I went to an outside commercial publishing company to scan her project. Even then it was not possible to capture every entry. Hopefully, I have included below just enough of the pages to give a visitor to the site an understanding of the historical range of Marina’s work. To read more of the work <a href="/assets/2009/10/Marina_La_Salle.pdf">go to the pdf version</a>.</p>

<p>After completing her law degree Marina continued her inquiries on the intersection of archaeology, ethnography, and history in urban Vancouver, BC. In her Ph.D thesis at the University of British Columbia Marina investigated how 'heritage' in Pacific Spirit Regional Park is constructed, communicated, and controlled by affected communities, and the role that such heritage plays in contemporary views of history. In what for many ‘settlers’ is an uncomfortable and challenging message she argued that "nature" in the park is manufactured to forget colonial violence and to feel better about ongoing environmental devastation.<sup data-fn="21dd4922-0020-4a48-b6c0-e7768d981601" class="fn"><a href="#21dd4922-0020-4a48-b6c0-e7768d981601" id="21dd4922-0020-4a48-b6c0-e7768d981601-link">1</a></sup></p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This dissertation investigates the ideology of Pacific Spirit Regional Park, an urban forest adjacent to the University of British Columbia in Vancouver, Canada. Using the tools of archaeology and anthropology, I analyse the history, landscape, performance, and discourse of the park to understand Pacific Spirit as a culturally-constructed place that embodies an ideology of imperialism. Central in this dynamic is the carefully crafted illusion of Pacific Spirit as a site of “nature,” placed in opposition to “culture,” which naturalizes the values that created and are communicated through the park and thereby neutralizes their politics. They remain, however, very political. The park as nature erases the history and heritage of the Indigenous peoples of this region, transforming Pacific Spirit into a new terra nullius—a site to be discovered and explored, militaristic themes that consistently underlie park programs and propaganda. These cultural tropes connect to produce a nationalistic settler narrative wherein class ideals of nature and community are evoked in the celebration of Canada’s history of colonialism and capitalist expansion—paradoxically, the very processes that have caused the fragmentation of communities and ecosystems. The park as nature also feeds into the portrayal of this space as having been saved from development and, as such, an environmental triumph. In this context, the park is viewed as escape from the psychological trauma and alienation of city living and is celebrated and revered as a sacred place. This portrayal enables the forgetting of injustice and promotes a collective amnesia through the creation of a fairy-tale version of reality. The result is to disperse emotion and energy that otherwise could be mobilized against capitalism to prevent ongoing global ecological devastation. The ideology of the Pacific Spirit as nature therefore constitutes social violence by rewriting both the past and present of this land and its peoples, thereby hindering recognition of and rebellion against power. Pacific Spirit is thus a hegemonic space that reproduces colonial relationships and naturalizes capitalism. Exposing the park as a cultural place and illuminating the ideology that it perpetuates may be a crucial first step towards disrupting power through the creation of counter-narratives.<sup data-fn="13aa2cd6-f297-4eb7-a240-3dda6258ab41" class="fn"><a href="#13aa2cd6-f297-4eb7-a240-3dda6258ab41" id="13aa2cd6-f297-4eb7-a240-3dda6258ab41-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
