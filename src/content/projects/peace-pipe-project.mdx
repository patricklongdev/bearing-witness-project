<p>Shawn Jodway in his 2004 project built one of the calumet pipes used in the treaty making process and other important ceremonial occasions and in his documented essay describes the significance of the pipe and how he crafted it.</p>

<p class="has-text-align-center">From the red stone of the quarry<br /> With his hand he broke a fragment, <br />Moulded it into a pipe-head,<br />Shaped and fashioned it with figures; <br />From the margin of the river<br />Took a long reed for a pipe-stem, <br />With its dark green leaves upon it; <br />Filled the pipe with bark of willow, <br />With the bark of the red willow; <br />Breathed upon the neighboring forest, <br />Made its great boughs chafe together, <br />Till in flame they burst and kindled; <br />And erect upon the mountains, <br />Gitche Manito, the mighty,<br />Smoked the calumet, the Peace-Pipe, <br />As a signal to the nations.</p>

<p class="has-text-align-center">From "The Song of Hiawatha" <br />Henry Wadsworth Longfellow</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I choose to build a clay peace pipe because of its significance in treaty negotiations between Aboriginals and non-Aboriginals. Peace pipes have a long and symbolic history in the relations between Aboriginal non-Aboriginals. This paper will briefly describe this significance, the process I followed in building the peace pipe, and the significance of its symbolic design.</p>

<p>When Aboriginals Nations dominated North America, carrying a ceremonial pipe signified peaceful intent. The first European to arrive in North America carried pipes in case of chance meetings with Aboriginals because treaties with non-Aboriginals were often ' sealed' by smoking a ceremonial pipe. The term "peace pipe" came from the idea that smoking the pipe during the signing of a peace treaty signified peace and friendship. Aboriginals have used peace pipe for many other ceremonies besides making treaties.</p>

<p>In combination with tobacco and other plants, peace pipes have been used for medicinal and religious purposes because tobacco is highly regarded as the one of the most sacred plants to many Aboriginal peoples. Aboriginals have used tobacco to show reverence for their Creator, and respect for each other. Tobacco is used in daily offerings and libations such as sprinkling tobacco to ask for strength or a safe journey to or from home. Today the peace pipe is used for even more types of events. Not only are pipes used for religious ceremonies they are also used to ' witness' an event, such as the swearing in of an elected official, naming ceremonies for children, and groundbreaking for new buildings and facilities. Aboriginals believe that the rising smoke form the pipe carries messages to their Creator.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>With the assistance of some selfies Shawn documents the step-by-step process in selecting and building up the clay and the choices he made in the decoration of the pipe reflecting both his Metis heritage and some traditional and contemporary themes.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I chose to carve the Metis symbol on the pipe' s bowl because I self identify as a Metis as my father s mother was Cree from the James Bay region. My family name was originally spelled " Joudion" but when my father was sent to a residential school in St. Albert, the priest ' Anglicized ' it for him so it is was spelled "Jodway" so the priest could pronounce it easier.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>After receiving the project I realized from the photographs that the studio in which he built the pipe is located some 200 yards from my house on the Vancouver seawall.</p>
