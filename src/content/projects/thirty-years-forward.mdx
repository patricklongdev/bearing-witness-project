<p>Isabelle Lefroy in her photo essay for the 2020 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar “Thirty Years Forward, One Standoff Back: Oka and Wet’suwet’en” frames her project around both the Oka crisis and a contemporary standoff 30 years later and and 2500 miles to the West. Members of the Wet’suwet’in Nation facing the construction of the Coastal GasLink pipeline in British Columbia through unceded Wet’suwet’en territory, despite the opposition of hereditary chiefs, established a camp along the pipeline route of way blockading construction workers, once more generating national attention and the intervention of the RCMP. Across the country other indigenous groups set up blockades some of which interrupted rail traffic. Isabelle in examining the connections between these events and the issues frames her inquiry in this way:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>A proposed development. A blocked road. A community divided by band council leadership and traditional forms of governance. A protracted, militarized standoff between armed forces and an Indigenous nation. Massive media coverage. Public outcry. Protests and blockades across the country. The brute force of the state brought to bear on Indigenous peoples.</p>

<p>Aboriginal law and political rhetoric have shifted dramatically in the last thirty years, yet the situation above could be used to described two situations that happened thirty years apart. In this paper, I will compare the Oka Crisis and the Wet’suwet’en land defenders to demonstrate that despite thirty years of law, policy, and international agreements, Indigenous groups in Canada may be no better off than they were in 1990.</p>

<p>Using photographs from the Oka Crisis and the Wet’suwet’en conflict earlier this year, I will highlight the similarities between the two standoffs despite three decades of new Aboriginal law doctrine and a large shift in political rhetoric. I will first explain how the Oka and Wet’suwet’en situations developed and unfolded. Next, I will explore three facets of Canadian law and politics that have changed over the last thirty years, using these examples to illuminate the similarities between the situations, despite the differences in the law. I will explore the duty to consult, the <em>Delgamuukw v British Columbia</em> decision, and public opinion. My analysis demonstrates that while the significant legal developments did not translate to any significant changes on the ground, they served to legitimize the Wet’suwet’en land defenders in the public’s eyes, which resulted in an outpouring of support. Ultimately, my paper argues that public and political opinions have outmatched the pace of Aboriginal law, and that the law needs to be brought up to speed.</p>

<p>In her analysis of the Oka crisis Isabelle examines how the conflict exacerbated tensions between traditional forms of indigenous governments and the Indian Act band council system.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Oka Crisis</h3>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the spring of 1990, Mohawks from Kanehsatà:ke set up a roadblock to stop the expansion of the golf course. It was a peaceful action that caused no inconvenience to the Oka townspeople. However, the mayor and golf course developers grew impatient and, after four months, obtained an injunction from the Quebec Superior Court declaring that the blockade was illegal. Instead of negotiating the removal of the blockade, Ouellete requested that the provincial police force, the Sûreté du Québec (“SQ”), to enforce the injunction.</p>

<p>In response, the Kanehsatà:ke requested support from Ahkwesáhsne and Kahnawà:ke. Members from those communities joined the blockade, and supplies like food, sleeping bags, andother equipment poured in from other Indigenous communities across North America. The mood at the blockade became increasingly more combative, which was not supported by the whole community.</p>

<p>Within the Mohawk communities of Ahkwesáhsne and Kahnawà:ke, there were conflictsbetween the elected band councils and more “traditionalist” Rotiskenhrakete or “Warrior Society” (the “Warriors”), which viewed band councils as an imposition of the colonial state. The Rotiskenhrakete saw the band councils as a form of colonial control, and often used tactics such as barricades and roadblocks to assert Mohawk sovereignty.</p>

<p>Similarly, the blockade occupiers from Kanehsatà:ke differentiated their authority from the federally imposed band council and named themselves the “Longhouse Council” or “People of the Pines”. In this circumstance, both Kanehsatà:ke groups were united in their opposition tothe golf course. However, as the situation escalated, opinions about how to approach the conflict further polarized the parties. The federal government refused to negotiate with the Longhouse Council because it would undermine the elected band council and the authority of the Indian Act,and would recognize a sovereign Mohawk Nation.</p>

<p>On July 11th, 1990, SQ raided the blockade. One hundred tactical force officers in riot gear arrived in trucks, police cars, and a front-end loader.19 After the front-end loader failed to take down the blockade, the officers charged the blockade, armed with assault rifles and concussion grenades. Shots rang out in a cloud of tear gas, and after the dust settled, the SQ discovered that Corporal Marcel Lemay had been fatally shot – whether from friendly fire or theWarriors will never be known. Both sides claim the other shot first, but police and media at thetime claimed that the Warriors were responsible. The SQ retreated, leaving behind police cars and the front-end loader. The Mohawks used the front loader to buttress their barricade usingthe police cars. A 78 day standoff had begun.</p>

<p>Shortly thereafter, the nearby Mercier bridge was blockaded by Kahnawà:ke supporters, forcing around 60,000 commuters to take a two-hour detour. At the Kanehsatà:ke blockade, the SQ set up their own blockade and refused to let people back in once they left. Human rights organizations condemned the province and Canada for attempting to starve the occupiers “into submission”. Quebec’s then minister of “Native Affairs”, John Ciaccia, realized that the issue of land was central, and therefore required federal participation. However, the federal Minister ofIndian Affairs refused to get involved. Eventually, the federal government’s position changed, and they were willing to purchase and transfer the land to the Mohawks. However, talks broke down repeatedly. Facing solidarity blockades across the countries, heightened tensions at Oka, and growing public frustration, the SQ requested help from the Canadian Armed Forces.</p>

<p>The Warriors, with some women and children, eventually retreated to a nearby building called the Treatment Centre. The military engaged in psychological warfare, shining spotlights,making noise, and flying helicopters right over the Centre to intimidate the Warriors. Eventually, the remaining Warriors decided to end the standoff on September 26th. They caught the military entirely by surprise as they exited the barricaded area, resulting in chaos and confusion. Some Warriors managed to escape arrest, while children were forcibly separated fromtheir mothers and handled forcefully. So ended the standoff.</p>

<p>The Oka Crisis has been referred to as an “awakening” in Canadian consciousness about Indigenous peoples and their treatment by the Canadian government. It was certainly a wake-up call for the provincial and federal governments, and there is little doubt that they wanted to prevent this sort of situation from ever happening again. In 1997, the federal government purchased the Pines for the Mohawk community of Kanehsatà:ke. The Oka Crisis also led to the Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples, the largest royal commission ever undertaken in Canada, as well as significant policy reform within Indian Affairs Canada. Perhaps most importantly, however, the Oka Crisis served to remind Canadians, and especially Indigenous nations within Canada, that Indigenous sovereignty was alive and well.</p>
</blockquote>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Wet’suwet’en Land Defenders</h3>

<p>In 2012, the Coastal Gaslink Project (“CGL”) was proposed. It is a natural gas pipeline that is currently under construction in BC. The proposed route starts in Dawson Creek and ends in Kitimat, where the liquid natural gas will be shipped to foreign markets. However, it has garnered extreme controversy and media attention over the last few years. TC Energy, CGL’s developer, started consultations with local First Nations in 2012. In 2014, TC Energy received an environmental assessment certificate from the provincial government with certain conditions. In 2016, TC Energy acquired the remaining construction permits needed for CGL. During the course of consultation, TC Energy came to agreements with twenty elected Indigenous councils along the pipeline route. However, the political situation on the ground mirrors that of the communities of Kanehsatà:ke, Ahkwesáhsne, and Kahnawà:ke. The elected band council does not always see eye to eye with the Wet’suwet’en traditional leadership structure. The Wet’suwet’en’s traditional leadership is comprised of “hereditary leaders,” language that suggests inherited, ancient authority. It should be noted that this explanation does not capture the sophistication and nuance of the Wet’suwet’en leadership structure, but rather gives a broad overview.</p>

<p>The hereditary leaders maintain that while the elected band council has jurisdiction and authority over reserve lands, the hereditary leaders maintain jurisdiction over the much more expansive traditional territory of the Wet’suwet’en. The hereditary leaders yoke their authority to the land. Freda Huson, a hereditary leader, describes the interconnected relationship: “Without our land, we aren’t who we are . . .The land is us and we are the land.” The hereditary chiefs claim that CGL, if constructed, will cause irreparable damage to their lands.</p>

<p>CGL is opposed on political, legal, and environmental grounds. For example, many opponents argue that because most of BC is unceded territory, Wet’suwet’en Aboriginal title over their lands has not been extinguished. All these concerns have bubbled over into escalated scenes of violence on multiple occasions. The Wet’suwet’en have maintained a checkpoint on the Morice West Forest Service Road, the road that enables construction of the pipeline, since 2009. In December 2018, the BC Supreme Court granted an injunction against the land defenders at the blockade. In January 2019, the RCMP raided the blockade and dismantled it, arresting several land defenders. Conjuring a scene right out of July 1990, journalist Amber Bracken described the scene as follows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“Dozens of officers, some equipped in military-style tactical uniforms and carrying semiautomatic rifles, stared across a plywood and barbed wire roadblock as the Wet’suwet’en people and their supporters gathered behind it. A helicopter circled overhead, and police boats were deployed on the pristine Morice River running nearby the protest site.”</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Fourteen land defenders were arrested that day. After the raid, the construction roadwas reopened for limited work such as soil and water testing. However, the blockade was eventually rebuilt by the land defenders after CGL construction workers started clearing treesand building roads. Additional checkpoints along the road were erected to impede CGL workers from working on the pipeline.</p>

<p>Attempted negotiations between the hereditary chiefs and the province were attempted, but broke down. On January 4, 2020, the hereditary chiefs issued an eviction notice to CGL workers. On February 6, 2020 just over one year after the initial raid, following an injunction from Justice Marguerite Church of the BC Supreme Court, the RCMP raided the blockades and violently arrested land defenders again. Solidarity protests sprang up around Canada, includingin Mohawk territory. All rail passenger trains between Toronto, Montreal, and Ottawa, were shut down.Highways all over the country were blocked, and the ports of Delta and Vancouverwere shut down more than once. Across Canada, a death knell rang: “Reconciliation is Dead.”</p>

</figure>

<p>An upside-down Canadian flag with the words “Reconciliation is Dead” painted over it. This phrase became an often-heard chant at demonstrations and sit-ins during early 2020. Image by Chad Hipolito.</p>

<p>Since February, 2020, there have been ongoing talks between the federal and provincial governments and the hereditary chiefs concerning land title and governance. A recent press release from the Gidimten Checkpoint social media account has announced that a Memorandumof Understanding has been reached with both levels of government, but does not address CGL’s construction or the RCMP presence on Wet’seuwet’en lands (yintah). However, CGL construction workers continue to trespass on Wet’seuwet’en territory and the RCMP continue topatrol and harass the land defenders by threatening them with arrest. The land defenders insist that they remain opposed to the project and that the hereditary chiefs’ eviction notice fromJanuary still stands.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Isabelle’s photo-essay poses the question “what has changed in the thirty years spanning Oka and the Wet’suwet’en events”?</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In both the Oka Crisis and the Wet’suwet’en events, Canada was forced to look in the mirror. The scenes of violence portrayed on televisions and more recently, on social media, forced Canadians to reckon with their understanding of their country, which is so often portrayed as an advocate for justice and human rights. Many Canadians in both instances realized that Indigenous peoples have not had that experience. More poignantly, Canadians saw their nation, represented by armed forces, turn against Canadian citizens with violence and coercion.</p>

<p>If these reflections and reckonings occurred thirty years ago, why were Canadians reliving them earlier this year? Why didn’t we learn from the Oka Crisis? The fact is that Canada did learn from the Oka Crisis. As mentioned, the Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples was a product of the Oka Crisis, as well as several other large policy changes within Indian Affairs Canada at the time.However, the lessons we learned were never applied. The Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples was never fully implemented, and questions of land and governance were left to the painfully slow land claims processes or painfully expensive litigation.</p>

<p>In fact, the courts were extremely productive with regards to developing Aboriginal rights after Oka. Starting with the <em>Sparrow</em> decision in 1990, followed by <em>Van Der Peet, Gladstone, Delgamuukw, Haida</em>, and <em>Tsilhqot’in</em> (to name a few), the Supreme Court of Canada(“SCC”) developed the theories of Aboriginal rights and title, slowly but surely.</p>

<p>Indigenous groups could claim practices, customs, or traditions under the “integral to a distinctive culture” framework. After <em>Delgamuukw</em>, Aboriginal title was on the table. After Haida, Indigenous nations were brought to the table before projects started, rather than having to repeatedly apply for injunctions against projects on their lands. For a while, these developments seemed promising. Internationally, there was even more development. The United Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples was developed and ratified in 2007. Legally, Indigenous peoples seemed to be in a better position than ever to vindicate their rights. Why then, did the Wet’suwet’en land defenders need to protect their lands? Why did the RCMP turn up with riot gear and helicopters? How can thirty years of legal developments result in the exact same situation?</p>
</blockquote>

<p>One of the lessons of history that links both the law in relation to Aboriginal and Treaty Rights and Prisoners’ Rights is that legislation and court decisions that are heralded by lawyers as recognition and vindication of these rights do not necessarily result in significant changes in the lives of those whose most affected. I have referred to this as a dissonance between the reality and the rhetoric of the law. In her essay Isabelle explores the significant changes in the jurisprudence of the Supreme Court of Canada establishing in the <em>Haida Nation</em> case a legal duty to consult and in the <em>Delgamuukw</em> case recognizing Aboriginal title as a property right carrying with it the power to make decisions affecting the use of land and why these important cases have failed to affect recognition of the rights of the Wet’suwe’tin hereditary chiefs.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Duty to Consult</h3>

<p>Prior to the modern framework of the duty to consult, developed in 2004 in <em>Haida </em>and<em> Taku River</em>, an application for an interlocutory or interim injunction was one of the few means of stopping unwanted development in Indigenous territory. However, injunction applications were usually unsuccessful because the balance of convenience test often favours economic interests and injunctions are usually an “all-or-nothing” solution because they are rarely granted if the applicants do not have a good chance of stopping the project altogether. These factors usually resulted in Indigenous lands being developed without any input from the Indigenous group affected.</p>

<p>The duty to consult responded to the temporal gap in Canadian law between an Aboriginal right or title claim being proved or settled and the devastation that certain projects wreak on Indigenous lands. The Supreme Court of Canada in <em>Haida</em> held that “[t]he Crown, acting honourably, cannot cavalierly run roughshod over Aboriginal interests where claims affecting these interests are being seriously pursued in the process of treaty negotiation and proof. It must respect these potential, but yet unproven, interests.”</p>

<p>The duty to consult is a constitutional duty grounded in the Honour of the Crown. The purpose of the duty is to advance reconciliation between Indigenous peoples and the Crown, recognizing the pre-existence of rights and the special relationship between the Crown and Indigenous peoples. The duty to consult arises when the Crown has knowledge of the potential existence of an Aboriginal right or title and contemplates conduct that may adversely affect that right or title. The requirements of the duty depend on the strength of the claim and the significance of the impact. It is a spectrum of consultation, ranging from merely letting an Indigenous group know about a planned project, to deep consultation and accommodation.</p>

<p>From the perspective of the Crown, the consultation process ideally results in an agreement about the project in question. These agreements often take the form of impact-benefit agreements, where Indigenous groups accept certain benefits (for example, increased safety measures, changing a proposed route of a pipeline, or financial benefits) in exchange for their acceptance of the project. However, there is no duty to reach an agreement. Where agreementis not reached, groups may pursue litigation to challenge the adequacy of the consultation.</p>

<p>In spite of the good intentions of the duty to consult, the process has many problems, as demonstrated by the vast number of consultation processes that have been brought before the courts. For example, the consultation process is designed by the Crown, which consolidates procedural power in their hands. Indigenous groups are only called to the table when the Crown is contemplating an action, which puts Indigenous groups in a reactive position. There is very little bargaining power available to Indigenous groups, unless the particular nation is politically powerful or wealthy. Compounding this structural inequity is the fact that the Crown determines when the consultation process is complete and the Honour of the Crown has been fulfilled.</p>

<p>Additionally, there are very few checks on the Crown’s power during consultation. The Honour of the Crown requires “honourable” behavior but does not offer much concrete guidance beyond that. The power imbalance perpetuates the relationship of colonial paternalism and undermines the idea that consultation processes are “nation-to-nation” dialogues. Power plays an enormous role in limiting the effectiveness of consultation in terms of the Crown’s resources andability to structure the process as well as the potential vulnerability of Indigenous groups.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Application to Oka and Wet’suwet’en</h3>

<p>The legal duty to consult was not in place in 1990, and the Mohawks were not consulted at any point about the expansion of the golf course. However, if it had existed, the duty to consult may have played a huge role and avoided the Crisis altogether. There can be no doubt that the pre-emptive approach of consultation would have offered more room for fruitful negotiation between the parties. The duty to consult allows Indigenous peoples to have some input in the projects that impact them, and their input no doubt increases the scientific, social, environmental, and democratic rigour of proposed projects. Theoretically, the duty to consult is avast improvement from the options that the Mohawks had in front of them in 1990: court or blockade. Why, then, did the Wet’suwet’en end up in almost the same situation?</p>

<p>In the case of CGL, the elected band council was the party that was consulted with about the project. The duty to consult did not prevent conflict in this situation because the colonial imposition of the band council structure prevented the hereditary chiefs from negotiating and expressing their deep concerns about the project’s impacts on their yintah (lands). Therefore, the duty to consult – this very significant legal doctrine that has been in place for over fifteen years –did not make any difference.</p>

<p>Despite the development of an entire doctrine aimed at eliminating the need to apply for injunctions and protests, the Wet’suwet’en ended up in almost the exact same position as the Mohawks in the Oka Crisis. This monumental failure can be attributed to the Canadian government’s refusal to address the political divisions that elected band councils create within Indigenous communities. This problem was present during the Oka Crisis, and it remains today. The Wet’suwet’en land defenders have demonstrated how the duty to consult cannot legitimately be carried out with only certain representatives of a community: it only serves to entrench political division. Hereditary chiefs under the Wet’suwet’en system serve a different function than the band council, and the Crown should take both functions into account. The duty to consult should enjoin a plurality of perspectives to achieve the best result possible. Plurality informs the spirit of democracy and Canada’s vision of its rich mosaic. Indigenous groups reflectt hat rich mosaic, and the duty to consult must acknowledge it.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Supreme Court of Canada decision in <em>Delgamuukw</em>, in which I was co- counsel, is one of the most important decisions of the 20th century. Hailed as a victory for Indigenous people the photo-essay explains why for the Wet’suwet’en Hereditary chiefs in whose name, along with Gitksan hereditary chiefs, the case was brought, the legal victory proved hollow:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><em>Delgamuukw</em> was the result of a decades-long attempt to stop clear cutting in Wet’suwet’en and Gitxsan territories. The case was originally filed in 1984, and took thirteen years to reach the SCC. <em>Delgamuukw</em> was a significant development in Canadian law. When the lawyers behind the legal action brought the case in the 1980s, there was no clear theory informing Aboriginal title. At trial Chief Justice McEachern rejected the use of Indigenous oral laws as admissible evidence and found that Wet’suwet’en and Gitxsan title had been extinguished. The judgement’s tone towards Indigenous peoples was extremely dismissive and intolerant.</p>

<p>After the loss at the BC Supreme Court, the legal team for the Wet’suwet’en and Gitxsan appealed the BC Court of Appeal, and then again to the SCC. The SCC[ based on errors in the trial judgment] ordered a new trial. However, the SCC also ruled that Aboriginal title had not been extinguished by the province. This was not necessarily an outright victory, but it was not a loss, either. The SCC recommended that instead of further litigation, the parties should enter negotiations to resolve the claims.</p>

<p>Twenty-three years later, the claim remains unresolved. Twenty-three years later, those same parties are locked in a dispute that has caught the world’s attention. In this particular standoff between an Indigenous nation and the Canadian state, the Wet’suwet’en, unlike the Mohawks, have a SCC decision that supports their view that their title to land has not been extinguished. As we have seen, however, having a legal case that supported their claim to the territory did not help much.</p>

<p><em>Delgamuukw</em> did not help stem conflict over CGL because it did not create certainty for either party. No doubt the Wet’suwet’en feel that <em>Delgamuukw</em> supports their Aboriginal title claim unequivocally. Similarly, the provincial and federal governments likely feel that the SCC’schoice to order a new trial conclusively supports their position that while the land may be unceded, it is nevertheless vested in the Crown. The land lies in interpretive limbo.</p>

<p>Regardless, it cannot be denied that the provincial and federal governments have failed torecognize inherent Aboriginal rights and title as they have often promised. This failure to recognize inherent Aboriginal rights and title was central during the Oka Crisis and is central to the CGL dispute. The Wet’suwet’en and Gitxsan spent millions of dollars bringing their claims to court, and still weren’t fully successful. If courts will not recognize claims, and land claim negotiations are untenable, who can blame the Wet’suwet’en for taking matters into their own hands?</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The last part of Isabelle’s essay locates the Oka and Wet’suwet’en conflicts within the lens of public opinion and the influence of social media:</p>

<p>Left image shows youth protestors in Victoria in 2020 (Kevin Light). Right image shows Mohawk supporters in 1990 (Sebastien St-Jean).</p>

<p>When <em>Delgamuukw</em> came down from the SCC in 1997, the law was likely more progressive than public opinion concerning Indigenous peoples. The contrast between the SCC’s decision and the trial judge’s opinion in <em>Delgamuukw</em>, which used offensive language to describe pre-contact Indigenous societies and rejected oral laws as admissible historical evidence, is a stark example of the ignorance of many Canadians. Indigenous peoples were not discussed much in politics, and did not make it into political platform promises as they do now.</p>

<p>In contrast, public opinion these days, for the most part, has outpaced the development ofAboriginal law. That is not to say that all Canadians are enlightened to the oppression that Indigenous peoples face at the hands of the Canadian state – racism, stereotyping, and bigotry are still very much alive and part of the Canadian psyche.</p>

<p>However, the events of the last decades have wrought changes in many Canadian minds. For example, the Truth and Reconciliation Commission informed many Canadians about the atrocities of residential schools. Many elementary, high school, and post-secondary curriculums in BC and other provinces require their students to learn about Indigenous peoples, their cultures, and the lasting effects colonialism continues to have.</p>

<p>This shift in public awareness has been coupled with progressive political rhetoric that touts reconciliation between the state and Indigenous peoples, and the emergence of legal recognitions of Aboriginal rights and title. These three developments have allowed many Canadians to perceive the injustices being wrought by the Canadian state on Indigenous peoples.Many Canadians now recognize that Indigenous peoples have the right to govern their lands and use their own legal systems. Those Canadians have been awakened to these truths and are callingfor change.</p>

<p>These calls for change have perhaps never been louder than they were during the months of January and February this year. The Wet’suwet’en land defenders enjoyed widespread support, as mentioned above. There were massive protests across the country and globally, calling for the RCMP and CGL to pull out of the yintah.</p>

<p>Social media also had a significant role to play in this national outcry. Social media platforms like Facebook allowed supporters across the country to call snap demonstrations, communicate quickly, and most importantly, broadcast violent arrests of land defenders by RCMP in real time. With social media, people did not even need to rely on the media to report what was going on. The land defenders themselves were broadcasting these arrests and provocations from their own phones. I myself watched multiple people being brutally arrested by the RCMP on at the Gidimten Checkpoint in real time. I was shaken. It was painful to watch. </p>

<p>Additionally, the land defenders published press releases every day, which allowed people to keep abreast of the situation from their perspective. This tactic was used successfully by the Mohawks during the Oka Crisis as well, though. advances in technology have made the technique even more effective today This easy access led to widespread settler support of Wet’suwet’en, more so than the Mohawks enjoyed in 1990.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Isabelle’s photo-essay concludes on a perspective of the state of the law and its relationship to public opinion which is both realistic yet hopeful for a future that can outdistance our colonial heritage:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>My analysis of the Oka Crisis and the Wet’suwet’en conflict is not meant to suggest that Canadians have recently achieved enlightenment or grasped some higher truth that eschews racism and embraces Indigenous sovereignty. Rather, I hope to show that despite the systemic racism and colonialism that pervade our society and history, many Canadians have developed anawareness and understanding about Indigenous peoples and their relationship with the Canadian state. I believe part of this understanding stems from the legal developments we have seen in the past thirty years, which include legitimizing Indigenous opinions through the duty to consult (ina limited way) and validating their rights and title claims in courts (again, in a limited way).</p>

<p>The comparison of the Oka Crisis with the Wet’suwet’en land defenders illustrate that these legal developments have translated in a very pale imitation of what was once imagined. However, other Canadians have picked up on this fact. In a crystallization of Canadian liberalism, other Canadians have realized that Indigenous peoples have rights, and that those rights are not being fulfilled. Those Canadians are simply asking for the same thing that they themselves receive.</p>

<p>The outpouring of public support of the Wet’suwet’en land defenders this year prompts me to argue that public opinion has outpaced the development of Aboriginal law. Aboriginal law and the duty to consult have serious limitations, and Canadians, Indigenous and non- Indigenous,are calling for improvements. Perhaps the picture of Aboriginal law will look different thirty years from now.</p>
