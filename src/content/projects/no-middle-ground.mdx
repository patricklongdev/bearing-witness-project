<p>The issues of consultation and accommodation and the challenges the Canadian legal system has in recognizing Indigenous peoples’ distinctive relationship with land is the subject of Gabriel Boothroyd-Roberts remarkable text, audio and video montage “No Middle Ground”. Gabriel’s essay at first glance looks like the conventional law review ‘Case Comment’ of a Supreme court judgment. It is, however, a much more elegant and multi-dimensional analysis. Gabriel sets the context of his project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the early 1990s, proponents began seeking regulatory approval for a new ski resort in the Jumbo Valley in southeastern British Columbia (BC). As part of this process, consultations began with First Nations in the area, including the Shuswap, who eventually supported the project, and the Ktunaxa. The Ktunaxa engaged in numerous consultations with government and project proponents but, nevertheless, expressed the conviction that the ski resort could not proceed as it would cause irreparable harm to the Grizzly Bear Spirit that lives in Qat’muk, where the resort was planned. In 2010, the Ktunaxa issued the Qat’muk Declaration, which expresses the Ktunaxa’s views regarding the future of the area, including that no permanent structures can be built within the area. In 2012, BC’s Minister of Forests, Lands and Natural Resource Operations approved the ski resort project and, subsequently, the Ktunaxa sought judicial review of that decision, alleging that it infringed their section 2(a) right to freedom of religion under the Charter of Rights and Freedoms and, due to insufficient consultation and accommodation, infringed their aboriginal rights under section 35.</p>

<p>After the BC Supreme Court and Court of Appeal ruled against them, the Ktunaxa appealed to the SCC. The majority, in a decision written by Justices McLachlin and Rowe, ruled that the Minister’s decision did not infringe the Ktunaxa’s section 2(a) Charter rights because it is only practices and beliefs that are protected, not the objects of those beliefs. Regarding the section 35 claim, the Court unanimously decided that the Minister’s conclusion he had adequately consulted and accommodated the Ktunaxa was reasonable, with the majority citing the length of consultations, the fact that “[m]any changes had been made to the project to accommodate the Ktunaxa’s spiritual claims”, and the Ktunaxa’s position that they could not accept construction of the resort, which made further consultation futile.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Gabriel, an accomplished musician, explains the multi-dimensional and multi- media scope of the analysis of this case is explained in this way:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the piece “No Middle Ground”, I explore the Supreme Court of Canada (SCC) decision in <em>Ktunaxa v British Columbia (Forests, Lands and Natural Resource Operations)</em>. Using audio from the SCC hearing of the appeal, visual montage, and original music, I reframe the Court’s descriptions of the Ktunaxa’s so-called “no middle ground” position, arguing they are more apposite for describing the colonial approach to Indigenous land. In the artistic work I have intentionally taken the judge’s words out of context and provided them with a meaning that was not originally intended. In this paper, I take a critical approach to the Court’s actual reasoning, as displayed by the written decision and by the judge’s statements during the appeal hearing. In the sections below, I introduce the case and then briefly discuss the Court’s resolution of the Ktunaxa’s Charter claim. I then focus on the issue of accommodation, showing how the decision displays apparent contradictions between the Ktunaxa’s purported stance that accommodation was impossible, the Court’s recognition of the accommodation the Ktunaxa actually sought, and the assertion that accommodation had already occurred. Next, I briefly consider a possible basis for an Indigenous-led approach to consultation and accommodation.</p>

<p>My intent with this piece was to place the words of the Justices in Ktunaxa in a new context, showing how these words more accurately describe the settler colonial approach to Indigenous land rather than the position of the Ktunaxa. To this end, I combined audio and a few brief visual clips of judges’ statements from the appeal hearing along with footage showing aspects of the inexorable process of settler colonialism that has continued to use and abuse Indigenous lands, processes that deny the possibility of accommodation. For example, Justice Moldaver’s statement that “there could not be any middle ground here, there could not be any accommodation” and Chief Justice McLachlin’s statement that “other interests are out of the picture” seems far more apt for describing this process of ongoing primitive accumulation, rather than the position of the Ktunaxa.</p>

<p>Beyond simply being visual materials that assist in demonstrating this point, many of the videos that I have used are themselves political statements intended to promote or justify resource extraction, urbanization, and other forms of colonial use of land. My intent was to recontextualize both the judges’ words and these political messages, juxtaposing them together to tell a story radically different than either intended to tell. To this end, I have used layered visuals, combining multiple scenes of logging old growth forests, open pit coal mining, ski chalet construction, condominium towers, dam building, and other symbols of Canadian sovereignty to show the diverse, yet interconnected, pathways through which these processes take place. Most of the footage is derived from activities taking place in BC. While I have incorporated historical and modern footage, I have taken a broadly non- chronological approach, combining these time periods in short succession in an effort to show the very brief history of colonialism on this land.</p>

<p>“No Middle Ground” is the first video I have edited and I was unfamiliar with the lexicon of art criticism that might describe my method. Since creating the piece, I have discovered that it falls into the category of “found footage filmmaking”, which “refers to the practice of appropriating pre-existing film footage in order to denature, detourn or recontextualize images by inscribing new meanings onto materials through creative montage.” Film studies professor Paul Arthur describes the history of this form of filmmaking, which dates from the 1920s, one tendency of which involves the “recalibration or inversion of scenes culled from ‘official’ newsreels and more marginal materials”. These descriptions aptly describe the intent of my efforts.</p>

<p>While the visuals consist of found footage, the music is my own. Aside from the statements of SCC judges from the Ktunaxa hearing, I have composed and performed on guitar, keyboards, bass, and programmed drums all other sounds heard in the piece. My intent was to convey a sense of foreboding related to the unsustainability of the relationship to land and Indigenous peoples conveyed in the visuals and in the judges’ recontextualized words. The use of sudden crescendos denotes the speed and forcefulness of these processes while otherworldly sounds transmit their foreignness on this land.</p>

<p>I end the piece with Justice Moldaver’s words “it’s vital to everything we are” echoing into background with multiple simultaneous images that recall the earlier scenes of colonial land use. Justice Moldaver made this statement in describing the position of the Ktunaxa, declaring that such a position made it impossible to engage in a balancing of multiple interests. I argue here that the statement “vital to everything we are” could well be describing the colonial state and its insatiable need to put more land to a particular form of economic use. In my view, justly balancing Indigenous and non-Indigenous interests is impossible while such a relationship with land continues to be privileged by the courts, governments, and society at large.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Gabriel’s essay grapples with the problem the Supreme Court had in its characterization of the Ktunaxa’s argument seeking protection of their sacred site:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the majority’s written decision, it is clear that the Ktunaxa’s refusal to accept construction of the ski resort in any form was perceived by the judges as an intransigent position, with the Ktunaxa described as making an “absolute claim” and adopting an “absolute position” that involved “no middle ground”. A review of the appeal hearing particularly highlights the focus on this aspect of the case, which was notable for its sheer frequency: combined Justices McLachlin, Abella, and Moldaver stated “no middle ground” seven times during the one hour of submissions from Ktunaxa’s counsel, and made many other similar references to the Ktunaxa’s staunch opposition to the project. The judges also made it clear that this purportedly obstinate position of the Ktunaxa was a central barrier to resolving the appeal in their favour, both in regard to the Section 35 and the freedom of religion claim. Beyond its potential relevance to a balancing under section 1, McLachlin indicated that the Ktunaxa claim to an “absolute right” was inconsistent with the Charter and, therefore, the court should not recognize the claim:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>If you have a sacred site, you have a sacred site and that’s it… other interests are out of the picture if we extend 2(a) to protect a sacred site… And wherever I go in this case I keep coming back to the ‘no middle ground’ and how that fits in with the Charter or even section 35 because all of those are built and premised, the section 2(a) right is premised, on the possibility of balancing with other rights…</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This reasoning was not mentioned in the majority decision, which resolves the section 2(a) question by stating that the Charter protects the freedom “to hold a belief and to manifest that belief” and not “the spiritual focal point of worship” without evincing concern over the creation of an “absolute right”.</p>

<p>…A major factor that, I think, underlies the Court’s decision was the way in which the Ktunaxa relate to Qat’muk. If this area were being “used” by the Ktunaxa in a way cognizable to the judges, such as for habitation or resource extraction, the government and the courts would very likely have treated the Ktunaxa’s claim and asserted rights differently. The lack of recognition for the Ktunaxa’s relationship with Qat’muk can, in my view, be understood as part of the long-standing colonial approach to Indigenous land. Land uses that do not conform to Western norms are seen as inherently illegitimate. The Court’s interpretation of consultation and accommodation is, in my view, related to this fundamental idea regarding land and an unwillingness to hinder land-based economic development. As Dene scholar Glen Coulthard writes, “in the Canadian context, colonial domination continues to be structurally committed to maintain—through force, fraud, and more recently, so- called ‘negotiations’—ongoing state access to the land and resources that contradictorily provide the material and spiritual sustenance of Indigenous societies on the one hand, and the foundation of colonial state-formation, settlement, and capitalist development on the other.” Though there are, of course, individual court decisions that prioritize Indigenous land interests over resource extraction to some extent, the continuing social structure and political economy of Canada deeply depends on the containment of Indigenous rights within firm limits.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Gabriel exposes the limited lens through which the Supreme Court viewed the duty to consult and accommodate:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The duty to consult and accommodate Indigenous peoples whose “unproven” rights may be infringed was established in <em>Haida Nation v. British Columbia (Minister of Forests)</em>. In that decision, the Court described accommodation as a “process of balancing interests, of give and take” and as “seeking compromise in an attempt to harmonize conflicting interests and move further down the path of reconciliation”. Quoting Haida Nation, the Ktunaxa decision repeatedly describes the process of accommodation as one of “give and take”. The judges, however, in their statements at the appeal hearing and in their written decision, display a narrow understanding of the scope of this process. The Court seems to focus solely on the ski resort proposal and ignore the wider context in which the project would take place. Within this limited frame of reference, the judges state that “many accommodations were made with respect to Ktunaxa spiritual concern, based on a realignment and resizing of the proposed resort, the creation of a wildlife management plan, and access rights for the Ktunaxa. Conversely, the Ktunaxa’s rejection of the proposal was characterized as “an uncompromising position” demonstrating an attitude of “give us everything we want”.</p>

<p>Seen in any reasonable context, the notion that the Crown has significantly accommodated the Ktunaxa while the Ktunaxa have adopted “an uncompromising position” is, I would argue, absurd. The Ktunaxa’s factum points out that the “Ktunaxa are not generally opposed to development and have worked with the Crown and its Ministries in a collaborative and open manner, engaging in land and resource management and development throughout the Ktunaxa territory.” In the appeal hearing, Ktunaxa’s counsel pointed out that the Ktunaxa have taken no issue with heli-skiing and other activities within Qat’muk and have not opposed Panorama Mountain Resort, which lies on the outskirts of the area. The Court, in its decision, did not address this point, or consider in any other way the larger context of the Ktunaxa’s relationship to the Crown. Described in terms of the “give and take” called for by Haida Nation, the Crown has “taken” Ktunaxa sovereignty, territory, and culture, and the Ktunaxa have (forcibly) “given” these all to the Crown. One such “taking” by the Crown specifically identified in the Ktunaxa’s factum was the damming of the Columbia river, which extirpated salmon from their territory and had a devastating effect on their community. An intervener makes a similar point regarding the necessity of context in considering the Ktunaxa’s Charter rights, arguing that the “ongoing, cumulative effects of past government conduct” must be considered by the Court.</p>

<p>Even within the limited frame that informs the Court’s understanding of accommodation, there seems to be some confusion about what the concept means. The majority decision states, "the Ktunaxa issued the Qat’muk Declaration in November 2010 declaring that no accommodation was possible." However, while the declaration states that permanent structures in Qat’muk are unacceptable, it also “invite[s] other governments and stakeholders to participate in developing a management plan for Qat’muk” and states that the area will continue to be shared with "non-Ktunaxa when such use is respectful of Ktunaxa spiritual values”. The Declaration makes no reference to the impossibility of accommodation. In addition, Ktunaxa’s factum in the SCC appeal did not argue that no accommodation was possible but, rather, that the Minister had not provided “accommodation to protect the Ktunaxa ability to exercise spiritual practices which rely on a sacred site and require its protection.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>“No Middle Ground” points the pathway towards real accommodation, paradoxically with reference to the Supreme Court’s own decision in the Tsilhqot’in case:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>One way in which a court could attend to the Indigenous perspective is by looking to Indigenous efforts to achieve reconciliation. While the Court in Ktunaxa characterized the Qat’muk Declaration as precluding the possibility of accommodation, it could be seen as a pathway to Indigenous-led consultation and accommodation in its call for collaboration and a balancing of interests. Given the power imbalance between the Crown and Indigenous peoples and the history of colonial domination, processes of reconciliation may require ceding significant control to Indigenous people. In fact, the SCC’s strongest declaration of Indigenous territorial rights, in its finding of aboriginal title in <em>Tsilhqot’in Nation v. British Columbia</em>, has begun negotiations that seem more likely to generate true reconciliation than the duty to consult and accommodate. Since that decision, the Tsilhqot’in Nation and the BC provincial government have agreed to the Nenqay Deni Accord, which sets forth a “comprehensive framework for longer-term negotiations to reconcile the rights, interests and goals of the Tsilhqot’in Nation and British Columbia in Tsilhqot’in Territory” and states that “the Parties will avoid adversarial positions in their engagement with each other and jointly address challenges and achieve progress through respectful engagement, mutual understanding and a spirit of collaboration.” Yet, the Court cannot see that the Ktunaxa’s own declaration of territorial rights could lead to such a process of collaboration involving mutual respect. Instead, the Ktunaxa are characterized as taking a position of “give us everything we want”.</p>
</blockquote>
