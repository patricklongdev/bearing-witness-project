<p>Andrew Guaglio, who in addition to being a student in the 2013 Penal Policy seminar was also engaged in the Law Schools Innocence Project, created a set of Comic storyboards and completed a comic strip titled “Innocence and Imprisonment”. These illustrated David Milgaard’s harrowing 23 years imprisonment for a 1969 murder of which he always claimed to be innocent but of which he was only fully exonerated in 1997. As Andrew explains in his accompanying essay the comic “highlights some of the injustices suffered by those in prison and the heightened impact of these injustices on the wrongfully convicted.”</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the comic Imprisonment &amp; Innocence, I have attempted to address several of the themes that were covered throughout the course Penal Policy, as well as several prison-related issues that are particularly relevant to wrongfully convicted individuals. I have had a long-standing interest in the treatment of persons in prison and wrongful convictions. With this project I hoped to highlight some of the injustices suffered by those in prison and the heightened impact of these injustices on the wrongfully convicted.</p>

<p>The central themes of the work are the negative institutional consequences, and the added physical and psychological suffering that are suffered by imprisoned, innocent individuals for failing to "admit" to their crimes. Negative institutional consequences was a theme that presented repeatedly throughout my research into the story of David Milgaard and one that I have encountered in many other cases through my work with the Innocence Project. In the words of Mr. Milgaard:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Keeping a person in prison until he is forced to tell a lie by incriminating himself is a form of torture. With no end in sight, the torment goes on forever; you never know when you can expect to be released. But only when the lie "Yes, I did it!" has been uttered can the whole system of justice, from police to judges, give a big sigh of relief and say "We knew it!" The reward held out to me if I chose to lie? Freedom! My punishment for being innocent, honest, for sticking to my principles and not lying? Almost 23 years of prison time! This situation is very, very wrong … But that's the kind of justice and penal system we have in Canada. Your Canada.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The parole prerequisite of making an admission of guilt has put many wrongfully convicted individuals in the catch-22 situation described by Mr. Milgaard. It leaves the wrongfully convicted person with an impossible choice: admit to a crime you did not commit - which may be an utterly reprehensible offence, as in the case of the murder of Gail Miller - or languish in the torture-like conditions of prison for an indeterminate period. While there is a legitimate societal interest in requiring true criminals to acknowledge responsibility for their actions, the study of wrongful convictions has demonstrated that this requirement cannot exist without exception. I suggest that a more tailored approach would allow for a relaxation of this requirement where an individual has consistently maintained his innocence throughout his incarceration and where some of the various factors that commonly lead to wrongful conviction are present in the case.</p>

<p>With respect to the physical safety consequences suffered by the wrongfully convicted, refusal to acknowledge guilt can also lead to animosity from both correctional staff and other inmates. As referenced in the comic, Milgaard reported suffering repeated physical abuse (he also suffered sexual abuse) in his first few years, both as the result of his refusal to acknowledge guilt and the nature of the crime for which he was convicted.</p>

<p>The last major theme that I sought to emphasize was the psychological impact of incarceration, which in all likelihood, is exacerbated for those who are wrongfully convicted. David Milgaard identified the reason for the added psychological consequences as follows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>It is horrible to spend time in prison for a crime you did not commit. Think of it: prison sentences are the worst punishment Canada has been able to come up with for the worst criminals committing the worst crimes. In the case of someone serving time in prison for something he or she did not do, the "punishment" is torture.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>As depicted in my comic, the psychological strain on Mr. Milgaard was so severe that he attempted suicide on at least four occasions, in several instances by gruesome means. He was also hospitalized numerous times and given a plethora of diagnoses, including: schizoid personality disorder, psychopathic personality type, manic depressive disorder, schizophrenia, personality disorder, sociopathic personality disorder, character disorder with strong antisocial features, and major affective disorder. Undoubtedly, many of these diagnoses resulted from psychiatric assessments founded upon the assumption that Mr Milgaard had murdered Gail Miller and was unwilling or unable to acknowledge responsibility for the crime. As a result of these psychological evaluations, Mr. Milgaard was put on a varied assortment of psychoactive medications, an issue raised repeatedly during our class discussions.</p>

<p>Sadly, the psychological impact of incarceration of David Milgaard was also used by the Parole Board as justification for the denial of parole. As noted by Carl Karp and Cecil Rosner in When Justice Fails: The David Milgaard Story, it is somewhat ridiculous that the negative impacts of incarceration can be used to refuse release, as it creates a catch-22 situation.</p>

<p>Beyond these central themes, I also attempted to make reference to, inter alia: the generally antagonistic relationship between correctional staff and prisoners; the stark conditions of Canadian prisons; the impact of involuntary transfer; and police tunnel vision in high profile crimes.</p>

<p>To create the comic Imprisonment &amp; Innocence, I used compressed white and black charcoal on coloured pulp paper. I employed a combination of sketching, rubbing, and erasing techniques to create the final product. The dialogue and narration in the comic are based upon David Milgaard's writings and interviews, as well as other archival materials, including reports of the CBC and scholarly writings on his case. All sources for dialogue are recorded below in the references section. I also based many of the images on archival photos and video of David Milgaard during his incarceration. I did so in the hope of representing the experience of wrongful l conviction and imprisonment as accurately as possible.</p>
