<p>Mohammed Veyaghan for the 2015 Penal Policy seminar presented a painting accompanied by a prison journal, titled “Notes from the Prison Underground”. The journal is based upon the life histories of some of the prisoners who I had interviewed for my book <em>Justice behind the Walls</em>. Linked to his journal Mohammad painted his protagonist looking back at the end of 19 years imprisonment.</p>

<p>These are some of the entries in the journal:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><strong>January 1, 1996</strong><br />I got 19 years and 348 days to go. It's going to be hell. I'm 27 now. I' II be 47 when I get out. God doesn't exist. If he does, he never showed himself to me.</p>

<p><strong>April 10 1996</strong><br />Today is my first day back from segregation. Segregation makes you bitter. You try to mentally escape but you can't. When you are in the hole you are learning nothing. You are detached from everything. At least back in general population I was learning something. I was going to school, getting focused, and trying to deal with some of my problems. In segregation you only learn to hate. The prison underground is hate.</p>

<p>When you are in segregation the time stops and you feel hopeless. I've experienced emotional and mental isolation before. That's what I experienced for much of my time in juvie and my ins and outs out of jail since my early 20s. But segregation brings about physical isolation and that is caustic on the mind. There is a twenty-three hour lock up and your brain slowly gets shattered. You start thinking you have lost your tongue and believe that you are mute. You don't even think you are on the same planet as everyone else. At nights you scream but no one cares. One time I played dead on the floor of my cell for an entire day. I knew that the guards noticed me lying there face down and motionless, but none of them cared. You wonder why they are doing this to you. I am a human after all. This shouldn't be happening. What did I do to deserve this?</p>

<p>Eventually, you ask yourself why this supposedly wonderful society expects me to be well­ behaved when this is how they treat me. There is no justice behind these walls - there is only human cruelty in its most savage form.</p>

<p><strong>June 14, 1996</strong><br />In prison, there is a huge disconnect between what the law is and how the law is actually applied. The other day I was reading Kent's Mission Statement that should supposedly guide how inmates are to be treated. It talks about balancing control and assistance. That sounds reasonable to me. A lot of these guys did something wrong and there is a need to prevent them from escaping. But the Mission Statement also says that they should get assistance in order to rehabilitate and learn to cope with their internal problems. But in reality there is none of that.<br />I've yet to see the hand of assistance. Whenever there is a problem, their first response is always to go to ·war. Just like how they treated me in my first week here at Kent: when I was supposedly disrespectful in my language to an officer, he returned with five guards and they beat me and threw me in the hole for seven days. There is no assistance here, it's only a "break-you-down" attitude. They want to strip you of your human identity. They want to break you. They want you to be a dog. But I'm a man! I will not break. Sometimes, l see nothing but red. I have these visions and I see the walls bleed red. I think I am going insane. I think there is something wrong with my kidneys. I feel hopeless. My captors aren't human, because if they were they would have compassion. I don't know if writing this journal is helping me. I think it makes me realize how. worthless I've become. I like to see myself bleed. It reminds me that I can die.</p>

<p><strong>November 4, 1996</strong><br />They put me in segregation for 90 days for getting into an altercation with one of the guards. He wanted to take my tattoo ink and I got angry. I just couldn't take it anymore. They are always harassing us. I didn't actually fight the guard, but in a moment of rage I pushed his hand as he was trying to close my cell. They later said that I pushed the officer against the wall and was trying to lock him inside the cell with me. When the incident happened I knew he was going to come back with five or six other guards to gas me and take me to the hole. At that point I had about 5 minutes before they came. I didn't want to give them the satisfaction of destroying my family pictures as they had done before. I immediately ripped them up and flushed them down the toilet. I didn't want to give them the opportunity to hurt me where I was most vulnerable- in this very dark way, I felt a supreme sense of gratification. Also, by destroying those photographs I was stripping myself of my human identity.</p>

<p>After all, I needed to get ready to play the role of an animal locked up in a cage. I wanted to get ready for what was about to come. And then, in that moment I had one of my visions. This time though the walls didn't bleed red. But they turned invisible and the light emitting from outside became brighter than I had ever seen it. Then my kidneys started hurting again. I think I was getting ready to die.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">19 YEARS LATER:</h3>

<p><strong>April 21, 2015</strong><br />I haven't written in my journal now for some 19 years. So much time has passed since then. My hair has grown long and now it's grey. I feel so much older than what I am. I was sitting on my bed and reading this little red journal of mine the other day and it made me smile - the feeling was cathartic. As the bright light from outside was shining into my dark cell, I realized that so much of what I wrote back then is still true today. In fact, in many ways it has gotten worse. Inmates are denied more of their rights, still treated as sub-humans, and locked up in segregation for longer periods without just cause. In this environment only hopelessness and defeat thrives. In the end, no one really cares. I'm glad I stopped writing back then; there was never anything nice to write.</p>

<p>I guess the reason I'm writing tonight is because my twenty year sentence will be coming to an end soon. I'll be released from Matsqui in a few months. I can almost taste freedom. But I'm scared of the outside. The scar of prison will always stay with me. I've been state raised behind the prison bars since I've been 15. I doubt I'm going to fit in when I get out.</p>

<p>A few weeks back I was reading my files. One thing that I noticed is that regardless of which institution they sent me, I was always judged in the negative. The reports were always about what I did wrong. Never once did they write something positive about my character. They never talked about how in prison I took up painting. They never talked about how I tried to help newcomers cope with prison life. They never mentioned that before I came to prison I would give my money to the homeless so they could buy food. They never mentioned any of that because they never looked there. They only wanted to paint me as this evil being that lacks all the qualities of humanity. That is what they want. But I'm going to persevere. When I get out, I will be strong. I won't let them bring me down. I'm a fighter. I've been fighting all my life. God, can you now show yourself to me?</p>
</blockquote>
