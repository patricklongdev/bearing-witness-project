<p>Heather Sorensen’s presentation, on the final day of the 2020 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar, was a memorable demonstration of the recovery and celebration of Indigenous identity and the power of language and art. Heather read to the seminar, in Cree, part of her essay which explained what her painting of the Bear- Maskwa - represented.</p>

<p>Heather’s family and community history reflects and documents the wheels of colonialism grinding Indigenous identity to the brink of legal extinction. Based on oral histories and traditional knowledge from elders and family members Heather provided this history:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Loss of culture is something all indigenous peoples have faced as a result of colonialism; my band is no different. We are unique, however, in that our loss of culture was augmented by the complete enfranchisement of our band, the Michel First Nation, in 1958. Prior to this, the band signed on to Treaty 6 by way of an adhesion in 1878. As a party to Treaty 6, the band was entitled to agriculture equipment, among other things. Despite this, in the early 1900s, the band was pressured to surrender parts of its reserve in exchange for the promised agricultural equipment. Starting in the 1920s many individual members of the band began to enfranchise in order to access the rights and freedoms afforded to non-indigenous Canadians. This became increasingly necessary for many members because the reserve lands were constantly shrinking and agricultural subsistence became virtually impossible. Without a means to support and feed their families, many members were forced to enfranchise. Many members also choose to enfranchise as a way to avoid having their children forced to attend residential school.</p>

<p>By 1958, with a large number of band members already enfranchised, and the reserve lands constantly shrinking, the entire Michel band was enfranchised. This meant that the band and reserve were dissolved, and small parcels were allocated to each enfranchised adult memberof the band. This was not done my means of taking scrip, instead it was meant to serve as a model for the federal government to assimilate first nations across the country, making Michel First Nation quite unique. Fortunately, this model did not take form, and Michel has been the only band to enfranchise in such a way. One of the unfortunate results of enfranchisement has been the loss of individual status for Michel people. Slowly, many individuals have been able to regain their Indian status following the passage of Bill C-31 and later following the <em>McIvor</em> &amp; <em>Descheneaux</em> Supreme Court decisions. To this day, however, the Michel band is not recognizedby the Government of Canada. There remains one individual member who kept his originally allocated parcel of land on the old reserve. The rest of the old Michel reserve land has been sold and developed. Lacking a land base, the rest of the remaining Michel members and descendants are largely scattered across western Canada.</p>

<p>In addition to the band enfranchisement, the Michel band was also subject to the same colonial policies that other first nations endured. Many members of the Michel First Nation were forced to attend residential schools, including my maternal grandmother &amp; great grandmother.</p>

<p>The intergenerational effects of this are apparent within my family, including a loss of culture and language. My Grandmother and her siblings retained their Cree language abilities despite their schooling, however, they did not pass this to my mother’s generation. My family has also been affected by the missing and murdered indigenous women and girls crisis. One of my aunties went missing in the 1980s on Vancouver’s downtown east side, joining a group of vulnerable, often indigenous women that went missing in that region over a span of several decades. In addition, my older half-sister was a victim of the sixties scoop after being adopted to a non- indigenous family as a newborn.</p>

<p>Another unfortunate result of our band’s enfranchisement and other colonial policies we’ve endured has been lateral violence. Within my own family this has been as issue. Within the band more broadly, lateral violence has become a major issue in recent years. As the legal &amp; political landscapes have changed over the past few decades, and reconciliation more widely accepted, the Michel First Nation has sought band reinstatement. This has caused a divide withinour nation largely due to the issue of Indian status. While many members have been able to regain their individual status, there are many descendants who are unable to either because their ancestors took scrip or because their situations do not fit within what is recognized in the Indian Act.</p>

<p>Consequently, there is division amongst the band about how best to proceed to rectify what happened to the Michel band back in 1958. We also lack organization, leadership and financial resources. Despite having the opportunity in 2017 to begin discussions with the federal government on how to proceed with band reinstatement, we have been unable to made any progress because of the deep divide amongst our people. Without a unified voice representing the band, the government is unwilling to proceed with these discussions. It seems until we as a people come together, we will be unable to move forward.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Against this historical background Heather described in Cree and then English her own journey to healing and celebrating her Cree identity. But first she explained the importance of using the Cree language:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I have chosen to write the portion of this paper that explains the meaning behind my painting in Cree language. The rough translation to English is also provided for reference. Writing in Cree is a way for me to honour an important part of my personal growth as well as to honour the message of the bear teachings, which I explain in the paper.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Kawôtinikewin</h3>

<p>Ewako ôma ohci sôpêkahikan maskwa pihtaw mistahi ispihteyehtakosowin nêhiyawîhtwâwin. Maskwa nîkâneyihtâkosiwin âtayôhkan nehiyawak. Wahkomakan ayisiniwak ekwa pahki nihtâwihcikewin âcimowin. Maskwa nimosôminan pihci nêhiyaw nihtâwihcikewin âcimowin. Maskwa awîyak kakiskeyihtâhk neheyaw maskihkîy ekwa petaw maskihkîy pahkisimôtâhk ochi. Niya ochi Mohawk ochi etah ka ohtâskanesihk maskwa.</p>

<p>Ketasinastek nîkâneyihtâkosiwin. Mihko naspisihcikan nehiyawak. Mihkonikâtêw mamihcitotamowin oye ekwa nitiyinîmak. Sîpihko ekwa askehtakawa naspisihcikan pisiskow acahkwak. Astamihk pahkisimôtâhk maskihkîy wâskâpayîs ketasinastek sîpihko ekwa askehtakawa ekwa maskwa pisiskow acahkwak. Naspisihcikan kîsohpikiwin, nâkateyimowewin ekwa pekweyihtamowin.</p>

<p>Nia kakehk maskwa sisopekaha sîpihko ekwa askehtakawa naspisihcikan pahkisimôtâhk kihcêyihtâkwan niya wiya ekwa niya peyakôskân. Kîyânaw wanisinowin nehiyawiwin ekwa nehiyawewin. Kîyânaw wanisinowin wâhkôhtamowin. Maskihkîy pahkisimôtâhk ochi ispîhk kîyânaw nistawinâkewin maskwa âtayôhkan. Nimosôminan maskwa âtayôhkan nokohtâwin wahkomâkanak kihchi. Niya pakoseyimiw itasinâsonak ekwa âtayôhkan sihkiskâkew nâkateyimowewin niyâk ekwa nîsopayihcikewin. Kîyânaw nisitohtamowin kiya oye ekwa nisitohtamowin wahkohtowak. Mihcetweyakisiwak ochi oye namatakon.</p>

<p>Mastaw, maskwa âtayôhkan pihtikwe niya pimâtisiwin ekwa petâw maskihkîy pahkisimôtâhk ochi. Peyakwâw nehiyawewin tahtapipimohtewin. Ana miyin âniskômohcikewin mâmawinitowin ekwa kiskinwâswêwitam. Niya mâmitonêyihtamowin namoya ihtakon Nakahpêhanohk, maka niya otihtikow maskihkîy omisimâw Vancouver ochi. Niya tâpwehtamowin maskwa nimosôminan petâw maskihkîy pahkisimôtâhk ochi.</p>

<p>Niya macihtaw pahkisimôtâhk tânispî ekwa nâkateyimowewin ochi nitawi-ayamihcikêw oyasiwewin. Niya nôhtê wichih nitiyinîmak sâposci kiskinwahamâkosiwin oyasiwewin. Kîkway kâwi tôtamihk. Ispîhk oskinîkiwkiskinwahamakosiwin nehiyawiwin ekwa nehiyawewin, wiyawâw maskihkîy nitiyinîmak. Maskwa âtayôhkan ekwa pahkisimôtâhk nîsohkamâkewin nisitohta oye ekwa nîsohkamâtowak yahkipita. Kîyânaw itôtâmowin maskihkîy pahkisimôtâhk ochi maskwa petâw ekwa mâcihta yahkipita. Hiy hiy. Kakiyaw niwakohmakanak.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Taking Back</h3>

<p>For this assignment I decided to paint a bear. The reason I chose a bear as my subject is because of its strong meaning within the Cree culture. The bear is recognized as the strongest spirit for Cree people. This is because it is considered a relative to humans and is part of the creation story for the Cree people. Through the Cree creation story, the bear is seen as a grandfather and accordingly, is often still referred to as a grandfather now. Bears are also recognized as medicine keepers and bring our people medicines from the west. Along with that, I come from the bear clan through my Mohawk roots, so the bear has special meaning to me through that as well.</p>

<p>The colours I’ve chosen for this painting also have meaning. The colour red represents the Cree people. I chose to use this colour as the background for this piece to acknowledge my people and heritage. The colours blue and green are meant to symbolize nature, animals, and other beings. On the Cree medicine wheel, the west direction is a dark blue or green. This direction on the wheel holds the bear as its spirit animal. It also is a representation of adulthood, a deeper emotional understanding, and a time for taking responsibility.</p>

<p>I choose to paint the bear in blue and green tones to represent the importance of the west direction wheel teachings at this point in time both for myself and for my band. Following our enfranchisement and other colonial policies, our band loss so much of our culture and language. Beyond that we also lost important connections. I think by recognizing the bear spirit, we will allow for medicines from the west to be brought to us.The bear spirit being our grandfather will show people the importance of recognizing our ancestors and history. My hope is that the meaning of these colours and this spirit will inspire our band to take responsibility for our future and come together. By having a deeper emotional understanding, we can hopefully understand our past and recognize we are all related and descend from the same ancestors. Whatever divisions we’ve had in the past should stay in the past as we begin a new stage of life, the west direction of the wheel. This is also a way for us to integrate Cree law in our interactions with Canadian law.</p>

<p>Personally, I have more recently allowed spirits like the bear to enter my life and bring medicines from the west. This began for me with Cree language class, but through my participation in the class, I have received so much more medicine than simply language. I have made connections with a community of Cree people. I have also received cultural teachings and been able to participate in ceremony through these connections, things that I thought were impossible to receive in Vancouver before starting to take these classes. Despite being the people of the plains, I have been very fortunate to receive this medicine in Vancouver from incredible matriarchs. I believe this is grandfather bear bringing me his medicine from the west.</p>

<p>I think in returning to school to study law, I have already entered the west phase of life and taken more responsibility and begun to try to gain a deeper emotional understanding. I hope through studying law, to one day be able to help my band on its path forward. This is a personal responsibility I believe I have. I also think that as more youth from my band begin to study language and learn more about our culture, they will also serve as medicine to our people. I am hopeful that the embodiment of the bear spirit and the west direction will help us understand our past and ultimately move our band forward. We as a people can act as the medicine from the west that the bear brings and begin our path forward. Thank you. All my relations.</p>
</blockquote>
