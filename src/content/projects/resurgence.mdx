<p>While beauty is in the eye of the beholder beautiful is not something that is often associated with legal writing. However, changing the medium in which legal analysis is presented permits us to describe some of the projects in the Archive not only as eloquent and elegant but also beautiful.</p>

<p>Rachel Garrett’s work for the 2021 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar, titled “Resurgence” is one of those projects. In the same vein in which <a href="/projects/reconciliation-blanket-for-modern-treatymaking-in-british-columbia/">Grace Lichtenwald’s</a> reconciliation blanket is a companion piece to <a href="/projects/truth-and-reconciliation-blanket/">Dawn Johnson’s</a> blanket, so Rachel’s embroidery reflects on themes first raised over twenty years earlier in <a href="/projects/robes-of-power/">Nadia Belokopitov’s</a> Haida button blanket.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Rachel_Garrett_01-1024x828.jpg" alt="Rachel Garrett's 2021 embroidery project titled &quot;Resurgence&quot;" class="wp-image-1919"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>In her accompanying essay Rachel explains how she chose the materials for her project and how each of the embroidery of the four corners critiques the four pillars of the colonial legal structure of the law underpinning Aboriginal and Treaty Rights.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The hand-embroidered art piece Resurgence analyses and critiques four of the pillars underlying the system of treaty rights in Canadian colonial law: the doctrine of discovery, the Indian Act, the honour of the Crown, and the reservation system. Each pillar is represented in one of the four corners of the piece, with the sun of Indigenous resurgence shining through and over each pillar. This original piece is designed and hand-embroidered by myself on a piece of blue dupion silk passed down through my family, and framed with wood taken from branches in my garden fallen in a recent storm, on the traditional and ancestral territory of the Tsawwassen people.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Materials</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Rachel_Garrett_02-1024x1021.jpg" alt="Rachel Garrett's 2021 embroidery project titled &quot;Resurgence&quot; detail" class="wp-image-1923"/></figure>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Rachel_Garrett_03-1024x1024.jpg" alt="Rachel Garrett's 2021 embroidery project titled &quot;Resurgence&quot; detail" class="wp-image-1922"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>This piece is situated on a background of blue dupion silk, cut from a larger piece of fabric passed down through my family. The issues this piece engages with are of serious weight and serious impact on the lives of Indigenous peoples across this land. To engage with these issues, it felt necessary to give away something important to me in return for the knowledge and learning that engaging with these issues has brought me. To learn is the greatest gift, and it feels important to provide something important and personal to me in return, with reciprocity.</p>

<p>This piece is framed in wood taken from branches that fell in my garden in a recent storm. As the scenes depicted in this piece intend to represent the land in itself, it felt important to bring the land back into this piece in a physical sense. Through the raw natural silk with all its imperfections from the silkworm still present in the texture of the fabric, to the wood of the frame brought down in a storm, the materials of this piece endeavour to bring parts of nature back into the world of Resurgence.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>I have set out below parts of Rachel’s guided illustrated analysis of her tapestry:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Each corner of the piece starts with the same core element: a scene of mountains, greenery, sand, and water. Representative of the land in itself, this scene is replicated four times but is changed and moulded by the legal concept in each of the four corners – just as these legal concepts mould, shape, package, and parcel the land itself in different ways:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>[W]e are nothing without the land. The land and ocean define who we are, and if the land and sea changes, we change. (Terri-Lynn Williams- Davidson (Gid7ahl-Gudsllaay Lalaxaaygans), “Weaving Together Our Future: The Interaction of Haida Laws to Achieve Respectful Co- Existence” (2012)</p>
</blockquote>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">TAKEN: The Doctrine of Discovery</h3>

<p>“[The doctrine of discovery] was invalidly based on the presumed racial superiority of European Christian peoples and was used to dehumanize, exploit and subjugate Indigenous Peoples and dispossess us of our most basic rights. This was the very foundation of genocide. Such ideology lead to practices that continue through modern-day laws and policies.” - Assembly of First Nations, 2018</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Rachel_Garrett_04-1024x1024.jpg" alt="Rachel Garrett's 2021 embroidery project titled &quot;Resurgence&quot; detail - a hand seizing the land. " class="wp-image-1925"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>In the top left corner of the piece, a hand has seized the land. The land has been taken, gripped, appropriated. This corner represents the doctrine of discovery. The Assembly of First Nations has described the doctrine of discovery as follows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Doctrine of Discovery emanates from a series of Papal Bulls (formal statements from the Pope) and extensions, originating in the 1400s. Discovery was used as legal and moral justification for colonial dispossession of sovereign Indigenous Nations, including First Nations in what is now Canada. During the European “Age of Discovery”, Christian explorers “claimed” lands for their monarchs who felt they could exploit the land, regardless of the original inhabitants.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The hand seizing the land represents this ‘claiming’ of lands by colonizers, a legal principle deeply entrenched in Canadian law. For decades, the leading case on Aboriginal title in Canada was the case of <em>St. Catharines Milling and Lumber Co. v. R</em>., decided by the Supreme Court of Canada in 1887 and affirmed by the Privy Council of the United Kingdom in 1888. This case relies upon earlier cases from the United States that are based on the doctrine of discovery, finding that “[a]ll this country was once occupied by Indian tribes. On its discovery by Europeans the discoverers acquired a right of property in the soil provided that discovery was followed by possession.” The doctrine of discovery is baked into the foundations of what is now called Canada.</p>

<p>But the doctrine of discovery represented by the fist clutching the mountain in Resurgence is not just a thing of the past - in the time since St. Catherines, we can still see the doctrine of discovery reflected in court rulings. In the 1997 <em>Delgamuukw</em> case, the Supreme Court of Canada finds that one of the objectives of Aboriginal title law is to reconcile the Crown assertion of sovereignty with the prior occupation of Indigenous peoples. In very same breath the Court posits that ‘the building of infrastructure’ and ‘general economic development’ can all be justified infringements of Aboriginal title. The very idea that Crown sovereignty can ‘win out’ against Aboriginal title in those areas is deeply rooted back in the initial assertion of Crown sovereignty itself – the doctrine of discovery. Later, in the 2014 <em>Tsilhqot’in</em> decision, the Supreme Court of Canada puts it plainly, finding that “[a]t the time of assertion of European sovereignty, the Crown acquired radical or underlying title to all the land in the province” and that this Crown title is “burdened” by Aboriginal rights. In this same case we review the test for proof of Aboriginal title, which includes asking whether before British sovereignty, did the group act in such a way that would signal to third parties that it held the land for its own purposes? To answer this question requires an intensive mission of assessing the characteristics of the group, the characteristics of the land, and looking for any site-specific intensive occupation, regular harvesting, hunting, fishing, or other strong presence. Even today, we can see the roots of the doctrine of discovery reflected within that element of the test. The fact that the test requires looking backwards from the point of British sovereignty reflects the ideology of the settler system that the Crown’s property rights derive from that moment of assertion of sovereignty, or ‘discovery’.</p>

<p>In 2015, the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada urged Canada to reject the doctrine of discovery and renegotiate a system that stands on a different foundation. Calls to Action 45 and 49 read as follows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<ol start="45" class="wp-block-list">
<li>We call upon the Government of Canada, on behalf of all Canadians, to jointly develop with Aboriginal peoples a Royal Proclamation of Reconciliation to be issued by the Crown. The proclamation would build on the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and the Treaty of Niagara of 1764, and reaffirm the nation- to-nation relationship between Aboriginal peoples and the Crown. The proclamation would include, but not be limited to, the following commitments: i) Repudiate concepts used to justify European sovereignty over Indigenous lands and peoples such as the Doctrine of Discovery and terra nullius.</li>

<li>We call upon all religious denominations and faith groups who have not already done so to repudiate concepts used to justify European sovereignty over Indigenous lands and peoples, such as the Doctrine of Discovery and terra nullius.</li>
</ol>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Today, despite these calls to action, the doctrine of discovery remains to be “the legal justification for the colonial occupation of our lands and our nations.” Scholars have posited that “[a]s long as Canada bases its existence on that Doctrine, it is hard to characterize it as anything other than a racist state where one race has been given the right to subjugate and confiscate the lands of another.” The hand continues to grip, even hundreds of years later.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">TIED: The Indian Act</h3>

<p>“The federal Indian Act stands in the way of good Indigenous governance while remaining resilient in the face of rights challenges.” - John Borrows</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Rachel_Garrett_05-1024x970.jpg" alt="Rachel Garrett's 2021 embroidery project titled &quot;Resurgence&quot; detail - land tied up with string. " class="wp-image-1932"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>In the top right corner of this piece, the land is tied up with string. This corner represents the Indian Act, and the ideological and physical confinement that came with it. The Indian Act, passed in 1876, still today usurps and ties down First Nations authority and power:</p>

<p>Since 1876, the federal Indian Act has inappropriately detracted from First Nations’ power. The Act flows from the idea that Indigenous people are inferior and must be schooled in Canadian forms to hasten assimilation. The Act is explicitly designed to rupture First Nations socio-political relations and forcibly absorb individual Nation members within broader Canadian society. Its formal operation has devastating effects. Its underlying philosophy damages most everyone it touches. Its provisions narrowly define and heavily regulate Indigenous peoples’ citizenship, land rights, succession rules, political organization, economic opportunities, fiscal management, educational patterns, and attainment. The Act makes First Nations largely subject to provincial legislation and regulation without their consent. It usurps First Nations’ authority and responsibility to deal with their own problems in an effective way.</p>

<p>In Resurgence, the physical tying up of the land in the top right corner represents these confinements and limitations imposed on First Nations under the Indian Act. One example of these ties is the discriminatory limiting of status rights based on the male line within the Indian Act, which persisted for over a hundred years until beginning to be addressed with amendments in the 1980s, and finally being fully addressed in Bill C-3 in 2011, and Bill S-3 in 2017-2019. In the beginning, a woman’s status under the Indian Act was entirely tied to her husband: women with status lost their status and rights if they married a non-status person, and if a woman married a status man of a different band, she would no longer be a member of her own band and would lose her status completely if she was abandoned or widowed. The impact of this discrimination eroded cultural values, practices, and undermined women in governance within First Nations.</p>

<p>This is undoubtedly a ‘tying’ of the hands of First Nations women under the Indian Act, confining their status and rights, represented in Resurgence with the tied string. The land itself, too, is tied under the Indian Act in Canada. In 1927, an amendment to the Indian Act was passed (and remained in force until 1951) that made it illegal for First Nations to hire lawyers or bring land claims against the government without the consent of the very government they were trying to bring a claim against:</p>

<p>The amendment was premised on the proposition that since there was no such thing as Indian title in British Columbia, Indians should be protected from those lawyers who would erroneously and mischievously advance a case on the basis that such a concept existed in law. That amendment remained in force until 1951. It effectively chilled any legal initiatives to advance the Indian land claim movement in the courts of British Columbia until the Nisga'a initiated their historic case in the late 1960s.</p>

<p>This is another of countless examples where the Indian Act ‘tied up’ the land in itself, creating further barriers to the pursuit of land claims by Indigenous groups. In a step towards ‘the untying’, National Chief Perry Bellegarde of the Assembly of First Nations has this to say:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>We all want to move beyond the Indian Act’s control and reconstitute ourselves as Indigenous peoples and Nations with fundamental inherent rights. We are self-determining nations with jurisdiction to take control of where we are and where we need to go as people and as nations.</p>
</blockquote>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">TENDRILS: The Honour of the Crown</h3>

<p>“This is the zone of the Honour of the Crown. … When my ancestors first met Philomen Wright, the first white settler, at the Sacred Chaudière Site on the Ottawa River in 1800, and asked by what right he cut down the trees and took the land, the stranger drew a paper from his pocket and read “The Indians have consented to relinquish all claim to the land, in compensation for which they receive annual grants from the Government, which shall be withheld if they molest settlers.” This paper, my ancestors saw as a big “loup garou”, an indescribable monster supposed to have supernatural powers, and in my own lifetime, I have experienced the deep fear this reference brings to native peoples. Such are the roots for the struggle for land.” - William Commanda, Algonquin Elder</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Rachel_Garrett_06-777x1024.jpg" alt="Rachel Garrett's 2021 embroidery project titled &quot;Resurgence&quot; detail - a crown sits atop the mountain.  " class="wp-image-1936"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>In the bottom left corner of this piece, a crown sits atop the mountain, with tentacle tendrils reaching out down the mountain to the land below. This corner plays with the mythology of monsters to question what it means to be ‘honourable’ in the context of the honour of the Crown, when the government fails to act honourably all too often.<br />The honour of the Crown operates doctrinally to give rise to Crown obligations to consult and accommodate Indigenous peoples before interfering with the exercise of their treaty or Aboriginal rights. In <em>Haida Nation v. British Columbia</em>, 2004 SCC 73, former Chief Justice McLaughlin wrote:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In all its dealings with Aboriginal peoples, from the assertion of sovereignty to the resolution of claims and the implementation of treaties, the Crown must act honourably. Nothing less is required if we are to achieve the reconciliation of the pre-existence of aboriginal societies with the sovereignty of the Crown.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>However, while the concept in itself is inherently tied to consultation and accommodations, and even to the concept of ‘reconciliation’ as McLaughlin brings up, the operation of the principle in practice has oft been found lacking and “the ‘honour’ of the Crown continues to be questioned, as the government fails to uphold its basic duty to Indigenous people time and time again.” The limitations in the honour of the crown can be seen in <em>R. v. Lefthand</em>, 2007 ABCA 206, in which Justice Slatter critiqued the use of the word ‘honour’ when discussing the Crown’s obligations, finding that “[t]he term “honour”, especially when used in the phrase “honour of the Crown” has an absolute, moralistic and inflexible connotation, which does not accommodate the type of balancing and evolution of ideas, rights and concepts that is required in constitutional jurisprudence.” When the colonial law pushes back against the very concept of having a responsibility to be honourable, as was the case in <em>Lefthand</em>, the ‘Honour of the Crown’ as a legal concept begins to lose its meaning. Despite claiming to uphold the honour of the Crown, the Canadian government has failed again and again to act honourably. For example, in 2018 the Federal Court of Appeal held the Crown had failed its duty to consult by taking a paternalistic and inadequate approach to consultation in the Trans Mountain pipeline expansion.</p>

<p>In Resurgence, this dark side to the honour of the Crown is represented within the mythology of monsters, with tentacles reaching out towards us, inspired by William Commanda’s description of the honour of the Crown as a “loup garou” or indescribable monster in the quote featured at the beginning of this section. Richard Hill, former Crown representative in New Zealand, also invokes monstrous imagery in discussing the Crown’s role in treaty negotiations:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>But I had to recognise that the Crown was like the mythical Greek monster, the Hydra, with its many heads, and that I and my [Crown] colleagues constituted just one new head.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>When the Crown fails to act honourably time and time again, the honour of the Crown as a legal concept begins to look more like an excuse to check the box of consultation without meaningful accommodations, let alone consent.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">FRAGMENTS: Reservations</h3>

<p>“The creation of reserves had far-reaching implications for all aspects of Aboriginal life. The reserve system was, on a fundamental level, a government-sanctioned displacement of First Nations. At the stroke of a pen, reserves divided up not only lands but peoples and Nations that had existed for hundreds if not thousands of years. Families, houses and clans that had hunted and gathered together for generations were abruptly and arbitrarily joined up with other families and houses, disrupting social networks and long- established kinship systems that determined who could hunt, fish, and gather in particular areas.” - Erin Hanson</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2024/06/Rachel_Garrett_07-851x1024.jpg" alt="Rachel Garrett's 2021 embroidery project titled &quot;Resurgence&quot; detail - the land is fragmented. " class="wp-image-1940"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>In the bottom right corner of this piece, the land is fragmented and split apart. This corner of the piece represents the system of reservations put into place in colonial Canada. Though today reserves continue to be important land bases for Indigenous peoples across Canada that are seen by some as a part of a “cultural spiritual, and physical homeland,” the historical roots of the reservation system began as the “forced displacement of a people”. The fragmented imagery of this corner in Resurgence represents that initial displacement, upon which much of the ideals of Aboriginal land law is built upon. Haida lawyer id7ahl-Gudsllaay Lalaxaaygans (Terri-Lynn Williams-Davidson) has posited that one significant barrier to recognition and transformation is the fundamental unwillingness of the state to recognize the capabilities of Indigenous peoples to manage their own lands:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>[One barrier is the] unwillingness to acknowledge the resilience, knowledge and capabilities of First Nations to manage land, waters and resources and to develop political institutions to govern in a new legal landscape. Indigenous peoples are living, developing people, who strive – just as the rest of Canadians – to find the best way to organize ourselves politically, to live with each other, to manage human interactions with the land, waters and resources, and to preserve our unique cultures. … The existence and perpetuation of above barriers prevent true reconciliation.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The fragmentation represented in this corner of Resurgence is also intended to reflect the ongoing violence inflicted by the colonial Canadian government on reservations today, through failing to provide safe drinking water. In 2016, Human Rights Watch published a report finding that the Canadian government had failed its international human rights obligations towards First Nations people in Canada by failing to remedy the water crisis on First Nations reserves. Today, over 60 long-term water advisories are still in place. Despite public attention, the crisis has not gone away:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Canada has an obligation to act to eliminate discrimination in law and fact,and ensure adequate safe drinking water and sanitation on reserves. While there is no dispute that the government has invested money to address this issue, the fact remains that a significant number of First Nations persons in Ontario cannot drink the water in their homes. The federal government has failed to address factors that contribute to this crisis. International human rights bodies and experts have raised concerns about Canada’s failure to do so.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The colonial Canadian government’s failure to provide safe drinking water on reserves is another way in which colonial governance has fragmented and broken down the safety of communities.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The unifying segment of Rachel’s project Resurgence begins with a quotation from a Cherokee scholar, Daniel Heath Justice “We can’t possibly live otherwise until we first imagine otherwise.” I found this to be particularly appropriate given my own research for the <em>Deigamuukw</em> case. It was the Cherokee Nation who in the early part of the nineteenth century transformed their cultural, economic and legal systems, including the promulgation of a Cherokee Constitution, in what has been referred to as a ‘renascence’, in their attempt to preserve their national identity in the face of the efforts of the State of Georgia and the US federal government to dispossess and remove them to west of the Mississippi. Despite the 1832 US Supreme Court decision in <em>Worcester v Georgia</em> finding the Georgia laws attempting to annihilate Cherokee political and legal institutions and redistributing their land to white settlers unconstitutional, as a violation of solemn treaties between the federal government and the Cherokee Nation, the Federal Government, under the Presidency of Andrew Jackson, proceeded to ‘negotiate’ a patently fraudulent treaty followed by the forced removal of the Cherokee nation in the Trail of Tears in which 4500 Cherokee men, women and children died.<sup data-fn="314a1fca-9e19-4612-8115-1050a5ed7cdb" class="fn"><a href="#314a1fca-9e19-4612-8115-1050a5ed7cdb" id="314a1fca-9e19-4612-8115-1050a5ed7cdb-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>The Federal Government proceeded to rely upon the terms of the New Echota Treaty and in the spring of 1838 the U.S. Army enforced its terms against those Cherokees who had refused to remove in the time allotted under the Treaty and they were "escorted" to the new lands allotted to them in what is now Oklahoma. A few paragraphs cannot do justice to the human tragedy of the Cherokees’ Trail of Tears but the chilling language used by those under whose authority it was carried into effect requires little commentary:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Two years later in the spring of 1838 the Army rounded up 15,000 Cherokees… They were seized as they worked in their farms and fields, separated from their possessions, and taken to military detention camps. They remained in captivity for months while hundreds died from inadequate, unaccustomed rations. The debilitation of others contributed to deaths during the removal march… During the last large scale Indian removal, 4500 Cherokees died.</p>

<p>President Van Buren [ Andrew Jackson's successor] and Secretary of War ignored the deaths. They congratulated themselves instead the removal was at an end and that they had finally permitted Chief Ross to lead the bulk of his tribe west, As Poinsett described the process,</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>by the General appointed. . . . The reluctance of the Indians to relinquish the land of their birth . . . was entirely overcome. … Humanity, no less than sound policy, dictated this course toward these children of the forest.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Commissioner of Indian Affairs amplified:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>A retrospect of the last eight months, in reference to this numerous and more than ordinarily enlightened tribe, cannot fail to be refreshing to well-constituted minds. . . . A large mass of men have been conciliated, the hazard of an effusion of blood has been put by, good feeling has been preserved, and we have quietly and gently transported 18,000 friends to the west bank of the Mississippi.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Instead of facing actual deaths, white policy-makers imagined Indian destruction as an abstract and generalized process removed from human control and human reality. To face responsibility for specific killing might have led to efforts to stop it; avoiding individual deaths turned Indian removal into a theory of genocide. In Jackson's words,</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Humanity has often wept over the fate of the aborigines of this country, and Philanthropy has been busily engaged in devising means to avert it, but its progress has never for a moment been arrested, and one by one have many powerful tribes disappeared from the earth. To follow to the land the last of his race and to tread on the graves of extinct nations excites melancholy reflections. But true philanthropy reconciles the mind to these vicissitudes, as it does to the extinction of one generation to make room for another.<sup data-fn="b14838f3-486c-49a0-8dd9-402cf3f807fc" class="fn"><a href="#b14838f3-486c-49a0-8dd9-402cf3f807fc" id="b14838f3-486c-49a0-8dd9-402cf3f807fc-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>In <em>Delgamuukw</em> I read this passage to Chief Justice McEachern late on a Friday afternoon in the Courthouse in Smithers BC. in the Spring of 1987 in what I hoped was a respectful barristerial manner. Not far below the surface of my recitation lay my unvarnished feelings, the compounding of rage and sorrow for the injustice of not only the Cherokee’s Trail of Tears but the many trails of dispossession experienced by other Aboriginal peoples, including the Gitksan and Wet'suwet'in. My purpose in relating to Chief Justice McEachern the history of Worcester v. Georgia and the cruel fate of the Cherokee Nation in its removal from its homelands was because they bore directly on the issues before the Supreme Court of British Columbia in <em>Delgamuukw</em>. The racist attitudes of Andrew Jackson and those who implemented Indian removal in the 1830s are not so far removed in time and tone from statements made in the 1860s by colonial officials such as Joseph Trutch in British Columbia.<sup data-fn="e2eb0eb7-8e1e-441d-a47e-6a6eaeae1252" class="fn"><a href="#e2eb0eb7-8e1e-441d-a47e-6a6eaeae1252" id="e2eb0eb7-8e1e-441d-a47e-6a6eaeae1252-link">3</a></sup> To conclude, as the Province of British Columbia argued in Delgamuukw, that the 45 square miles of reserve lands represented the only lands to which the Gitksan and Wet'suwet'en had Aboriginal rights was, in effect, an argument that they have been legally removed from the balance of their ancient territories, and would give British Columbia the same rights over the territories of the Gitksan and Wet'suwet'en that the State of Georgia sought to usurp over the Cherokee territories in 1830. Those rights formed the basis upon which the Cherokee nation had rebuilt itself in the early 19th century. In the same way the territories of the Gitksan and Wet'suwet'en not only formed the foundation of their distinctive culture and economy but provided the basis upon which their future strength and survival depended. I urged Chief Justice McEachern to find that Aboriginal rights defined in the manner of the Hereditary Chiefs’ Statement of Claim were the legal foundation to ensure that Andrew Jackson's contemplation of the disappearance and extinction of Indian nations would never come to pass in Canada. Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982 was designed to ensure that Canada is built upon the vitality and not the demise of Indians nations.</p>

<p>In Rachel Garrett’s narrative the contemporary resurgence of Indigenous Nations is a more optimistic reimagining in which indigenous peoples and their political and legal orders find their rightful place in the Canadian Federation.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>‘Imagine otherwise’ is the motto of Cherokee author and scholar Daniel Heath Justice, describing the opportunity to imagine a different way of being: "Many of our ancestors were able to imagine otherwise. And I think we are the embodiment of their dreams for the future. And so we've got to dream for those yet to come, and hopefully dream a better world and a better set of possibilities for them." In this piece, despite the harm caused from the system of treaty rights in Canadian colonial law in the four corners, the sun still rises. The sun still rises, and we are able to imagine otherwise. ‘Imagine otherwise’ is resurgence.</p>

<p>Haida lawyer Gid7ahl-Gudsllaay Lalaxaaygans (Terri-Lynn Williams-Davidson) has done the work of imagining otherwise, looking at how the Haida have fostered transformation:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>We learn from Haida art and songs that sometimes transformation involves partial states of transformation. Often poles contain figures that are half human and, for instance, half Thunderbird. The “Eagle Spirit” dance depicts the power of a human being that is partially transformed into an eagle. For the CHN, the application of this law has meant utilizing any tools that will help take us closer to our objectives – even if they are not perfect in form, and even if they are part of another’s culture – such as drawing lines on maps, creating land use zones, and engaging in land use planning and joint management with BC and Canada. In a real sense, the Haida, BC and Canada have entered into states of partial transformation, with more work to do to achieve complete transformation.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>These transformations are a core part of imagining resurgence. The four corners of the art piece have all played a role in acting as blocks to that transformation. These blocks, and colonialism more broadly, have “caused immense damage and loss in Indigenous legal systems.” The sun in this piece represents Indigenous resurgence in the many different forms it can take, moving past a world with these barriers in place and imagining otherwise. For Terri-Lynn Williams-Davidson, part of resurgence is the transformation that can be achieved through the weaving together of legal systems: “Without this weaving together of legal traditions, we will not create new legal landscapes”. Resurgence may also look like oral histories and origin stories that are “instructive for dreaming our future”. Whatever that resurgence may look like, whatever ‘otherwise’ we can imagine, “[t]he work of transforming that vision into a reality is slow – and like Raven we may make mistakes along the way – but we must always keep our eye on the goal of transforming and creating new legal landscapes.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The concluding part of Rachel’s project is a personal statement of her own healing journey and the inspiration for her use of embroidery in animating her reflections on the seminar.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">HEALING: My Story</h3>

</figure>

<p>Throughout the process of planning and creating this piece, I often found myself reflecting back to the article by Nigel Grenier, Esdii Wal: Gitxsan Law Grounded in Epistemology. Upon first reading the article I was particularly struck by the author’s description of the Gitxsan way of teaching, where he describes receiving an amhalyte (headdress) as a graduation gift, wearing the headdress while dancing his family’s limk tsim halyte (chief’s dance), and finding himself embodying the story of Esdii wal through the dance, using this experience as an example of deeper learning and knowledge:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This is the Gitxsan way of teaching, an intergenerational process of transmitting embodied knowledge across a plethora of interconnected practices. When writing an academic paper, it is not possible to replicate this method of teaching. The relationship between the author and the reader is too impersonal.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The fact that I found myself returning to this quote time and time again inspired me to step away from the medium of an academic paper for this project. Grenier opened my mind to better understanding the epistemological divide between the reader of an academic paper and the author, where readers may be “unwilling to put in the effort required to be an effective listener.” As someone who has written countless academic papers, I think there is also a phenomenon of the author, too, being unwilling to put in the effort required to be an effective listener. I am certain I have fallen into this trap in the past. To write an academic paper is to inherently position oneself as teacher, and the readers as listeners and learners. In reality, I wonder if it would not be more effective for all parties involved if we viewed the process of writing as much as about learning and listening as it is about teaching. The process of creating Resurgence was, for me, an experiment in better weaving that balance of listening. As well, one of the themes of this class that has stood out to me is the way stories are distilled down and packaged to be presented in our legal system. I remember a discussion in class of how 3000 pages of arguments, stories, and histories can be required to be distilled down into a 60 page factum. Choosing to do an art piece instead of an academic paper was, to me, an attempt to get away from this distillation and represent these ideas in a more free and interconnected way through art instead.</p>

<p>I think back to Daniel Heath Justice’s concept of ‘imagine otherwise’, referred to above in the discussions of resurgence in this write-up. In a way, the process of turning away from the academic medium into something entirely new to me was my own miniature way of imagining otherwise, through healing. Embroidery for me has always been inherently tied into healing. About four years ago, I had to take time out of my undergraduate studies to get surgery for an ongoing medical condition. At that time I also learned that my condition would likely lead to infertility. In the weeks I spent healing not just from the surgery itself but also from that distressing news, I picked up embroidery for the first time. I couldn’t stand up or walk around, but I could sit and embroider all day, every day. So I did. The repetitive act of pushing a needle through cloth and creating something beautiful at the end was healing. It was not only therapeutic in helping me feel productive, but it also felt like a way of connecting me to my female ancestors who had spent many of their days working with needles and thread. That interconnectivity helped me come to terms with my new reality and empowered me to push for what they could only have dreamed of. The Daniel Heath Justice quote I used above means a lot to me for that reason: "Many of our ancestors were able to imagine otherwise. And I think we are the embodiment of their dreams for the future. And so we've got to dream for those yet to come, and hopefully dream a better world and a better set of possibilities for them." A few months ago, at the end of last semester, I took time out again to get my second surgery for the same condition. I came to this class for the first time just a few weeks after, still healing. I picked up my needle and thread again, and created this piece.</p>

<p>I normally wouldn’t share such a personal story in a project for a class, but I am trying to ‘imagine otherwise’. I am trying to imagine a future where learning is personal, interconnected, and intergenerational. In return for the immeasurable learning I am so grateful to have received from this class, in reciprocity I give my personal story, and a piece of silk passed down through my family, embroidered with my healing.</p>
</blockquote>
