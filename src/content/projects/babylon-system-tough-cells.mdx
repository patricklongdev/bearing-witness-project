<p>Christian Pearce, was not only a law student in the 2007 Penal Policy seminar, he was also the senior editor for Pound magazine. Pound was a Toronto-based hip hop magazine that was distributed for free across Canada. Founded in 1998 and beginning publication in December 1999, Pound was published quarterly.&nbsp;It ceased publishing in 2010, after releasing its 45th issue. For the June 2007 issue Christian interviewed me and wrote an article on the problematic way in which hip-hop and rap addressed the issue of the prison.</p>

<Slideshow images={[{"src": "/assets/2007/02/Christian_Pierce_05-1024x683.jpg", "alt": ""}, {"src": "/assets/2007/02/Christian_Pierce_04-1024x683.jpg", "alt": ""}]} />

<p>The lead editorial explained the significance of the article:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>My original plan for this editorial was to read or rather reread every issue of XXL from 2006 and write a salvo against the promotion of criminality in hip-hop media.. Why XXL? As the most popular magazine in hip-hop today, it’s pages influence the iconography and codes of hip-hop culture more than any others. And for a few years now I felt that the magazine has blindly persisted on revealing, and reveling in, the harsh realities of hip hoppers in their pages without providing context, balance or alternatives to the message .</p>

<p>My highly unscientific survey revealed that amongst the twenty-one features in the January February and March publications of XXL, sixteen of them included a reference to at least one of the following: drugs, pimping, violence, jails, guns and more obliquely “the hood”.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Christian’s article was intended to provide that context, balance and alternatives:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>While hip-hop’s relationship with imprisonment runs deep, the reality of prison life seems to have escaped the culture in recent years. Prominent acts count their time on lockdown like it was a stripe of street cred, critiquing the legitimacy of one another’s bids. New rappers use sentences to push product, first tasting commercial success while numbered in the system. And “Free My Jailed Homeboy” T-shirts have become a trendy statement of association with the crime life from the T.Dot down to H-Town.. On September 27, 2006, the Los Angeles times in an article entitled “Raps Captive Audience” observed, “the penitentiary has come to take on an almost mystical importance within hip-hop”.</p>

<p>Like hip-hop fans, most people don’t know what prisons really all about, the complex dynamics operating inside. Nor, unfortunately, most people much care.</p>

<p>Law Professor Michael Jackson on the other hand, has made a career out of carrying what happens within prisons. It’s a rare and passionate concern that Jackson attempts to pass on to students in his Penal Policy class at the University of British Columbia. Last year I got the chance to be one of those students. Enrolled in Jackson’s Penal Policy seminar you learned a lot of things about prison in Canada that can’t be learned elsewhere (except of course in prison itself – an even more costly education than law school). </p>

<p>At times, we heard stories of real-life prison breaks involving hijacked helicopters that pass over prison grounds and hang down ladders while guards blast away at escapees, of the social order within prison which places sexual offenders in the greatest danger from other inmates, and of at least one muscled prisoner who held Professor Jackson responsible for his continued incarceration and threatened to kill him (“You just fucking try” was Jackson’s reply, the issue thereafter rested). Unlike most rap music, which does a superficial pass over the particulars of prison life, most of our class time was occupied by the gruelling details – physical spaces, the arbitrary exercise of authority, the indefinite stays in solitary – that make the daily experience of imprisonment so harsh.</p>

<p>For most of us, what happens in the prisons is beyond our gaze, the British born and raised Jackson said during an interview in his UBC office. The only time we see it is in the form of a television series like Prison Break or Oz, where it’s entertainment and the violence is not real. It’s an interesting way to spend an hour a week, with telegenic actors and a really great plotline, but that’s the extent of our fascination or interest in prisons. It’s a twenty-four seven experience for those people who are sentenced to prison.</p>

<p>In this issues Babylon, Pound takes a closer look at the realities of life deep inside the system, a few reasons to avoid becoming an incarcerated pseudo- Scarface, and the significance of upholding justice at society’s coldest reaches.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In three densely packed pages Christian set out the basic unvarnished realities of imprisonment in Canada. One of those realities is the overrepresentation of indigenous offenders. Christian writes:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the past crime has been described as a sickness suffered by poor people who lack a properly functioning moral compass. But as much as crime results from systematically enforced poverty, prisons reflect deeper social and historical injustices. Aboriginals make up about 3% of the Canadian population, but represent around 18% of inmates in federal prisons and nearly and nearly 30% of federal prisoners age 20 and under. According to the Correctional Investigator, this latter group of young prisoners, many with gang affiliations, is more vulnerable to violence from other inmates, segregation, and a lack of lack of success to and success in prison programming. When Pound editor Roderigo Bascunan interviewed the War Party’s Rex Smallboy, representing the Hobbema Reservation in Alberta, he added to a short list of contributing factors including alcohol, residential schools and religion, the corrupting influence of mainstream rap music on reservation youth.</p>

<p>“The more gangster rap hit the Rez”, Smallboy said, “the more kids wanted to be gangsters because it was cool. They wanted to be dealers because it was cool, have a gun because it was cool, go to jail because it was cool. The reality that came with our newfound gangster popular culture was nothing close to the glamorized lifestyle on the rap CDs.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Writing for a very different audience than the one I address in my work as a professor, lawyer and advocate Christian’s article, honed with the skills of a journalist, not only reflected his understanding of the issues we had discussed in the seminar but circulated that understanding into a hip-hop community well beyond my range.</p>
