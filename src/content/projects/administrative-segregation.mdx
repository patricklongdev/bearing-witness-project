<p>In my academic writings I have described the practice of administrative segregation, which as practiced in Canada equates to solitary confinement, as the litmus test of the legitimacy of imprisonment. In <em>Prisoners of Isolation, Solitary Confinement in Canada</em> I wrote:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The focus on solitary confinement is not a random choice. As the ultimate exercise of state authority over a prisoner, it can be seen as a litmus test of the morality and legitimacy of the state's dealings with prisoners in lesser exercises of authority.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Contemporary correctional systems, using a variety of different terms, typically distinguish between two types of segregation. The first commonly referred to as punitive segregation can only be imposed following conviction of a prison disciplinary offence and is subject to a maximum length, within Canadian provincial systems this is usually fifteen days and until recent changes in the federal system the maximum sentence was thirty days for a single offence. The second type of segregation is administrative segregation. While sounding more benign, administrative segregation is in practice a more severe form of confinement primarily because it is not dependent upon conviction of an offence and has no fixed time limit. Placement in administrative segregation is based upon the institutional authorities believing that the prisoner constitutes a danger to the safety of other prisoners or the security of the institution or is himself or herself in danger from other prisoners. The federal regime of administrative segregation was subject to no external or independent oversight and in practice prisoners could spend months and in some cases years locked in their cells for twenty-three hours a day with no meaningful human interaction or programming. I have documented the systemic abuses of administrative segregation in my books <em>Prisoners of Isolation</em> and <em>Human Rights in Canadian Prisons</em>. Following litigation that successfully challenged the constitutionality of the federal regime, in 2019 the <em>Corrections and Conditional Release Act </em>was amended, administrative segregation was abolished and was replaced with what are termed Structured Intervention Units. The stated purpose of the new regime was to provide prisoners with greater time out of their cells, increase programming and a limited form of external review of placements. However, based on the first few years experience of the new units, many of the systemic issues of the old regime have not been redressed but replicated.<sup data-fn="4b6332c4-8a4b-40fe-89b7-cc1e6a7ee718" class="fn"><a href="#4b6332c4-8a4b-40fe-89b7-cc1e6a7ee718" id="4b6332c4-8a4b-40fe-89b7-cc1e6a7ee718-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>It is not therefore surprising that the practice of segregation/solitary confinement has been the subject of a number of projects in the Archive. In <em>Prisoners of Isolation </em>I quoted the powerful damning words of Charles Dickens published in his 1842 American Notes following his visits to prisons in North America where solitary confinement was practised, including Kingston Penitentiary:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In its intention I am well convinced that it is kind, humane and meant for reformation; but I am persuaded that those who devise the system and those benevolent gentlemen who carry it into execution, do not know what it is they are doing. I believe that very few men are capable of estimating the immense amount of torture and agony which this dreadful punishment, prolonged for years, inflicts upon the sufferers; and in guessing at it myself, and from reasoning from what I have seen written upon their faces, from what to my certain knowledge they feel within, I am only the more convinced that there is a depth of terrible endurance in it which none but the sufferers themselves can fathom, and which no man has a right to inflict upon his fellow creatures.</p>

<p>I hold this slow and daily tampering with the mysteries of the brain to be immeasurably worse than any torture of the body; and because its ghastly signs and tokens are not so palpable to the eye and sense of touch as scars upon the flesh, because its wounds are not on the surface and it extorts few cries that human ears can hear; therefore I denounce it as a secret punishment which slumbering humanity is not roused to stay.<sup data-fn="b075df01-da02-4aae-8bbc-25195dcefa93" class="fn"><a href="#b075df01-da02-4aae-8bbc-25195dcefa93" id="b075df01-da02-4aae-8bbc-25195dcefa93-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
