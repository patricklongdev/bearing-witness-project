<p>Like some of the other creative forms represented in the Archive, Sculpture is not normally associated with Law, finding its representation largely in marble and bronze portraits of important judges and other lawmakers, typically located in courthouses and legislative buildings. In the Indigenous societies of the Pacific Northwest Coast, however, monumental sculpture has long placed an important, indeed, central form for the expression of both legal history and Indigenous law.</p>

<p>The most emblematic and iconic feature of Northwest Coast Indigenous culture is the totem pole. It is the sculptural feature that introduces travellers to Canadian soil at Vancouver International Airport and is the largest and most visible exhibit at many museums of anthropology. But for Northwest Coast Indigenous Nations a pole is much more than an impressive physical sign of welcome. Doing justice to the intellectual, historical and legal content of the pole requires understanding the meaning of the crests and images carved into the monumental cedars. That understanding requires a journey through history, a history encoded in the oral histories, the re-telling of which is entrusted to each generation of Indigenous historians. The totem pole as legal sculpture encodes and evidences, metaphorically, physically and legally, the root of Indigenous title to the land.</p>

<p>In <em>Delgamuukw</em> the Hereditary Chiefs presented evidence to Chief Justice McEachern of the oral legal history - their adaawk - encoded in the Gitksan totem poles as a record of Gitksan concepts of ownership of their territories. Chief Hanamuxw, Joan Ryan, explained the significance of the raising of the pole of the former Hanamuxw, Jeffery Johnson, and the raising of her own pole, as the present Hanamuxw.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>First and foremost, it means that what I am doing now is preserving the past for the nieces and nephews that we have now and the future members of my family. Also following the tradition that is required for the chief that you put up your own pole, which indicates that there is continuity in the history of the house, and that putting of the pole indicates that you intend to maintain that link with the past, it means that you are careful about preserving the history of the house It's like a history book of your house. It's evidence that Hanamuxw's house did exist, does exist and will continue to exist. So maintaining your poles is really important.</p>

<p>It's a way of maintaining the heritage from our grandfathers. We have taken [Jeffrey Johnson’s] down to restore it, and also to put a new one with an added in crest to indicate that it's my pole as opposed to the previous owner. It's a way of reaffirming and confirming the Daxgyet of Hanamuxw. It's a way of establishing that the property of Hanamuxw has not been abandoned, nor will it be in the future. It's a way of telling the other chiefs that the house is as strong as it was before, and that it will continue to exist. It's also a way of releasing the spirits of those who have passed on. It's a way of giving thanks for having had them in -- in your house. It's a way of giving recognition to the contributions that they have made to your house. It's a way of acknowledging to our creator too the privilege of having had them share their lives with you. It's a way of saying that we have completed the business of caring for them. It's a way of saying that they have added a dimension to our lives. And it's a way of saying that we respect them, and we are saying that they were important to us. That's it.</p>

<p>Cameron Pollock, for the 2020 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Seminar, presented a sculptural piece created from a piece of a cedar tree, the material source of totem poles. The sculpture was an integral part of his reflections on the importance of oral histories and the difficulties the courts have had, working within the common law legal tradition, giving these histories the respect and weight they merit, which, was a central issue in <em>Delgamuukw</em>.</p>

<p>Cameron explains why he chose cedar and a sculptural form:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>It is rare to have the opportunity to express one’s creativity within the normal, narrow confines of a legal education, aside perhaps from creating novel arguments or drafting a particularly unique paper. However, any creativity usually remains limited to a written or spoken format. Representing a legal issue visually helps cut through the prose and jargon. In this case, the issue that I felt needed and deserved a visual representation was the Canadian legal system’s short and inadequate relationship with Indigenous oral histories as evidence in Aboriginal legal cases. I wanted to show just how recent that relationship is, how little substance there has been to it, and how much more it needs to grow.</p>

<p>The piece is made from a cedar tree round, cut into a thin disc. The tree grew on Gambier Island in Howe Sound. The Squamish nation called the island Cha7élkwnech, which refers to its deep, protected bays. It began growing in 1933 and was cut in August 2019, when it was 86 years old. On one face of the round, four copper nails have been driven in at specific growth rings that correspond to calendar years in which significant developments occurred in Canadian legal history.</p>
</blockquote>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2024/11/Cameron_Pollock_04-646x1024.jpg" alt="Cameron Pollock'a 2020 sculpture project titled, &quot;Into Time Out of Mind&quot; detail view" class="wp-image-2652"/></figure>
</figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The nail closest to the centre marks the year 1951, when the Indian Act was amended to allow Indigenous peoples to practice their cultures, dances, ceremonies, and to remove the ban on legal claims. The second nail marks the year 1973, when <em>Calder v British Columbia (AG)</em> [1973] SCR 313 was decided, which recognized that Aboriginal title to land existed prior to colonization and derived from Indigenous legal orders, society, and occupation of the land. The third nail marks the year 1997, when <em>Delgamuukw v British Columbia</em> [1997] 3 SCR 1010 was decided, which expanded on the content and nature of Aboriginal title and recognized oral histories as evidence acceptable to the courts, placing them on equal footing with historical documentary evidence. The final nail marks the year 2014, when <em>Tsilhqot’in Nation v British Columbia</em> 2014 SCC 44 was decided, the first case to recognize and declare Aboriginal title to traditional territory and which relied in part on oral history evidence.</p>

<p>The nails are connected by a thin, red thread that represents the narrow path the Canadian legal system has trodden on its way to recognizing oral histories as valid and valuable. The nails themselves, driven into the tree, represent the imposition of Canadian law on the lands and traditional territories of Canada’s Indigenous peoples. Copper nails driven into a young tree can cause that tree to become diseased and even die, but older tress are more resilient and will likely recover The spacing of the nails marks out the short period of time the system has actually engaged with the concept of Aboriginal title and the recency of the recognition of oral histories as evidence. The small size of the tree round and the short period of time its age represents, less than one hundred years, helps show the comparative infancy of the Canadian legal system. Overall, this face of the tree round works to illustrate the relatively barren landscape of the of Canadian law’s interaction with Indigenous legal orders and the oral traditions that help constitute and maintain them.</p>

<p>On the reverse face of the tree round are the words “Into Time Out of Mind,” which are arranged in a circle and repeat three times. This face represents the incredible time-depth of Indigenous oral histories and traditions in Canada, that they pre-exist the Canadian state and legal system, and constitute an enormous, collective repository of traditional knowledge, laws, and culture. The words are painted into the tree round, representing the how oral histories are so often embodied in and derived from the land. Finally, the depictions of oral histories and of Canadian law’s engagement with them on the two sides of the piece represent both Indigenous and Canadian legal orders, and that they must co- exist.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Cameron’s essay discusses the Canadian courts treatment of Indigenous oral histories within a broad history of colonial assumptions about Indigenous peoples:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Thinking back to the discussions in the seminar, as they relate to oral histories as evidence, the main question that I kept coming back to was why, as in the case of McEachern in Delgamuukw, have courts been so unwilling or unable to accept Indigenous peoples’ own accounts of their histories, in their own words? I think there are a myriad of interconnected factors and reasons that persist despite positive changes.</p>

<p>In <em>Delgamuukw</em>, the SCC’s decision was that laws of evidence would have to be adapted to accommodate oral histories and place them on equal footing with the historical evidence with which courts are familiar. This may have removed a significant procedural hurdle, but the structural problems that remain are both pervasive and troubling. Canada’s highest courts have continued in some cases to contest oral histories as sources of evidence; Val Napoleon’s analysis of <em>Benoit v Canada</em> (a case about whether Treaty 8 included a tax exemption clause) showed that the Federal Court of Appeal relied heavily on an expert witness who stated that there is “nothing inherent in orality that fosters accurate transmission of information and no evidence to suggest genetic or cultural immunity from the forgetfulness that plagues the rest of the human species.” Despite this problematic language, and after its judgement in <em>Delgamuukw</em>, the SCC refused to hear an appeal. Lower courts also continue to devalue oral histories as evidence in disregard of established legal principles, which works to defeat Indigenous interests and claims and to subordinate them to the maintenance of Canadian state sovereignty.</p>

<p>Underlying the widespread rejection of oral histories as hearsay pre-<em>Delgamuukw</em> and the spotty application of the principles enunciated in that case since are structural and historical issues with not just the Canadian legal system and state, but with the Western model of law. One major problem is the continued presumption of ultimate Crown sovereignty, which as was discussed numerous times during the seminar, seems to have no firm legal basis and results from nothing other than the historical process of colonization and imperialism. This is a continuation of colonial attitudes of European superiority that have been expressed since contact.</p>

<p>The standard against which Indigenous peoples have been measured for so long has been European society and culture, in a clearly racially supremacist manner. This has translated into numerous failures to recognize Indigenous societies and cultures on their own merits, as sovereign peoples, and as having their own legal systems and laws. The failure to understand and accept Indigenous histories in terms of their purpose, form, and content serves to deny the history and therefore legitimacy of Indigenous society and sovereignty. This was required in order to justify European imperialism and colonization to extract wealth and resources from the land; Indigenous legal orders, cultures, and societies were therefore denied on the basis of racial superiority and economic necessity.</p>

<p>This was continued in Canadian law and policy from the beginnings of Canada’s statehood. Confederation and the constitution, especially section 91(24), and the many governmental projects that followed such as the Indian Act and the program of residential schools, served to intervene in the affairs of Indigenous peoples, to break up their societies, and to force their assimilation into the European state that had been created on their traditional territories. While policies and attitudes may have changed, the underlying presumption that the Canadian Crown is sovereign remains. As was stated by Gitx’san Chief Delgamuukw in the opening statements to the BC Supreme Court in the <em>Delgamuukw</em> litigation, for the Gitx’san the ownership of and sovereignty over territory derives from the marriage of the Chief and the land; that power is reproduced by the maintenance of laws that ensure respect for the spirit in the land, and is carried in part in oral histories. Oral histories are a source of sovereignty for many other Indigenous peoples in Canada as well. This means that no matter the weight or respect accorded to oral histories, the presumption of Canadian sovereignty necessarily denies Indigenous sovereignty, and so denies the deeper role and importance of oral histories.</p>

<p>Chief Delgamuukw’s explanation of the role of oral histories in establishing authority over and ownership of territory also helps explain why courts have failed to accept or struggled to understand the importance of oral history evidence. The Canadian legal system is a cultural enterprise based on Euro-Canadian conceptions of law, and the courts ability to understand and interpret Indigenous oral histories is affected by the inherent biases of that system. </p>

<p>The judiciary is largely schooled in a western legal system that imports a particular way of thinking, rejects interactions between humans and non-humans, denies supernatural and spiritual elements, and generally adheres to a positivist, scientific perspective. This creates an inherent difficulty in trying to understand and employ Indigenous oral histories, which is exacerbated by the fact that most judges and lawyers are not Indigenous and have little experience with Indigenous cultures. I was reminded of Finch CJ’s discussion in his paper “The Duty To Learn” about this issue; he quotes Professor John Borrows as stating that the risks for misunderstanding Indigenous oral histories and legal orders are already enormous as a result of the lack of familiarity with Indigenous cultures. This is compounded by the fact that Western and Indigenous cultures have different conceptions of time, space, historical truth, and causality, which are often the very things under examination in a legal setting. A serious result is that while oral histories may be admitted as evidence, judges unfamiliar with or biased against Indigenous knowledge systems and laws may accord them little or no weight, as was the case in McEachern CJ’s judgement in <em>Delgamuukw</em>.</p>

<p>The dangers that these issues pose are considerable and various. A first and serious consequence is the potential violence damage done to the Elders who are frequently called upon to relate oral histories to the courts. As knowledge keepers and repositories of culture and law, elders in Indigenous cultures are in a respected position where their words have value and are replete with important lessons and teachings. Having oral histories placed in a position to be questioned and cross-examined is a humiliating and vulnerable experience. In the words of one elder who testified before McEachern CJ in <em>Delgamuukw</em>: “We told that judge things we don’t even tell our own grandchildren. We made ourselves naked in front of him. And he did not believe us.” This loss of authority to be believed and have beliefs and statements respected, key aspects of the position as an elder, is damaging to personal dignity and identity and the cultureand laws that an elder may carry.</p>

<p>Finally, while courts have begun to engage with oral histories and traditions as and acceptable form of evidence, this framing has severely limited the status of oral history. Oral history is not merely a cultural archive or record, it is itself law. In <em>Tsilqhot’in</em>, oral histories about law were limited to the evidentiary stage for the purposes of establishing the extent of occupation, and not as laws themselves. This supports the contention that Canadian courts are only engaging with Indigenous laws as historical fact and for evidentiary purposes, and that they deny its status as an extant normative order.</p>

<p>Despite these serious issues, which require considerable structural and attitudinal changes, there are some hopeful points. A more positive note was struck in the trial decision of <em>Tsilqhot’in</em> by Vickers J’s treatment and use of oral histories. Vickers J’s attitude towards oral histories is a marked improvement from the days of McEachern CJ at <em>Delgamuukw</em>, where the latter refused to accept them as evidence of fact and argued that they were too wrapped up in belief and mythology. By contrast, Vickers J said he was “privileged” to hear the oral histories presented before him. He also defended oral histories as carrying independent evidentiary weight where appropriate, and appears to have demonstrated a reasonably good grasp of the nature and form of Tsilqhot’in oral histories, in line with some of the principles about cultural competency that Finch CJ would outline several years after the decision. While the Tsilqhot’in Nation’s title was not ultimately recognized until the Supreme Court’s decision in 2014, the judgement stated that approaching questions like that of Aboriginal title must necessarily involve the Aboriginal perspective, including the laws and traditions of the group.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Cameron’s essay ends with a hopeful prospect for change for which his and the other projects in the archive are powerful demonstrations:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>As the “old school” justices who decided cases like <em>Delgamuukw</em> and <em>Van Der Peet</em> are replaced by a younger generation of lawyers and judges who are more educated in and familiar with Indigenous laws, legal orders, and cultures or who are themselves Indigenous, we may see the changes to the Canadian legal system and culture that are necessary to fully implement an Indigenous perspective, and place Indigenous legal orders implemented in a manner that respects the inherent sovereignty of Indigenous nations.</p>
</blockquote>
