<p>Laura Beaudry, for her 2021 the project in the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar, also examined the history of Treaty 8, but brought to bear her own family history as participants in the treaty-making. Her PowerPoint presentation entitled “The Illusion of Treaty Eight: Forged Signatures, Gross Misrepresentation and Broken Promises”. documents the rushed timeframe for the signing of treaty, the suspicious duplication of identical X marks, the inclusion of the Métis along with First Nations in the treaty process and revisited the dissonance between the text of the treaty as a surrender of title and the Cree and Dene Nations’ understanding of the treaty as one of peace and friendship.<sup data-fn="beed738b-2452-496c-9970-38fc561f60d1" class="fn"><a href="#beed738b-2452-496c-9970-38fc561f60d1" id="beed738b-2452-496c-9970-38fc561f60d1-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Laura grew up near the site where Treaty Eight was first signed and her first two slides locate her family in the historical narrative and the way in which the Indian Act provisions regarding women have legally disconnected her from her treaty status and that she “can no longer live in the house she grew up in or on the land that taught” her.</p>

<p>Laura’s narrative explains the inclusion of the Métis in the Treaty, including her ancestors, and the circumstances under which negotiations took place which compromised any meeting of the minds regarding the terms of the treaty. Quoting historian Rene Fumuleau she writes: “The haste of the Treaty Commissioner in securing Indian signatures on a piece of paper removes any illusions that the Treaty was a contract signed by equal partners.”</p>

<p>Laura’s PowerPoint addresses the most significant issue arising from Treaty 8 and that is whether, in light of the common intent of the parties, the treaty was legally capable of being a complete surrender of their Aboriginal title. As I have previously described this issue was addressed by Justice Morrow in the <em>Paulette</em> case who concluded that there was a prima facie case that there was no meeting of the minds on the issue of surrender but rather, based on the Treaty Commissioners oral assurances, an affirmation of indigenous hunting fishing and trapping rights.</p>

<p>In the <em>Paulette</em> case, there was evidence before Justice Morrow regarding Treat 11 that “Some signatures purport to be what one would call a signature, some are in syllabic form, but most are by mark in the form of an "X". The similarity of the "X"'s is suggestive that perhaps the Government party did not even take care to have each Indian make his own "X" in a series of slides Laura documents a similar problem with the signatures on Treaty 8.</p>

<p>Laura’s concluding thoughts suggest that the history of Treaty 8 still has some final chapters to be written regarding its renegotiation or replacement with a modern day treaty. In this regard it is important to note that in relation to that part of Treaty 8 which extends into what is now the Northwest Territories as well as with Treaty 11, the federal and territorial governments have negotiated modern-day treaties without prejudice to the historical treaties.</p>
