<p>The Royal Proclamation of 1763 was issued by King George III on 7 October 1763. It established the basis for governing the North American territories surrendered by France to Britain in the Treaty of Paris, 1763, following the Seven Years’ War. The Royal Proclamation also set the constitutional structure for the negotiation of treaties with the Indigenous inhabitants of large sections of Canada. It is referenced in section 25 of the Constitution Act, 1982. As such, it has been labelled an “Indian Magna Carta” or an “Indian Bill of Rights.” The Proclamation, while in its form a unilateral statement by the British Crown, in substance reflected the principles of recognition and respect for Indigenous rights, that had been shaped by and was reflected in the Covenant Chain.</p>

<p>Part IV of the Proclamation of 1763 sets out the British Crown’s law and policy on Indian-Crown relationships. It recognized that lands possessed by Indians anywhere within British Dominions in North America are reserved to them unless and until ceded to the Crown or its representatives and protected this territorial integrity by restrictions on grants, settlements and purchases. It also temporarily closed to settlement and development, subject to Indian consent, large parts of the North American interior, reserving it for use of the Indian nations but designating it a free trade zone. To the extent that future settlement was permitted by the Crown on lands which Indian nations were prepared to sell, such purchase was to be made by a representative of the Crown according to the treaty protocol at a public meeting and assembly of the Indians. Moreover, the Proclamation contemplated no intrusion into the powers of Indian self government.</p>

<p>The principles of respect and recognition of Indigenous rights reflected in the Covenant Chain and embodied in the Royal Proclamation of 1763 provided the legal framework in the post-1763 period within which the Crown negotiated for the purchase of lands in Canada in possession of the Aboriginal peoples. This framework was applied in the old Province of Quebec in what is now Ontario, and as colonial expansion pushed westwards, in what is now Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta and in parts of British Columbia and the Northwest Territories.</p>

<p>Robert Williams has characterized the Indigenous vision of treaties as “jurisgenerative devices, transmitting and sustaining the legal meanings of a relationship of close connection between different peoples on a multicultural frontier”. Thirty years after the Treaty of Lancaster and a year after the Royal Proclamation of 1763 there was another significant treaty council in what is now Canada at Niagara. In her essay on the Two Row Wampum Belt Rhaea Bailey essay explains the significance of the 1764 Treaty of Niagara.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The two-row-wampum belt was of great significance …when the Royal Proclamation came into effect right after the treaty at Niagara in 1764. The treaty at Niagara was entered into in the summer of 1764 and was looked upon as the "most widely representative gathering of American Indians ever assembled." After the terms of this agreement were presented gifts along with a two-row-wampum belt were exchanged to represent the Aboriginal perspective on the terms of the treaty. Again, this belt was presented as it represents an understanding of peace, friendship, and respect where each nation can remain sovereign uninterrupted by the other.</p>

<p>However, the Royal Proclamation was interpreted without factoring in the Treaty of Niagara and the important Aboriginal understanding of that treaty. Instead, the Royal Proclamation has been interpreted to exercise sovereignty over First Nations. This claim was asserted even though the man who presented the treaty and its terms, Sir William Johnson, stated that " these people had either have arisen from the ignorance of the interpreter, or from some mistake; for I am well convinced, they never mean or intend anything like it."</p>

<p>The two-row-wampum belt was critical to the way that these First Nations interpreted the proclamation and its concepts have to be interpreted with regard to the meaning of the belt. When the Proclamation is read in conjunction with the two-row wampum belt it brings the meaning of various concepts stated in the proclamation into a new light. These are concepts that discuss self ---determination, in which the two-row wampum and its conveyance of such concepts have not been included.</p>
</blockquote>

Unfortunately, apart from the physical belt itself I cannot locate his essay file with his photographs in which he documents the construction of his Niagara belt and at this point his contributions to the Archive are anonymous. I am hoping however that its online publication will bring with it attribution. This is the belt.</p>

<p>For the historical context of this belt I will draw upon the work of my colleagues, Professors John Borrows and Darlene Johnson, both Indigenous legal scholars, who have written about these events, incorporating both the documentary and oral history. Their accounts provide powerful, indeed compelling, evidence of the continuity of Indigenous-Crown treaty-making traditions and the critical importance of understanding Indigenous perspectives on those traditions.<sup data-fn="8b00b893-5c9a-42b5-a1dc-181c0300ba8e" class="fn"><a href="#8b00b893-5c9a-42b5-a1dc-181c0300ba8e" id="8b00b893-5c9a-42b5-a1dc-181c0300ba8e-link">1</a></sup> As described by Professor Borrows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The treaty at Niagara was entered into in July and August 1764, and was regarded as 'the most widely representative gathering of American Indians ever assembled,' as approximately two thousand chiefs attended the negotiations. There were over twenty-four Nations gathered with representative nations as far east as Nova Scotia, and as far west as Mississippi, and as far north as Hudson Bay. It is also possible that representatives from even further afield participated in the treaty as some records indicate that the Cree and Lakota (Sioux) nations were also present at this event. It is obvious that a substantial number of First Nations people attended the gathering at Niagara. Aboriginal people throughout the Great Lakes and northern, eastern, and western colonial regions had travelled for weeks and months to attend this meeting.</p>

<p>When everyone was assembled, William Johnson presented 'the terms of what he hoped would prove a Pax Britannica for North America.' Johnson read the terms of the Royal Proclamation to representatives of the nations and a promise of peace was given by Aboriginal representatives and a state of mutual non-interference established. Presents were exchanged to certify the binding nature of the promises being exchanged. Johnson then presented the Covenant Chain and wampum belts and stated: </p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Brothers of the Western Nations, Sachems, Chiefs and Warriors:</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>You have now been here for several days, during which time we have frequently met to renew and Strengthen our Engagements and you have made so many Promises of your Friendship and Attachment to the English that there now remains for us only to exchange the great Belt of the Covenant Chain that we may not forget out mutual Engagements.</p>

<p>I now therefore present you the great Belt by which I bind all your Western Nations together with the English, and I desire that you will take fast hold of the same, and never let it slip, to which end I desire that after you have shewn this Belt to all Nations you will fix one end of it with the Chipeweighs at St. Marys [Michilimackinac] whilst the other end remains at my house, and moreover I desire that you will never listen to any news which comes to any other Quarter.<br />If you do it, it may shake the Belt.– but keep your Eyes upon me, &amp; I shall be always ready to hear your Complaints, procure you Justice, or rectify any mistaken Prejudices. If you will strictly Observe this, you will enjoy the favor of the English, a plentiful Trade, and you will become a happy People. On the contrary, if you listen to any People whatsoever, who do not like the English you will lose all these Blessings, and be reduced to Beggary &amp; Want.</p>

<p>I hope you are a People too wise to prefer War, and Ruin to Peace &amp; Prosperity. You have already felt some Wants, which must make you sensible of the necessity you are under to respect, and Esteem the English.</p>

<p>I Exhort you then to preserve my Words in your Hearts, to look upon this Belt as the Chain which binds you to the English, and never let it slip out of your Hands.</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>Professor Darlene Johnson has expanded on this account and the meaning of the presentation of the two covenant chain wampum belts:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Sir William Johnson offered the great Covenant Chain Belt to the Anishnaabeg. He assured them that he was not interested in stealing their lands.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>My children, I clothe your land, you see that Wampum before me, the body of my words, in this the spirit of my words shall remain, it shall never be removed, this will be your Mat the eastern Corner of which I myself will occupy, the Indians being my adopted children their life shall never sink in poverty.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In the metaphorical usage of the Anishnaabeg, the Mat refers to their country. The British required only the eastern corner of the Great Lakes region and the Anishnaabeg would flourish with them as allies.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The 2015 Final Report of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission, (TRC) after referencing the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and the Treaty of Niagara, illustrated and emphasized that these foundational documents have contemporary significance and recommended that Canada promulgate a new Royal Proclamation of Reconciliation.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>On October 7, 2013, Canada marked the 250th anniversary of the Royal Proclamation of 1763. The governor general of Canada, His Excellency the Right Honourable David Johnston, spoke about the proclamation’s importance.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This extraordinary document is part of the legal foundation of Canada. It is enshrined in the Constitution Act of 1982, and it sets out a framework of values or principles that have given us a navigational map over the course of the past two-and-a-half centuries…. Its guiding principles—of peace, fairness and respect—established the tradition of treaty-making, laid the basis for the recognition of First Nations rights, and defined the relationship between First Nations peoples and the Crown…. All history reverberates through the ages, but the Roya Proclamation is uniquely alive in the present-day. Not only is it a living constitutional document, its principles are of great relevance to our situation today in 2013 and to our shared future…. Without a doubt, we have faced, and are facing challenges, and we have much hard work to do on the road to reconciliation, but it is a road we must travel together. In modern time, the successful conclusion of comprehensive land claims agreements are an example of the principles of the Royal Proclamation in action.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Across the country, Indigenous peoples also commemorated the anniversary, calling on Canadians to honour the spirit and intent of the Royal Proclamation. In British Columbia, where very few Treaties were signed, the First Nations Summit leaders issued a statement reminding Canadians that the principles set out in the proclamation were still relevant in present-day Canada. They said,</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>With Confederation, the First Nations–Crown relationship has regrettably been guided by federal control under the constraints of the Indian Act, not by the principles articulated in the Proclamation…. The time has arrived for all Canadians The Challenge of reconciliation to move into an era of recognition and reconciliation between First Nations and the Crown. Although there is general recognition of Aboriginal title and rights, far too often these rights exist without an effective remedy. There are many solutions that have the potential of moving us to where we need to be. Such solutions include the negotiation of modern-day treaties, agreements and other constructive arrangements, consistent with the principles of the Proclamation.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Across the river from the Parliament Buildings in Ottawa that October, Idle No More supporters gathered in Gatineau, Québec, at the Canadian Museum of Civilization to commemorate the Royal Proclamation as part of a national and international day of action. One of the organizers, Clayton Thomas-Muller, said, “We are using this founding document of this country and its anniversary to usher in a new era of reconciliation of Canada’s shameful colonial history, to turn around centuries of neglect and abuse of our sacred and diverse nations.”</p>

<p>In Toronto, the focus was on the Gus-Wen-Tah, or Two-Row Wampum Treaty belt, used by the Mohawk in Treaty negotiations with colonial European officials. As Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal people gathered to mark the historic day, speaker Davyn Calfchild said, “Everyone needs to learn about the Two-Row and the nation to- nation relationships it represents. It’s not just for Native people; it’s for non-Native people too.” The gathering ended with a march as people carried a replica of the Two- Row Wampum through the streets of the city. Those who commemorated the Roya Proclamation and the Two-Row Wampum emphasized that the principles and practices that cemented the Treaty relationship remain applicable today.</p>

<p>The Royal Proclamation of 1763, in conjunction with the Treaty of Niagara of 1764, established the legal and political foundation of Canada and the principles of Treaty making based on mutual recognition and respect. A royal proclamation is also an important symbol. Issued at the highest level, it sends a message to all citizens about the values and principles that define the country. There is a need for a new proclamation that reaffirms the long standing, but often disregarded, commitments between Canada and Aboriginal peoples. The proclamation would include an official disavowal of the Doctrine of Discovery and commitment to the full implementation of the United Nations Declaration.<sup data-fn="98c455ea-7f17-4afe-b235-0b788ae4f064" class="fn"><a href="#98c455ea-7f17-4afe-b235-0b788ae4f064" id="98c455ea-7f17-4afe-b235-0b788ae4f064-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
