<p>Kepler Rotheistler for his 2017 project for Penal Policy turned to storytelling as his methodology in reflecting on themes in the seminar. Drawing upon his experience in the UBC Indigenous Community Legal Clinic, the seminar’s visit with prisoners at Matsqui, a review of the jurisprudence on self-defence in the context of a prison culture of violence, and a chapter in <em>Justice behind the Walls</em> in which the scenario of kill or be killed was played out in real time before my eyes, Kepler in “The Last Kills” constructs a riveting story of his own. At the end of the story Kepler describes how his experiences inspired ‘The Last Kills’</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The idea for this story came to me after having done some preliminary research on self­ defense in the context of prison culture. I had planned on exploring the likely consequence of the increase of violence in prisons over the past decade on the frequency of self-defense being used as a defense by offenders in prison.</p>

<p>After reading some case law and publications related to violence in prison, however, it became clear that the answer to my question was obvious: it would increase. At the same time as I was realizing there was not much for me to argue in a legal argumentative essay, we visited Matsqui prison and had the chance to talk with inmates. One of the most common refrains I heard that night was that these men just wanted to be treated like human beings. I'm not sure there are less effective ways of humanizing individuals then writing a legal argumentative essay about them killing each other.</p>

<p>So I decided on a story. The plot itself is a combination of elements of the "Life and Death of the Electric Man" chapter, R v McConnell 1995 ABCA 291, and R v Short ONSC 4594. In R v Short, E.M Morgan J asks the question that would serve as the backdrop to the events of the story: "What happens," he asks, "when a maximum security facility produces maximum insecurity?"</p>

<p>The characters are collages of individuals I've met, read about (often injustice Behind the Walls), or seen in the criminal justice system. I spent the Fall semester at the UBC Indigenous Community Legal Clinic, and while I was there I saw a man being sentenced in First Nations court after a brutal assault on an elderly man. He had been in Kent previously after a series of break and enters. It was one of the most affecting things I've ever seen, with the offender and his family all crying profusely as they tried to explain how this young boy went into Kent a fool and came out a violent criminal.</p>

<p>The story became a search for an answer to that question. I read about administrative segregation and what it can do to people. In the article "Buried Alive: Stories From Inside Solitary Confinement" in GQ Magazine I came across some of the horrifying self-mutilation that happens in solitary, including one of the speakers' experience of watching someone across the corridor from him slit his own throat in the middle of a conversation.</p>

<p>I also read with some frustration some of the solutions offered by governmental departments. In Standing Against Violence: A Safety Review of BC Corrections, Parliamentary Secretary for Corrections Laurie Throness calls to meet the increasing violence with signs on prison walls that remind prisoners of a zero tolerance approach to violence. He mentions that "As we have seen, there are many assaults in correctional centers - some with the use of weapons and some which cause serious bodily harm. If inmates knew that they would be prosecuted to the fullest extent of the law for violence or threats of violence, thus carrying the potential of more serious time in the federal system, it might act as a deterrent." He also calls for an increase in the number of guards and harsher punishments for inmates found with narcotics. In other words, more of the same.</p>

<p>The story is a reflection of the frustration I felt between speaking with inmates and reading solutions like those suggested by Mr. Throness. The ramifications of engaging or not engaging in violence in prison don't need to be written on signs and posted on walls; the prisoners already know full well what they are. The only question is whether or not official sanction is worse than the consequences of not doing something. In this story at least, the answer for Tom is clear.</p>

<p>A final note on the language in the story. Obviously most of it is not fit to print in a law paper, and some of it is language I myself would never use. I tried, however, to mimic at least some of the cadence I heard when visiting Matsqui. The language seemed so often to reflect the sad reality of prison life, one in which the necessity to look strong could wage war with the desire to be a good person. It was hard, angry, and profane, and yet there was something so human about the bitter voice behind it.</p>

<p>As I mentioned in my email, I've never really written fiction. I've tried a few times, but I never found anything that I felt was worth writing about. The chance to explore the ideas we've discussed in class has been profoundly engaging for me. I hope you enjoyed reading it.</p>
</blockquote>
