<p>Predating both the documentary film and the prison diary is an audio interview and accompanying essay by Emily Casey and Heather Pineo that was completed as part of the 2008 Penal Policy seminar. The interview was with a client of mine whose history of abuse and dysfunction as a young Indigenous woman is reflected in<a href="/projects/the-worst-punishment/" data-type="link" data-id="/projects/the-worst-punishment/"> Carolynn</a> <a href="/projects/the-worst-punishment/" data-type="link" data-id="/projects/the-worst-punishment/">Conron’s</a> diary. In order to protect confidentiality of both mother and child the full essay and the audio interview are not included as part of the Archive but nevertheless they merit inclusion. The names of the mother and child have also been changed.</p>

<p>I had suggested to Emily and Heather when they expressed interest in a project involving the experience of mothers in prison that they interview my client, who I shall refer to as Lucy. Lucy had given birth to a baby girl, Amy, while awaiting sentence in Alouette Correctional Centre and was able to bond with her daughter as part of the provincial mother child program before its cancellation. After receiving a federal sentence she was transferred to the Fraser Valley Institution for Women and was able to care for baby Amy as part of the federal mother child program. At the time of the interview with Emily and Heather Amy was fourteen months old, the last twelve of which had been spent at Fraser Valley. The following account is taken from Emily and Heather’s essay, based on their interview with Lucy.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In her interview Lucy described her chaotic early life. Her mother had left her at a very young age. She remembered her mother packing her belongings in black garbage bags and turning to the children and asking them, "Who do you want to live with, your mother or your father?" Her sister said her mother, so Lucy followed her as well. Her mother had several abusive boyfriends, with one of them sexually and physically abusing the girls. When her mother found this out, she sent her daughters away to live with their father and stayed with the boyfriend. Lucy started drinking and using drugs at the age of 12, a couple years after moving in with her father.</p>

<p>At 17, Lucy was raped by a man who had been her friend for 5 years. He raped her, strangled her, and left her for dead. Lucy acted unconscious until the man reached his truck to leave, at which point she ran. He chased her, caught her, and raped her and strangled her again. As a young adult Lucy had three children all of who were removed from her care by the Ministry of Children and Family Services. After losing her children, Lucy fell into a deep depression and started thinking a lot and using crack cocaine. She began dating Amy’s father. During this period of alcohol and drug abuse Lucy robbed a Burger King using a pellet gun to get some cash to buy more crack. This led to her first prison sentence of two years. At that point in her life she was not interested in changing and refused to take programs. After her release from prison she went back to her previous drug life with the same partner who was very physically abusive. It was after an incident where she anticipated a particularly violent outburst from him that she took a shotgun that was kept on the premises to protect against wild animals and shot and killed her partner. Unbeknownst to her at the time she was already pregnant with Amy.</p>

<p>After her arrest on July 2, 2006 and charged with murder, Lucy awaited her trial date at Alouette Correctional Centre for Women. While she was in custody she gave birth to Amy in March 12, 2007. While at ACCW, Lucy and her baby daughter lived in a single cell together. ACCW has had 13 babies go through the prison, and Amy was the 9th baby to come into the prison. At ACCW the mothers were given a temporary absence to go the Fir Square, located at the Woman's Hospital, when it is time to give birth to their babies. Fir Square is a unit that helps with women who have drug problems that may affect the babies health when they are pregnant. Many pregnant inmates at ACCW fit this criteria and consequently will use the programs at Fir Square. Fir Square gives support to the prison mothers and ensures the babies get the care they need in order to be healthy. After the babies are given a clean bill of health, they then advocate for the women to take their babies back to ACCW with them. Lucy explained that she did not fit the criteria required by Fir Square as "Amy was a healthy baby, I wasn't taking any medicines or anything when I was pregnant with her, but she was able to get into Fir Square because the nurses at Alouette helped out, got her in there" so she could be able to bring her daughter back to ACCW with her.</p>

<p>When the mothers are at Fir Square, the Ministry of Children and Family Development (Ministry) became involved and assessed if the child should be allowed to return with the mother to ACCW. In Lucy's case the Ministry decided that Amy would be allowed to return to ACCW with her mother, but under a Supervision Order. In February 2007, Lucy pled guilty to the manslaughter of her partner, and was sentenced to six years minus time served. Her sentence would be served at the Fraser Valley Institution for Women which is a federal prison.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>It was just prior to her sentencing hearing in Prince George that I became involved in Lucy’s case. Lucy had been told that the Ministry did not support her taking Amy with her to serve a federal sentence and pursuant to the supervision order had plans to apprehend Amy while Lucy was transferred to Prince George for sentencing. Lucy contacted Sarah Rausch, the then Director of UBC Law School’s Aboriginal Community Legal Clinic. Sarah’s intervention and advocacy forestalled Amy’’s apprehension and I agreed to represent Lucy in advocating that Fraser Valley accept her and Amy as part of the federal mother child program. Although the program by this time had been part of federal corrections for some years, Fraser Valley institution had only had one other mother and child at the institution. Fortuitously the then Warden of Fraser Valley, Nancy Renshall, had been the warden of the BC Correctional Institution for Women and had overseen a mother child program there and was well-versed in the benefits of these initiatives and accepted Lucy and Amy into the program. Emily and Heather’s essay continues the narrative:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The successful application of Lucy and her daughter to be part of the mother child program gave rise to a number of media stories. Some were evenhanded representations of the program, reporting on the positive effects of having mother and baby stay together as well as the safeguards in place to ensure the safety of the baby. Others were less favourable with headlines such as "Killer to Raise Baby in Prison". Emily and Heather in their review of media and blogs identified some of the crueler comments. One website, "imperfect parent," commented “Seems like that baby is off to a bad start, but at least she'll know at an early age how to shank somebody." Other reactions focus less on the babies safety and more on the "punishment" of her mother. One reader to an article on the United Press International website: "Can they come up with a more lame, sexist, and absurd idea than this? They are rewarding her for killing her husband. She should not even have supervised visitation with that child. If a man kills his wife, he hardly gets treatment even remotely close to this example. The world has truly lost his mind and social conscience”.</p>

<p>The negative media, particularly in the Fraser Valley area which has long been a conservative stronghold, was not lost on the new Harper Conservative federal government whose electoral platform had proposed a tough on crime agenda. Shortly after Lucy’s transfer to Fraser Valley a new policy directive was issued from Ottawa which considerably narrowed the criteria for admission to the mother child program, the effect of which would have precluded Lucy’s admission. Warden Renshall, understanding more than anyone the benefits of the program, interpreted the change in policy as not having retroactive effect and in light of Lucy and Amy excellent start at FYI continued to support Lucy and Amy’s inclusion.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>These benefits become evident from Emily and Heather’s interview with Lucy:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Amy 's presence has been very beneficial to Lucy's rehabilitation. Contrasting this sentence with her first imprisonment for armed robbery helps to illustrate this. She refused all programs in her first term, and went back to substance abuse immediately after being released. Amy’s presence has given Lucy the impetus to remain clean and partake in the programs offered in prison. It is acknowledged that drugs are available in prison, despite the efforts of corrections staff. Lucy says, "I guess there are drugs in jail, but you know, you make that choice. If you're going to use drugs in jail, you're going to risk losing your child. I want to change my life. I don't want to go back to that life.. The best thing about my life has been my children. When I lost my children, my life got really bad. . .1 think that Amy has given me a chance to have a good life again, and with that, I have to change everything about my old behaviour… She's got to come first.</p>

<p>Another concern that is often raised is in regards to the safety and health of a baby in prison. Lucy stresses how well looked after Amy has been: "She's really well taken care of. . . What better supervision could there possibly be?"</p>

<p>Mother and child spend nearly all of their time together, and Lucy has not experienced any ill will towards herself or her daughter. If anything, Amy makes the other women happy, and they tell Lucy that her daughter brightens their day. Many women in the FVI have children of their own on the outside, and although Amy’s presence may make them wistful, Lucy says, "In the long run, if it makes them miss their children, maybe it will help them change their lives for the better."</p>
</blockquote>

<p>FYI is organized on a cottage model where are a small group of women are assigned to each cottage, and are able to cook their own meals. The primary difference in the setup for the mother and child program is that mother and daughter have their own cottage which is not shared with other women and adjacent to the cottage is a small play area enclosed by a wooden fence so that Amy can play outside in good weather. The only other prisoners allowed into the cottage are those prisoners who are screened and designated as babysitters and who like Lucy have taken first-aid and parenting courses.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The babysitters look after Amy when a mother is doing programs, but Lucy is selective with her time and makes decisions to increase the time she can spend with her daughter. "I was offered a program, Spirit of the Warrior, which covers a lot of issues, but I'm not wanting to take it, only because it's an all day program for 3 months and 1 don't want to be away from her all day for 3 months. That's too long. I like to work outside but I would much rather be with her." Lucy is enrolled in a parenting program, however, which she says helps her deal with her self esteem and her feelings.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Lucy explained that Amy is not isolated in her home at the FVI though. She takes Amy along to the Native Sisterhood meetings, where Amy enjoys watching the women drumming. Lucy says, "She is really social. She dances in front of everybody and loves the drums." She is being socialized amongst adults, but child development experts also stress the importance of children being able to interact with other children. There are plans being made for Amy to be able to go out in the community to interact with other kids, either with Lucy or a friend from the community, but Lucy is hesitant to take the latter approach, and plans to do it on a trial basis. Regardless, Lucy points out that she will be eligible for parole in 2009 and Amy will be able to play with other children before she reaches the age of three: "Long before she has any bad effects from it, she is going to be out playing with other children."</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Emily and Heather described their first meeting with Lucy and her daughter:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Waiting in the visitor's room for Lucy and Amy, we did not know what to expect. When they entered, Lucy pushing Amy in her stroller, we were quickly impressed by what a happy and well-adjusted baby Amy seemed to be. The mother-child bond was palpable, and after witnessing five minutes of interaction between Lucy and Amy, the thought of them being separated is heart wrenching. Amy is walking and talking, and we are inclined to believe she is developing more quickly than the average baby. Dr. Ruth Elwood Martin examined Amy and reported her findings in a January 6, 2008 letter to Sarah Rauch, Director of the First Nations Legal Clinic at UBC:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>On Dec 4 2007, I had the pleasure of conducting my most recent routine well-baby check of baby and mother in ACCW health care clinic. Amy is developing exceptionally well. She is an extremely social and responsive baby who demonstrated paediatric development milestones that were equal to and advanced for a baby aged 8-months. For example, she was starting to mimic individual words, she claps her hands when her mother sings songs with her and she plays peek-a-boo. She had been sitting well for several months; she crawled with agility, pulled herself to a standing position and could stand for a few seconds on her own. Her fine motor skills were well developed - she could pick up individual cheerios from a table. She was breast feeding exclusively for her milk nutrition. She took juice from a cup with a spout. She ate regular food at meal times. [As a post-script to this examination - subsequently, Amy began to walk at 9 months of age.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Lucy also feels that Amy’s development is "above average," and believes this is because the two of them spend so much time together. "I don't have to worry about rent, bad relationships, bad influences. I think it’s good that the babies can stay with their moms for a while. She's so happy and healthy, and I think developmentally she is doing really good."</p>
</blockquote>

<p>A year before Emily and Heather’s interview I had began meeting with Lucy and her daughter just after their arrival at Fraser Valley Institution and over the next two years saw them on a regular basis. As did Emily and Heather I met mother and daughter in the visiting area. While I have spent time in all of the visiting areas in the federal penitentiaries in the Pacific region this particular visiting area at Fraser Valley has a particular association for me. Another of my clients who as a teenager was sentenced to a life sentence for murder but is now on parole, had some years previously spent time at Fraser Valley and as the resident artist had painted a series of butterflies in a glorious spectrum of colours and sizes on all the walls of the visiting area. After Amy had got used to my visits I was able to pick her up and carry her around the room introducing her to one particularly beautiful blue Brazilian butterfly, Bella Blue, a butterfly who had been the centrepiece of many sleep time stories I had told my own children. At another level of connection my daughter Melissa had given birth to her first child, and my grandchild, Jackson, just a few months after Amy was born. Melissa and her family now lived in Hawaii where she had opened a boutique for expectant mothers and babies. On one of my visits to Hawaii Melissa gave me some baby clothes from the store for Amy that were a little more special (and Hawaiian) than the clothes that Lucy was able to order from catalogs available to her. Also the owner of a Vancouver young children’s store who had read one of the positive news reports about Lucy and Amy’s experience at Fraser Valley contacted me and donated some other baby gifts for Amy.</p>

<p>From my visits to Fraser Valley and my conversations with correctional officers at the front gate the contrasting views in the community about having women keep their babies while undergoing imprisonment, reflected in news reports, was mirrored inside prison. Some officers, knowing that I was visiting Lucy and Amy, volunteered their opinions, expressed in a variety of ways, that having the baby in the prison grounds reminded them that, despite the serious crimes that the women at Fraser Valley had committed, both prisoners and staff shared a common humanity reflected in the presence of baby Amy. That recognition of their common humanity was often a difficult thing to maintain in a correctional culture. For some officers just hearing the toddler’s giggles made their day. A few officers who remembered Lucy from her previous stay at Fraser Valley were clearly impressed by her changed attitude and the care and affection she showed towards her daughter.</p>

<p>But there were other officers who were clearly resentful that Lucy was not receiving her full measure of punishment by being allowed to have her baby with her. This was reflected not just in the curt reception I sometimes received at the front gate but also the hand search of my briefcase lest it conceal yet more gifts other than those that I had officially handed to them for scrutiny. To these officers new baby clothes were presumptively contraband. On one occasion I had to seek the intervention of the Warden to ensure that the clothes reached Amy before she had already grown out of them.</p>

<p>In my later interviews with Lucy, shortly before she was released on parole with Amy, she told me how important the interview with Emily and Casey had been as it provided her the space to tell her story to people who, unlike correctional and parole officials, were listening without the purpose of making judgements about her.</p>
