<p>The final project in this section of the Archive is that of David Khang. David produced his work for the 2020 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar and in 2021 was my research associate in documenting and helping me curate the archive. Elsewhere I have acknowledged David’s contributions to the archive but here I want to introduce David’s project “The Unsettling of Turtle Island: a Visual Essay”. David, prior to his coming to Law School, had already established a career as a doctor of dentistry and as an avant-garde visual and performance artist. His project for the seminar drew upon his impressive body of performance art. In his essay David describes his gradual metemorphosis from a 10 -year- old immigrant to Canada from Korea to a member of the settler population:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>[I]t would not be until I began law school that I felt emotionally ready to shed the protective cocoon of “immigrant” identification in a white dominant society (Anglo or Franco –choose your poison). It is an identification with inherent limitations – one that relegates persons of colour to non-naturalized second-class category of belonging, no matter what one’s identification documents say, no matter how long one’s length of habitation, or settlement. Today, I properly claim the self-identification as a settler, as well as a migrant, on Turtle Island.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>David’s essay presents a narrative, grounded in his performance art, centering on the migration of the Monarch butterfly. It is a narrative that in terms of being a medium for legal analysis is unprecedented in any legal literature.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Monarch butterflies (Danaus plexippus) are one of the indigenous habitants of Turtle Island. They know not any borders, though they have well-travelled migratory routes. While there are several migratory paths of the monarch butterfly, the largest migratory group travels between Niagara, Ontario – in the summer, to Michoacán, Mexico – its wintering ground. Lepidopterists recognize these migratory routes as integral (not incidental) and distinctive (though not unique) to the species that pre-date European arrival. The routes are regularly, sufficiently, and continuously used by the monarchs. <sup data-fn="6dba437a-685c-4e66-8596-d6441b671143" class="fn"><a href="#6dba437a-685c-4e66-8596-d6441b671143" id="6dba437a-685c-4e66-8596-d6441b671143-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Monarch butterflies freely fly over the borders of the three nation-states that have now colonized Turtle Island. The monarchs need not ask permission from anyone to travel, nor are they required to obtain permission prior to travel. They do not need special passes or passports to cross these borders, including the overlapping borders of the Haudenosaunee.</p>

<p>The Haudenosaunee – the Six Nations made up of the Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and Tuscarora, do use a passport. Their passport is a statement, a declarationthat affirms their sovereignty and nationhood, as well as a demand for nation- to-nation relationship, as recognized by the signatory parties in a treaty signed in the hot, late summer days of 1764, in the summering grounds of the Danaus plexippus.</p>

<p>…<br />In 2003, during my graduate studies in Master of Fine Arts at UC Irvine (CA), I had a dream. I dreamt that I was floating in space, and butterflies were attached to my tongue, and flying in swirling patterns all around me. I was speaking in butterflies. I woke up from my dream, and quickly made a sketch of the image from my dream.</p>

<p>One year later, I incorporated live monarch butterflies into a live art performance work. The butterflies were temporarily and gently tethered to silk strings, which were in turn sutured to my tongue. For the duration of the performance, we were connected to one another bodily and materially. As the butterflies awoke from their slumbers, they slowly crawled out from the darkness of their temporary home – a tome that was a hollowed-out book, towards the light outside. Once the strings were cut, the butterflies freely flew away – knowing no borders.</p>

<p>More than ten years later, in 2015, a year before starting law school, I printed large swaths of fabric with repeating patterns of monarch butterflies. With the fabric, a military uniform was custom tailored (coincidentally by a tailor who happens to make legal robes in Vancouver). I wanted to see what would happen when two culturally contradictory signifiers – the classic beauty of a butterfly and the accoutrement / paraphernalia of war – were collapsed and mixed together.</p>

<p>I also used some of the remaining fabric to create legal tabs as part of yet another uniform for my next endurance performance project – as a law student, and possibly as a future legal “warrior”.</p>

<p>To complement his essay David presented a framed paper collage titled “The Metamorphosis of Turtle Island”.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For my class project, I used the same printed fabric, and used it as a backdrop against the shape of Turtle Island, cut out into a sheet of paper. I wanted to see what the outcome would be when the two animals were visually and symbolically juxtaposed and layered together.</p>

<p>The shell (or carapace) of the turtle is made up of 13 (larger) main scutes and the 28 marginal (outer) scutes. According to the tradition shared by Anishnaabe (Objiwe), Cree, and Haudenosaunee (Mohawk), the turtle’s scutes represent the 13 lunar months and the approximately 28 days that make up each lunar month.</p>

<p>In experiencing art, size – or more properly scale – does matter. Looking at an image of something is not the same as “experiencing” the art – not only with one’s eyes, but as well with one’s body, sensing the material beyond the optical and the intellectual, by being in a spatial relationship with the art. I plan to use the current work that is 22 inches by 18 inches in dimension, and scale it up to produce a larger 3-dimensional sculptural object, closer to 6 feet by 4 feet (or perhaps larger), to be installed hanging in air or submerged in a pool of water.</p>

<p>As David prepares for his latest career as a lawyer he reflects on the future that lies ahead in the relationship between indigenous peoples and the settler populations:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>What would a metamorphosis of Turtle Island look like? Does it include Reconciliation as laid out by the Truth and Reconciliation Commission? Is it, in part, the enactment of United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples? How will Bill 41, the 2019 Declaration on the Rights on Indigenous Peoples Act be implemented in British Columbia (or is it realisticallyimplementable)? Given that Bill C-262 died on the order papers in 2019, what might this mean for the rest of Canada? Does the metamorphosis of Turtle Island require interconnections and solidarity with the Indigenous Peoples in the southern portions of the continent?</p>

<p>Without yet having satisfactory answers to these questions, I look to the future with both hope and trepidation. I am reminded of the story of the hummingbird that was shared with us in the first weeks of working at the Indigenous Community Legal Clinic. The hummingbird worked diligently to put out a spreading fire in its community by dropping one water droplet ata time to make small, yet impactful difference over time. As baby lawyers graduating from law school, we are vulnerable to the weight of the profession’s deep conservatism, the temptations of a comfortable life as mere cogs in the legal machinery that is designed to preserve the status quo, and the fear that we may not be able to make a difference in the end.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>An unanticipated but highly appreciated part of David’s project was a parting gift to other participants in the seminar:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For my baby lawyer friends / colleagues soon to leave the protective nest of law school (and our seminar class together), I wanted to make a small and intimate parting gift for each participant. As a response to how much I have received from the experience this semester, this was an opportunity for a gesture of reciprocation – my humble version of a potlatch of sort.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Each card has a baby turtle design, and each turtle has a unique monarch butterfly pattern. Each card also has a word written in Korean, which is translated into English. I chose the words intuitively, after reflecting on each seminar participant as a person whom I got to know over time, and then asking what unique gift he/she/they brought to the seminar. My hope is that the word chosen resonates with each person, as it does for me. This small gift carries with it the fragile hope that metamorphosis of Turtle Island is indeed possible.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Metamorphosis of our relationships and our societies require open hearts and minds. Being communicative creatures that we are, old language can sometimes prevent us from getting to the next stage of experience. Continuously dreaming up new languages to describe a reality that is not yet is vital for change, growth, for metamorphosis.</p>
</blockquote>
