<p>Karen Osachoff in her project “The Spiritual Significance of Treaty 4”, for the 2008 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Seminar, created a traditional Lakota Starblanket to reflect on the conflict between the different interpretations of Treaty 4, signed in 1874 in what is now Saskatchewan.</p>

<p>It is not usual in legal essays to begin with a statement of the writer’s family history. However, in many of the projects, the authors, in explaining the source of and inspiration for their work, begin by locating themselves in a social and cultural context.<sup data-fn="901e81b0-fec6-4990-a439-d5700078acc4" class="fn"><a href="#901e81b0-fec6-4990-a439-d5700078acc4" id="901e81b0-fec6-4990-a439-d5700078acc4-link">1</a></sup> Karen writes:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>My spirit name is Quietly Sitting Bear Woman. My English name is Karen Osachoff and I am Cree/Saulteaux/Lakota from the Pasqua First Nation. My mother's name is Beverly Kahnapace and she is Cree/Saulteaux. My father's name is Lionel Obey and he is Cree/Lakota from the Piapot First Nation. Both of these reserves are located in Treaty Four Territory near Fort Qu'Appelle in Saskatchewan.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Karen’s essay expounds on a theme that it is explored in other projects regarding the dissonance between the written text of Treaty Four and the other Numbered Treaties and the oral understanding of the Treaty Nations. According to the text which the Indian Nations “cede, release, surrender and yield up to the Government of the Dominion of Canada for Her Majesty the Queen and Her successors forever, all their rights, titles and privileges whatsoever, to the lands included within the following limits”. The Treaty Nations’ interpretation is that the treaties created a relationship based on sharing the land and mutual respect and not a surrender or extinguishment of their rights to their territory.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>My paper and project is based on my past academic experience with the legal/written text of Treaty Four and the conflict between the different interpretations of Treaty from an oral/spiritual understanding of my Nation. While academically, I thought I understood Treaty Four and what it meant, through an oral history experience with my people and other Indigenous peoples, I have come to realize some of the depth and true meaning behind our treaty. Treaty Four to our people represents a relationship, a spiritual relationship that has not been broken over time, but continues to exist. What my ancestors agreed to in 1874 is still relevant today, in that we agreed to share the land with our settler brothers and (sisters), and we agreed that we would respect the way of life of the settlers but that reciprocally, our way of life would be not interfered with. Regardless of how colonization has unfolded, the intent of our ancestors has always existed, and while there may have been a long, painful, genocidal process that we had to go through, our people are still here, thriving and spiritually recovering.</p>

<p>A Lakota star blanket is traditionally made and given to honor someone or an experience, and to me, this blanket represents honoring my ancestors for their tremendous vision, fortitude and love as they signed Treaty Four. While the blanket is based on a traditional Lakota blanket, it is also my contemporary understanding of the treaty relationship. My experience in bringing this star blanket to life is much more significant than what can be seen. To begin the process, I approached a well respected woman in the community to help me, Gloria Larocque, whose expertise in sewing made the blanket come to life. The process of getting to know her, and see the care she put into the project with me was overwhelming and beautiful because it felt like a spiritual process in itself to seek guidance from a traditional woman who knew how to sew.</p>

<p>My current Chief also stated that the main spiritual significance of our treaty is that we survived the brutal cultural and physical genocide that has been inflicted on us since Treaty signing. This has been a span of 133 years from beginning to present. In my family, it has been seven generations since Chief Piapot, my great-grandfather on my dad's side, signed Treaty Four. And while many of our family members have died from the effects of this genocide, there are still many of us left, coming back to our culture, getting higher education, and walking the red road. My Chief walks the red road and hopefully many more of our people will walk this road too.</p>

<p>The colors I picked for this star blanket are the four colors that are used at sun dance. I picked them because they mean healing, gratitude, insight, cleansing of negativity and the red road. They also represent the four directions, and the four races. The four races being represented in this blanket is interesting too, because there are representatives of all four races in Treaty Four territory now. The orange burst in the center represents a contemporary understanding of our people, that we are bursting out from our colonial shackles and with the spiritual foundation of our treaty we will only flourish. The original spirit and intent of treaty still exists!</p>

<p>I dedicate this project to the thousands of Treaty aboriginal people who have lost their lives in this country and their ancestors who are still here.</p>
</blockquote>
