For her project in the 2004 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar Jennifer Yong  created a sculptural ceramic bowl which both in its execution and thought her artist statement demonstrates the evocative power of art to bring into focus the evolving life of the law.

Jennifer’s bowl is one of a number of projects where although I have retained possession of the work, through the course of several moves including the building of the new Allard School of Law, I have lost the accompanying artist statements or essays and therefore had not originally included them in the Archive. In Jennifer’s case it was only very recently that I realized that her name was inscribed on the bottom of the ceramic bowl and through that I was able to contact her and obtain her consent to use her work. Although Jennifer also had not retained a copy of her original artistic statement she reconstructed it, which as I remembered it retained its elegant brevity, but with  the added benefit of looking back some twenty-four years since its creation and the important developments in the law and in our collective growing understanding of Indigenous peoples relationships to their ancestral territory. Jennifer explains the layering of her glazes:

>This glazed, ceramic bowl sculpture was hand-built using a slab building and mould technique. The clay of the bowl represents the land. The translucent white glaze represents First Nations’ intrinsic relationship with the land; the spherical shape reflecting the sui generis, expansive, and continuing nature of this interplay since time immemorial; the “dance” of translucency between the glaze and the material beneath signifying the multi-layered, interconnectedness of the land and aspects of First Nations life including culture, traditions, language, spirituality, and knowledge. The green glaze was poured over the edge of the bowl, this technique reflecting the intention, violence, and acuteness of contact and colonialism, and the glaze itself representing the imposition of fee simple ownership. Where this glaze “breaks” on the rim, the white glaze and clay can be seen, ever present, persisting, and resisting. When I made this piece, I felt that the rim of the piece signified the contemporary moment, with the jagged edges representing our society’s collective movement into an unknown future around the recognition of Aboriginal title in British Columbia. Twenty-two years later, the jurisprudence has evolved. There have been declarations of Aboriginal title over certain, discrete lands in British Columbia. My response to this piece today connects its jagged edges to the pain of this history and process and the need for collective healing.
