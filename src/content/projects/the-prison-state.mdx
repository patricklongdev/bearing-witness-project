<p>Tarek Elneweihi for his project in Penal Policy Seminar in 2013 chose the medium of song in composing “The Prison State” that reflected the “chord deep within me” that the seminar readings evoked. In his Artist Statement Tarek sets out its inspiration and the written lyrics.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This is a song I wrote and recorded for the last assignment of my academic career. It is a commentary on the Harper government's eagerness to introduce new laws and policies under the pretense that they are "waging a war on crime". The government passed bill C-10 and they are currently implementing their new policies. These policies will result in longer prison terms, mandatory minimum sentences, and a loss of many of the rights that prisoners in Canada previously had.</p>

<p>Many Canadians are not sympathetic towards those who will suffer as a result of the new policies. Many Canadians share a view that once convicted, a person is no longer deserving of human rights or of the effort and time invested by those opposing C-10 and the Harper government's policies. While I understand that there may be individuals in prison that have done terrible things and thus should not be on the "top of the list" of people deserving support from other Canadians, there are far more individuals who aren't much different from you or I that will suffer as well.</p>

<p>This song avoids the controversy relating to the debate about whether convicted criminals are deserving of rights and what those rights should be. The message of this song is that regardless of what you think about the convicted criminal, for every person put in prison there are typically many more innocent individuals who will suffer as well. These individuals consist of family members, friends, and other people who have formed close bonds with the imprisoned person. Any pain inflicted on the convicted criminal will affect those other individuals as well. It's what we all commonly refer to as the "ripple effect". How the other individuals are affected will depend on the circumstances and will vary from person to person. Often times the effect on the others can lead to the same mental and emotional problems that help cause people to participate in criminal behaviour. Thus, the cycle continues. As the close relative of a man who I believe was wrongfully convicted of a heinous crime and consequently spent years in prison, I speak from experience when I say that the "ripple effect" can have extremely negative consequences for others. Regardless of whether a person deserves to be in prison, we should be careful about the policies we allow to be applied within the Canadian Justice system. Putting an individual in prison is a serious matter and should not be taken lightly. In many cases it may be necessary, but we should keep in mind that to some degree, imprisoning people is always a lose-lose situation for all of the members of our society. You never know what the consequences of the ripple effect will be, and we should only take that risk when absolutely necessary.</p>

<p>During the Penal Policy seminar Tarek had told me that his uncle had served many years in prison for a crime he always claimed he did not commit. Tarek’s uncle, 37 years after his original conviction for second degree murder, was exonerated in 2021.</p>
