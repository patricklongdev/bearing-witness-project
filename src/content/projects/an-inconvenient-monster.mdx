<p>Greg Sexton, for the 2008 Penal Policy seminar created a suite of four drawings as “an artistic rendition of issues within the Canadian correctional system.” In the introductory essay to his drawings Greg locates his work in the context of the Harper Conservative Government’s pivot to a tough on crime agenda, where harsher sentencing sanctions were introduced and important principles established in the 1992 Corrections and Conditional Release Act came under attack. The drawings and essay address four issues that highlight the deeply problematic state of corrections in Canada; the 1994 incident at the Kingston Prison for Women, the lack of independent adjudication in correctional proceedings, the mental and physical strain of arbitrary segregation, and the overrepresentation of Aboriginal People in the Canadian correctional system.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Drawing 1: The incident at the Prison for Women</h3>

<p>The 1994 commission of inquiry conducted by Madam Justice Louise Arbour examined a series of events that took place at the Kingston Prison for Women from April 22 to 26 of that year. Arguments began amongst several prisoners and female guards resulting in violent clashes between the two groups. In an unprecedented assault on staff, a female was guard hung by her neck and almost killed. This produced tremendous hostility, resentment and fear among the female staff at the institution. Fearing that the situation was growing out of control, warden Therese Leblanc issued an emergency response requesting assistance from the nearby Kingston Penitentiary. A six-man incident and emergency response team (IERT) was assembled and ordered to the Prison for Women to quell the uprising violence.Ordinarily an IERT is not supposed to be called until all alternative measures and negotiations have failed. After hearings were held into the events that unfolded thereafter, Justice Arbour determined that:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>…it is inconceivable to suggest that between the evening of April 22nd up until the evening of April 26th when the IERT was called in to intervene, nothing could have been done to bring the situation in that unit under control.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>By the time the IERT arrived the violence had subsided and the women involved in the attack had been placed into segregation. Male guards are not allowed to strip female inmates except in an "emergency" situation, which by the time that the cell extractions were preformed, was certainly not the case. The entire incident was filmed as per protocol in these types of situations and the events eventually witnessed by the entire country were horrifying. One inmate subject to the IERT cell extractions, Brenda Morrison, described the events that unfolded as follows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>It kind of happened fast. The men applied the body belt and the handcuffs. That is when I offered to take my shirt off, or something. But they kept putting the chains on. Then they pushed me down on the cement and ripped my clothes off… l don't know how anybody can do that to somebody and live with themselves. How they can walk in there, rip my clothes off and say "Its okay, l was doing my job; it was professional"… l don't know how any man can do that to any woman and say it was their job. As far as I know, it's a crime.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Several women were then subsequently required to undergo illegal cavity searches. This is the most intrusive form of searching a person other than surgery and the consent of the person undergoing the cavity search must first be obtained. CSC witnesses testified that inmates were offered inducements such as cigarettes, showers and the removal of restraints in exchange for their consent. Threats or inducements by persons in authority have long been held to vitiate voluntariness and Justice Arbour expressed her disgust at this "absence of a culture respectful of individual rights." All agencies involved continue to hold the position that they acted responsibly and have not done anything wrong.</p>

<p>The drawing is an attempt to portray one particularly violent scene from the video aired on CBC's The Fifth Estate where the IERT performed a strip search on the inmate Joey Twins. The unnecessary brutality was appalling as they forced Ms. Twins to the ground and ripped the clothes off her back. The needless fear and intimidation of the entire process was hard to watch and burnt this image into my mind. The impulsive decision making process taken by prison officials bear an eerie resemblance to a shoot first and ask questions later approach; and although questions are being asked, no one is to be held accountable for this injustice.</p>

<p>Therese Leblanc is now a Deputy Commissioner overseeing all female correctional institutions in the country.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Drawing 2: Quis custodiet ipsos custodes?</h3>

<p>The title Quis custodiet ipsos custodes? is a Latin phrase that translates to "Who watches the watchmen?" The essential problem was originally posed by the Greek philosopher Plato in his work on government and morality. It has since been adopted by many theorists and philosophers over the years and has been a prevalent concern in Canadian corrections throughout the past decade. In the events described above, the segregated women were not given the opportunity to exercise their right to counsel as required by CCRA Regulation 97(2) and §10(b) of the Charter of Rights and Freedoms. One of the many glaring deficiencies elucidated by the Arbour Report was the lack of compliance with the law by those entrusted to enforce it:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>One can fairly predict that unless some sanction is attached to the lack of compliance, the entitlement to legal assistance upon placement in segregation will remain largely illusory. This is an instance where, although the law is clear, it was largely unknown to those responsible for administering it; it also does not seem that the law is perceived within CSC as particularly appropriate.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Upon release of the Arbour Report in 1996, then Commissioner of Corrections, John Edwards, announced his resignation stating that every prison in the country was not following the law with regard to their segregation units. His successor, Ole lngstrup, immediately formed the Task Force on Administrative Segregation to investigate compliance with the CCRA within segregation units in Canadian prisons from coast to coast. The task force reported that while initial compliance with the law within segregation units was almost nil, following their tour it had increased to approximately 90%. While this new level of compliance was satisfactory to the commissioner, the task force would not settle for anything less than strict 100% compliance, as is required in every single other area of law. In order to achieve this goal the task force recommended independent review of all decisions to send a prisoner to segregation, to ensure compliance with the CCRA.</p>

<p>However, the Commissioner continues to refuse to allow independent arbitration, preferring the regional oversight mechanism, which takes an ex-post-facto review approach (too little too late). Despite the findings of the task force and support from parliament, the segregation process takes place today exactly as it did 10 years ago. There is absolutely no training in this area and very few new guards have even read the report. The training course on how to conduct a segregation hearing has only been held once.</p>

<p>The drawing for this section represents the continued resistance by CSC to allow outside governance. There exists a mentality within such historical institutions among the staff that others cannot understand the complexities of their jobs and no one is in a better position to make these decisions than them. The law is seen as nothing more than a hindrance to their ability to properly performed their duties. The robed judge represents the efforts of the Task Force on the Administrative Segregation to get independent adjudication within prison walls and bring justice to those often forgotten. The man in a suit is a representation of CSC administration accompanied by the staff that protect and foster this mentality. I felt the imposing riot gear was again appropriate to showcase the culture of intimidation involved not only from keeping outsiders from seeing in, but also maintaining institutional customs. Progressive training and legislation only go so far if it is all ignored the first day reporting to duty. The question of "who watches the watchmen" is magnified in situations were such imbalances of power exist and independent adjudication is the best available solution.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Drawing 3: How to break a man</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>You spend twenty hours a day on your back, somewhere between waking and sleeping, trying to keep your mind out of the dark places but you can't.<br />-Glen Rosenthal, Edmonton Max Segregation Unit 1996</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The condition and treatment of prisoners in segregation units throughout Canada is far below the socially and legally acceptable threshold. The case of <em>McCann v. The Queen</em> outlines the following dreadful conditions of the British Columbia Penitentiary segregation unit. Inmates assigned to segregation were housed in tiny concrete cells measuring 11 by 6½ feet with a 5-inch square window facing the hall that could only be opened from the outside. Prisoners slept on a raised cement slab covered only by plywood and a 4-inch thick foam pad. The lights were on 24 hours a day but dimmed from 100 watts to 25 watts at night. The only piece of furniture in the room was a combination toilet and water basin just at the end of the bed. A radio was available just outside the inmate's reach which the guards would tune to loud static or foreign talk programs. Exercise was permitted for only 30 minutes a day which was confined to the 75 foot caged walkway outside the cell, during which guards would have a gun trained on the inmate through the fence the entire time. Eventually these conditions were found to be unacceptable by the courts, however Correctional Services Canada (CSC) ignored most of the orders and made very minimal improvements to the cells.</p>

<p>Guards are able to justify this behaviour because day in, day out, they are faced with the reality of men who are locked in cages. There has to be some justification of why these men are there and why the guards are able to treat them the way they do. The rationale is that the inmates are so dangerous to society and themselves, that treating them as a constant threat it the only way to ensure safety. Because these men are beyond the pale of humanity, the guards are justified in their actions towards them.</p>

<p>BC Penitentiary was closed in 1980, however we continue to see this problem repeated in prisons across the country. In 1996 a survey was sent to 400 prisoners in segregation units throughout Canada to gauge the treatment of prisoners within these units compared to the general population, which was supposed to be the same. However very similar problems were found and one prisoner (quoted above) provided a disheartening account of the conditions he faced and the toll it takes on a prisoner's life:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>You've gone from 150 pounds to 190 pounds and every muscle in your body is either knotted or atrophied … You look older, fatter, disgusting to yourself when you look in the mirror. Your self esteem is sub-zero … You want to complain about not being transferred but you know that will piss somebody off and you will never get out…Your mind seems full of dark thoughts that don't belong there. You can't carry a conversation anymore because you're afraid one them might slip out.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Prisoners report that the uncertainty is the worst part; that they don't know why they're in there and what they can do to get out. It is a situation that fuels anger and violence which in turn results in more punishment. Prisoners often resort to self-mutilation just to ensure they are still human and can still experience human feelings. This is the paradigmatical example of inescapable punishment. When a man is reduced to an animal, he will behave like an animal, like a monster.</p>

<p>The account of Glen Rosenthal quoted above was the inspiration behind this third drawing. His words so clearly described the desperation and injustice faced by those detained in segregation. The simplicity of this piece represents the barren day to day life experienced by many prisoners throughout our country. There is absolutely nothing to pass the time other than staring at the cracks in the wall and retreating to your own thoughts. The few items present in the cell symbolize the bare minimums for survival provided to inmates, as they are afforded very little, if any, personal items and luxuries. Unlike the other drawings, a distinct frame was drawn enclosing the image to truly accentuate the level of confinement faced by those in segregation. The effect this can have on such hardened men is heartbreaking and should gnaw at the very conscience of society. We must ask ourselves what it is that truly gives us the right to treat other human beings in this way and why society continues to condone it. This is fundamentally a system designed to break a man in the only way left possible and, as seen above, this goal is unfortunately being realized.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Drawing 4: An Inconvenient Monster</h3>

<p>As of 2001, Aboriginals accounted for approximately 3% of the Canadian population, however 21% of male prisoners and 30% of female prisoners were of Aboriginal descent. In British Columbia Aboriginals accounted for 20% of the prison population versus 10% of the provincial population. Systemic and societal assimilation has created this situation of overrepresentation as many Aboriginal teens grow up in poverty and are pressured with the influence of drugs and alcohol at an early age. "The prison has become for many young Native people the contemporary equivalent of what the Indian residential school represented for their parents." Many of these Aboriginal offenders learn very little about their ancestral culture in their youth and often only begin to reconnect with their roots once incarcerated. Aboriginal brotherhoods within prison walls help each other make this connection and begin their healing journey. Educating themselves about Aboriginal spirituality is often the only way to regain a sense of purpose and make through their days behind bars. The following quotes from Aboriginal prisoners illustrates this realization:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I'm trying to learn, I'm trying to relearn what it is to be an Indian person. Because jails, partying, suicides and death are not the Indian way.</p>

<p>Most of us in here never knew Native spirituality until we came to prison. Now we have found it. Now we are bringing it back to our people.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Aboriginals have struggled and argued over the years for the right to practice their spirituality within correctional institutions in order to aid in their rehabilitation and reintegration into the community. Both §2(a) (freedom of religion) of the Charter and §35 (Aboriginal rights) of the Constitution protect their right to participate in spiritual and healing ceremonies and in 1983 a group of prisoners at Kent Institution, underwent a hunger strike in support of this cause. Additionally, Corrections and Conditional Release Act provides specific provisions (§§80-38) to address the needs of Aboriginal offenders.</p>

<p>In 1993, Roger Brock, warden of Matsqui Institution, put law into practice and fully supported a spiritual journey for four Aboriginal offenders to go on a supervised day trip; during which they would gather materials for a traditional powwow ceremony to be held on prison grounds for the benefit of the entire Aboriginal brotherhood. Men like Roger Brock have been few and far between within the correctional institution, but the sad reality is that all that he was attempting to do was see the law function as it was originally intended to.</p>

<p>This drawing is a representation of the spiritual journey undertaken by many Aboriginal offenders. It begins at the root of the problem where Christianity was used to abuse and assimilate Aboriginals into our culture. The rotting totem represents the loss of Aboriginal identity, first through residential schools (cross), which subsequently led to drugs (needle) and alcohol (bottle), fueling the rise of Aboriginal overrepresentation in Canadian prisons. The roots engulfing these items symbolizes the systematic effect this continues to have on Aboriginals to this day.</p>

<p>The center of the piece depicts the current struggle to maintain and find that lost spirituality within the confines of their current situation. The sweatlodge enclosed by barbed wire accentuates this constant struggle and how much they have had to resist and fight to simply reacquaint themselves with the true meaning of who they are. Finally, "the prayers of Aboriginal supplicants rise with the smoke and mingle with all living creatures." The rising smoke signifies the final self-actualization reached by many offenders as they regain their sense of spirituality. I chose the mythological spirit animal Thunderbird as he is the creator and protector of all elements and spirits. It has been claimed that this mystical bird is the link between the Aboriginal people and the Great Spirit.</p>

<p>The title of this drawing was used to represent the overall project as it truly epitomizes the theme of self-created societal ills. Albeit, there exist a monster within this system, this society, but lest we forget it is a monster that we have created.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Greg’s drawing of the Inconvenient Monster references the 2001 statistics where “Aboriginals accounted for approximately 3% of the Canadian population, however 21% of male prisoners and 30% of female prisoners were of Aboriginal descent”. As I have referenced in other parts of the Archive, in 2026 Aboriginal offenders now account for 30% of the male federal population and 50% of the female.</p>
