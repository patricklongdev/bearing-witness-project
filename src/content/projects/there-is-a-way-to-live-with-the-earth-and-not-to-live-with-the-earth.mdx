<p>Joanne Pompana’s project for the 2008 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Seminar is the largest in the Archive in size and is in two parts. The first part is a reflection essay on the meaning of Covenants and the Indigenous relationship to land and spirituality; the second part is a 8’ x 4’ foot storyboard, incorporating photographs, text and art forms reflecting a Lakota Indigenous framework of legal understanding. I have included some of the images of the storyboard in this commentary with Joanne’s narrative guide:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Justice is defined in myriad ways across the planet. In the western hemisphere, the blindfolded woman holds the scales of justice (balance) weighing truth on one sidewith law on the other. Our various cultures; and in particular with our Indigenous Sioux culture (that I am a part of), strive to find our way forward through multiple upsets and disagreements (legal issues), by returning to our ceremonial practices; and thereby, comingback into a semblance of balance (John Rawls, Original Position). So we don’t… ‘jump to conclusions’ …about people, places and things. In 2020 reality, this is easier said than done. Many of our folk have been seduced by the western legal world hype, to set our Indigenous truth and laws aside for temporal baubles; to see our justice ways as lacking in some form. Indigenous justice generally, has always been a form of restorative justice. Whether with the Indigenous peoples on this side of the big water, or the other side. One was required to compensate for an injury in the community (tribal circle- encampment),through some act of reconciliation, that was recognized by all of the community; and involved all of the community.</p>

<p>As John Trudell proscribed in Thunderheart :</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><em>There is a way to live with the Earth, and a way not to live with the Earth. We choose the way of Earth.<br />And further on…<br />There is a difference between freedom that is illusion, and freedom that is reality. We choose reality.</em></p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The other six ceremonies that Pte Skan Winyan, White Buffalo Calf Woman (WBCW), brought to the people; when she fell from the universe as Wohpe (the falling star painted on vision board); and later came to us as a female buffalo woman, can take place at this time. She is the law for our Pte Oyate (Buffalo People, the Sioux Nation). Our Akicita, (our dog soldiers, veterans, military, police, lawyers, judges), were the first to recognize WBCW and invite her to their camp; where Chief Standing Buffalo Walking, upon notification, waited to greet her and make her welcome. Therefore, also at the center of the vision board and sundance tree, just before it branches out, are photos of our Akicita; Leonard Peltier (Dog Soldier); Clifford Pompana (Lakota Dakota Sioux), Floyd Wood (Cree Sioux), Ken Jobin (Iroquois Cree) – PPCLI, Signal Core and Airborne Veterans respectively; Laurence Flewelling Lethbridge (Grumbly) on the other side (Hunkpapa Lakota) was a Pilot Officer RCAF WW ll Veteran. All actively protect (and protected) the sacred; the sacred hoop (the surrounding red circle painted on the vision board and reflected in the star constellation above); the children, the ancestors, the ceremonial land, the old people, the two legged, four legged the ones that swim, crawl, fly and walk…so the people can keep living in a good way, in a sacred way, from generation to generation; within the scared hoop.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>At the top of the vision board is a photograph of Frank Fools Crow, with arms outstretched, holding our lives in the air through prayer, who was Black Elk’s nephew, and Clifford Pompana’s grandfather. Frank Fools Crow died in 1989 at 99 years old. No one has replaced this ceremonial chief. And in Sioux culture, there is no rush. When the person that is to take that position arrives, then that position will befilled. Black Elk found his recipient of his knowledge a year before he died. He travelled through the States and lastly through Alberta Saskatchewan and then Manitoba in Canada where he found Clifford Pompan and anointed Clifford Pompana at nine years old, to carry the culture for his generation. In our oral history, we roamed through three provinces and 10 states; which is why there is an 1820 map of North America on the lower part of the vision board.</p>

<p>Creating The Red Road Healing Society with the logo remember, restore, renew, as seen at the base of the vision board with the attached pamphlet, was foundational to keeping the ceremonial spirit alive out west in the urban community of Edmonton. The Veteran’s Hall on reserve in Sioux Valley, Manitoba(photo at top with all the hanging star blankets); where all the veteran names are encoded on the feathers of the Chiefs Head Dress Design that adorns the hall entrance; and with further veteran inscriptions inside the hall, also keeps the sprit of the people alive. Clifford Pompana, his sister Joyce Pompana RCAF and his brothers Wayne RCAF and Calvin Pompana SEAL are all inscribed there.</p>

<p>On one side of the vision board is a plastic rosary with a recitation of the fourth glorious mystery quoting the Ark of the Covenant being visible in the Jerusalem Temple of heaven; the sacred law of the Jewish people; and next to it, are words from John Trudell’s Hangin’ from the Cross song. (The idea was to make that song interactive on the vision board by pressing a button.) Guadalupe, the brown Madonna of the America’s is synonymous with WBCW to many of our traditional Sioux. Juan Diego who first saw her has already been sainted.</p>

<p>Hehaka Sapa, Black Elk whose quote, Know the Power that is Peace, is photographed at the top of the vision board above Fools Crow, and who he is to be beatified soon, was able to draw parallels and bridges between our seven sacred ceremonies that WBCW brought to the people; and the seven sacraments of Catholicism. Black Elk, Clifford Pompana’s great grandfather, predicted that our tree would flower for all races and nations; and indeed, at this time, there are sundances everywhere (not so 25 years ago). Some are more traditional than others. The idea from Black Elk was not to become Lakota; the idea was to become themselves in relationship to Mother Earth.</p>

<p>We are living in very exciting times; although, the ‘pain of the past’ and ‘fear of the future’ is ever present in our lives today, for ongoing reconciliation. The Red Road Healing Society has grown to become the Red Road, Pte Oyate, Indigenous West, Family Resource Network; and is located in ORANGE HUB of Edmonton, Alberta. We are currently working on providing an exchange with our young relatives in the States, for a horseback ride in this area; to further Wipe the Tears of our people. I hope you enjoy this vision board!</p>

<p>Joanne Lethbridge <br />PompanaNiya Pte <br />Win<br />Pejuta Waste Win</p>

<p>Hunkpapa Lakota Sioux and Highlander Celt</p>

<p>Joanne’s essay, “Covenants: The Indigenous Original Position in Relationship to the Land” presents a comparative analysis of indigenous and particularly Lakota understandings of covenants as relationships with land and those of Western philosophy, religion and law:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The word covenant in its most conventional sense means “a solemn and bilateral promiseto do or not do something specified”. Wikpedia then goes on to describe the word covenant in theology, law and government, places and institutions, in literature, in film and television, in music and in video games. In the Oxford English dictionary covenant is described as “a formal agreement, a contract. In Black’s Law Dictionary the first definition of a covenant is “[a] formal agreement or promise, usu[ally] in a contract”; then sixty more entries follow defining particular forms of covenants related to property and contractual obligations.</p>

<p>Historically, the word covenant pertained to a contract of sort between a people and the Creator over the dominion of Earth. Later came another covenant between thosethat understood ‘this relationship to the Earth’.</p>

<p>In 1689, one of the founding fathers of liberalism, John Locke described property as a relationship between a person’s toil with the Earth. Years later another scholar named John Rawls described what he termed the “original position”, a ‘form of innocence to return to in order to be able to think things through, basically with an uncluttered mind’, and whether it was a possible for mankind to ever return to this state.</p>

<p>Eventually these types of thinkers made contact with the Indigenous groups of North America (also known as Turtle Island) in the form of theorists, philosophers, discoverers and theologians. I suspect upon contact they realized that these people in this new land were extraordinary, as they lived in “the original position”. The Indigenous people would of unknowingly held up the proverbial mirror and showed the newcomers their conscience. On reflection of this fact, the newcomers decided to make a covenant with them. The Indigenous people of Turtle Island welcomed their new relatives and wished to “kindle a flame of learning and knowing” with them. And so a relationship began, in peace and friendship, with each party offering their best gifts to the other.</p>

<p>However the relationship was far from being on an equal footing literally, because one group was much more solid on the Earth then the other. The group from across the ocean was so far from the Earth, and the gift of life that flows from the Earth, that they had left the Earth to be on the sea instead. They had been searching for that point of origin, on the deep ‘sea of unconsciousness’ out in the middle of the ocean.</p>

<p>Today, most people who enter into relationships with Indigenous people know that most Indigenous groups have a peculiar relationship to the land. In law, this is termed a sui generis relationship, a unique relationship; but is it really? Or was this a way of ensuring redemption in the future? This paper is going to explore “the original position” from the perspective of the Indigenous relationship to the land in the form of “covenant”.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Indigenous Relationship to the Land</h3>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2008/10/Joanne_Pompana_05-1024x1024.jpg" alt="Original Living Wheel of
First Nations diagram" class="wp-image-2474"/></figure>
</figure>

<p><em>There’s a way to live with the Earth, and a way not to live with the Earth; We choose the way of Earth. </em><br />John Trudell – Sisseton Dakota Sioux</p>

<p>These words may sound simplistic but they are loaded with a complete world view that is pertinent to most Indigenous groups around the world. This is the great law, the great peace as the Haudenosaunee recorded in their two row wampum belt that became the Covenant Chain in 1692. “You in your boat and me in my canoe”, we travel parallel paths. We can join these paths when we brighten the relationship between us through mutual understanding, respect, honour, justice. When those ideals are misplaced we cannot brighten our converse. As often said in law of contracts, the agreement is only as good as those who undertake it. The covenant cannot be engaging for both parties if it does not brighten the mutual understanding between them.</p>

<p>How can such covenants happen if both parties are not regularly communing on the mountain with the Creator?</p>

<p>Indigenous people did not have exactly the same experience as those on the other side of the world with Moses, Noah and Jesus; however, there were holy people throughout the nations. For the Sioux Nation, and the Lakota in particular, they are called “wicasa wakan or winyan wakan”. Many nations still practice ceremonies that put them in touch with a power greater than themselves; and many nations still have holy people that have carried the knowledge down through the generations through trial and tribulation. As well, there are still many who try to possess this knowledge or put on the guise of being wisdom keepers; however, like those Israelites the knowledge is within the blood memory. That knowledge may awaken in life or remain dormant for a generation. It may come alive through cultural practices; but it is never for sale, or to be solicited.</p>

<p>There is a way to live with the Earth, and a way not to live with the Earth.. In the last 100 years this has become a very tricky process, even for those that had been steeped in cultural practices, as the confusion and chaos is everywhere. However; nevertheless, there are those that continue to endure no matter what. Possibly this is due to their genes, their blood memory, their relatives and/or simply the sequence of events in their lives. No one knows, and it is not to try and figure this out, as the sacred is not for sale. Often we hear the old people say you can have everything I have; you just have to live as I have lived.</p>

<p>Hehaka Sapa, Black Elk, a ‘wicasa wakan’, saw the ‘tree of life’ die and come to life again through seven ascents (generations) when he was nine years old. Wovoka, a Paiute, saw Christ appear as a red man and teach the people how to dance. The Paiute were the first to learn this dance, and then within five years all the plains people in NorthAmerica were ghost dancing. The ghost dance prophecy was that if the people kept dancing till spring, the wasichus would be gone and the buffalo would return. In Lakota prophecy, a day is a year. In 1990, the springtime came and the people were still dancing.Many don’t know that they are in fact ghost dancing as the tea dances and round dances are an offshoot of the ghost dance. The tree of life is our sun dance tree that is flowering again for all races and nations. The book Black Elk Speaks is translated in numerous languages, and people practice these spiritual ways all over the world.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Then I was standing on the highest mountain of them all, and round about beneath me was the whole hoop of the world. And while I stood there I saw more then I can tell and I understood more than I saw; for I was seeing things in a sacred manner the shapes of all things in spirit, and the shape of all shapes as they must live together like one being. And I saw that the sacred hoop of my people was one of the many hoops that made one circle, wide as daylight and as starlight, and in the center grew one mighty flowering tree to shelter all the children of one mother and one father. And I saw that it was holy.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The covenant that Indian people have with the earth, and all their relations is revealed in the cultural practices of every day life. Knowledge is not shared if a person is out of balance because they may twist that knowledge, become fanatical, try to sell it or misuse it in some other way. Our Sioux people, (known to each other as the ‘buffalo people’ or the ‘pte oyate’ or tatanka oyate’ in the Lakota language), are compelled to share this renewing process with our relatives as this was the task (the covenant) that was given to us by the holy woman Pte San Winyan, White Buffalo Calf Woman or White Bison Spirit Woman. However, there has been much confusion with these teaching as many people did not have the cultural framework to receive this understanding.</p>

<p>There are as many covenants between the Earth and Indigenous groups as there are Indigenous groups in the world. These covenants are in practice today with greater and lesser degrees of understanding and relevance depending on how the Indigenous group weathered the last 100 years.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Joanne explains the importance of Lakota ceremonies as part of a spiritual relationship with the land and the ancestors and the personal and spiritual journey of her husband Cliff Pompana:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For the Lakota groups there was a pipe and seven ceremonies passed to the buffalo people from an animation of the buffalo spirit in the form of a woman. These practices came about in a certain way through fasting, praying and visioning with a pipe that is still in existence today. If people maligned this understanding they suffered, because there is a way to live with the Earth and a way not to live with the Earth. In 1939, a catholic priest came to Sioux Valley as a sundance was being reintroduced there from South Dakota. He chopped down the sundance tree. He then went crazy and was institutionalized for the rest of his life. Shortly thereafter, Black Elk put his hands on a young Sioux boy’s head before he passed on. The child was nine years old in 1949, and the reason he did this was to pass on to this boy whatever knowledge he had left in him as a holy man. A few years later the child went vision seeking and the eagle came. Just before this boy entered the military, his Mother (a descendant and holder of Sitting Bull’s bundle) took the boy to be ordained in the Native American Church. Then the boy entered the military. While he was involved with the services all over the world he conducted sweat lodges in Ireland to assist revitalization of Gaelic people there, he went to medicine lodges of the Zulu, and wherever he was with the military, he was also involved with the holy people. This was in the early sixties. He was not immune to what was around him; he witnessed the horrors of war, and was near death many times…</p>

<p>Joanne and Cliff were married in 1992. Cliff went on to get his Master’s degree; and is completing his doctorate in a field that he created. It is a combination of comparative religion, psychology and anthropology. He is also an ordained minister. Cliff has worked with many people in Alberta with respect to healing, and two of his male relatives continue to work with many people in British Columbia and Manitoba.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Joanne’s essay concludes with some probing comments that link to a theme of many of the projects in the archive- the necessity for our conceptions of Canadian law to include and find an honourable place for indigenous law:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The idea of spiritual law and covenants is nothing new. It is in many of our United Nation documents today and it is in the Preamble of the Charter, therefore the only place it is truly missing is in the mainstream practice of law.</p>

<p>On the other hand, Aboriginal law has renewed the ‘spirit of the law’ in relationship to the Earth and Indigenous people, through a number of landmark decisions beginning with Calder and most recently in the Tsilhqot’in decision of 2007. There is a way to live with the Earth and not to live with the Earth and it is most reflected in these later decisions that pay attention to that sacred covenant that the Indigenous people have with the Earth. That is ‘respecting the Earth’ returns us all to our “original position”, beyond our blood parents, to our universal parents Father Sky and Mother Earth, so we remember, restore and renew those sacred relations in life and know we are never truly alone.</p>

<p>The relationship with the “original position”, the Earth for Indigenous people, is the first and foremost covenant in life.</p>
</blockquote>
