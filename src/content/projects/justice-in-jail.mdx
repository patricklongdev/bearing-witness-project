<p>Lyma Nguyen, as an exchange student from Australia, for the 2005 Penal Policy Seminar used a multimedia format that included collage, poetry, quotations from literature, including Oscar Wilde, Fyodor Dostoevsky, Giovanni Casonova, Albert Camus and a board game she designed to introduce readers/viewer to some of the themes of the seminar.</p>

<p>In her introduction Lyma sets out the ambitious scope of her work:</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large" style="margin-right:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80);margin-left:var(--wp--preset--spacing--80)"><img src="/assets/2025/02/Lyma_Nguyen_05.jpg" alt="Collage fromLyma Nguyen's 2005 “Penal Policy” Booklet " class="wp-image-3748"/></figure>
</figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This “Penal Policy” Booklet Aims to Demonstrate the Culture of Penitentiaries in Canada. This Is done through Quotes, Poetry, Collage and a Board Game. there are also two tables depicting model legislation (for the best human rights outcome prisoners) against current current practices in relation to prison disciplinary hearings and segregation. An explanation of the poem, “A Cage inside a Cage”, and the Board Game, “Justice in Jail” is given later on in the book.</p>

<p>The title page of the book is a collage of the man behind bars. Headings from newspaper clippings surround him. This depicts how the public is exposed to the penal system through the media, and how the public may view penal policy.</p>

<p>The book begins with a quote from Giovanni Casanova’s “Histoire de ma Vie”: “to judge a man, must one examine his conduct when he is healthy and free – sick or in prison, he is no longer the same”. What is meant by this quote is that imprisonment in itself can change a person. People are moulded by their environment. Most inmates are people who have deviated from the law, the rules of society. The reason they have behaved in criminal ways often stems from poor upbringing, exposure to crime from an early age, and lack of &#123;social) direction.. Some have been in government institution since early childhood, and these institutions have been all they have known of life.</p>

<p>Prison is not a place where love fosters, where peace is prevalent, where people are at the happiest. Being imprisoned is not conducive to rehabilitating and reintegrating offenders back into society, and yet after a certain sentence, it is expected that some inmates will reintegrate. Imprisonment and its effects on prisoners has been the topic of literature, and quotes from Oscar Wilde, Feodore Dostoyevsky and Albert Camus are given at the end of the book, for further reflection on penal policy.</p>

<p>Although the Canadian legislative framework is one of the best in the world, there is still a wide gap between what the legislation commands and what occurs in practice inside penitentiaries. Prison is a place out of reach of the general society (both literally, in terms of isolation and socially, in terms of prison culture). It is easy to humanize prisoners, and too often forgotten that those who are imprisoned by human beings before they are offenders. This forgetfulness is demonstrated in the public’s general lack of awareness or a concern for the types of human rights abuses that can still take place Canadian prisons.</p>

<p>The human rights abuses that occur inside penitentiaries are not necessarily deliberate violations. Prison administrators have a job to do, and often human rights abuses occur from inadequate compliance with procedural justice in the name of efficiency, or out of a lack of understanding of the proper function of due process in legal hearings. The importance of legal procedure is heightened when what is at stake in someone’s rights and liberty. A portion of this book has therefore been devoted to demonstrating the tension that exists between (ideal) legislation in practice, looking at the current practices undertaken by prison staff in disciplinary hearings and segregation review boards, against practices that should be adhered to.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her commentary on the poem and collage Lyma writes:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the first stanza, I liken the concept of imprisonment with Civil War, in a civil war people are turned to lawlessness. Within the prison system, civil war arises between the prison staff and prisoners. Although they both parties share a common space there is a deep disconnect with the process of justice from both points of view.</p>

<p>In the second stanza, “the sorry life that you once knew” speaks to the experience of many prisoners who, before serving time, came from broken homes, a history of violence, a place where engaging in criminal activities was part of survival.</p>

<p>In the third stanza “time is the weapon, space is the talk” expresses the purpose of imprisonment (loss of liberty) as punishment for deviating from social norms. The punishment is only meant to be a deprivation of one’s right to freedom of movement. However, in reality, it often entails deprivation of one’s dignity, one’s sense of self respect, one’s hopes and expectations. It is therefore arguable that imprisonment (the prison experience with all that it entails) also operates for the purpose of punishment. Time spent with nothing to do; or worse still still, time spent in an environment where love is seldom displayed, where authoritarianism rules, and where there is constant misunderstanding or need to prove oneself is further punishment on top of loss of liberty.</p>

<p>The collage opposite the poem aims to portray “a cage inside a cage” with a single cell surrounded by pictures of barbed wire prison grounds. The fingerprint on the bottom represents that every person, including every prisoner, is a unique human being, entitled to equal treatment under the law and the same respect and dignity as that enshrined in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In explaining the board game “Justice in Jail” Lyma writes:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>...the purpose of the board game is to demonstrate prison culture by having players land on various acts and be subject to the consequences of those events. The incidents take place in a prison context, and the corrollary of each event (forward or backward movement on the board, taking an extra turn on missing the turn) reflect on the positive or negative consequences they may have for a prisoner in those circumstances.</p>

<p>It is not intended that the number of forward or backward steps indicate the severity of the effects of the corresponding events. For example, for the event, “visitor shows positive on the IONSCAN machine,, the corresponding instruction is “back one step”. This indicates that it is a negative occurrence for the prisoner, but is not intended to indicate that the effects of this event on the prisoner are minimal. For some prisoners, for whom visits from loved ones is a rare luxury or an important event, having heightened surveillance in the visitations or having their loved ones subjected to the humiliation of being suspected of drug trafficking may well be an incident worse than segregation. Hence it must be emphasized that the number of steps forward or backward is somewhat arbitrary and does not accurately depict the degree of the real-life effects of those events on a prison.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>At the beginning of her book Lyma wrote this dedication:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This book is dedicated to the inmates and staff at Matsqui institution with whom we spoke to Professor Michael Jackson for his dedication to the human rights of those most forgotten by society.</p>
</blockquote>
