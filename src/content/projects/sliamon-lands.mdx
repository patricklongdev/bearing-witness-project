<p>In 2005 two students, members of the Sliammon First Nation, presented projects that in different formats document and illustrate their Nation’s history and journey towards self determination. Melissa Louie in a cut-out multi levelled binder, under the headings “In the Beginning”, “First Contact”, and “Exercising Rights Today, Tomorrow”, describes the features of Sliammon social organization, relationship to lands and resources and legal traditions.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>As a child, I had the good fortune of spending my summers with my grandparents. We traveled through-out our territory and my grandparents provided a traditional education. At the time, I was not aware of the significance of the knowledge that they were sharing with me. Looking back, I am thankful for every lesson and for every moment I had with them.</p>

<p>During our travels, I learned where our people gathered resources such as clams, shellfish, and cedar roots. I was taught how and where to fish by my grandfather. I also learned traditional resource management. Every summer we traveled to different locations. As a result I became familiar with different parts of the Slammon territory.</p>

<p>I was given the opportunity to create memories, to make attachments, and develop an understanding of different areas of Sliammon lands. When I visualize the mental map of the areas that I have been to, each area is associated with a lesson or a memory. This helps me to recall what I have learned.</p>

<p>While working on this project and participating in this class, I have reflected on the meaning of land ownership, resources, and treaties. I have reached the realization that a treaty for Sliammon is about more than just monetary gains, "western legal concepts" of rights and title, and governing.</p>

<p>A treaty is about telling the world who Sliammon people are. It is also about our special connection to our lands and ensuring its survival. This is critical for future Sliammon people so that they may have the opportunity to know our territory, obtain a traditional education, and to participate in a way of life that has been practiced since time immemorial.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Experiencing the richness of Melissa’s work is best done by scrolling through the binder but I have reproduced some of the pages here to see the power of what most of us only remember from childhood cut-out books.</p>
