<p>Katie McGreer, whose Time Capsule interpretation of the 1850 Robinson Huron Treaty is featured in the Treaty Rights section of this Archive, for her project in the 2010 Penal Policy Seminar, turned to prison poetry to better understand the disconnect between the reality and rhetoric of rehabilitation - a theme of the seminar readings. Why poetry? In “Insight from the Prison -A Close Reading of Prison Poetry” Katie explains:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In Canada, imprisonment is used as a means of punishing criminals for their wrongdoings. Imprisonment represents strict adherence to the rule of law: culpability begets punishment. But the reasons for detention are not merely punitive. There is a corrective element to our prison system promulgated in section 3(b) of the Corrections and Conditional Release Act which stipulates that “the purpose of the federal correctional system is to contribute to the maintenance of a just, peaceful and safe society by assisting the rehabilitation of offenders and their reintegration into the community […].” This corrective element of imprisonment suggests that Canadian legislators have a robust idea of justice.</p>

<p>For the purposes of this paper, I did a close reading of poetry to gain insight on whether the Canadian prison system actually facilitates the rehabilitation of inmates. The study of poetry gives us a view of the prison system that stretches beyond the four corners of the law. Poetry is testimony off the stand, outside the courtroom, and behind the prison gate. The poems My Heart by Steven Gareau, He took up clay by John Rives, Of the Fittest by an anonymous author in Kent, and Have You Ever by Sean Jerome shed light on the mind and heart of the prisoner; they account for reality in a way that is “boned with ideas, nerved and blooded with emotions, [and] all held together by the delicate, tough skin of words.” The poems help shape our understanding of prison life by giving an insiders view of how it feels to be incarcerated-- a perspective that rarely permeates Canada’s political-legal discourse. Unfortunately, the poems reveal an ugly truth: prisoners are emotionally wounded, psychologically perturbed and devoid of hope.</p>

<p>Yet, there is room for optimism. Poetry provides us with a valuable tool for meaningful legal reform: empathic concern. With a greater understanding of how prisons affect inmates, we are better equipped to draft fair, progressive penal policy.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Here are two of the poems with Katie’s close reading:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">He took up clay</h3>

<p>by John Rives</p>

<p><em>At the encounter group<br />he took up clay<br />to represent himself.<br />Slate-grey clay<br />was moulded into a single hollow sphere,<br />dark and featureless<br />without. Then,<br />with a fierce cut,<br />he divided<br />it in half – in shrieks –<br />revealing<br />all the nothingness inside.<br />He picked up clay<br />to represent himself,<br />the seventeen year old,<br />the accused,<br />the murderer.</em></p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Of the Fittest</h3>

<p>by an anonymous author in Kent GP 1989</p>

<p><em>I survive<br />solely by conforming<br />to the rules<br />of those I am<br />imprisoned with.<br />Rules that are<br />harshly enforced.<br />Deviation from the code<br />is met with<br />punishment and<br />no hope of appeal.<br />A man’s voice<br />cannot be heard<br />from a numbered<br />grave.<br />I accept this state<br />outwardly.<br />Inwardly, I rage<br />against the injustice.<br />Yet I dare not<br />speak out,<br />for there is<br />no one to listen.<br />So I wear<br />a perpetual mask<br />and concentrate on<br />surviving<br />one more day.</em></p>

<p>John Rives succeeds in conveying a prisoner’s sense of inner turmoil when he describes a seventeen year old’s self representation in He took up clay. Rives’ subject takes “slate-grey clay” and moulds it into “a single hollow sphere, / dark and featureless/ without.” The colour grey is dreary and sets an ominous tone which reflects the mood of the poem. Similarly, clay, by its very nature, is dark and damp. This helps to convey a sense of desperation. The tools used by the subject reflect his anguish just as much as the object he forms out of the clay, which is also hollow, dark, and featureless. This creates a sense of hopelessness.</p>

<p>Rives conveys a sense of frailty in He took up clay when he describes the hollowness of his subject: “with a fierce cut, /he divided/ it in half – in shrieks --/ revealing/ all the nothingness inside.” The extract shows the vulnerability of the human spirit; the young prisoner in the poem has no feeling of self worth. He suffers from the ultimate sign of resignation. Even in Of the Fittest, where the poet seems to express a will to survive, his perseverance ends at the end of each day- his endurance follows no trajectory: “concentrate on/ surviving/ one more day.” This is an impoverished notion of survival. It is not a question of thriving; rather, it is about enduring pain and suffering.</p>

<p>In fact, the poems opens with the idea that to survive is to conform: “I survive / solely by conforming/ to the rules / of those I am / imprisoned with.” And thus, the poet suggests that to cope and to survive prisoners must surrender something else: their individuality and their autonomy. The idea that death of the individual looms within the prison walls is conveyed when the poet states that “a man’s voice / cannot be heard / from a numbered / grave.” Prisoners’ voices are muted in prison and completely lost at the grave.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Katie reflects on the importance of poetry in understanding the hidden dimensions of imprisonment:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The selected poetry shows the extent to which prisoners internalize their pain and resign themselves to a sense of hopelessness. Loneliness holds them hostage and tortures their hearts, minds, and souls. Indeed, inmates fall prey to both physical and emotional isolation: it mars them, batters them and eventually defeats them. The poems show the toll suffering takes on a persons’ resilience. While prisoners are able to endure their imprisonment, it crushes their souls in the meantime. This is a reality which escapes the current dialogue regarding corrections: the impact imprisonment has on the human spirit. The effect imprisonment has on inmates’ self-worth is especially disconcerting. If prisoners hold themselves in low esteem, give up on their chances of happiness, and channel their pain through hostile and aggressive mediums, rehabilitation is stymied.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>While the poetry and Katie’s discussion of the way in which imprisonment crushes the human spirit is a dark message, Katie’s conclusion points to the lessons to be learned as “a springboard for activism”:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Truth is more than facts or legal dicta; some things exists beyond the paradigm under which Corrections now operates. Prison poetry is an instrument of rendering. Truth is revealed by intellectual abstractions and leads us toward a broader understanding of life in prison. Poets beckon us to consider or re-evaluate our notions of justice by reminding us that they are suffering under the harsh realities of prison life.</p>

<p>As such, poetry compels a humanist response to drudgery. By reading inmates’ poems, we come to understand that prison not only deprives people of liberty, it also deprives them of hope. The condition of the prisoner, casts a negative impression on the prison system in general and the poems indicate that prisons actually frustrate recovery. If the prison environment is stifling and depressing then how will it be a successful venue for rehabilitation? Since loneliness and hopelessness are the greatest breakers of the human spirit, the biggest challenge for Canadian corrections will be to create a prison environment that is both safe and supportive and, in a sense, liberating. And so, we should use prison poetry as a springboard for activism. Because at the end of the day, the measure of our convictions is our actions.</p>
</blockquote>
