<p>The Nisga’a have achieved the resolution of their historic land claims through a modern treaty. Virginia Sarazin in her 2010 photo essay project “The Algonquins of Pikwakanagan” for the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar, sets out the history of the Algonquin claim and explains what a settlement in the form of a modern treaty will mean for her family.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Growing up I was fortunate to be in my home community, surrounded by my family and my culture. Many Aboriginal peoples are not as lucky as me, many people do not grow up with the ability to know their culture intimately and be able to carry on their traditional culture. I grew up in a family that is proud to be descendants of the first peoples of this land and strives to promote the well-being of the culture. My family continues many of the traditional practices of our Algonquin culture, including being one of the few families left who maintain the building of the birch bark canoe in the traditional way.</p>

<p>I have pursued 'western' post-secondary education so that I might also contribute to my culture in a 'modern' way. I have come to law school so that when I receive my degree and become a lawyer I might contribute to the Algonquins of Ontario Land Claim and its finalization in the near future.</p>

<p>The settlement of the Algonquins of Ontario Land Claim will be a monumental moment in history. It will mean many things to many people, but I am sure that my family will feel especially proud and gratified being some of the people involved and some of those who may abIe to continue an integral traditional practise of our culture through a modern Treaty.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>As Virginia explains:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Algonquin peoples have lived in what is now known as the Ottawa Valley long before European peoples ventured to this part of the world. Archaeological information indicates that Algonquin people have lived in the Ottawa Valley for at least 8,000 years before the Europeans arrived in North America' as a hunting and gathering society. The communities that the Algonquin peoples lived in were located in the present-day provinces of Ontario and Quebec, on either side of the Kichi Sibi (meaning 'great river' in the Algonquin Language, referring to the now named Ottawa River). The territory that the Algonquin peoples of Ontario traditionally occupied covers over 9 million acres, is within the watersheds of the Ottawa andMattawa Rivers and includes Canada's National Capital Region and most of Algonquin Park. The Algonquins of Ontario have never surrendered their rights and title to the lands that they have lived on for time immemorial.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Algonquin of Ontario website provides this overview of the treaty negotiations.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Overview of Treaty Negotiations</h3>

<p>Algonquin petitions to the Crown seeking recognition and protection for Algonquin land and other rights date back to 1772. In 1983, the Algonquins of Pikwàkanagàn First Nation (known at the time as the Algonquins of Golden Lake) commenced the land claim by formally submitting the most recent petition, with supporting research, to the Government of Canada in 1983 and the Government of Ontario in 1985. The Province of Ontario accepted the claim for negotiations in 1991 and the Government of Canada joined the negotiations in 1992.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Algonquins of Ontario claim includes an area of 9 million acres within the watersheds of the Kichisippi (Ottawa River) and the Mattawa River in Ontario, an unceded territory that covers most of eastern Ontario. More than 1.2 million people live and work within the Settlement Area. There are 84 municipal jurisdictions fully and partially located within the Settlement Area, including 75 lower and single tier municipalities and 9 upper tier counties.</p>

<p>The Algonquins of Ontario (AOO) and the Governments of Canada and Ontario are working together to resolve this land claim through a negotiated settlement. If successful, the agreement we reach will take the form of a modern-day treaty with aboriginal and treaty rights protected under Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982.</p>

<p>The Algonquin Treaty will provide certainty about the ownership, use and management of land and natural resources for the Algonquins and everyone else in the Settlement Area. This will remove the barriers to economic growth created by existing uncertainties and contribute to a more stable social, political and economic environment with greater potential for regional economic development, jobs and growth.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Virginia’s project provides a historical timeline for the Algonquin claim that takes us back to the earliest days of French settlement in what is now the Ottawa Valley:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><strong>1603</strong> -One of the first recorded encounters between the Algonquin people and European explorers. Samuel de Champlain made note of meeting a group of Algonquin traders.</p>

<p><strong>1613</strong>- When the Europeans later encountered the Algonquins in 1613, they were the occupiers of what is now known as the Ottawa Valley.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Algonquins have lived in the valley of the Ottawa River at least as long as the French have lived in France or the English have lived in England. Before there was Canada, before Cartier sailed his small ship up the great river, Algonquins lived in, occupied, used, and defended their home in the Ottawa Valley.</p>
</blockquote>

<p><strong>1713</strong>- Champlain was prevented from journeying deeper into the interior than the Algonquin chief Tessouat would allow at their tribute point on the Ottawa River.</p>

<p><strong>1720</strong>- After 1720 an increasing number of Algonquins, as well as Nipissings, spent the summer at Lake of Two Mountains.</p>

<p><strong>Pre-1763</strong>- The principal residence during the pre-1763 period, for the Algonquins was Allumette and Morrison's Islands, now Quebec, the interior of the territory consisted of hunting grounds used from fall to spring, and areas along the Ottawa and other rivers were used for summer gathering.</p>

<p><strong>1760-1763</strong>- During the time when the British Crown asserted sovereignty free and clear of French claims, 1760-1763, the Algonquins were the occupiers of the Valley.</p>

<p><strong>1763</strong>- Royal Proclamation, 1763, R.S.C. 1985, App. Il, No. 1<br />After the 1763, and the Royal Proclamation, the Algonquins were repeatedly assured by officials of the Crown, Sir William Johnson and his successor Sir John Johnson, that they could rely on the Royal Proclamation, in that they would not be disturbed in their<br />hunting grounds until such a time when they were purchased by the Crown. They received a signed copy of the Royal Proclamation and made reference to it and the promises made in asserting their continued land use and interest.</p>

<p><strong>1764</strong>-Through the Treaty of Niagara peace and trade were restored to north-eastern North America. Reassured by the Royal Proclamation and the Treaties, the Algonquin people went back to their hunting and trapping life.</p>

<p><strong>1772</strong>- The first of dozens of petitions were made, by the Algonquins to the Imperial Crown, seeking out protection of their hunting grounds from 'squatters'. In these petitions they referenced the promises made to them, and the fact that they had never relinquished their land.</p>

<p><strong>1774</strong>- Lord Dorchester, the governor, once again gave the Algonquin people specific reassurances with respect to their rights: "All of that which belonged to the King of France belongs to your present father, the King, but no one can give to another that which does not belong justly to him. That is why if you anciently held the rights to these<br />lands, and if you have not been paid, the rights belong to you still."</p>

<p><strong>1775</strong>- The deputy-superintendent general of Indian Affairs asked the Chiefs to describe "the nature of your claims to these lands, how you became originally possessed of them, and at what time." The Chiefs replied "We ask you to observe that we have never been conquered by the French, that on the contrary we have always been the protectors of<br />the white skins even against the Indian nations…you have asked us to prove how these lands belong to us: the best proof we can give is that [we were] created [Ion these lands.”</p>

<p><strong>Soon after</strong>- Algonquin leaders protested against Whiskey traders moving into their hunting grounds. In a petition they wrote that the traders could travel as far as Carillon, but only with no liquor. They also said that they would "clear their hunting grounds all the way to Lake Nipissing of any traders that may reside thereupon, and so follow their hunting peaceably and quietly to the better interest of themselves and the Indian trade in general.”</p>

<p><strong>1776</strong>- An ordinance was passed prohibiting the taking of liquor into Indian villages after the government had asked the Algonquins to be patient and not to meddle with the traders so that they could deal with the problem.</p>

<p><strong>1791</strong>- After 1791 the old Colony of Quebec was divided into Upper and Lower Canada, with the Lake of Two Mountains mission being in the responsibility of the Lower Canada<br />administration.</p>

<p><strong>1794</strong>- The promises previously made by Sir William Johnson, were reiterated again in 1794 by Lord Dorchester.</p>

<p><strong>1812</strong>- In the War of 1812 and the American Revolutionary War, the Algonquins took an active part on the side of the British. An Algonquin war chief, Amable Chevalier, was<br />instrumental in taking Michilimakinack in 1812 and the Algonquin warriors took part in the Battle of Beaver Dams on the Niagara frontier.</p>

<p><strong>1820</strong>- The Algonquin people had to, yet again, petition the governor general, Lord Dalhousie, about lost land. Reminding him that the Royal Proclamation of 1763 had accorded them their hunting grounds exclusively and adding that the number of settlements continued to grow in their hunting grounds causing the game “to become distant” and therefore “the petitioners find themselves in a great need, which can only increase from day to day by this use of the lands on which they do their hunting.”</p>

<p><strong>1827</strong>- European settlement of the Ottawa Valley was underway, with the construction of the Rideau Canal the settlement of Bytown had begun. The chiefs responded by pressing for the protections they had been guaranteed by the Crown more than 60 years before. In February the Chiefs asked the government to create an inquiry into their claims and to determine the compensation they were entitled to by the Royal Proclamation, 1763. In March the government warned the Chiefs that any viotence against trespassers would be “subject to the utmost penalty of the law.” In July the Chiefs petitioned the governor general again noting their possession of the Ottawa Valley “from time immemorial”.</p>

<p><strong>1829</strong>- Officials of the Indian department urged the government to prevent further encroachment and later that year the Indians were advised that trespassers on theirhunting grounds would be prosecuted, however there isn't record of any prosecutions.</p>

<p><strong>1835</strong>- The petition made, by the Algonquins, to the new 'Upper Canada' in 1835, highlighted the fact that other nations in Upper Canada had entered into treaty arrangements in which they acquired a specific land for settlement and also annuities for surrendering their hunting grounds. This petition also made note that the Algonqu ins would respect third party interests that already existed in exchange for compensation for those losses. During a meeting with the chiefs in Toronto in the summer of 1835, Sir John Colborne apparently agreed to assist the Algonquins in their desire to occupy Allumette Island and also the lands to the south of it, which was not written down at the time but the promise was referenced in documents to follow.</p>

<p><strong>1837</strong>- The various petitions that were made by the Algonquins were discussed by many Crown officials and also various commissions including the Lower Canada Executive Committee in 1837. The petitions were also discussed at the Bagot Commission in 1845. The conclusion developed and it was also the recommendation made, that the Algonquin claims should be converted into "an equitable right to be compensated" and also should include an area of land for occupation.</p>

<p><strong>1830</strong>'s- By the late 1830's though, the Algonquin Nation had expressed its desire to enter into an arrangement similar to the treaties that had been made with other Aboriginal nations within Upper Canada. This expressed desire carried with it an emphasis on an area of land to be set apart for the exclusive Algonquin use and occupancy, and also monetary compensation for the relinquishment of hunting grounds. This proposition was never formally agreed to for one reason or another.</p>

<p><strong>1853</strong>- After the 1853 creation of the Maniwaki reserve and others in Quebec, the government believed that it had satisfied all Algonquin claims.</p>

<p><strong>1873</strong>- The Golden Lake reserve (now called the Algonquins of Pikwàkanagàn) was acquired and all subsequent requests for reserves were eventually denied with the opinion that all Algonquins should relocate to the Golden Lake reserve.</p>

<p>Virginia’s timeline links the reemergence of the Algonquin land claim to the pivotal decision of the 1973 Supreme Court of Canada decision in Calder recognizing Aboriginal title as a legally enforceable right based upon historic possession and occupation of ancestral lands, a right not dependent on government recognition and the subsequent agreement in 1973 by the federal government, in light of Calder, to negotiate modern claims agreement based upon such title under its Comprehensive Claims policy. Since the ten nations that make up the Algonquin Nation span both Ontario and Québec the claim was originally conceived as a joint one but the claim eventually submitted in 1983 was on behalf of the Algonquins of Ontario. The claim was based on the fact that they had never ceded their rights through a treaty nor had they sold or lost their territory through war and linked the claim to the series of petitions to the Crown by the Algonquins dating back to 1772.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Virginia also includes in her timeline the decisions of the Supreme Court of Canada that make up the contemporary jurisprudence on Aboriginal and Treaty Rights and how they have shaped the Algonquin claim. Writing in 2010 she sets out the then state of the negotiations.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><strong>2006</strong>- The Statement of Shared Objectives was re-affirmed by the three negotiating parties in March and signed by the three principle negotiators and sixteen Algonquin Negotiation Representatives.</p>

<p><strong>2007</strong>- The Government of Ontario has made a Declaration Order related to the Environmental Assessment Act (EA Act) to set out an environmental review process for projects and activities being considered for the Algonquin Land Claim Settlement Agreement. The Declaration Order provides a transparent and coordinated process for Ontario ministries and agencies to meet the objective of Ontario's EA Act. It includes screening processes to evaluate potential environmental effects, and requirements for consultation with the public and Aboriginal communities.</p>

<p><strong>2009</strong>- A renewed Framework for Negotiations Agreement was signed by all three parties to the negotiations in July. This updates the original Framework Agreement and also sets out the common understandings on the negotiation process that will guide the talks. The three parties also signed a Consultation Process Interim Measures Agreement designed to facilitate ongoing business within the land claim territory during the ongoing negotiations. It provides a "one-window" approach for the government to meet their legal consultation obligations with the Algonquins of Ontario. The consultation office will also provide support to the Algonquins, in the land selection process and in building land management capacity before the final Treaty is concluded.</p>

<p>Agreement-in Principle negotiations are currently underway. Once it is reached it will have to be approved by all parties. Then a target date will be set for completing the final Treaty and the implementation agreement. The Treaty will require formal approval by Algonquin eligible voters and the Ontario legislature and the Parliament of Canada before it can be finalized.</p>

<p>The settlement of the claim and the anticipated Treaty is expected to include transfer of settlement lands to Algonquin ownership, a financial package, cultural and economic development opportunities for the Algonquin people, as well as appropriate arrangements respecting harvesting rights for hunting, fishing, trapping and gathering.<br />The rights that the Algonquins of Ontario will hold pursuant to the Treaty will be constitutionally protected and will clarify the interests in the lands in the region, achieve certainty regarding rights and interests also relating to the natural resources within the Ottawa Valley.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Six years after Virginia submitted her project, on October 18, 2016, the Algonquins of Ontario and the Governments of Ontario and Canada reached a major milestone in their journey toward reconciliation and renewed relationships with the signing of the Agreement-in-Principle (AIP). The signing of the AIP was a key step toward a Final Agreement that will clarify the rights of all concerned and open up new economic development opportunities for the benefit of the Algonquins of Ontario and their neighbours in the Settlement Area in eastern Ontario. For the latest information on the current state of the Algonquin negotiations visit <a href="https://www.tanakiwin.com/our-treaty-negotiations/treaty-negotiations-update/">www.tanakiwin.com</a></p>

<p>What makes Virginia’s project so much more than a historical text is the connection to the role her family has played in the claim, illustrated by the photos in the essay.</p>

<p>Virginia’s grandfather passed away in 2009. She describes the manner in which her family decided to memorialize his life and contributions.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The passing of my grandpa was devastating to my family. He was the last one who knew the whole process of building a birch bark canoe in the traditional way from start to finish. He always had a canoe on the go. </p>

<p>This past summer my grandma, my dad and my uncle so to build a birch bark canoe in the traditional way, that my grandpa practised his entire life, by piecing together the elements that each of them knew from helping my grandpa over the years. They recorded the process through pictures and video and included the grandchildren where ever possible.</p>

<p>Through the next generation my grandpa’s legacy will live on, and through the Algonquins of Ontario Treaty the harvesting practices that are involved can be continued in our traditional territory.</p>
</blockquote>
