<p>Kate Stoeckel, a visiting student from Australia pursuing an LLM, in her 2003 project for Aboriginal and Treaty Rights, utilized storytelling in conjunction with a unique triptych painting that drew upon two distinctive Indigenous art forms from opposite ends of the world, the one inspired by Australian Indigenous dreaming art and the other by Pacific Northwest Coast formline art. Kate’s painting and accompanying essay compares developments in the recognition of the rights of Indigenous people in Canada and Australia and in her assessment the scales of justice are weighed in favour of Canada’s record.</p>

<p>Kate explains the question that prompted her project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Coming from Australia and now living in British Columbia, I am often asked the question: "Aboriginal people seem to have a rough lot in Australia - how does that compare with the situation here?"</p>

<p>This is not an easy question to answer.</p>

<p>This assignment attempts to explore this difference, and in so doing delve a little further into questions and thoughts that recur during my stay here. Although there are extensive and serious problems for Indigenous peoples in numerous areas - education, healthcare and disproportional poverty rates are just a few - the focus here is on Aboriginal title to land. This is because title is fundamental to all other issues, for, as discussed below, connection with the land is central to the Indigenous societies in both areas. Land links the Indigenous people with their rich cultural heritage, which includes their legal systems, governing relationships with the land and animals, and between humans.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her essay Kate explains the themes reflected in her painting and why she draws upon Indigenous storytelling:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>My painting reflects three themes: creation, colonisation and today's discrepancy in Aboriginal title. I must, however, start with a caveat. This project explores the modern situation faced by two Indigenous peoples sharing a similar colonisation background. The similarities between Australia and British Columbia are striking: they share a similar colonial history; in neither area were treaties signed with the Indigenous inhabitants of the land; there is a lack of political will to recognise title today; and, both regions share legacies of monumental cases recognising Aboriginal title at common law - Mabo v State of Queensland (No. 2) and Delgamuukw v British Columbia.</p>

<p>First I will discuss the two (broadly representative) Indigenous creation stories, which· show that the respective mythological creator-being made the landscape and people, and in so doing bestowed laws and religion upon humans since the beginning of time.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Rainbow Serpent</h3>

<p>The Rainbow Serpent on the left side of the painting is a creation story common to many Aboriginal peoples in Australia. It is set in the Dreamtime, also known as the Dreaming - the ancient period when the world was created. All the plants, animals and humans were created, and the land was shaped into its present-day form. The spirits of the ancestral creator-beings live on today - inhabiting the rocks, waterholes, trees, mountains, stars and other natural features of the landscape. Every area of land is associated with some Dreaming spirit.</p>

<p>This is why land is so important to Aboriginal people - it provides the link between an Indigenous group and their Dreaming. Thus it is vital to protect and preserve Aboriginal title to land, because the Dreaming was also the period where the rules and laws governing the social relationships between people, and between people and the land, were established. Without the connection to land, and therefore the Dreaming, the most fundamental aspects of Indigenous culture and the framework for living are liable to be destroyed.</p>

<p>Dreaming stories vary between region and Indigenous group. I have chosen Rainbow Serpent Dreaming even though it is only one creation story among many, because it is one of the most widely shared beliefs among Aboriginal groups. The Rainbow Serpent is the protector of the people and the land included in its Dreaming, and it is usually attributed with the source of all life particularly through its link with water. It is a persistent theme among Aboriginal communities, and images of Rainbow Serpent Dreaming have been found in rock art as old as 6,000 years.</p>

<p>Rainbow Serpent paintings tell the story of the Dreamtime Serpent as it moves across the land. As it traverses the landscape, its track creates waterholes (represented by concentric circles), rivers, valleys and other natural features. Each various artistic rendition of the story of the Rainbow Serpent is therefore specific to a particular piece of land, represented by the dot background over which the Serpent is travelling.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Raven, the Trickster</h3>

<p>On the coast of the Pacific Northwest, it is another creature - the raven - that enjoys status as the most widespread figure in Indigenous mythology. Raven is the being most commonly associated with creation among the coastal Aboriginal peoples in the Pacific Northwest. Raven is a wise, yet mischievous character, also known as the Trickster. Raven is attributed with placing the sun, moon and stars in the sky, and with creating the natural features of the landscape, such as mountains, valleys, islands and rivers. Raven brought fire and salmon to humans, and his mischievous tricks provide endless material for amusing tales. As in Australia, social relationships and connections to the land were governed by laws passed down from the time of creation, and were inseparable from these myths. The First Salmon Ceremony - revering and thanking the salmon spirits in a strict ceremony- is one example of this.</p>

<p>In my painting, Raven is depicted holding the sun in his beak. This story tells of how Raven stole the sun and placed it in the sky in order to bring light to the world. Raven is also associated with the creation of humans. For example, the Haida people of Haida Gwaii believe that Raven found the first humans in a giant clamshell on the beach at Rose Spit. Haida artist Bill Reid depicts this story in a stunning sculpture entitled Raven and the First Men, situated at UBC's Museum of Anthropology. Reid recounts the creation story:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>As the raven scanned the beach, a white flash caught his eye, and when he landed he found at his feet, half buried in the sand, a gigantic clamshell. When he looked more closely still, he saw that the shell was full of little creatures cowering in terror of his enormous shadow.</p>

<p>Well, here was something to break the monotony of his day. But nothing was going to happen as long as the tiny things stayed in the shell, and they certainly weren't coming out in their present terrified state.</p>

<p>So the Raven leaned his great head close to the shell, and with the smooth trickster's tongue that had got him into and out of so many misadventures during his troubled and troublesome existence, he coaxed and cajoled and coerced the little creatures to come out and play in his wonderful shiny world.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This story illustrates both the Indigeneity of the First Nations people in British Columbia as the first people to inhabit the 'shiny new world' of the Pacific Northwest , and Raven's role in bringing this about. The second concept that my painting symbolises is the colonisation process `on both continents. On the Aboriginal Australian side, the red 'U' shapes signify the arrival of the First Fleet in 1788 and the coming of white convicts and settlers in countless later ships. Between 1788 and 1850 over 162,000 English convicts were sent to Australia. Free settlers began arriving in 1793, and the discovery of gold brought many punters willing to try their luck - over 1.6 million settlers arrived in 1871 alone.</p>

<p>This new age brought with it English law and assertions of sovereignty by the Crown. Traditional Aboriginal territory and hunting grounds were lost, Aboriginal laws were ignored, Indigenous populations succumbed to European diseases, and native title to the land began to be extinguished piece by piece as settlement proceeded inland from the coast. The 'ships' in my painting represent this gradual process of colonisation, and the tragedy that it entails.</p>

<p>On the First Nations side, the top image is a representation of a steam train. As in Australia, white settlement devastated native communities by many means, such as smallpox plagues, the loss of land and traditional hunting grounds, and cultural loss from schemes such as cultural assimilation, Residential Schools and the ban on the potlatch. Although settlement was already proceeding apace in British Columbia, the transcontinental railway facilitated a far more extensive movement of people by uniting west with east. In his study of the Great Canadian Railway, historian Pierre Berton notes, "Of the 133,000 who arrived in Canada in 1883, two-thirds sped directly to the North West." For the native peoples of the area, the railway symbolised the end of the world as they knew it, instantaneously ushering in a terrible era where wholesale losses of land, culture, health and dignity were commonplace. For example, Berton documents the changes faced by the Plains Indians as they were forced onto reserves as their hunting grounds shrank when the railway advanced:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Within six years, the image of the Plains Indian underwent a total transformation. From being a proud and fearless nomad, rich in culture and tradition, he became a pathetic, half-starved creature, confined to the semi-prisons of the new reserves and totally dependent on government relief for his existence. Without the buffalo, which had supplied them with food, shelter, clothing, tools, and ornaments, the Indians were helpless. By 1880, after the three most terrible years they had ever known, the emaciated natives were forced to eat their dogs and their horses, to scrabble for gophers and mice, and even to consume the carcasses of animals found rotting on the prairie.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>These events mention just one tragedy among the many faced by the Indigenous people of the Northwest. In British Columbia, the government's harsh refusal to negotiate treaties with the Aboriginal people compounded their hardship. The Aboriginal people of the Pacific Northwest shared a similar fate to other colonised peoples, such as the Aboriginal peoples in Australia.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Kate’s painting reflects her assessment why, writing in 2003, the scales of justice for Indigenous Peoples’ rights were balanced in favour of the Canadian record:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Despite the hardships, noted above, for Aboriginal peoples in British Columbia, their land title situation compares somewhat favourably to that of the Indigenous Australians. This discrepancy in strength of Aboriginal title in the two regions is portrayed by the traditional legal image of the scales of justice, tilted to show that title is stronger in Canada's Pacific Northwest. This image is superimposed upon the Union Jack, symbolising the common heritage in the Crown shared by both regions.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her essay Kate links this assessment primarily to the constitutional protection of Aboriginal and Treaty rights in section 35 of the Constitution Act and the absence of such protection in either the Australian federal or state system.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In particular, Aboriginal rights in Canada were recognized and affirmed in section 35(1) of the Constitution Act 1982, where it provides that: "The existing aboriginal and treaty rights of the aboriginal peoples of Canada are hereby recognized and affirmed". This includes rights recognised and protected by the common law. Section 35 is a unique provision, as it entrenches Aboriginal rights in the Canadian Constitution. This entrenchment gives them weight and strength, although they are not absolute and may infringed upon by the legislature provided the infringement is 'justified'.</p>

<p>In contrast, there is no constitutional comparator for either section 35 or 25 in Australia, for nothing in the Australian Constitution guarantees, recognises or protects Aboriginal rights and title. Further, rights protection generally in Australia is scant - Australia does not have any provisions in its Constitution offering rights protection similar to the Canadian Charter, nor is there even a legislative Bill of Rights. The Commonwealth Racial Discrimination Act of 1975 serves the purpose of protecting against actions conducted under laws that are discriminatory on the basis of race. However, protection is ruled out against the numerous acts from earlier settlement eras that effectively extinguished native title when the act/s in question pre-dated 1975, the year the Racial Discrimination Act came into effect.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Kate also describes the differences to the history of treaty-making in Canada and the continuation of the tradition in modern treaties and the absence of any such tradition in Australia. A further difference identified is the way in which the Canadian Supreme Court, since its landmark decisions in <em>Calder</em> and <em>Delgamuukw</em> has been able to develop the jurisprudence of Aboriginal title incrementally; in contrast the High Court Australia, having made its landmark decision in <em>Mabo</em>, recognizing for the first time in Australia the common law doctrine of native title surviving the assertion of Crown sovereignty, has since had its role significantly circumscribed by the enactment of the Native Title Act:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>There have been numerous and interesting developments in the common law in both jurisdictions. However, the legislature has taken the lead over this issue in Australia with a highly complex and detailed Native Title Act in 1993. The Native Title Act was amended in 1998 as a legislative response to a High Court decision stating that native title could co-exist with certain other proprietary interests, and now purports to cover the field. A legislative approach must necessarily attempt to codify and clarify all possible concepts, and to cover existing situations and both foreseen and unforeseen future circumstances. The imposition of the Native Title Act in Australia has constrained the judiciary to lengthy interpretations of its complex legislative scheme, which purports to cover the field in this area. Consequently, developments in British Columbia, including developments in the Canadian Supreme Court, have progressed further than in Australia.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>An example of this greater role of the judiciary in Canada is the way which the Supreme Court has developed the concept of fiduciary duty and the honour of the Crown in cases such as Guerin. At the time of Kate’s writing 2004 the Supreme Court of Canada had not yet rendered its ruling in <em>Haida Nation</em> imposing a duty to consult on the Crown, where its activities might affect asserted but not yet proven Aboriginal rights and title, and she pointed out that under the Australian Native Title Act legislation had already imposed a duty to negotiate when activities such as mining exploration or other development affects areas subject to native title claims.</p>

<p>In light of (1) significant developments in Canada since 2004, particularly the decision of the Supreme Court in <em>Tsilhqot’in</em>, the expanding jurisprudence on the duty to consult and the further examples of modern treaty-making in British Columbia and the Northwest Territories, (2) Australian jurisprudence recognizing the legal interests of indigenous peoples in marine areas and settlements pursuant to the Native Title Act (3) the endorsement of the Universal Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous peoples in both Canada and Australia, in an email exchange in an email with Kate, I asked if she were revisiting her painting in 2021, would she change the scales of justice reflected her middle part of her painting. Her response was that the balance would be even more tilted askew . While there have been movements in the right direction, like the historical apology<sup data-fn="4b9d81f7-7914-4b1a-b800-76eb66fc32d6" class="fn"><a href="#4b9d81f7-7914-4b1a-b800-76eb66fc32d6" id="4b9d81f7-7914-4b1a-b800-76eb66fc32d6-link">1</a></sup> and the Uluru statement<sup data-fn="b14dc0d3-8eb7-43c6-b3bb-241ccdd04b56" class="fn"><a href="#b14dc0d3-8eb7-43c6-b3bb-241ccdd04b56" id="b14dc0d3-8eb7-43c6-b3bb-241ccdd04b56-link">2</a></sup> disappointingly these have yet to be translated into meaningful action on the ground with treaties, legal changes or greater representation of indigenous voices in Parliament. Some of the states are working on their own processes though, such as Victoria working on a treaty this appears to be moving very slowly.</p>
