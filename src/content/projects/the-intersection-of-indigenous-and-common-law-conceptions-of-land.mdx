A powerful medium represented in the Archive is photo-journalism. Rahal Muhammed in his 2018 photo book "The Intersection of Indigenous and Common-Law Conceptions of Land: a Musqueam Case Study" explains why he chose this format.

![Rahal Muhammed's 2018 photo essay project titled, 'The Intersection of Indigenous and Common-Law Conceptions of Land: a Musqueam Case Study'](/assets/2024/10/Rahal_Muhammed_01-1024x769.jpg)

> This photobook seeks to explore the relationship between Indigenous peoples and land using photographic images and commentary. The images will primarily be used to examine the unique spiritual nature of First Nations' connections to their ancestral territories while trying to demonstrate how Indigenous conceptions of land go beyond reductive Euro-centric concepts of title and private property. The accompanying commentary will argue that Indigenous conceptions of land have been subsumed within the colonial legal framework of title codified in Canadian statute and common law, thereby impairing Indigenous connections to their ancestral territories while fuelling the continued commodification of land in Canada. Due to the accessibility of their history and sacred sites, this essay will moreover focus on the experiences of the xwməθkwəy̓ əm (Musqueam) First Nation in exploring these concepts.
>
> I have chosen to use photography as a medium for elucidating these ideas as I believe it allows for a deeper and more holistic understanding of Indigenous peoples' profound relationship to their lands. For Indigenous peoples, connection to one's land goes far beyond Common Law concepts of title or private property; as the book will explain, they tend to take on a far more esoteric element, in which the land possesses spiritual significance and informs the collective identities of First Nations. I would argue that simple writings on a page thus do not suffice in illuminating these complex ideals. My hope is that through photography, with its inherently immersive and visceral nature, Indigenous conceptions of land can be more fully explicated and appreciated.

What is particularly impressive with Muhammed's work is that his photos are of places that Vancouverites visit for recreation but which, through his lens, are shown as important sites for gathering food and celebration as part of the Musqueam Potlatch.

Muhammed's commentary, again through the lens of the camera, examines the Indigenous and Musqueam conceptions of land, and the many ways in which those conceptions have been subordinated to the interests of the settler population. His commentary also reviews some of the important litigation involving the Musqueam litigation, particularly the precedent setting cases of the *Supreme Court of Canada in Guerin v The Queen*, (1984) and *Sparrow v The Queen* (1990). In *Guerin* the Court recognized a legally enforceable fiduciary duty on the Crown in relation to the administration of reserve lands. In *Sparrow v The Queen*, the first case on the interpretation of section 35 of the Constitution act 1982, The Court affirmed that the Musqueam had an Aboriginal right to fish had not been extinguished but was protected by section 35.

The commentary concludes with a review of how the University of British Columbia, which is situated on Musqueam lands, is working with the Musqueam First Nation in a process of reconciliation and that while important initiatives have taken place reconciliation must involves more than changing street signs and requires recognition of and making space for Indigenous legal orders.[^1]
