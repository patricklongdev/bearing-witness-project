<p>Kate Bond in her meticulously drawn trypych story board created for the 2011 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar focusses on the 2005 Supreme Court decision on <em>Mikasew Cree</em>. The Mikisew Cree Nation is located within Treaty 8, which was made in 1899. The treaty surrendered 840 000 square kilometers of land to Canada in exchange for reserve land. The treaty rights were however;</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>subject to such regulations as may from time to time be made by the Government of the country, acting under the authority of Her Majesty, and saving and excepting such tracts as may be required or taken up from time to time for settlement, mining, lumbering, trading or other purposes.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The Minister of Canadian Heritage approved a winter road that was first going to go directly through the Mikisew Cree Nation Peace Point Reserve, and later changed to go around it, but still within their territory, without consultation from the Mikisew Cree Nation. The Mikisew Cree Nation objected to the building of the road, as it was in direct conflict with their Treaty right to use the land. The Supreme Court, applying the framework of the Duty to Consult articulated in the 2004 <em>Haida Nation</em> and the <em>Taku River Tlingit First Nation</em> cases, decided that the Crown must engage in a meaningful process of consultation and accommodation when a contemplated “taking up” may have an adverse effect on the exercise of a First Nation’s treaty rights. The Court held that the road did infringe upon the hunting rights of the Mikisew Cree Nation, that there was a duty to engage in consultation prior to e approval and building of the road and the Crown had failed to consult adequately<br />in the circumstances.<sup data-fn="cffb73f5-2c2c-4545-b457-e4c205a53d3a" class="fn"><a href="#cffb73f5-2c2c-4545-b457-e4c205a53d3a" id="cffb73f5-2c2c-4545-b457-e4c205a53d3a-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>The purpose of Kate's storyboards is not however to replicate a law review article analysing the Supreme Court’s judgement; her triptych takes us deep into the landscapes of the northern woods of the Mikasew Cree territory, the characters that live and sojourn there, the diverse and often conflicting interests they represent and their differing relationships to the land. In her Artist Statement she explains:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I used to work as a wildfire lookout in northern Alberta. I did the job for four years. When I made this project in 2011, for Professor Jackson’s seminar in my final term of law school, it hadn’t been long since my last season in the woods.</p>

<p>The boreal forests have been called “the spruce kingdom”—millions upon millions of square kilometers of black spruce, jack and lodgepole pine, trembling aspen, thickets of blueberry, willow, water birch, prickly rose; fireweed and yarrow and ox-eye daisies; lynx, moose, arctic hare, caribou, beaver, bears—also industry roads, cutlines, drill sites. Snow in June, lightning fires in August. The silence is absolute, except for the wind in the trees, the birdcalls, the thump of grouse, and at night, the howling of the timber wolves. The high wind carries the lonely fragrance of boreal trees; sometimes—at closer range—the dizzying, sugary scent of red clover thickets. The wind clears the biting flies and mosquitoes, but in the close air of the forest they mercilessly reassemble.</p>

<p>The world we inhabit in the city exists in a different plane from that of the woods. I liked studying law but in every fibre I ached to go back. I was “homesick” for the northern forest the first time I read Mikisew Cree, and experienced with a vague sense of disquiet the same landscape as seen through the law’s sharp-focused, monochrome lens. My little “graphic novelization” wasn’t intended as a critique of the judgment or its statement of the law. I guess if there’s a thesis to the project it has to do with a sense of fatalism over a court of law, or law itself, being able to grasp all the relevant points and points of view that any real story’s made up of. It’s a thesis that I’m not really able or qualified to articulate myself when it comes to Indigenous land and land-rights claims. I may have tasted the sweetness of life in the boreal forest, for a few years, but I can’t know what real, inherited existence there consists of, life for people like, say, the claimants in Mikisew Cree whose livelihoods, and more, were at stake. To the extent I can conceive of what it means to them, my sympathy’s immeasurable.</p>

<p>The Cree characters living in the north, in my depiction, are all based, roughly, on people I met in those years working in the woods, as are the industry workers. The lawyer and law student characters are all based in reality too. The character Josh Marten is fictional.</p>

<p>Part of my intention was to present the "story" (stories?) as highly fragmented and disparate (as well as not requiring additional exposition/explanation, other than (presumed on the part of law students/lawyers) that Mikisew Cree is a major Supreme Court of Canada case dealing with treaty and constitutional rights): my hope is that the various characters and their relationships (or lack thereof) to the issues central to Mikisew would be inferrable if not always clear from the dialogue and structure (contrasting the Indigenous longtime/year-round residents of the remote communities, for example, with the transitory/seasonal presence of resource workers and researchers; likewise contrasting the immediacy and impact on everyday life for those actually affected with the workaday detachment of the lawyers (on all sides) and law clerks; conveying (I hope) the difference in the case's significance to law students depending on the perspective each brings to bear on the decision; Justice Binnie as a character I left deliberately somewhat opaque, in the nature of judges; and my hope is that the end sequence strikes a balance among the hope, stoicism and realism with which these high-level decisions are met by those affected, and a realistic/pessimistic outlook on their individual effect.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The second storyboard takes us from the site of conflict in the northern woods to the converasations in the south and the images that Justice Binnie and other justices of the Supreme Court may have entertained in thinking about the Mikasew Cree case coming before them.</p>

<p>The third storyboard it takes us inside the courtroom of the Supreme Court of Canada where the robed figures of lawyers and judges argue the balance between the rights of indigenous hunters and the interests of large-scale resource development.</p>

<p>The last line of the story board highlights a probing question from Justice Binnie to counsel for Canada that while capturing the impoverished view of the Crown’s understanding of treaty hunting rights also reflects the gulf of difference between the indigenous and eurocentic world views.</p>
