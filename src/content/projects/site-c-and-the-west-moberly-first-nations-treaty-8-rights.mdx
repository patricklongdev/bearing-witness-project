<p>There several projects in the Archive that consider the contemporary context of Treaty 8 and how Crown governments and the courts have weighed the scales of justice in balancing the treaty protection of indigenous harvesting rights with the right of the Crown to take up lands for the all-encompassing purposes of “settlement, mining, lumbering trading or other purposes”. This clause has enabled the Crown to privilege the interests of a settler society and systematically undermine the treaty promises as they were understood by the Treaty Nations. This failure of the Crown to live up to promises in the Numbered Treaties as they were understood by the Treaty Nations is a theme explored across many forms of media.</p>

<p>Julia Roe in her project for the 2020 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation seminar addresses the frustrated efforts by Treaty 8 First Nations, including West Moberly to oppose the construction of the Site C hydroelectric dam. Her project consists of a fine-grained legal analysis, together with a painting that symbolically represents the struggle of West Moberly to meaningfully assert their rights. Her essay summarizes the project:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In July of 2015, BC Hydro began construction work on Site C, the third massive hydroelectric dam on the Peace River. The previous two dams “caused considerable dislocation and hardship” for Indigenous peoples in the area, making it unsurprising that Site C has been the target of significant community and Indigenous opposition. The 5,550 hectares of river valley allotted to be flooded by Site C is all land covered under Treaty 8. Treaty 8 First Nations, including West Moberly, descendants of the Dane-zaa, have consistently held that Site C is an unjustified infringement of treaty rights that protect their “practical, traditional, cultural, [and] spiritual connection to the land.” In 010, the West Moberly and other First Nations signed a declaration “vowing ‘to use all legal means to stop the Site C Dam from proceeding.’”</p>

<p>This paper will chronicle West Moberly’s persistent efforts to assert their treaty rights and the resultant series of administrative and judicial decisions that gave a greenlight to the Site C dam, while resolutely declining to determine whether or not the project infringes the Treaty rights of West Moberly. First, administrative decision makers decided that a determination of treaty infringement would not be made during the project’s regulatory approval process. This administrative decision was upheld under judicial review. Furthermore, judicial review courts declined to make a determination of infringement and directed West Moberly to commence a separate civil claim to resolve the matter. The courts then denied West Moberly’s request for an injunction on construction until the conclusion of their trial, scheduled for 2023. I will argue that the rights conferred on West Moberly by Treaty 8, and enshrined in section 35 of the Constitution Act, have essentially become stranded assets. Indeed, “at no point prior to the issuance of the Environmental Certificate, or since commencing construction, has the Crown or a court determined whether the taking of land for the purpose of Site-C will result in an infringement of Treaty 8.” Thus, although the Canadian legal system acknowledges that West Moberly holds the claimed treaty rights, Aboriginal law – as it is currently articulated by the administrative decision makers and the judiciary – renders the treaty rights virtually inoperative because determination of whether Site C represents an unjustified infringement has been postponed until after construction of the impugned project is nearly complete.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This is Julia’s painting and Artist Statement:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">

<p>I created a piece of art that is meant to symbolically represent the struggle of West Moberly to meaningfully assert their rights recognized in relation to Site C. The larger dome in the foreground represents West Moberly’s treaty rights. The bird in the center of the dome is the public-facing logo of West Moberly. The scene behind the bird depicts the Peace River valley thriving in its natural state, and therefore capable of facilitating West Moberly’s treaty rights to hunt, fish, trap, and gather, as well as their spiritual and cultural rights. The dome, representing West Moberly’s treaty rights, is suspended in the air by a chain that is strung up around a tree branch and attached to another dome. The second dome is filled with a money bag and is firmly on the ground, clearly outweighing the first dome. The two domes are meant to loosely resemble the scales of justice and symbolically represent how the economic and financial factors weighing in favor of proceeding with Site C have been prioritized over the risk of unjustifiably infringing on West Moberly’s treaty rights. The background depicts the construction of Site C and destruction of the Peace River Valley that results from this imbalance. The treaty rights are present in the scene but are “trapped” and therefore are powerless to do more than overlook the destruction. Overall, the art is intended to communicate the frustrating futility of the Canadian executive and judiciary recognizing the existence of West Moberly’s substantive, constitutionally protected rights under Treaty 8 while also implementing a regulatory and legal framework that makes it nearly impossible for West Moberly to effectively assert their treaty rights.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Julia’s essay argues that the case study of West Moberly with Site C demonstrates that “in the balance of convenience test, courts are more receptive and sympathetic to harms expressed in economic terms rather than harm to Indigenous peoples rights. Consequently, constitutionally protected rights are consistently, irreparably infringed as Indigenous Nations wait for their day in court”.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Ultimately, the courts have repeatedly held that neither the administrative decision makers in the regulatory process, nor judges hearing judicial review challenges, are competent to “adjudicate the scope and implications of [the petitioners’] treaty rights.” Moreover, the issue of treaty infringement is “best left to the courts in a separate action.” This puts a massive burden on Indigenous Nations to challenge unconstitutional government action by commencing a full, separate civil claim, rather than requiring Ministers to ensure government action is constitutional in the first place, with judicial review as an enforcement mechanism.</p>

<p>Committed to upholding and protecting their Treaty 8 rights, West Moberly followed the procedure for asserting a treaty right infringement laid out by the judicial review courts and commenced a separate action to determine the question of infringement and justification. West Moberly’s civil claim alleges “that the Project infringes West Moberly’s rights under Treaty 8 as protected by section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982, and that the infringement cannot be justified.” However, by 2018 when the claim was filed, after years of participating in the regulatory process and fighting the approval of Site C in various courts, project construction was well underway. The much of the activity which is alleged to infringe West Moberly’s treaty rights will already have been carried out prior to the conclusion of their civil action. Indeed, the trial is not scheduled to occur until mid 2023. Therefore, West Moberly applied for an injunction ordering BC Hydro to pause work on the Site C dam. West Moberly’s application for an interim injunction requested an order to:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“(i) stop all work on the Project pending trial, except for works required for safety, to protect the environment and to maintain works in place; (ii) in the alternative, on certain conditions, stopping (i) flooding of the reservoir and (ii) work within certain areas called "critical areas" by West Moberly.”</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Unfortunately for West Moberly, BC Supreme Court Justice Warren Milman was “not persuaded that either form of the injunction sought should be granted.” In applying the three part test for granting an injunction articulated by the SCC in RJR Macdonald, Justice Milman found that West Moberly (1) “raised a serious question to be tried” and (2) demonstrated “there is a risk that it will suffer irreparable harm if an injunction is not granted;” however, the Court concluded that (3) “the balance of convenience lies against granting either form of the injunction.”</p>

<p>From the outset of the judgement, it is clear that two different worldviews and value systems are being weighed against each other. Justice Milman notes that “West Moberly emphasizes the centrality of the Peace River and its environs to its culture, identify, and way of life,” and the importance of the area for hunting, trapping, fishing, gathering vocations, as well as “spiritual ceremonies and celebrations.” However, these points are juxtaposed with the Peace Valley’s potential for energy production and economic development. Numerical economic factors are emphasized such as the fact that BC Hydro had already invested 2.4$ billion, worked with 200 contractors, and employed 2000 people. The judgement marvels at the estimated capacity of the Site C dam to “generate up to 35% of the power produced at the WAC Bennett dam using a reservoir that will be only about 5% as big as Williston.” Justice Milman chooses to reference a quote from the JRP report that proposes future generations will view Site C as a “wonderful gift from the ancestors” and forget the loss of the Peace Valley just as we have forgotten the loss of the pristine wilderness flooded by previous hydroelectric projects on the Peace River1 The implication appears to be that it in time everyone will understand that any cultural, emotional, or spiritual attachment to the Peace River Valley was worth sacrificing for the benefits of heightened electricity consumption and economic development. I wonder whether the Indigenous Nations whose traditional territories were flooded without their consent by BC Hydro in 1967 for the WAC Bennett Dam and 1980 for the Peace Canyon Dam would agree with this sentiment.</p>

<p>After establishing that West Moberly has raised a serious question to be tried (though the court casts doubt on the strength of their claim) and recognizing that refusing an injunction will result in irreparable harm for West Moberly, the court moves onto considering the balance of convenience. Justice Milman opines that granting an injunction would result in chaos, disarray, delays, job losses, and increased costs that would constitute irreparable harm to BC Hydro and third party stakeholders, particularly in light of West Moberly’s refusal to provide an undertaking in damages in the event they lose their civil claim. Indeed, Justice Milman is unconvinced by West Moberly’s argument that, considering the 10.7$ billion price tag of the project, the additional costs that would be incurred by an injunction – particularly one that only protects “critical areas” – is not an unreasonable price to pay for ensuring constitutionally protected treaty rights are not infringed. Moreover, Justice Milman concludes that “irreparable financial harm” and economic consequences of granting an injunction outweigh both the “irreparable harm flowing from an unjustified infringement of Treaty 8” and “the public interest in advancing the cause of access to justice and reconciliation.” The judgement acknowledges that this conclusion may seem discordant with the guidance provided by the SCC in Haida to take care that jobs, government revenues, and the economy not receive “undue emphasis” in the balance of convenience test… with the result that Aboriginal interests tend to “lose” outright pending a final determination of the issue.” Justice Milman justifies his decision partly by arguing that there are there are “Aboriginal interests weighing on both sides” because BC Hydro has contracts to employ certain Indigenous groups. He declines to reckon, however, with the fact that the “Aboriginal interests” of West Moberly are constitutionally protected rights and therefore must be significantly more prioritized the economic interests of Indigenous peoples who rationally seek to benefit financially from incoming major projects.</p>

<p>In addition to denying an injunction, Justice Milman also rejected West Moberly’s request for an expedited trial within 18 months. The sole protection given to West Moberly’s treaty rights was an order that the trial be completed before the reservoir is actually flooded (mid 2023). However, all other work can go ahead up until that point to strip the flood zone bare and construct the colossal dam. Therefore, even if West Moberly wins at trial in 2023 the victory will be a moral and legal one – the lands, river, and ecosystems required to practice their treaty rights will have been irrevocably destroyed years prior.<sup data-fn="97ce0e64-21e6-4ca7-99fd-40680f6c4432" class="fn"><a href="#97ce0e64-21e6-4ca7-99fd-40680f6c4432" id="97ce0e64-21e6-4ca7-99fd-40680f6c4432-link">1</a></sup></p>
