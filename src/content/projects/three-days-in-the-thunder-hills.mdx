<p>Jennifer Dyck, as part of her LL.M program, for the 2012 Penal Policy seminar and as a Directed research project, created a quilt to explore, as a non-Indigenous woman, issues with which she had engaged in her graduate studies. Jennifer also presented a photo essay “Three Days in the Thunder Hills - A White Woman’s Reflections on the Okimaw Ohci Healing Lodge.</p>

<p>Jennifer explains in her essay accompanying the quilt how this deepened and personalized her understanding:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For the creative component of my directed research project, I am submitting a quilt that I have sewn. The quilt is designed to represent the drastic rate of over-incarceration of aboriginal women in Canada, who today make up 37% of the women in sentenced custody across the country.' The issue of aboriginal over-incarceration is very dear to my heart, and the manner in which it is differentially experienced by aboriginal women is the topic of my LL.M. thesis. However, before I made this quilt I had only ever really engaged with the issue of aboriginal female over-incarceration on academic and personal levels, never in a creative way. The experience of doing so was incredibly meaningful for me, and I feel it brought me a deeper and different understanding of this phenomenon.</p>

<p>The quilt is meant to be a representation of the life experiences of an aboriginal woman from British Columbia who has experienced a pattern of criminalization and incarceration for much of her life. I have used two different types of squares : pieces of clothing that represent life on the outside, and pieces of "prison uniforms" that represent life on the inside. I made the "prison uniform" squares by stenciling the names of various women's prisons (including the Prison for Women, Fraser Valley Institution, Okimaw Ohci Healing Lodge and various remand centres) onto rough blue fabric.</p>

<p>The "prison uniform" squares are nearly as great in number as the "life outside" squares, which was done to show how hard it can be to stay out of prison once you go in. The prisons were chosen to represent certain trends in the carceral experiences of federally sentenced aboriginal women, such as being forced to serve sentences far from home (e.g., at the Prison for Women) and having to choose between being close to family and actually receiving meaningful access to aboriginally-centred corrections (e.g., at Okimaw Ohci). While I used primarily British Columbia remand centres and provincial prisons, I also included other institutions (namely the Vanier Centre remand in Ottawa and the Edmonton Institution for Women) to demonstrate both the transience of some criminalized aboriginal women and the experience of failed attempts at moving on and staying away from prison.</p>

<p>On the other hand, the clothing squares are supposed to represent normalcy and hopefulness. I used T-shirts that reference British Columbia (e.g., the Vancouver Canucks and 2010 Olympics shirts) to reinforce the theme of where this woman came from. I also selected clothes that were printed with wording related to the experience of being released (e.g., the t-shirts printed with synonyms for the word "free") and the hope of staying out (e.g., the shirt printed with the word "believe"). The sparkling blue squares are meant to symbolize the experience of seeing the stars at night as a free woman, while the jeans are meant to represent the challenges of everyday life on the outside.</p>

<p>I learned to sew by making this quilt. In that way, the process of making it has reminded me of the challenges I face in my LL.M. work. As a white woman who has never experienced criminalization or incarceration in my personal life, I am an outsider to the experience of over- incarceration, no matter how much it may move me. However, the more I immerse myself in the issues, piece by piece as I did with the quilt, I develop a deeper and more personal connection to this reality, one that serves me greatly as I conduct my graduate work.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her second reflections essay Jennifer describes her journey to the Okimaw Ohci Healing Lodge.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I am from Saskatchewan, even though I have lived only two years of my life in the province. My family, whose origins are mainly British but with a healthy dose of Mennonite, settled in the province many generations ago. My maternal grandparents moved to Saskatoon from the small farming community of Tisdale shortly before my mother was born. My father, on the other hand, grew up on his family's homestead outside of Borden, about an hour's drive northwest of Saskatoon. The farm, now close to 7,000 acres in size, is still operated today by my uncle and cousins. We are, in short, a rather typical whitesettler Saskatchewan family.</p>

<p>I cannot help but feel that it was this Saskatchewan influence that peaked my interest in the aboriginal peoples of Canada, and particularly in the injustices that have been done to them by settlers and our colonial government. In Saskatchewan, the toll that colonialism has taken and continues to take on aboriginal Canadians is often inescapable: you need only walk down the streets of downtown Regina or the west side of Saskatoon to see firsthand the poverty, dislocation and social disorder that colonialism has wreaked upon them. This legacy has also translated itself into drastic rates of aboriginal over-representation within the province's criminal justice system. In fact, at most recent count, more than 80% of Saskatchewan's provincial prison population was of aboriginal descent, as compared to just 11% of the province's general population.' It was statistics such as these, which I first encountered during my years as an undergraduate student, which sparked my interest in the treatment of aboriginal people by the Canadian criminal justice system. As such, it was ultimately my connection to the province, and to its original inhabitants, that brought me to Maple Creek, Saskatchewan, to visit the women prisoners of the Okimaw Ohci Healing Lodge.</p>

<p>Jennifer’s paper summarizes the infamous history of The Prison for Women (P4W) at Kingston:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>At the time of its opening in 1934, P4W became the first exclusively female penitentiary in the country. However, the creation of a federal women's prison did not herald a new era in terms of the quality of the treatment accorded to federally sentenced women. In fact, as early as 1938, the Archambault Commission, a royal commission charged investigating the Canadian penal system, found the conditions at P4W to be significantly worse than those at other prisons in the country, stating: "the Women's Prison presents a marked contrast to any other institution.., visited in this or any other country, whether for men, women or children". Only four years after P4W opened, the Archambault Commission recommended that it be closed, making it the first of nine major government task forces and commissions to make such a recommendation By 1977, the MacGuigan Report of the Parliamentary Sub-Committee on the Penitentiary System in Canada concluded that P4W was "unfit for bears, much less women".</p>

<p>Likely the most devastating consequence of the decision to house all federally sentenced women at P4W was the impact that the resulting geographic dislocation had on the women and their families. The vast majority of the women at P4W hailed from outside of Ontario, and thus were forced to serve their sentences not only outside of their home provinces but also, in many cases, thousands of kilometers from their families. The significant distances involved, as well as the limited financial means of most prisoners and their families, meant that the vast majority of the women at P4W had only limited contact with their friends and families for the duration of their sentence. Enduring such distance was particularly devastating for those prisoners with children on the outside. As one former P4W prisoner explained : "There is nothing harder than facing kids that don't know you. Doing time is easy compared to that."</p>

<p>Life at P4W was especially difficult for aboriginal women, who faced multiple levels of discrimination within the prison system (e.g., on account of gender, race, and gender and race combined), and who were also dramatically over-represented within the P4W population. Aboriginal inmates had only limited access to culturally-appropriate programming at P4W, and often found themselves struggling to secure even basic recognition of their rights to engage in their traditional spiritual and cultural practices, such as participating in sweats and healing circles. For example, sweetgrass, whose burning is considered by many aboriginal cultures to have a medicinal effect, was deemed to be contraband by certain P4W staff." Similarly, staff would touch prisoners' medicine bundles, under the pretense that they had to be searched, with no regard to the fact that this act of touching would cause the medicine to be desecrated and to lose its spiritual power.' Nevertheless, aboriginal federally sentenced women had greater access to culturally-appropriate programming at P4W than they would have in the various provincial prison systems. This put aboriginal inmates in the unenviable position of having to choose between proximity to home and family on the one hand and a modicum of culturally-appropriate prison programming on the other. As a former aboriginal parolee explained: "I only stayed there (at a provincial institution) because family ties were important to me. But there were no programs for someone serving a long term. I don't think I should have to choose between a prison life and my life outside."</p>

<p>The harsh realities of life at P4W took a very serious toll on a great number of the inmates. Violence, both self-inflicted and as against guards and other prisoners, was commonplace. Sadly, informal estimates put the number of P4W prisoners to have committed suicide between 1977 and 1991 at no less than twelve, with eight of these women being of aboriginal descent.' Angela, a former P4W prisoner whom I met at the Healing Lodge, spoke to me of the prevalence of self-injurious and suicidal behaviour at P4W. A lifer, Angela was sent to P4W at the age of 20, where she remained until P4W's partial closure in 1995. She told me of the countless slashings she had witnessed at P4W, and of the cellmate who had "strung up" in their shared cell, leaving Angela to cut down her corpse. In the words of another former P4W prisoner, "Prison is frustration and anger so intense that cutting into the arteries of my own arm only alleviates some of the pain".</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In 1989, the federal government convened the Task Force on Federally Sentenced Women which recommended that P4W be closed, that five new regional institutions be built and that a separate Healing Lodge for Aboriginal women be opened. The creation of the Healing Lodge is a particular focus of Jennifer’s essay:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Indeed, as the Task Force acknowledged in its final report creating Choices, aboriginal women experience incarceration in different ways than do their non-aboriginal counterparts:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For Aboriginal women, prison is an extension of life on the outside, and because of this they find it impossible to heal in prison. They asserted that for them, prison rules have the same illegitimacy as the oppressive rules under which they grew up. In ways that are different from the world outside, but are nevertheless continuous with it, prisons offer more White authority that is sexist, racist and violent. Those few "helping" services in prison that are intended to heal are delivered in ways that are culturally inappropriate to them as women and as Aboriginal people. Physicians, psychiatrists and psychologists are typically White and male. They therefore symbolize the worst experiences of the Aboriginal women. As a result, Aboriginal women express anger at these caregivers, refuse to become involved, and then are further punished because they fail to seek treatment. </p>

<p>Almost all of the healing experiences that the Aboriginal women who have been in prison reported, lie outside of the conventional prison order. They come through the bonds formed with other women in prison, through the support of people on the outside, and from the activities of the Native Sisterhood.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Concluding that the problems facing aboriginal women at P4W could not be rectified by simply "tacking on" aboriginal women to corrections policies designed to meet the needs of non-aboriginal women, and bearing in mind the aboriginal prisoners' professed needs to be with other aboriginal women and to have meaningful access to aboriginal cultural teachings and healings, the Task Force recommended that a healing lodge prison for aboriginal women be opened somewhere in the Prairie provinces.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Jennifer describes the planning, building and opening of the Okimaw Ohci Healing Lodge:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In order to ensure that the Healing Lodge buildings were designed and constructed in an culturally-relevant way, an aboriginal architectural firm with experience in working for Bands and in building aboriginal ceremonial spaces was retained. The result would be a series of buildings that looked fundamentally different from any prison ever constructed in Canada. In addition to a main lodge housing administrative space, offices and a common eating area, a spiritual lodge, shaped like a large wooden tipi, was also constructed. The prisoners, in groups up to four, would be housed in self-contained cottages, which like the rest of the buildings, were designed with aboriginal conceptsof spirituality, including the sacred four directions, in mind.</p>

<p>The Healing Lodge was formally opened over the 23 and 24 August, 1995, making it the first of the Task Force's recommended regional women's federal prisons to open its doors. The opening itself was auspicious, as three eagles flew overhead during the opening ceremony, and wild horses watched from the surrounding hills. After countless hours of work and more than five years since the Task Force had recommended its establishment, the opening of the Healing Lodge heralded a new beginning for aboriginal women's federal corrections in Canada.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Based on her interviews and observations, including her participation in the morning healing circle and a sweat lodge, Jennifer assesses how well the Healing Lodge has lived up to the high expectations of a new chapter in Canadian corrections:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Easy and meaningful access to aboriginal teachings and spiritual practices was, and remains, a cornerstone of the Healing Lodge Vision. Many, if not the majority, of the prison's residents have had little to no personal experience with aboriginal cultural traditions and ceremonies prior to their arrival at the Healing Lodge. For many of the residents, the opportunity to learn about aboriginal culture and spirituality is deeply meaningful and contributes in a substantial way to their personal healing. The Healing Lodge provides its residents with access to aboriginal spiritua1ty in three main ways: through the teaching of elders, through the morning healing circle and through the conduct of sweats, sundances, potlatches and other cultural gatherings. At the Healing Lodge, aboriginal spirituality is not an afterthought or simply another component of the prison's programming, rather it permeates daily life at the institution.</p>

<p>However, in recent years the Healing Lodge has imposed greater controls and limitations on resident access to aboriginal spirituality. In response to misuse of the Spiritual Lodge by a small group of inmates, all of the residents are now banned from the space in the evenings, a source of significaint discontent. Andrea, a resident in her late 30's who had been at the Healing Lodge in the years shortly after it opened and then returned in 2010, told me that there have been significant changes in terms of the prevalence of and access to aboriginal spirituality at the Healing Lodge. While Andrea blamed more restrictive CSC policies, she also blamed the changing prison demographics, explaining that the younger residents (especially the non-aboriginal ones) did not respect the spiritual teachings and practices, and that their actions contributed to the prison administration's decisions to limit access. Nevertheless, over the course of my time at the Healing Lodge, I began to notice that there were in fact many ways in which the prison administration was unduly limiting access by residents to the aboriginal spirituality that they so deeplv need and desire.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Elders</h3>

<p>Access to Elders is an incredibly important part of the healing program that is offered to the residents of the Healing Lodge. The Elders, many of whom I had the chance to meet, offer the women guidance, support and advice, as well as a path towards a better understanding of aboriginal ways and beliefs. As far as I could tell, the Elders are, with the exception of Mary Louie (the British Columbia Elder who sits on the Keepers of the Vision Committee and occasionally visits the prison), drawn from the local Neekaneet community, and are paid employees of the Healing Lodge. The Elders are an essential component of the spiritual component of life at the prison: not only do they teach and guide the women, they also facilitate the morning healing cirde, sweats and other ceremonies and practices.</p>

<p>However, as Andrea explained to the Keepers of the Vision Committee, today the residents have only limited access to one-on-one time with the Elders. Furthermore, it seems that the Healing Lodge has not necessarily been respecting the appropriate way of interacting with the Elders, and for example has been failing to consistently make the weekly tobacco offerings that had previously be made to them. During my visit, I noticed that there may have been a certain amount of conflict between aboriginal spirituality and CSC policies at the Healing Lodge. For example, on my second day at the prison I brought a pouch of loose tobacco to offer to the Elder whose sweat I would be attending later that day. After being subjected to significant security screening, I was told that due to a ban on all tobacco products in federal prisons, I would not be permitted to bring in the tobacco, regardless of whether it was meant as a traditional offering to an Elder. I was surprised to find the (non-aboriginal) officers to be so intransigent and formalistic in their application of the tobacco ban, and couldn't help but wonder what other CSC rules were applied in a way that minimized and disrespected aboriginal cultural practices.</p>

<p>The role of the Elders has also been diminished in other more administrative ways, which in turn have a negative impact on the connection between the Elders and the residents. For example, as was noted in the 2010 Okimaw Ohci Healing Lodge Joint Review conducted by the Nekaneet First Nation, the Elders were no longer being consulted with respect to the admission of new residents.</p>

<p>Nevertheless, I saw first-hand the positive impact that access to Elders was having on many of the Healing Lodge residents. Diane, an aboriginal woman in her 40's, hold me that she felt as though the Elders "knew her better than she knew herself" and were helping her to deal with the feelings of guilt she experienced knowing that she had only a comparatively short sentence to serve at the Healing Lodge, while so many of the other women had to be there for such a long time. The prisoners refer to the Elders, all of whom are female, as Koo/eum, meaning "grandmother" in Cree. I noticed that even the non-aboriginal residents seemed to feel very connected to the Elders, and showed them significant respect, even through small gestures such as clearing the Elders' lunch trays.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Morning Healing circle</h3>

<p>Another important aspect of spiritual life at the Healing Lodge is the conduct of a daily healing circle, which is held at 8 a.m. every morning in the Spiritual Lodge. The interior of the Spiritual Lodge is circular, and beyond a peripheral hallway opens up into a round circle room with vaulted ceilings. Within the room, an inner circle surrounds a large wood-burning stove, and an outer circle (to be used by women during their moon time (menstrual period)) lines the walls. On my second day at the Healing Lodge I had the opportunity to participate in the morning circle, along Elder Mary Louie, Rose Bird and the Neekaneet Chief Jordi Fourhorns, as well as the residents and a couple of Healing Lodge staff.</p>

<p>After my participation in the circle, the residents were much more eager to speak with me, and began to seek me out to converse with me. Some of them shared their concerns about how the healing circles operate, noting that the circle I had attended was an unusually powerful one. For example, the women told me that Healing Lodge staff rarely attend morning circle. This is apparently especially so with respect to the non-aboriginal staff (who today make up approximately 20% of the prison's staff), many of whom have never attended morning circle. Indeed, of the three staff who were present at the circle I attended, only one was non-aboriginal, and she told me this was the first circle she had attended in her six months of employment at the Healing Lodge. This non-attendance conflicts significantly with the initial vision for the role of the morning circle, an important component of which was the creation of trusting relationships amongst staff and residents. Norma Green, the first Kikan'inan' of the Healing Lodge, wrote of the promise of the morning circle: "Talking and spiritual circles provide a safe and supportive avenue to deal with all issues. It breaks down the hierarchy that is often perceived of staff. In a circle everyone is equal and all the information that is shared is a learning experience. Generally, everyone learns from listening to one another."</p>

<p>However, staff attendance at the daily Healing Circle also poses its own set of challenges, in that it creates an uneasy interaction between the healing and punitive aspects of life at the Healing Lodge. While staff attendance would likely serve to create a better rapport and understanding as amongst staff and residents, it also has the capacity to create a conflict between staff's obligations with respect to upholding CSC policies on the one hand, and their duty to respect the confidentiality of the circle on the other. As Stephanie Hayman explained:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>While women are likely to reserve certain matters for private discussions with Elders, the circle in its intimacy invites relaxation of a woman's guard. Should a woman inadvertently reveal something during the circle that might be thought relevant to her correctional record, staff members are then brought face-to-face with the reality of their own positions: they are correctional officers and they should record the information.</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This conflict drives home an important point: as authentic and healing as the morning circle may be, it remains spiritually-driven prison programming. That fact is also reinforced by the fact that participating in morning circle is compulsory, as opposed to other ceremonies (including sweats), which remain voluntary.</p>

<p>My three days in the Healing Lodge in the Thunder Hills were among the most meaningful and inspirational in all my life. They reminded me of the reasons why I have chosen to dedicate my professional life to law, and what it is I find so compelling about the way the criminal justice system treats aboriginal women. They gave me a chance to put faces and life stories to the endless statistics that otherwise dominate most accounts of the over-incarceration of indigenous women in Canada. I will always be grateful to the women who shared their stories with me, some of whom I hope to keep in contact with, and to the Elders for what they showed me about the power of simply taking the time to listen and show compassion. I feel honoured to have been shown such kindness, and feel obliged to do my utmost to live up to Katie's stated wish that one day, I make a difference for aboriginal women in Canada.</p>

<p>However, my trip to the Healing Lodge also taught me a much more practical lesson, namely that a prison is a prison, is a prison. Despite the dreams of the Task Force and the Planning Committee, the best intentions of the CSC and staff, the expectations of women's groups and of course the hopes of many of the residents themselves, my time at the Healing Lodge served to reinforce for me the extent to which the institution very much remains a federal women's prison, albeit a more humane and culturally-relevant one. While the Healing Lodge has succeeded in some very real ways (such as through the adoption of culturally-relevant programming and the provision of access to spiritual practices) in creating a new kind of women-centred corrections for aboriginal women, it has also fallen down in others (such as by limiting Elder involvement, increasing the use of traditional security mechanisms, failing to provide cultural training to staff and so on).</p>

<p>At this point, the direction that the Healing Lodge will take in the future remains unclear. The current political climate with respect to corrections in Canada certainly does not bode well for the reinvigoration of the Healing Lodge as it was initially envisaged. However, the renewed Keepers of the Vision Committee, Elders, Neekaneet First Nation and some of the CSC staff seem truly committed to seeing the Healing Lodge reach its full potential. Ultimately though, I am mindful of the fact that no matter how healing and culturally-relevant it may become, at the end of the day the Healing Lodge is but a rejigged version of a federal women's prison. As Fran Sugar and Lana Fox wrote in "Nisturn Peyako Séht'wawin Iskwewak: Breaking Chains", their report on the experiences of aboriginal federally sentenced women produced at behest of the Task Force:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>No amount of tinkering with prisons can heal the before-prison lives of the Aboriginal women who live or have lived within their walls. Prison cannot remedy the problem of the poverty of reserves. It cannot deal with immediate or historical memories of the genocide that Europeans worked upon our people. It cannot remedy violence, alcohol abuse, sexual assault during childhood, rape, and other violence Aboriginal women experience at the hands of men. Prison cannot heal the past abuse of foster homes, or the indifference and racism of Canada's criminal justice system in its dealings with Aboriginal people. However, the treatment of Aboriginal women within prisons can begin to recognize that these things arc the realities of the lives that Aboriginal women prisoners have led. By understanding this point, we can begin to make changes that will promote healing instead of rage.</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>If we apply Fox and Sugar's marker, the Healing Lodge has been but a partial success: while it may have tipped the scales towards the promotion of healing and away from the inculcation of rage so common at P4W, whether it will succeed in doing more than that remains unknown.</p>
</blockquote>
