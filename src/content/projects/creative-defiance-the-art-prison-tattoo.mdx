<p>Lucas McFadden in his 2015 project reflected on the way tattoos were one of the ways in which prisoners could express individual identity in an oppressive environment. As he explained in his essay:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Spring 2015 Penal Policy seminar revealed to me the impact of imprisonment and segregation on the individual. As a class we have read that "the experience of imprisonment uniquely retains the ability to crush the human spirit," that the bodies of inmates can be "broken on the anvil of imprisonment," and that the deprivation associated with segregation "ultimately dissolves the hope of the strongest person and generates a cycle of alternating rage and despair." This onslaught on the individual facilitated by imprisonment has led to a personal preoccupation with the ways in which the human spirit can avoid the danger inherent in the experience of imprisonment and find spaces in which to express itself within the physical and psychological confines of the prison world. Specifically, I have spent a great deal of time considering methods of individual creative expression on the inside as well as methods of asserting and expressing individual identity in the oppressive prison environment.</p>

<p>When our class visited Matsqui Institution on March 3, 2015 I noticed at least one element of the expression of individuality and identity in the form of jailhouse tattoos, that is, tattoos done within the prison using materials readily available in the prison. Tattoos are commonly permanent markers of identity and/or commitment to certain, goals and values. This is especially true where tattoos indicate gang or group affiliation, identity, and/or membership. There were a number of such tattoos on display at Matsqui.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Lucas himself has tattoos and moving beyond a conceptual framework he constructed a set of tattoo guns using the materials which would be available to a prisoner.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the wake of the Matsqui visit I decided that my final project would be the construction of a series of functional jailhouse tattoo machines using only those materials available to prisoners pursuant to Commissioner's Directive 566-12: Personal Property of Offenders. After successfully building the machines I used the best one to tattoo myself. What this project revealed was the high level of ingenuity and sheer hard work that is required to facilitate expression and identity via tattoos within the context of imprisonment.</p>
</blockquote>
