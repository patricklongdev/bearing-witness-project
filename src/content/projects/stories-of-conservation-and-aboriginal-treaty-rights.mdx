<p>Storytelling is now recognized as an important Indigenous methodology particularly in the transmission of oral histories and drawing out Indigenous laws. However, storytelling also plays a significant role in non-Indigenous cultures and indeed in many of our experiences of childhood, parenthood and grandparenthood. Shirley Smiley, in her project for the 2011 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar created two stories about two cases in the British Columbia Court of Appeal that address issues of treaty interpretation and their intersection with principles of conservation. The first case <em>Saanichton Marina Ltd v Claxton</em> arises from one of the 1850's Douglas Treaties on Vancouver Island, and the other <em>Halfway River. First Nation v British Columbia Ministry of Forests</em> involves the 1899 Treaty 8 in Northeast British Columbia. Shirley explains her reasons for choosing storytelling:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In the stories that follow I attempt to retell the facts and principles set out in these cases for a young audience . This is my first attempt at writing for children or youth. In preparation I studied examples of children's literature written from, or telling of the experiences of Aboriginal people. I have borrowed several techniques from these examples including: (i) writing with an intentionally personal, and informal voice; (ii) repetition of words, sentences, and ideas; (iii) use of poetic or metaphorical phrasing to ensure the stories are sufficiently rich for adult readers, but remain interesting and digestible for young people(iv) use of indeterminate timelines to reflect the mix of historical and contemporary elements which shape Aboriginal experiences; and (v) telling the stories out loud.</p>

<p>With regard to this last point, I have included a voice recording of each story in this submission. Children's stories, not just those about Aboriginal experiences, are told out loud. In this way they fall uniquely between the archetypes of traditional western literature and Aboriginal oral history. I felt that it was important to capture the intersection of these storytelling techniques by capturing the rhythm and inflection I impart on the words in each story.</p>

<p>I made the decision to stick closely to the facts presented in the judgments for two reasons. First, I am not an Aboriginal person. I do not want to be perceived as co-opting the stories of the people and nations that feature in these cases. Rather, I aim to draw from the public record and tell the stories as I understand them, from the perspective of an outsider. Second, I recall when I was nine years old moving from London to Brantford, Ontario. Brantford is situated on the Grand River adjacent to the Six Nations Reserve, the most populous reserve in Canada. When I started attending school in Brantford and first realized that some of my classmate s were Aboriginal, I was shocked. At that point, all I had learned about Indians led me to the conclusion that they were fictional, certainly they no longer existed (if they ever had). It occurs to me that Canadian parents and teachers may well wish to do more than to leave children and youth with a similarly mystical image of Indians. I hope that this pair of stories, inspired by contemporary facts drawn from the public record, might provide a platform for children and youth to be engaged in realistic, balanced, and informed discussion about Aboriginal experiences in Canada.<sup data-fn="c2402270-8c4b-49d4-93e4-dacf9a9414cf" class="fn"><a href="#c2402270-8c4b-49d4-93e4-dacf9a9414cf" id="c2402270-8c4b-49d4-93e4-dacf9a9414cf-link">1</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
