<p>20 years after Kate’s reflections, Gwen Hamilton, an exchange student from Aotearoa New Zealand, in her project for the 2023 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar examined how the courts have interpreted legislation allocating Maori customary rights to marine areas and the interweaving of Maori customary law, Tikanga Māori, into the common law system in Aotearoa New Zealand. Her analysis is reflected in her painting that incorporates a Maori Kōwhaiwhai design pattern.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped">
<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2026/01/Gwen_Hamilton_02-1024x521.jpg" alt="Painting from Gwen Hamilton's 2023 project titled, &quot;Oceans and Origins.&quot; " class="wp-image-4634"/></figure>
</figure>

<p>Gwen’s painting and her accompanying essay is a timely comparative contribution to what has emerged as an important and central part of the contemporary Canadian public discourse and evolving jurisprudence on the recognition of pre-existing Indigenous legal orders and their rightful place in legal pluralism that includes not just the common law and civil law, but also Indigenous laws, and conceptions of federalism that acknowledges federal, provincial and Indigenous jurisdictions.<sup data-fn="cfd5e64c-8252-4321-ae18-8fe65e5a226f" class="fn"><a href="#cfd5e64c-8252-4321-ae18-8fe65e5a226f" id="cfd5e64c-8252-4321-ae18-8fe65e5a226f-link">1</a></sup></p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This artwork reflects the interweaving of Tikanga Māorii into the common law system in Aotearoa New Zealand. This artwork was inspired by my research into how the courts have interpreted legislation allocating customary rights to marine areas. The current legislative framework in Aotearoa continues to stifle indigenous customary rights to marine and coastal areas. However, the courts have taken a more creative approach in interpreting this legislation. In particular, an understanding of the genealogical connection to those marine bodies, as well as a recognition of indigenous models of ownership, has been utilized in establishing customary rights.</p>

<p>The painting depicts a Kōwhaiwhai koru pattern on the left which is eroding the steps of Parliament. Kōwhaiwhai has great spiritual significance in te ao Māori. It can symbolize an ancestral connection, the meeting of Papatuanuku and Ranginui, and waves. The Kōwhaiwhai pattern in this painting is referential to the ancestral origin story of Māori and the Earth - Papatuanuku (earth) and Ranginui (sky) who were separated by their sons to create space for life on Earth. The two colours intertwining symbolize the connection of the two ancestors. Kōwhaiwhai are often used to symbolise geneology for a particular iwi. By using the pattern and transforming it into a wave, the artwork reflects the deeper genealogical connection between Māori and the natural world.</p>

<p>The pattern I have chosen for the Kōwhaiwhai is a simple koru, which is based on the unfurling of a silver fern. The circular shape of the koru is referential to constant movement and a return to a point of origin. By using this simple shape, I have attempted to symbolise the way that law in Aotearoa will return to the origin: the aim is to restore customary interests back to Māori by a re-imagining of common law.</p>

<p>I have chosen to depict the erosion of Parliament, rather than the interweaving of two laws, because it seems that in order to truly accommodate Tikanga into the common law the current Western system must give way. Furthermore, the courts decisions that I have analysed appear to show that the judiciary is actively shifting away from the intended meaning of legislation, in order to reach a meaning that is more consistent with Tikanga. This holds great significance for future claims to Aboriginal title in Aotearoa and other countries where Indigenous law is recognised as co-existing alongside the settler legal system.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In appreciating Gwen’s essay and painting I would point out that there is a long narrative arc linking Canada and New Zealand in the recognition of Aboriginal rights in both the common law and treaties. The importance of understanding Aboriginal rights in a comparative framework in light of the experience of other colonies, and especially New Zealand, established in a similar timeframe as British Columbia, has been recognized in the most recent jurisprudence of the New Zealand Court of Appeal. President Cooke, in a 1990 decision observed:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>More recently in Canada Indian rights have been identified as preexisting legal rights not created by royal proclamation, statute or executive order. It has been recognised that, in some circumstances at least, the Crown is under a fiduciary duty to holders of such rights in dealings relating to their extinction. The judgments in Guerin, cited earlier, delivered by Dickson J. and Wilson J. seem likely to be found of major guidance when such matters come finally to be decided in New Zealand. The approach of this Court in the <em>Maori Council</em> case to the principles of the Treaty of Waitangi and the partnership and fiduciary analogies there drawn are consistent with them. … There are constitutional differences between Canada and New Zealand, but the Guerin judgments do not appear to turn on these. Moreover, in interpreting New Zealand Parliamentary and common law it must be right for New Zealand Courts to lean against any inference that in this democracy the rights of the Maori people are less respected than the rights of aboriginal peoples are in North America.<sup data-fn="0d3d864b-b84e-41c0-a6e6-5cf1841a2d32" class="fn"><a href="#0d3d864b-b84e-41c0-a6e6-5cf1841a2d32" id="0d3d864b-b84e-41c0-a6e6-5cf1841a2d32-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>150 years earlier in <em>R v. Symonds</em> Justice Chapman in his N.Z. Supreme Court decision relied upon "principles of universal application" to resolve a dispute relating to the private purchase of lands from its Maori proprietors:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The intercourse of civilized nations, and especially of Great Britain, with the aboriginal Natives of America and other countries, during the last two centuries, has gradually led to the adoption and affirmation by the Colonial Courts of certain established principles of law applicable to such intercourse. Although these principles may at times have been lost sight of, yet animated by the humane spirit of modem times, our Colonial Courts and the Courts of such of the United States of America as have adopted the common law of England, have invariably affirmed and supported them; so that at this day, a line a judicial decision, the current of legal opinion, and above all the settled practice of the colonial Governments, have concurred to clothe with certainty and precision what would otherwise have remained vague and unsettled. These principles are not the new creation or invention of the colonial Courts. They flow not from what an American writer has called the 'vice of judicial legislation', they are in fact to be found among the earliest settled principles of our law; and they are in part deduced from those higher principles from charters made in conformity with them, acquiesced in even down to the charter to our own Colony; and from the letter of treaties with Native tribes wherein those principles have been asserted and acted upon.<sup data-fn="4d300fd9-05c6-4d61-94b0-1c7a38616189" class="fn"><a href="#4d300fd9-05c6-4d61-94b0-1c7a38616189" id="4d300fd9-05c6-4d61-94b0-1c7a38616189-link">3</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>In his judgment Justice Chapman references the 1840 Treaty of Waitangi, a foundational part of the Aotearoa New Zealand legal landscape. Leila Nasr in her paper “Customary Marine Title in Aotearoa and Lessons for the Canadian Context”, a project for the 2021 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar, summarized the genesis of the Treaty:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Shortly after the British arrived in Aotearoa, a set of common principles were drafted which would guide the ‘peaceable and respectful’ settlement of the area by the colonial power. This document, entitled the ‘Treaty of Waitangi’, was signed on February 6, 1840 between some 540 Māori rangatira (Māori chiefs or leaders) on behalf of their hapū (sub-tribes which form part of a wider iwi) and representatives of the British Crown.<sup data-fn="5b8d4d2d-1c12-4c6c-9155-c9435f3d7fc7" class="fn"><a href="#5b8d4d2d-1c12-4c6c-9155-c9435f3d7fc7" id="5b8d4d2d-1c12-4c6c-9155-c9435f3d7fc7-link">4</a></sup> The principles underlying the Treaty are the subject of some interpretive debate, but are said to include: (i) a duty to act in good faith, reasonably and honorably, (ii) the principle of partnership between the two peoples, and (iii) the principle of protection.<sup data-fn="b4ab286b-dad3-4c67-989d-ec0fdbd7554b" class="fn"><a href="#b4ab286b-dad3-4c67-989d-ec0fdbd7554b" id="b4ab286b-dad3-4c67-989d-ec0fdbd7554b-link">5</a></sup> In some respects, these principles are very similar to those which underlie section 35 of the Constitution Act in Canada, such as the duty of good faith, honor of the Crown and the Crown’s fiduciary duty in relation to Indigenous Peoples.<sup data-fn="106515b0-b7ef-486d-aaac-734f4ef77e40" class="fn"><a href="#106515b0-b7ef-486d-aaac-734f4ef77e40" id="106515b0-b7ef-486d-aaac-734f4ef77e40-link">6</a></sup></p>

<p>Despite the abovementioned interpretive debates, there is consensus today that the Treaty of Waitangi affirms at least one thing: that all land in Aotearoa was once Māori customary land, held in accordance with Māori laws and customs (tikanga Māori).<sup data-fn="df373e6c-cc3b-4be0-bdc0-a33e6fc7851b" class="fn"><a href="#df373e6c-cc3b-4be0-bdc0-a33e6fc7851b" id="df373e6c-cc3b-4be0-bdc0-a33e6fc7851b-link">7</a></sup> According to Jacinta Ruru, the Treaty of Waitangi ultimately “endorsed the position at common law that a change in sovereignty does not extinguish indigenous people’s property rights, and specifically, Māori remain the proprietors [of their territories] until they wish to sell to the Crown”.<sup data-fn="42b4186f-8357-416a-953f-5180e8b8e0f5" class="fn"><a href="#42b4186f-8357-416a-953f-5180e8b8e0f5" id="42b4186f-8357-416a-953f-5180e8b8e0f5-link">8</a></sup> This extends to Māori marine areas, too. While there are disagreements about the meaning of particular articles in the Treaty between the English and the Māori versions (which the majority of rangatira signed), both versions acknowledge the Māori not only owned the dry land, but the fisheries and ‘other properties’ in marine landscapes too. As Ruru points out, this aligns with the Māori worldview which makes little distinction between land above and below water: “It was all considered one country.”<sup data-fn="1af31337-745f-4263-99c1-0c0c70997dc7" class="fn"><a href="#1af31337-745f-4263-99c1-0c0c70997dc7" id="1af31337-745f-4263-99c1-0c0c70997dc7-link">9</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Most recently, and significantly, the New Zealand Supreme Court in a case unrelated to marine title had occasion to consider the comparative law from both Canada and the UK on the issue of whether a criminal appeal survived the death of the appellant.<sup data-fn="4634f1da-1408-4292-928d-66e1694e9811" class="fn"><a href="#4634f1da-1408-4292-928d-66e1694e9811" id="4634f1da-1408-4292-928d-66e1694e9811-link">10</a></sup> In addressing the issue, the Court considered the principles of Māori tikanga and their relationship to the common law. Justice Glazebrook J. in a judgment supported by a majority of the Court stated:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>[107] I adopt the Statement of Tikanga’s discussion of the nature of tikanga as including all the “values, standards, principles or norms that the Māori community subscribe to, to determine the appropriate conduct”. I also adopt the Statement of Tikanga’s description of tikanga as comprising both practice and principle. I acknowledge that tikanga Māori was the first law of Aotearoa.<br />[110] It is worth saying something more about values. It is the function of this Court to declare the law of Aotearoa/New Zealand and we must do so mindful of the values that in combination give us our own sense of community and common identity. We share some of these values with other nations, especially those founded on the common law tradition. Other relevant values may be unique to our nation’s history and circumstances. Tikanga and kaupapa Māori belong to this latter category and are of<br />particular importance as tikanga is the first law of Aotearoa/New Zealand and Māori are tangata whenua: tikanga is part of the values of the New Zealand variety of the common law. The consideration of common values is important when applying the common law to new or novel situations or when considering the need (or otherwise) to develop or modify the common law.<br />[118]… tikanga principles and values may have an influence on the development of the common law. They can also provide a new vocabulary or new way of thinking about new concepts of law or a new intellectual framework for those concepts.<br />[119] Challenging issues may arise where there may be a difference between the process or result indicated by tikanga principles and that under the current common law. Such issues may arise due to the traditionally more individualistic nature of the common law and the more relational and communitarian perspective of tikanga. That does not necessarily mean the two are irreconcilable or necessarily by default sit in opposition. The methodology of resolving any differences will need to be worked through on a case by case basis.<sup data-fn="058b737d-9a11-4e41-97a1-d418aed2cc2d" class="fn"><a href="#058b737d-9a11-4e41-97a1-d418aed2cc2d" id="058b737d-9a11-4e41-97a1-d418aed2cc2d-link">11</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>In the Canadian context within the framework of section 35, to use the language of Chief Justice McLachlin in <em>Tsilhqot’in</em>: reconciliation is not just a methodology of resolving competing interests but the “governing ethos.”<sup data-fn="b255c700-ac88-4bc7-b812-97b0eb8367ed" class="fn"><a href="#b255c700-ac88-4bc7-b812-97b0eb8367ed" id="b255c700-ac88-4bc7-b812-97b0eb8367ed-link">12</a></sup></p>

<p>Gwen Hamilton in her essay addresses the issue what has only recently been addressed in the Canadian jurisprudence -that of Aboriginal title to marine spaces. In the <em>Saugeen</em> case, the Chippewas of Nawash Unceded First Nation and the Saugeen First Nation, collectively referred to as “SON”, sued Canada and Ontario for a declaration that they have Aboriginal title to submerged lands in a large section of Lake Huron and Georgian Bay, surrounding the Bruce Peninsula. In rejecting the claim The trial judge found that navigable waters were subject to the public right of navigation which, she concluded, was incompatible with SON’s claim of exclusivity. The trial judge also reviewed regimes in place in Australia and New Zealand and concluded that they did not support SON’s argument.<sup data-fn="51cadec7-da6e-4c0d-90b4-f10abab442f4" class="fn"><a href="#51cadec7-da6e-4c0d-90b4-f10abab442f4" id="51cadec7-da6e-4c0d-90b4-f10abab442f4-link">13</a></sup> On appeal the Ontario Court of Appeal in referring the case back to the trial judge stated:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>[96] At this stage, it is unnecessary for this court to assess whether the public right of navigation is incompatible with Aboriginal title.</p>

<p>[97] Whether or not Aboriginal title to a portion of the Great Lakes is compatible with the right of public navigation cannot be assessed until the extent of Aboriginal title in submerged lands is determined. If SON were able to satisfy the <em>Tsilhqot’in</em> test and establish Aboriginal title to the claim area, or a discrete portion or portions of the claim area, a court would then be able to assess whether such Aboriginal title to submerged land was not cognizable due to common law public rights, or whether such Aboriginal title would have such a substantial effect on public navigation as to create an incompatibility between Aboriginal title and the public right.<sup data-fn="c03e29ba-93b0-433d-a223-b64e6493ac2d" class="fn"><a href="#c03e29ba-93b0-433d-a223-b64e6493ac2d" id="c03e29ba-93b0-433d-a223-b64e6493ac2d-link">14</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>The issue of Aboriginal title to marine spaces is also before the Courts of British Columbia in the Haida Nation’s title case and the Haida Nation intervened in the Saugeen appeal arguing that title to ocean spaces was reconcilable with the public right of navigation. In the 2025 decision of the BC Supreme Court in <em>Cowichan Tribes v. Canada (Attorney General)</em> Justice Young rejected BC’s arguments about the incompatibility of Aboriginal title to submerged lands with the public rights of navigation and fishing, stating,</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I agree with the plaintiffs that BC is attempting to force Indigenous ancestral practices into the square box of common law concepts, which McLachlin C.J.C. cautioned against in <em>Tsilhqot'in</em>. One cannot disregard a pre-existing Aboriginal right because it does not accord with the common law. The correct approach is to define Aboriginal title as it existed at the date of sovereignty, and then reconcile any incompatibility. It is not consistent with the approach set out in Tsilhqot’in to define and limit the existence of Aboriginal title by other common law rights which may exist in respect of the same land. It is not appropriate to discount a claim for Aboriginal title by waving a generic spectre of incompatibility with the public right of navigation. new<sup data-fn="3be0646b-f4a5-429f-bcab-38cc0de52d53" class="fn"><a href="#3be0646b-f4a5-429f-bcab-38cc0de52d53" id="3be0646b-f4a5-429f-bcab-38cc0de52d53-link">15</a></sup> <em>Cowichan Tribes v. Canada (Attorney General)</em>, 2025 BCSC 1490, para.1620.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Though the lens of both the <em>Ellis</em> case and <em>Re Edwards</em> Gwen maintains that despite statutory limitations on Maori marine claims the courts in Aotearoa New Zealand are opening legal spaces for Maori legal traditions<a href="/assets/2023/01/Gwen_Hamilton.pdf">.</a></p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Indigenous Law and Common Law</h3>

<p>In Aotearoa, there is no equivalent to the Aboriginal title protected by Section 35 of the Constitution Act. Although Māori are able to claim that they have rights to tino rangatiratanga through the Treaty of Waitangi, there is limited space to recognise spheres in which Māori are able to use Tikanga Māori (Māori law) to govern their own communities.</p>

<p>However, recently there has been recognition that Tikanga Māori may form part of the common law of Aotearoa. A key area where this is occurring is entitlement to coastal and marine areas of land. As this paper will show, Tikanga has been used by the courts to reshape an understanding of rights to marine bodies that is more encompassing of Māori interests, going against the limiting statutory framework.</p>

<p>This short paper will discuss how the next step in incorporating indigenous law into the common law system should be a recognition of the genealogical connection between indigenous peoples and the natural environment. Cases such as <em>Ellis v R </em>and <em>Re Edwards</em> Whakatohea are a starting point for this development.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><em>Ellis v R</em> and Tikanga Māori</h3>

<p>It has now been recognised, unanimously by the New Zealand Supreme Court, that Tikanga Māori forms part of the common law of Aotearoa New Zealand. The Court, in <em>Ellis v R</em>, recognised that “tikanga has been and will continue to be recognised in the development of the common law of Aotearoa/New Zealand in cases where it is relevant”. <em>Ellis</em> concerned an appeal of a criminal conviction. Tikanga Māori was engaged in this case because the defendant passed away before the case could be heard by the Supreme Court. In the application for continuance, it was pleaded that the interests of justice included considerations of Tikanga Māori. Each judge on the Supreme Court considered that Tikanga Māori may be a relevant factor to some extent.</p>

<p>The court took care to stress that the incorporation of Tikanga is still being worked out, and will continue to occur in a very context-specific way. Principles of Tikanga should be considered as relevant when assessing the specific factors of a case, but cannot be isolated or separated out. Winkellman CJ noted that the incorporation and role of tikanga in the common law is best approached on a case-by-case basis, because “Tikanga remains rooted in its own world”. Similarly, Williams J asserted that “tikanga will be relevant when the facts suggest it is and the common law has not otherwise excluded it”. Tikanga may also have relevance “where the common law in a particular area is developing, and such development would benefit from a consideration of relevant tikanga principles”</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Indigenous Law and Marine Interests</h3>

<p>In Aotearoa, Tikanga Māori is slowly being incorporated into the common law in a way which reshapes the laws requirements for ownership and title. This is most apparent in the area of customary marine rights under the Marine and Coastal Area (Takutai Moana) Act 2011 (Takutai Moana Act). This Act enables iwi, hapu, and whanau to have their customary rights recognised through Customary Marine Titles (CMT) and protected customary rights (PCRs).</p>

<p>Customary Marine Title is a statutory version of aboriginal title in Aotearoa, that applies specifically to marine areas. Māori can apply to have their customary rights to the common marine and coastal area recognised through the Takutai Moana Act, if it can be shown that they hold the specified area “in accordance with tikanga”. A declaration of Customary Marine Title means Māori are entitled to give or decline consent for activities occurring on the area, or consent for the establishment of a reserve. They may also, in some instances, be entitled to a “wahi tapu” protection right - the right to prohibit others from entering the area - which the Court will enforce. The title cannot be sold or converted, and is symbolic of the historical relationship that existed between Māori and the marine areas. A CMT also does not allow the title holder to alienate or dispose of any part of the land.</p>

<p>The courts, in determining whether a CMT should be granted, have taken the perspective that Tikanga concepts are to be applied rather than common law concepts. This was exemplified in the case <em>Re Edwards Whakatohea</em>. Because the Takutai Moana Act only confers limited rights, not ownership akin to fee simple, Churchman J in Re Edwards took the position that “attempting to interpret or define concepts used in the [Takutai Moana Act] by reference to common law proprietorial rights is inappropriate”. Instead, the inquiry was wholly focused on Tikanga principles of ownership, rather than traditional common law proprietary principles.</p>

<p>The Takutai Moana Act requires that the Māori group show there has been exclusive occupation of the common marine area. This is a clear reference to a common law concept, which the Court found is not present in Tikanga. Churchman J noted that:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“the principle of whanaungatanga emphasizes an inclusiveness and collectiveness which is contrary to the exclusionary exercise of rights which often forms the basis of the common law legal system, suggesting here that if those rights were grounded in tikanga, recognising them on a purely exclusionary basis by refusing to grant a PCR to more than one group when it is established on the evidence that multiple groups exercised these rights, would be unreasonable”.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Whanaungatanga is a principle which is inherently connected to kinship. As Joe Williams identifies, whanaungatanga is the integral principle of tikanga Māori. It is the “rights and obligations of kinship”, and extends not only to family and community but also to the physical world. The reference to whanaungatanga in Re Edwards signals an understanding of this genealogical relationship to land.</p>

<p>In line with this central tenet of whanaungatanga, all rights and obligations to natural resources are shaped to reflect a reciprocal relationship. Legal connections to land under Tikanga Māori are inherently reciprocal, reflecting an obligation to maintain a relationship to the resource in order to be entitled to use it. The legal obligation that arises out of this reciprocal ongoing relationship is kaitiakitanga; “the idea that any right over a human or resource carries with it a reciprocal obligation to care for his, her or its physical and spiritual welfare.” The relationship and obligations to the land reflect the spiritual and familial relationships between Māori and the natural world. The personified versions of the Earth (Papatuanuku) and Sky (Ranginui) were born from a single ancestral spiritual source, and their descendants form a web of interconnection “where ongoing relationships, care and responsibility mark the actions of the different entities charged with ruling the environment”. The principle of whanaungatanga reflects these relationships by giving a legal right and obligation to the kinship between Māori and the natural world.</p>

<p>The recognition of whanaungatanga as creating legal obligations therefore erodes historical principles of land ownership. Accordingly, it was determined that the Takutai Moana Act should be interpreted to allow for jointly held CMT, in recognition of the concept of groups holding shared exclusivity over areas. Furthermore, although it was predicted that this may require some coordination and agreement, the Court was confident in the ability of Tikanga to provide for these jointly held rights, as it had done in the past for so many years. This shows an interweaving of Tikanga principles into the common law in a way that was likely not contemplated by Parliament at the time the legislation was passed. The courts in Aotearoa are willing to engage in a judicial exercise which places Tikanga at the center of statutory interpretation where there are marine interests involved.</p>

<p>Admittedly, this incorporation of Tikanga can only go so far, because the CMT scheme that the Courts are dealing with is very limited. It does not compare to a full fee-simple ownership over marine areas, nor does it compare to the full Aboriginal title that is constitutionally entrenched in Canada. However, now in the wake of <em>Ellis</em>, there is the chance that the common law will continue to be developed in line with Tikanga to bridge this gap and strengthen Māori rights to land.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Tikanga and Land Rights</h3>

<p>Tikanga Māori is recognised in <em>R v Ellis</em> as “the law that grew from and is very much embedded in our whenua (land)”. The incorporation of Tikanga Māori into the common law system means that whanaungatanga and kaitiakitanga, and the legal obligations that manifest from these values, could also be recognised in the common-law. Together, these values create a set of obligations for one’s own family as well as one’s ancestral place and environment. Future litigation about indigenous rights to land now has the chance to point to those genealogical links between Indigenous peoples and the land.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h3>

<p>The painting that I have done reflects this hope that the courts will continue to recognise alternative forms of rights, weaving indigenous understandings of connection into the common law to reflect Indigenous interests. Cases in Canada which seek to recognise rights to marine areas may be influenced by this development in Aotearoa. In particular this may mean encouraging the courts to engage with alternative models of ownership and connection to marine areas.</p>
</blockquote>

Canada, 2014 FC 747).<br />[10]      There has recently been a renewed interest in Indigenous legal traditions. <li id="058b737d-9a11-4e41-97a1-d418aed2cc2d">In his concurring reasons Justice Williams explained the Māori concepts of Mana tangata, whanaungatanga and ea<br />[250] Mana transcends death, the mātanga advised. They said that this has always been the case in tikanga, but they noted that the modern State understands this too. They referred to the 2019 example of the posthumous pardon granted by the legislature to the Tūhoe prophet Rua Kēnana. The pardon was granted in part to remove the continuing injury to the mana of Rua and his descendants as a result of his treatment at the hands of the law. There are other legislative examples.<br />[251] And Mr Ellis has mana, the mātanga explained. That is, his own standing, dignity and authority. Death does not extinguish that mana because, the mātanga explained, mana is not an individualistic phenomenon. It exists because of relationships with others in accordance with the principles of whanaungatanga and whakapapa. Such mana-sustaining relationships do not end at death, even if they are changed by it.<br />[252] Two further concepts discussed in the statement are also of importance in my view. The first is whanaungatanga, or kinship. Whanaungatanga, the mātanga said, “creates rights and responsibilities within and between whānau and reflects the importance of community”. To put it another way, whanaungatanga provides that individuals, whether offenders or victims of offending, are never just individuals; and hara are always, in the end, a community responsibility<br />[253] The second is the concept of ea or — loosely — a state of balance. It is akin, perhaps, to common law ideas of justice and finality. In tikanga, ea is the point. It can be achieved by agreement or imposed by someone with the mana to impose it. Tikanga too, has no time for process without end.<br />[254] Mana, whanaungatanga and ea are, the mātanga suggested, tikanga lenses through which to consider the prospect of further inquiry into a hara. And they are interconnected. Mana occupies the same space as common law principles of individual dignity and integrity, but it is a more woven, less individualistic concept; and, because of this, its posthumous influence is stronger than that of the common law conception of individual reputation. Whanaungatanga places great value on the maintenance of community cohesion, though this is framed in terms of kinship. In Te Ao Māori (the Māori world), for obvious reasons, kinship and community are co-extensive ideas. Sometimes the judgement of whanaungatanga will be to put a problem behind us and sometimes it will be that the problem cannot be ignored. As with the common law, context is important. Ea is both the objective and the key. It asks whether balance has already been achieved between the mana of those affected and the needs of community, or whether more must be done to restore balance. As the mātanga noted, achieving a state of ea does not necessarily mean that all parties are happy with the outcome. 256] It is plain, at least to me, that these tikanga principles provide a very helpful perspective on the issues in this case. This is not because they provide any particular answer. Rather it is because the Māori legal tradition, whose values are so different from those of the common law, still echoes, in its own way, the underlying considerations which the common law takes into account.
