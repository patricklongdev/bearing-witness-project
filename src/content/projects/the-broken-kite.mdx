<p>Vanessa de Jong for her project in the 2016 Penal Policy seminar, drew upon her experience as a prisoner and used a distinctive medium of communication -the ‘Kite’- that has a long cultural significance for prisoners. Vanessa in her essay “The Broken Kite – My Experience in Provincial Remand Custody” explains her choice of this medium:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For my project I have written a number of handwritten letters in the form of jail kites. Kites are passed in various forms from inmate to inmate as a method of communicating to friends or associates who are not on your unit. Each kite is dated to an approximate day that I spent in custody. In 2006 I spent approximately three months in pre-trial custody in Alberta, before being released on bail conditions. I spent just over a week at the old Edmonton Remand Centre before being transferred to the Fort Saskatchewan Correctional Centre. During my time in custody I really did pass kites. As there is always the threat of your kite being discovered by correctional staff, names are in initial form only. Some of the kites are directed to a male friend who was in custody at the same time as I was, and a few are to my co-accused, who unfortunately is currently back in the prison system. Each of the kites details my real memories and experiences to the best of my recollection. I have folded the kites as my co-accused taught me to when we were in the Fort Saskatchewan Correctional Centre together. I have put the kites into an oatmeal box beneath some packets of oatmeal, as this is a place that things could be hidden within your cell. Packaged oatmeal was an item available for purchase through canteen, and since it was likely the healthiest option on the menu it was always on my list of purchases. Below, I have included a brief description of the events surrounding each letter, as well as some of the relevant provisions from the Alberta Corrections Act, which relate to the events in question. The Act and its Regulations guide the treatment of inmates serving time in provincial facilities in Alberta.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The eight kites in their Quaker Oaks’ box proved difficult to photograph. True to their original purpose the letters are tightly wrapped, both to compress space and facilitate their secret exchange, and when unwrapped do not lend themselves to legible high-resolution photos.The Archive therefore contains a series of four photographs that will give you a sense of the work. Vanessa in her essay explains the subject of each of the eight letters.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Kite# 1: August 6th, 2006</h3>

<p>I wrote this kite in response to the time I spent in holding cells in Grand Prairie prior to being transferred to the Edmonton Remand Centre, as well as to my initial transfer. I was in fact held, along with my co-accused, in the holding cells of the Grand Prairie RCMP station for seven days and nights. During this time I was allowed one phone call. My repeated requests to contact my lawyer after the first night were denied. I was less distressed about not being able to speak to my lawyer as I was about being held in cells without the ability to wash or even brush my teeth for such a long period of time. Only once were we let out for hygienic purposes. We were repeatedly told that the officers were only required to allow us to use the phone or to wash if they had the time available. However, in the seven days we were there they must have been extremely busy.</p>

<p>In regards to our pick up of the woman from Alberta Hospital, and her treatment by the intake staff at the Remand Centre, that was one of the most profoundly disturbing things I have ever borne witness to. It is a vision that I will never be able to get out of my mind. Why this woman would have been being transferred out of a mental health facility and into a prison setting simply escapes reason. According to a report issued by the Office of the Correctional Investigator of Canada in 2011:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Prisons are not hospitals, and the conditions that prevail there are far from therapeutic or rehabilitative. Incarcerating persons with mental health problems in conditions and environments that are poorly suited to meet their needs promotes neither public safety nor rehabilitative objectives. Simply put, there is not enough capacity, resources or professionals to meet the increased demands being placed on a system that was never intended to cope with such a profoundly ill population.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>While this report was written in response to the conditions in federal facilities, I find it to be equally applicable to provincial detention. According to the report over half of women admitted to federal facilities are in need of mental health assessment. It was clear at least from my own experiences that correctional staff were unequipped to deal with a woman suffering from a severe mental health issue, in this case likely schizophrenia. Watching a woman in her condition, naked, pepper-sprayed, beaten, and in handcuffs surrounded by five uniformed officers, mostly male, is a searing depiction of what can be a reality for inmates suffering from mental health problems.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Kite #3: August 13th, 2006</h3>

<p>I wrote this kite in response to my first week at the ERC. While there were a lot of things that were very difficult for me, what made it easier were the other women on the unit. I was surprised at how easy it was to integrate myself into the community. As I mentioned in the kite, I developed a bit of a friendship with my cellmate, a young First Nations woman. It was apparent to me she was suffering from some developmental type disorder. She was a kind woman. She immediately let me borrow some of her shampoo and conditioner, as mine had not yet arrived through the weekly canteen order. On my unit and on the women's unit in the Fort, I would estimate that indigenous women made up approximately 75-85% of the population. According to a report issued by the Office of the Correctional Investigator of Canada, Aboriginal peoples are grossly over-represented in the federal correctional system. This problem is even further compounded when it comes to Aboriginal women. According to the report in the Edmonton Institution for Women, Aboriginal offenders made up 56% of the inmate population from March of 2010 to January of 2013. From my own experience I would estimate that the percentage of Aboriginal offenders in provincial institutions is even higher, and possibly closer to the figures I projected above. While the report lists several factors that are likely contributors to the problem, such as the multi-generational impacts of residential schooling, displacement, and governmental policies that promoted the destruction of indigenous cultures, the take-away point from all of it is captured by the title of the report. The over-representation of Aboriginal offenders in the Canadian justice system really is a, "[c]ritical [s]ituation", which has changed very little in the ten years since I was incarcerated.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Kite #6: September 27th, 2006</h3>

<p>Kite 6 addresses both strip searches and segregation. According to section 10 of the Regulations strip searches may be conducted at any time on the direction of the Director. Section 10 also explains that searches should be conducted by a member of the same sex, but only where possible. While I do not have access to the institutional policy regarding strip searches, it was my experience that searches were conducted every time before leaving the institution, before returning to the institution, and after any visitation, even where the visit occurred behind glass. Strip searches were also at times conducted for other reasons at the discretion of any of the correctional officers on duty. To say that the random strip searches were directed by the Director would certainly be a stretch.</p>

<p>In regards to segregation, the segregation practices at the Fort seemed fairly barbaric. To my knowledge the inmate I spoke of received no real notice of the reasoning behind her segregation, nor was she given an opportunity to have the segregation decision reviewed. She ended up being released after three days, so I am not able to comment on what would have happened had the segregation gone past the 72 hour mark. I will say that the fact she was put into a cell with nothing but a blue jumper and a bible was nothing short of cruel and unusual punishment. There could be no possible reason for her to have been denied her undergarments. In the end, she was never even charged. It appears more likely to me that the segregation was simply a lesson in humiliation, as she was essentially unable to have contact with anyone, and yet on display at the same time. I cannot see how this form of segregation is meant to be rehabilitative, or even a deterrent. To me this is punishment, plain and simple.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Kite #7: October l8th 2006</h3>

<p>Aside from the events that occurred with the older woman at my intake into the ERC, the events described in kite 7 is one of my worst memories during my time in custody. Section 48(2).2 of the Regulations provide that where a urine sample is required, the sample must be taken in the presence of a staff member who is the same sex as the offender. Nowhere in the Regulations does it require that a urine sample be given while naked in front of two staff members. I felt completely degraded. What made the whole thing even worse was that I was not a drug user. The residue at the bottom of my shampoo bottle really was nothing more than dried shampoo. The arbitrariness of the entire ordeal really struck me. I was a cleaner, and essentially at the time was considered by most of the guards to be a model inmate, and yet it was entirely possible to be falsely accused of something that had no basis in reality, and then subjected to the demeaning procedures implemented by prison staff. At the time I just remember feeling helpless.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In her comments on her final kite Vanessa reflects on her experience as a prisoner and the enduring lesson she is taken from it of the importance of respect for human dignity.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Kite #8: November 3rd, 2006</h3>

<p>My eighth and final kite is meant to be a conclusion to my project. Shortly after my experience at the smudge I was released on bail conditions, and eventually ended up serving a two-year conditional sentence. My time in custody, though it was short, taught me a number of things that I hope will carry with me into any future I have in the legal profession. So many of the women I was incarcerated with had suffered such immeasurable hardships. When I think of theses women criminal is the last word I would use to describe them. Victim is a word that is much more relevant. Most of the women I spoke to had been victims of physical or sexual abuse or worse. Many began engaging in prostitution as children. Addiction is a systemic problem. Rates of disease in prison seem rampant. But despite all this, and despite all the hardship, I believe if I had to use one word to describe the women I met along the way it would be resilient. Despite being dealt a bad hand in life, there was a kindness on the women's unit, and a sense of camaraderie. I was shown a considerable amount of compassion and respect. I learned that respect, if given, is usually reciprocated. If I could impart one message to correctional services and staff everywhere it would be just that. You don't need to rule by degradation and fear, rather if you give people respect, and show them your own humanity, they will likely be willing to show you that they too are only human.</p>
</blockquote>
