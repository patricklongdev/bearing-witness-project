<p>One of the many pleasures I have experienced in assembling the archive of Bearing Witness and revisiting projects spanning many years, is realizing the connections between projects. A prime example is the connection between <a href="/projects/the-great-escape-reattempted/">Marya Akhtar’s</a> “Great Escape” and Achilles Zyakris, 2011 project “Blues in the Big House”. Like Marya’s work Achilles project is a reflection on the way prisoners deal with the loss of freedom and the myriad ways in which this is expressed. Occupying a unique place in the archive, Achilles’ essay reviews the history of the blues and its origins in the most brutal prisons in the United States, invoking the poetry/ lyrics of the blues. As he explains in his introduction:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Music From the Big House (2011) is a documentary directed by the Canadian Bruce McDonald featuring the Toronto-based blues singer Rita Chiarell and the inmates of Louisiana's Angola Prison. Angola Prison is a massive maximum-security prison that holds 5,000 inmates convicted of the worst of crimes and serving the longest of sentences. Music from the Big House is ultimately about the power of the blues to provide for freedom in unpropitious times and places. Chiarelli often speaks of the "redemptive power of music" and it is only upon reflection that we begin to understand what she means. At its simplest the blues provides an outlet for self expression. As she says at one point: "when you're singing, you're not angry." There is more to it than that and I think one of the great achievements of the movie is that it gets far beyond the limitations of words, and allows the music to convey its points. We hear the power of the blues to set the worst of human experiences in such a way that a measure of peace and almost grace can be achieved. It is, fundamentally, one of the most humane and human of arts. The blues singer is a potter who is not afraid to get his hands dirty.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Another of the pleasures and gifts I have derived from the projects in the Archive is learning about subjects which are outside of my professorial range. Achilles’ reflection on the loss of freedom opens up a world of understanding of music, the meaning of the Blues and in particular the significance of Prison Blues.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Blues: Meaning</h3>

<p>Emotion brings us to the spirit of the blues, a subject frequently misunderstood, even by its admirers. The music gets its name from the Elizabethan phrase, "the blue devils" meaning a fit of bad temper of melancholy. Historically, the topics addressed by the blues make for a very long list. Here are a few, taken from the great blues historian Paul Oliver's landmark study of traditional blues lyrics: employment and the lack thereof; color prejudice, among blacks as well as whites; standards of beauty and dress; flirtation, romance, courtship, and marriage; fidelity and infidelity; sex in all its permutations, including sexual boasting and insult; folk beliefs, magic and "hoodoo"; gambling; carnivals, juke joints, and vaudeville; liquor, prohibition, and drugs; conditions in various regions and cities; prostitution and vice; weapons and fighting; gangsters and crime; the KKK; prison and convict labor; the abuses of the criminal justice system; prison escape and family breakup; capital punishment; the Mississippi River; floods, tornadoes, dust storms, and hurricanes; housing, insurance policies and fires; military service, wars and veterans; diet, working conditions, injury and disease; death, funerals and cemeteries; heaven and hell; bereavement and hero-worship.</p>

<p>This is a capacious list but there is an obvious tone that connects the various topics. It is only natural that the blues would have a reputation for being depressing, as would befit the "devil's music." The greatest of all gospel singers, Mahalia Jackson, had this to say: "Blues are the songs of despair, gospel songs are songs of hope." The same idea is more sympathetically expressed by Oliver: "The blues is primarily the song of those who turned their backs on religion." But bad temper and melancholy are merely the starting point of the blues, not its destination. But if the blues teaches us anything, it is that despair is not the only alternative to faith. For all the emotionalism found in blues performance, the music's basic philosophy is stoic. Fundamentally the blues is a style that is a ritualized way of coping with the harshness of life.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Prison Blues</h3>

<p>Nowhere was the cultivation of a stoic attitude more necessary than in the black experience of prison during the first half of the twentieth century. The history of black incarceration makes for troubling reading. That a repertory of blues songs arose from such conditions is a testament to the tremendous strength of the men and women who were incarcerated under the most terrible of conditions. The "prison blues" still remains largely unexplored as a separate sub-genre of blues, though there are songs that have become established standards of the blues repertory. Oliver's yeoman's work in identifying and thematically cataloguing several thousand blues songs has been an invaluable source for much of what follows. Of course, I can only scratch the surface of the topic, but I hope this gives a fitting overview of how well-suited the blues was to purifying the experience of these men and women and how it worked to set so much that was chaotic and ugly in order.</p>

<p>In some important respects, prison life for blacks was a continuation of slavery under a different name. Life in southern penitentiaries was filled with countless indignities and savage cruelties. It is worth noting that the same conditions that gave birth to the blues- out of the slave experience and the vicissitudes of emancipation - also led to a similar outpouring of song. It is no coincidence then that the greatest concentration of songs occurs in prisons where there were work programs.</p>

<p>Except for one isolated example, most of the prison blues we have come from men who did hard time in some of the most infamous prisons of the South and the later sang of the experience. These include the indisputably impressive figures of Leadbelly &#123;Huddie Ledbetter) and Bukka White but there are also a number of other, minor blues singers now largely forgotten who used their experiences as prisoners as subject matter for the blues. The variety and texture of these songs is staggering; nearly every aspect of a prisoner's life, from trumped-up charge, to conviction, internment and release, are enacted in the songs. The tone of the lyrics is all I can direct you to listen for in this paper. And though that is a woefully incomplete encounter with the art form, there is a quality to these lyrics that conveys the power of the blues to limit, set and overcome the most horrible of experiences. The blues are fundamentally cathartic, but never histrionic. One of the more well-known (this is relative) blues songs addressing the false charges that were endemic to the period &#123;1920s) is "Third Degree" by the southern bluesman Eddie Boyd. The first two stanzas are as follows:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Got me accused of peepin' - and I can't even see a thing. Got me accused of beggin' and I can't even raise my hand.<br />Bad luck, bad luck is killin' me,<br />I just can't stand no more of this third degree</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>Achilles tracks the litany of abuses characterized the historical US prison regimes, a history that is reflected in the recent film “The Mauritanian” the United States military in its treatment of prisoners at Guantánamo Bay has reinvigorated,</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>For infringements of the rules prisoners could be punished quite severely, losing opportunities for their cases to come before the parole boards, being sent into solitary confinement or to the ill-ventilated, lightless cells known everywhere as "the hole." In the early part of the century, prisoners in San Quentin would be punished by being forced to spend a day up to their necks in water; tepid bath punishments were still widely practiced in the 1950s. In the South the punishments were especially ferocious.</p>

<p>Throughout Georgia in the twenties and thirties, black prisoners were tortured by being tied in a distorted position round a pick, were racked in medieval fashion or were committed to the "sweat-box" and left to fry in the sun. The "sweat-box" remained in use after World War II in Florida, and the doors of "the hole" in Jackson Prison still had a second, curved metal door hinged to them between which recalcitrant prisoners were confined, unable to move.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The conditions placed an enormous psychological burden on the prisoners and it was not unusual for them to have complete mental breakdowns. There is a small subset of blues songs devoted to this type of anguish. Small events assumed exaggerated importance, minor grievances became obsessions, and the prisoner with time on his hands and on his mind could sit and brood:</p>

<p>All last night I sit in my cell alone,<br />I was thinking of my baby and my happy home.<br />Sometimes I wonder, why don't you write to me? If I been a bad fellow, I did not intend to be.<br />Baby, you may never see my smilin' face again, Whoa-oo, my smilin' face again,<br />You can always remember, Frank Busby been your friend.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Echoing the theme of human dignity that animates many projects in the Archive Achilles relates the story of the prison blues singer, not the outlaw. Jesse James:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>On June 3, 1936, by some unexpected arrangement, a prisoner is reported to have been brought under guard into the Decca studios in Memphis to record four blues songs. Only one record was issued under the artist's name, Jesse James. His rough voice sings what became his last testament. It is an acutely poignant song that evokes the strange expressive world of the blues, what Oliver has called in a marvelous phrase, "spirit with little hope." It can be assumed that James had been severed from the outside world for so long that Oklahoma was to him still the Indian "Territory." He interrupts each stanza with the refrain, that is both a warning and a plea, "You heah me talkin' to ya, buddy, what made ya stop by heah?" Here again, the spiritual capital of the African Americans is drawn upon at a time of great need. It would be easy to forget one's basic humanity in his circumstances; but here the quiet dignity of the song reaffirms the dignity of the man singing it. He is still communicating with a community. This is itself an example of achieved rehabilitation:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Now the day's been a long, lonesome day,<br />-Ya heah me talkin' to ya, did ya heah what I say?<br />Lord, the day has been a-long, old lonesome day, And tomorrow eeh, Lord, will be the same old way.</p>

<p>I been to the Nation, I been all through the Territo'<br />-Ya heah me talkin' to ya, gotta reap what ya sow, I been all round the Nation, I been all through the Territo', But I found no heaven on earth, Lord, nowhere I go.</p>

<p>I'm going to the Big House, and I don't even care,<br />-Don't ya heah me taIkin' to ya, scaldin' to ma death!<br />I'm goin' in the mawnin' an' I don't even care<br />I might get four or five years, Lord, and I might get the chair.</p>

<p>Ah, stop an' listen, see what t'morrow bring,<br />-You heah me talkin' to ya, start to prayin!<br />You better stop'n'listen, an' see what t'morrow bring,<br />It might bring you sunshine, Lawd, an' it may bring rain.</p>

<p>Some got six months, some got a solid year,<br />-You heah me talkin' to ya, buddy, what made ya stop by heah?<br />Some of them got six months, pardner and some got a solid year, But I believe my pardner, Lawd, got lifetime heah.</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>The essay concludes with Achilles’ reflection on the continuing power of the blues and its relevance to our understanding of the pains of imprisonment:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The blues is part of a proud tradition; the self-pity never lapses into sentimental wallowing (though it does often flirt with masochism). Misery is tempered by resilience. It is the personal emotion of an individual finding through music a vehicle of self-expression. We hear blues songs in the first person: an assertion of "I" where it would be easy to think "it." Yes, the prison blues is often the wail of the forgotten and brutalized, but these songs also assert independence, autonomy, lust, anguish, laughter and a wry cynicism - they create persons.</p>

<p>And it is a social music too. The blues is the creation of artists within a folk community, whether it's in the deep, rural South or in the congested ghettoes of the industrial cities or the terrible State Farm prison system. The blues were, particularly through the 1920s, an intensely commercial style. At root it is about the audience, which it never takes from granted.</p>

<p>Perhaps this is why the blues, though replete with a sense of defeat and down-heartedness, never sounds at least to my ears, as intrinsically pessimistic; their burden of woe and melancholy is redeemed through a stubborn hold on life, sex, freedom and home. No matter how repressive the American environment, the bluesman never fell completely silent, and he never became brutally wrathful. Something important was lost when the quiet dignity of the blues was replaced by the starkly violent hyper-realism (or fabulism) of rap.</p>
</blockquote>
