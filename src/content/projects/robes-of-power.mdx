<p>On the West Coast of Canada we have become accustomed to Indigenous art forms as powerful and visually evocative communicators. On arrival at Vancouver International Airport new and returning travelers to Canadian soil are met with a visual feast of Indigenous totem-poles, masks, carved panels and tapestries in the architectural construction of an Indigenous landscape. However, it is a pristine landscape that hides the impact and legacy of colonialism and Canada’s and British Columbia’s dark history of suppression of Indigenous cultures and, until only recently, the refusal to recognize Indigenous nations’ rights to their traditional territories and their legal orders.</p>

<p>This colonial history, its impact and continuing legacy, and the struggle by Indigenous peoples for the legal and political recognition of their rights are issues that lie at the heart of the seminars I have taught on Aboriginal and Treaty Rights, Penal Policy and Criminal Justice. Law students have engaged with these issues in a variety of ways and particularly through the use of Indigenous mediums of communication.</p>

<p>The first piece in the archive was in fact the inspiration for giving students the opportunity to work across different media in their law school studies. Nadia Belokopitov a member of the Haida Nation, House of Yuuglaanas, Raven Clan, in 1999 was a student in the seminar First Nations and the Administration of Justice. As an alternative to writing a traditional seminar paper on the impacts of overrepresentation of Indigenous people in the criminal justice system, an overrepresentation which the Supreme Court of Canada had characterized in the Gladue case as a “staggering injustice”, Nadia asked if she could use a Northwest coast button blanket is a visual representation of the effects of colonization on Aboriginal peoples from a criminal law perspective. Entering uncharted waters for the Law School I welcomed her initiative. Nadia’s creative work ‘Robes of Power’ is both a powerful and accessible statement of the Indigenous colonial experience and the journey to self determination, and is one I have every year since its creation shown to students in my seminars.<br /><br />Nadia provided this text to explain the genesis of the project, the Indigenous methodology in its creation involving family and community, and the significance and meaning of the four panel theme.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-gallery has-nested-images columns-default is-cropped"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2023/12/robesofpower_02-1024x810.jpg" alt="Nadia Belokopitov's 1999 textile project titled &quot;Robes of Power&quot;" class="wp-image-178"/></figure></figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>This Northwest coast button blanket is a visual representation of the effects of colonization on Aboriginal peoples from a criminal law perspective. I would like to thank Professor Michael Jackson for providing a flexible and alternative method of legal education. Please accept this button blanket in the spirit that it is being offered. It is my sincere hope that it will be used as a teaching aid for future courses relating to Aboriginal peoples and the administration of justice. My appreciation is also extended to my sister, Tamara Bell, who provided invaluable assistance in creating this button blanket. Tamara is a well know Haida artist and owns Cedar Root Gallery which is located in the Aboriginal Friendship Centre. In addition, Tamara is a performer in the Rainbow Creek Dancers (Robert Davidson's group) where she sings traditional Haida songs and dances with the group. Tamara's contribution to the making of the button blanket was essential and, without her, I could not have even considered attempting such an undertaking. The button blanket designs were all created with acrylic paints and only black and red were used which is a Haida tradition. We used a regular sewing machine and a surger for the outside seam of the blanket, however, all the buttons around the edge were hand sewn.</p>

<p>The project was created at Tamara's house which is located on the Squamish reserve in North Vancouver. Because of the powerful spiritual aspects of creating 'Robes of Power', it was significant for the blanket to be created on an Aboriginal reserve. Moreover, Tamara's house is visited by many Aboriginal individuals on the reserve who contributed their ideas to the making of this button blanket. This button blanket was created in community spirit and we all hope that it contributes to the decolonization and freedom of Aboriginal peoples.</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Four Panel Themes</h3>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The Northwest coast button blanket has four separate themes relating to First Nations and the administration of justice. The number four is important in all Aboriginal cultures because it symbolizes the four races of people, the four seasons, the four winds and various other spiritual elements which represent four distinctions.</p>
</blockquote>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2023/12/robesofpower_03-1024x448.jpg" alt="Nadia Belokopitov's 1999 textile project titled &quot;Robes of Power&quot; detail panel 1" class="wp-image-258"/></figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The first panel (left to right) embodies Northwest coast Aboriginal culture in pre-contact times. The totem poles and long house illustrate the setting of a Haida village where the community gathered to celebrate ceremonial rituals such as the potlach.</p>
</blockquote>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2023/12/robesofpower_04-1024x358.jpg" alt="Nadia Belokopitov's 1999 textile project titled &quot;Robes of Power&quot; detail panel 2" class="wp-image-257"/></figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The second panel (on the top) depicts the colonial era when techniques of colonization were forced upon Aboriginal communities. One of the most veiled, yet, damaging aspects of colonization was religious conversion. In the centre of the panel is a church which is perched at the top of a hill and surrounded by crosses. On one side of the church is a residential school named St. Michael's which was located in Alert Bay on Vancouver Island. Jonathon Nolie is a twelve year old Kwakuitl boy who spends time at the Aboriginal Friendship centre and at my sister's house. His grandmother attended St. Michael's residential school and when I was searching for a name, Jonathon telephoned his grandmother for this information. The various books at the left hand side of the second panel demonstrate the loss of our oral traditions which was replaced by a literary culture. The Criminal Code of Canada, with s. 266 provisions, represents the dominant legal regime's most coercive form of colonization on Aboriginal peoples. On the right side of the second panel is a factory and a twenty dollar Canadian bill which characterizes elements of capitalism which transformed the economic system of Aboriginal peoples.</p>
</blockquote>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2023/12/robesofpower_05-1024x363.jpg" alt="Nadia Belokopitov's 1999 textile project titled &quot;Robes of Power&quot; detail panel 3" class="wp-image-260"/></figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The third panel (on the right) depicts the effects of colonization on Aboriginal peoples. After John A. McDonald's government enacted and implemented the Potlach laws, Northwest coast totem poles were burned or transported away to museums all over the world. The judge and scale represent the dominant legal culture's system which has created oppressive laws for Aboriginal peoples. The needle embodies drug addiction or relates to diabetes (an insulin needle) which is a disease common to Aboriginal communities. The two different glasses contain alcohol which is one of the most pervasive problems amongst Aboriginal peoples. The First Nations woman crying relates to her incarcerated husband at the end of the third panel.</p>
</blockquote>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2023/12/robesofpower_01.jpg" alt="Nadia Belokopitov's 1999 textile project titled &quot;Robes of Power&quot; detail panel 4" class="wp-image-248"/></figure>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The fourth element of the button blanket (in the centre) is a Northwest coast eagle which represents freedom, decolonization and self-determination of Aboriginal cultures. The eagle is a powerful symbol in all Native nations because of its dominance and wisdom in the animal world.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>As Nadia explains ‘Robes of Power’, while using Haida imagery, reflects an ongoing story that is shared by other Indigenous nations across Canada. It is also a story that the Haida Nation has invoked before Canadian Courts. In the <em>Haida Nation</em> case, where the Supreme Court of Canada established the duty on governments to consult with and accommodate and in some case obtain consent from Indigenous nations before undertaking developments that affected their Aboriginal title and rights, Terri-Lynn Williams- Davidson, K.C. Gid7ahl-Gudsllaay Lalaxaaygans), a member of the Raven Clan from the Haida Nation and general counsel to the Haida Nation, invoked Haida history and imagery in taking the Supreme Court of Canada judges into the heart of Haida culture and the heart of the cedar tree. Terri-Lynn asked the court to look at the eight foot long enlarged historical photograph taken in 1878 of the village of Skidegate that had been set up on an easel in the courtroom, a photograph reproduced in the ink on the left side of Nadia’s blanket.</p>

<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img src="/assets/2023/12/robesofpower_06-1024x510.jpg" alt="Historical photograph taken in 1878 of the village of Skidegate" class="wp-image-265"/></figure>

<p>With reference to the photograph Terri-Lynn explained the cultural and economic importance of the forests of Haida Gwaii. The photo dramatically illustrated the prolific use of the forests and the fact that the entire village was brimming over with value-added old-growth cedar. This cedar had been carefully turned into: house posts (including carved house posts); planks used to enclose each house structure; various types of totem poles; paddles; incredibly seaworthy canoes carved and steamed out of a single tree; drying racks for salmon, halibut and cedar bark for weaving. Taking the Court inside one of the longhouses Terri-Lynn told the justices they would be literally overwhelmed by interior totem poles; bent-wood boxes; the regal Chief’s Settees; ceremonial masks and other ceremonial objects; baskets; tools for weaving, hunting, fishing and sculpting; clothing woven from softened cedar bark; and the incredible Raven’s Tail and Chilkat robes, which are woolen blankets made with the structural strength of cedar bark warp and weft. As to the many totem poles visible in the photo each totem pole, each was also a “legal document” of history, recording the oral histories of people, families, clans and important events in history<sup data-fn="cc601d69-d2d6-4008-b678-83bb578dcf24" class="fn"><a href="#cc601d69-d2d6-4008-b678-83bb578dcf24" id="cc601d69-d2d6-4008-b678-83bb578dcf24-link">1</a></sup>.</p>
