<p>Robyn Gervais, in her 2005 journal for the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights seminar, using a variety of media, including text, photos maps, leaf pressings and story boards, offers a collage style set of reflections on the nature of both Indigenous law and common law language and also some key stages in the conception of the relationship between Indigenous peoples and colonial governments.</p>

<p>Robyn’s journal includes in story board form a synopsis of the Metis Red River Settlement and the history of the broken promises of the 1870 Manitoba Act guaranteeing 1.4 million acres to the Metis children, and events leading to the trial and execution of Louis Riel.</p>

<p>Robyn’s journal concludes with a reflection of why students have embraced the opportunity to engage in the archive projects.</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I would like to thank you for allowing me to engage in the class material in a way that makes sense to me, without the fear of a 100% exam or thirty pages paper interfering with the free flow of thoughts, Your class shall remain in my psyche long after the black letter law has faded.</p>
</blockquote>
