<p>A theme of my own academic writing one also reflected in the annual reports of the Correctional Investigator - the Ombudsman for federal prisons - is that despite court cases, recommendations of Parliamentary reports, judicial commissions of inquiry, and task force reports, federal correctional authorities have resisted changing the regime of administrative segregation. It was more than forty years after the first successful challenge to the regime in the old BC Penitentiary in the 1975 <em>McCann</em> case that in 2018 the regime was held to be unconstitutional. Kathryn Kinsey in her 2015 project ‘A Timeline of Segregation’, using a poster board as her medium, tracked and documented this resistance. In doing so Kathryn reviewed key events in the history of segregation that are the subject of other projects later in the Archive.</p>

<p>Kathryn provides this guide to her poster board:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The idea behind this project was to document significant events in Canada, particularly British Columbia, since the 1970's that reflect the ongoing issue of segregation in penitentiaries. I was inspired to focus on segregation specifically from the beginning of term when the topic was continuously raised. This project includes insights into the <em>McCann</em> case, the introduction of the CCRA regulations, The Arbour Report, The Task Force on Segregation and more recently the events leading up to the death of Ashley Smith, which were all widely publicized incidents centered on the topic of segregation. After having the opportunity to talk in depth with the group of prisoners at Matsqui I was struck by this theme of inaction the inmates observed. Or put differently, despite action and changes being implemented, these have not been reflected in practice within penitentiaries. The topic of inaction can be likened to segregation, and how despite the legal changes over the years such as the CCRA, combined with the various recommendations made in formal reports such as the Arbour report, the illegality of segregation continues to pervade across prisons alike in Canada.</p>

<p>There has been a repetition since the 1970s of incidents surrounding segregation coming to light, changes being implemented in some form, and often, a few years on, similar issues are being seen again. The notion of segregation constituting cruel and unusual punishment has been strongly argued since <em>McCann</em> in 1975 yet it is still being raised in 2015, for example the case of <em>BCCLA and JHSC v Attorney General of Canada</em>. After the <em>McCann</em> case the only changes adopted and implemented by the CSC were taken from a snippet of the judge's obiter including basic renovations to the segregation cells such as the size of the windows and change in the inmates exercise location. This was one of the first indications that minimal attention was being given to the circumstances surrounding segregation.</p>

<p>In addition to this idea of repetition, the concerns raised in the 1997 Task Force on Segregation were raised again ten years later in the Ashley Smith inquiry. This reflects the lack of measures learnt by the CSC from history, and how despite seemingly being responsive to recommendations for change in 1997, there has been very little, if any, impact on segregation. Moreover, the knowledge and understanding of the effect solitary confinement has on people has been growing over the years. It has been widely recognised by many psychologists to have damaging consequences on inmates when it is sustained for a long period of time. The effects spoken about by the psychologist in the <em>Mccann</em> case of violence and mental illness can too be seen as a prediction of devastating events to occur later if no action was taken. Ashley Smith being a prime example of a situation that could have been prevented and helped if the necessary changes were implemented.</p>

<p>My project on segregation was intended to be a visual representation providing insights into the incidents over the years surrounding segregation, including the few changes implemented after each one. By juxtaposing the events with the incidents that later followed emphasizes the idea of inaction and repetition that is, and has always been present in Canadian prisons. The blue flower standing in stark contrast to the black and white events documented on my project was intended to symbolize hope for the future of segregation. The story of Gary Weaver caring for the blue flower whilst spending his time in segregation and upon leaving, passing it to the inmate in the cell next door has stayed with me. By including a blue flower on my board, it is a reflection of all the individuals that have experienced the struggle of segregation and represents the need for more action to be taken. It is clear from my timeline the action that has, and is being taken, is simply fuelling this ongoing cycle of events over the past forty-years. There needs to be a real change at both the legal level and on the ground in practice in prisons across Canada for there to be any chance of finally breaking this repetition of history and provide hope for future inmates to avoid facing similar situations documented in my project board.</p>
</blockquote>
