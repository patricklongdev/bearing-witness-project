<p>Alexandra Djordjevic, who was also in the 2020 Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Litigation Seminar, presented a three part project. The first one was a Word Spiral as a representation of how she saw Aboriginal law being developed in Canada; the second part was a photography hummingbird project based on the seminar discussions; and the third was a rewrite one of the BCCA decisions related to the challenges to the Site C Dam, by the Prophet River First Nations. I have included just the first two parts in the archive. You can read more about the challenges brought by several other First Nations in the project by <a href="/projects/site-c-and-the-west-moberly-first-nations-treaty-8-rights/">Julia Roe</a> in the Treaty Rights section of the Archive.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Reset</h3>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>In electronics and technology, a reset button plays an important function. The usual effect of it is that you often lose progress and have to restart from the beginning. I have often wondered why not just do that when it comes to aboriginal law? Why not just hit a hard reset. It is a difficult thing to do when you have a spiraling amount of common law jurisprudence that is not easy to untangle.</p>

<p>I created a word spiral out the aboriginal and treaty litigation cases discussed in our seminar and in the first year of Law School. As a person with a civil law background I often look at the way common law is built through case law and as it spirals through the earlier decisions into the latest legal precedent.</p>

<p>The Word Spiral below represents the words and phrases that have shaped the current law governing Crown and Aboriginal relations. The words represent several decisions: from the Marshall decisions through to<em> St Catherine’s Milling, </em>via<em> Calder, Sparrow</em>, and to <em>Raincoast Conservation</em>. The focus in this illustration is on the duty to consult and accommodate in the context of environmental assessment.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Communicate</h3>
</blockquote>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The hummingbird piercing the crown was a doodle I made during my temporary articles at the Indigenous Community Legal Clinic. A hummingbird is the clinic’s logo and it symbolizes advocacy and communication. The first time I saw a hummingbird was a few days after I arrived in Canada. I thought initially that I was being attacked by a giant bee. Soon I became quite obsessed with them and would try to spend much of my free time stalking hummingbirds in Stanley Park.</p>

<p>There have been several discussions in our seminar about the task we have not gotten to: building a mechanisms where the two legal orders (Indigenous and Common Law) could communicate with each other. We went through several cases where the courts had the opportunity to learn but in the end they were unable to listen. For example, the trial judgement by McEachern C. J. in <em>Delgamuukw</em>. Throughout all those discussions I had an image of my hummingbird doodle with the hummingbird trying to communicate with the Crown. Inspired by love for hummingbirds and classroom discussion I decided to create a series of three photographs that represent a hummingbird attempting to communicate with the Crown. All of the photographs were taken by me during my bird walks in Stanley Park.</p>
</blockquote>
