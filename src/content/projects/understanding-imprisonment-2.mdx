<p>One of the great challenges in teaching law students and educating judges about what we do as a society when we sentence people to prison is that the experience of imprisonment, while a well entrenched feature of movies and TV series, is not one that most of us will ever suffer. In <em>Justice behind the Walls</em> I wrote:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>The journey, begins and ends in a place -- the federal penitentiary -- where most people have never been, and where they hope that neither they nor their loved ones will ever be sent. Yet while the penitentiary lies beyond the borders of most Canadians’ personal experience, it is a looming presence in the public imagination.</p>

<p>The powerful focus the prison provides in the formation of visions of justice is not solely a function of media fixation. Since the end of the eighteenth century, when imprisonment replaced the gallows and deportation as the primary penalty for serious crime, the intertwining of crime and punishment with the prison has been played out in literature, music, theatre, and film. </p>

<p>The success of Les Miserables and Kiss of the Spider Woman, as novels, films and most recently musicals, is neither surprising nor accidental. It is a reflection of the huge space the prison occupies in the physical and moral architecture of punishment — bars cast deep shadows on the stage as they do over the lives of prisoners. They  also illustrates a central theme of this book — that the personal, public, political and cultural threads of life are interwoven in the search for justice and human dignity behind prison walls.</p>

<p>As I ask my students at the beginning of their first year of law school, is it not strange that lawyers and judges, as gatekeepers of the only process that can result in a sentence of imprisonment, know or care so little about what happens inside prisons? Lawyers and judges who participate in criminal trials well understand the rules of criminal procedure and the principles of substantive criminal law. It is in the acquittals or convictions that flow from the application of process to principle that we see the model of justice according to law. But what happens when justice results in a prison sentence, once all appeals have been exhausted, is rarely subject to scrutiny by the legal profession. The point at which the criminal justice system will have its greatest impact on the individual is the point at which for the legal profession the process has run its course. It is simply assumed that the prison sentence, whether by deterring, apportioning just deserts, rehabilitating, or simply incapacitating the prisoner for the duration of the sentence, serves the ends of justice.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>In the context of these challenges the contributions of the archive are remarkable. Through their creative work students in the Penal Policy Seminar have opened their own minds to the contemporary realities of imprisonment and, in sharing it through the archive, opened windows for the rest of us. These realities are not monolithic; Indigenous prisoners, whose overrepresentation in the prisons of our country has been described by the Supreme Court of Canada as “a staggering injustice’ - and one that is accelerating not slowing - experience imprisonment as the continuing legacy of colonialism and for this generation represents a replication of what the residential school system did to their parents and grandparents; women experience prison differently, particularly for those who are separated from their children; prisoners who experience long periods in solitary confinement in the deep end of the system suffer what is now recognized by both international human rights standards and Canadian courts as torture and cruel or unusual punishment. The archive, explores these and many more realities using creative talents that help take us beyond only the language of books, reports and commissions of inquiry.</p>

<p>Because imprisonment has loomed large in the historical and contemporary landscape of Indigenous peoples I have chosen to begin this part of the archive with the voices of Indigenous law students. Significantly, these projects not only document the legacies of colonialism but also set out some of the pathways, based on Indigenous legal traditions and Indigenous conceptions of justice, that would move us in directions that address and redress some of the continuing injustices.</p>
