The bridge that has linked my academic and professional work has been the devastating impacts that the Canadian legal system and the continuing legacy of colonialism has had upon the lives of Indigenous peoples. That legacy was summarized in the 2022 judgement of the British Columbia Supreme Court by Justice Kent:

> The legacy of 150 years of systemic discrimination and attempted assimilation is bleak and intractable. It has resulted in cultural erosion and alienation, relentless intergenerational trauma, and socio-economic marginalization. While representing only five percent of Canada’s population, Indigenous people endure massively disproportionate rates of poverty, interpersonal violence and family breakdown, addiction and substance abuse, youth suicide, lower levels of education, and higher unemployment. Many reserves lack basic human needs such as decent housing and clean water to drink. And mostly as a cumulative result of the foregoing, Indigenous people are hugely overrepresented in both the child welfare and the criminal justice systems of this country.[^1]

The legacy of colonialism informs many of the projects in the Archive. Anishnabe artist and legal scholar Don Ahnahnsisi McIntyre, whose representation of the Oka crisis appears elsewhere in the Archive, in his directed research project completed while a law student at UBC, created this window into the dispossession and imprisonment of Indigenous people. It is titled “NDN Problem”.\\In his artist statement Don explained the images behind the fence:

![Don McIntyre's mixed media project titled "NDN Problem"](/assets/2024/05/Don_McIntyre_01-1024x685.jpg)

> There have been numerous articulations of the “Indian Problem” throughout Canada’s history. These have been addressed in numerous reports commissioned by Canada for Canada. This narrative is generally a 2-dimensional descriptor of what Indians have done, and continue to do wrong, within our Canadian society. Indians are outsiders in their own territory through an autopoietic monologue Canada continues to tell itself and the world.\
> \
> The work addresses the 3-dimensional nature of the ‘Canadian Problem’. It shows a traditional Indigenous man, looking out at a traditional Indigenous landscape separated by a chain-link fence. The Indigenous man has a transparency to his being that connects him to the land but for the chain-link fence. In the Canadian paradigm, fencing is used to demarcate property ownership. It creates the ability to keep people out of territory through registered ownership, denying the belonging of the individual. The creation of an underlying [Crown] title that supersedes Indigenous title and the criminalization of Indian qua Indian removes Indigenous people from the land. Without land for the people, there can be no nationhood. This new Settler system created means by which Indigenous people could be refused access to Indigenous territories. Upon ‘trespassing’ within this new system, Indigenous peoples are fenced into prisons, further restricting their mobility. This is the Canadian System imposed on Indigenous peoples and their nations. This is the ‘Canadian Problem’.

[^1]: *Thomas and Saik’uz First Nation v Rio Tinto Alcan Inc.*, 2022 BCSC 15 para 177
