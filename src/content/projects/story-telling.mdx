<p>When thinking and teaching about the law and legal analysis story telling has not been high on the list of teaching methods with its elitist academic label of pedagogy. Outside of the legal academy, however, in both the practice of law and the media presentation of law, stories have a higher profile. Media presentations, even those that fall outside of the journalistic cri de cour “if it bleeds it leads”, are all about telling stories, even if often partial and misleading accounts of the complexities of the underlying events. Lawyers, particularly those who participate in jury trials, understand that effective advocacy bears heavily on persuading the trier of fact that your story is more persuasive than the other side’s. Chief Justice Robert Bauman in a 2021 speech titled “The Duty to Act” acknowledged how both the common law and Indigenous legal systems draw upon storytelling:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Another similarity I see between Indigenous law and the common law lies in the centering of narrative in both systems. Narratives give meaning to legal rules; Stories matter. They expose the wisdom of the law, the evils that the law seeks to prevent or denounce. They tell of the evolution of the law, as well as its meaning today.Importantly, stories contextualize the law, making it relevant to our lives. Our court process, while adversarial, is a constructed form of storytelling, after all. Our technical rules regulating the admissibility of evidence signal that the law recognizes the power of storytelling. While much would need to change to create space for non-Western narratives within the court process, we know that we must avoid interpreting stories to fit the typical Western narrative. That may be the more personally comfortable path for some, but it is not one that sits well with the truth- seeking function of the courts. And as newcomers, we must acknowledge that we may be unprepared to interpret or contextualize these narratives.<sup data-fn="8161a97d-d7c7-469c-bcfe-f0cd6a7666ae" class="fn"><a href="#8161a97d-d7c7-469c-bcfe-f0cd6a7666ae" id="8161a97d-d7c7-469c-bcfe-f0cd6a7666ae-link">1</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>As we have already seen, many projects in the Archive incorporate storytelling, often relating their project to their own personal, family and cultural experiences.</p>

<p>Eualayai/Gamillariu legal scholar Larissa Behrendt, one of the leading Indigenous scholars in Australia who has uses her writing, filmmaking and journalism to advance understanding of indigenous worldviews, explains the larger dimensions of Indigenous storytelling both within and outside the academy:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>Storytelling not only challenges or decolonizes institutions, it is a way of reasserting Indigenous voice, perspective, and experience. Storytelling is an act of sovereignty that reinforces Indigenous identity, values, and worldview. …</p>

<p>Storytelling is ancient and potent. It is our most instinctive and human form of communication, of teaching, of persuasion, of validation, of healing, of transformation. …</p>

<p>Storytelling also plays a key role in our resilience as the world’s oldest living culture and in our assertion of sovereignty and in the countering of colonial narratives, the colonial stories, which have spread across our lands. Reasserting our stories on our land is a way of reasserting our ownership. This is an important role in the academy, in spaces that have been unwelcoming and alienating for Indigenous people and in disciplines that have privileged colonial perspectives over Indigenous ones.<sup data-fn="7882fc77-24cb-4f3b-bf58-b7c2c71ba671" class="fn"><a href="#7882fc77-24cb-4f3b-bf58-b7c2c71ba671" id="7882fc77-24cb-4f3b-bf58-b7c2c71ba671-link">2</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Storytelling and the role of Indigenous oral narratives has assumed greater legal significance in the context of litigation in which Indigenous peoples seek recognition within the Canadian courts of their rights to Aboriginal title and rights to governance. The admissibility and weight to be given to oral histories was a central issue in the Delgamuukw case, where the Hereditary Chiefs of the Gitksan and Wet’suwet’in Nation presented evidence of their adaawk and kungax as part of the proof of their ancient possession of their traditional territories. The Gitksan adaawk are the oral histories of each House commemorating the House’s origins, odysseys from ancient villages, moments when the people drew upon the assistance of spirit power, the defeat of neighbouring peoples who threatened their security, or the discovery of new ways to survive the natural disasters they periodically experienced and the other major historical events from the House's history. What the adaawk are for the Gitksan, kungax, the trails of song are for the Wetsuwet’in. As the Wetsuwet’in followed the trails to the territories, so they seek to capture the songs that go with their titles to their territories. The songs link the land, the animals, the spirit world and the people. The power of hereditary names and crests is continually renewed for the Wetsuweti’n by the highly personal and individual experience of being captured by song.<sup data-fn="3c277c8e-dfe9-42d0-81f4-ccd46563d8eb" class="fn"><a href="#3c277c8e-dfe9-42d0-81f4-ccd46563d8eb" id="3c277c8e-dfe9-42d0-81f4-ccd46563d8eb-link">3</a></sup></p>

<p>At trial Chief Justice McEachern ruled that the adaawk and kungax were admissible as evidence but were to be given very little weight in determining proof of possession sufficient to ground a claim to Aboriginal title. His central concern that the adaawk and kungax could not serve “as evidence of detailed history, or land ownership, use or occupation”. He discounted the adaawk and kungax because they were not “literally true”, confounded “what is fact and what is belief”, “included some material which might be classified as mythology”, and projected a “romantic view” of the history of the Gitksan and Wet’suwet’in.<sup data-fn="89e748f2-d388-4caa-bcf2-3e9ec8147105" class="fn"><a href="#89e748f2-d388-4caa-bcf2-3e9ec8147105" id="89e748f2-d388-4caa-bcf2-3e9ec8147105-link">4</a></sup></p>

<p>On appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada the Court held that Chief Justice McEachern was wrong in his findings on the evidentiary weight to be given oral histories. Chief Justice Lamer stated that:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>“The implication of the trial judge’s reasoning is that oral histories should never be given any independent weight and are only useful as confirmatory evidence in aboriginal rights litigation. I fear that if this reasoning were followed, the oral histories of aboriginal peoples would be consistently and systematically undervalued by the Canadian legal system.</p>

<p>Notwithstanding the challenges created by the use of oral histories as proof of historical facts, the laws of evidence must be adapted in order that this type of evidence can be accommodated and placed on an equal footing with the types of historical evidence that courts are familiar with, which largely consists of historical documents.<sup data-fn="cbc80228-a9c9-4359-9dd5-ab2056623ce3" class="fn"><a href="#cbc80228-a9c9-4359-9dd5-ab2056623ce3" id="cbc80228-a9c9-4359-9dd5-ab2056623ce3-link">5</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
