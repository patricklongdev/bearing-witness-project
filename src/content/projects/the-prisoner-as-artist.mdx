<p>The final project in this part of the Archive is not one created by a law student in my seminars but a former client, Gary Weaver, who on several occasions while he was out on parole, came to the seminar on Penal Policy and talked about his experiences bearing the weight of imprisonment while serving a life sentence for second-degree murder.</p>

<p>I first met Gary Weaver when he was at Kent maximum security institution in 1993 awaiting a transfer to the super maximum-security Special Handing Unit. In the following years I interviewed him on many occasions as part of my research for <em>Justice behind the Walls: Human Rights in Canadian Prisons</em>. After my research was completed and switching from my professorial to my professional role as a lawyer I represented Gary in his several applications for parole before the Parole Board of Canada and in a habeas corpus petition to BC Supreme Court. Because Gary Weaver’s journey through the Canadian federal prison system from super maximum to minimum security traversed so many of the human rights issues with which my work has engaged I devoted a whole chapter of <em>Justice behind the Walls</em> to Gary’s life as a prisoner.<sup data-fn="b897740a-418e-4a85-af0b-e72af377087c" class="fn"><a href="#b897740a-418e-4a85-af0b-e72af377087c" id="b897740a-418e-4a85-af0b-e72af377087c-link">1</a></sup></p>

<p>Gary’s honourable place in the Archive derives from two intertwining sources. His evocative recounting to law students about his life as a prisoner and the vast distance between the rhetoric of correctional law and policy and the realities of imprisonment inspired several of the projects in the Archive. It is only recently that both the academic community and correctional establishment have begun to take seriously the contributions of the lived experiences of prisoners in the search for justice behind the walls. Gary Weaver brought his lived experiences to the Law School.</p>

<p>The second source for Gary’s inclusion in the archive is that throughout his life as a prisoner and parolee, spanning over thirty five years, Gary was an artist whose work engaged several media. Gary explored his indigenous identity in wood carving, creating West Coast style cedar Eagle, Moon and other masks such as the Moon mask displayed here.</p>

<p>Gary later turned to wood burning but took the medium into a new dimension by burning images of people and animals taken from actual photographs. It is difficult to imagine how a wood-burning, existing in only one dimension and without the use of colour. could capture vivid photographic images. Such was his talent that Gary’s work was much sought after by other prisoners, correctional staff ( including the Warden in one institution) and friends and family. After my daughter Melissa had her first child, Jackson, Gary asked me for a photograph and gifted a wood-burning which despite the limitations of the medium captured the essence of the photo. What is reproduced below are two photos, the first being Gary's woodburning and the second, taken fifteen years later, of the wood-burning and the original photograph on which it is based, in the hands of Melissa and Jackson.</p>

<p>Gary also produced many drawings and paintings which were gifted away. The first drawing he showed me was of a family of wolves, which he had drawn while he was in the Special Handing Unit in Québec. Gary was already a talented prison tattoo artist and another prisoner admired his work and suggested that he develop his artistry through drawing and painting. Gary continued to cultivated his love for wildlife in creating images of wildlife and sealife, wolves, eagles and dolphins.</p>

<p>In the last part of his artistic career he explored these themes in crafting soap sculptures. During the Covid pandemic in 2020 Gary was in Mission medium security institution. Because Mission was the first federal institution to experience Covid in the spring of 2020 and the first prison in which a prisoner died of the virus, the Covid protocols in force at Mission were particularly rigorous. As the Correctional Investigator has documented in the first three months under the Covid lockdown at Mission, the conditions were harsher than the conditions in the former administrative segregation regime. Prisoners were confined in their cells 23 hours a day with only limited time out for showers.</p>

<p>Gary’s last creations are perhaps the most remarkable, born as they have been from the rigours of Covid restrictions during the extended lockdown at Mission. Mr. Weaver took the small bars of soap that he was given, crushed them into powder and reconstituted them as the basic material from which to carve sculptures, using the most basic of tools. This is a photograph of the largest of Gary’s sculptures, another family of wolves. It is made from 22 pounds of soap and took seven months to complete. It may be one of the most impressive legacies of resilience from the Covid years.</p>

<p>Mr. Weaver has also used this soap sculpture medium to create and donate what he called ‘spirit soaps’ formed in the shape of an eagle, moon, dolphin and frog. He explained to me:</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p>I am focussed on the positive energy the entire time I am sculpting and carving them and praying that whoever receives each individual piece will personally benefit from my intentions. I include a handwritten note with each piece encouraging the recipient to wash, shower a or bathe with it when they are most in need for something uplifting to manifest in their life, (for example healing restoration, mending relationships).</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Gary Weaver donated 85% of his smaller soap art pieces to the Art and Justice project at UBC.</p>

<p>Gary Weaver died in March of 2024. He was 55.This entry in the Archive is in memoriam.</p>
