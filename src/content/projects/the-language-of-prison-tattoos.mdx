Tattoos, once the bodily preserve of seamen, prisoners and many Indigenous cultures have gone mainstream but they remain of particular significance to prisoners. Stuart Darrach-Cottick’s project for the 2014 Prisoners’ Rights seminar was an ink drawing of a silhouette marked with prison tattoos.

![Stuart Darrach-Cottick's 2014 project illustration of prison tatoos.](/assets/2026/03/Stuart_Darrach-Cottick_02-667x1024.jpg)

This seemingly stark drawing encodes a wealth of meaning and Stuart in his accompanying essay relates his choice of project to both his visit to Matsqui but also to the experiences of Gary Weaver who talked about his history of tattooing when he was a guest speaker at the Prisoners' Rights class in 2014 while out on parole.

> I have chosen to express my final project for Prisoners' Rights as an ink drawing of a silhouette with prison tattoos. I chose this to draw this for a number of reasons. First, I noticed a common theme of art in statements from the inmates at Matsqui and Gary who came in to speak for the last class. In Gary's case, he mentioned how he was always artistically inclined, but found that art offered him and other inmates solace during the emotionally demanding experience of incarceration. Gary also described having to learn how to use art as a direct outlet for the anger he felt at having every element of his life controlled by an unfair system instead of acting out in defiance and boredom. I found this very inspiring as I had similarly used music as an outlet during difficult portions of my life. While I have never had to undergo anything as emotionally devastating as surviving incarceration, I have previously dealt with stress and anxiety by writing and playing music. Another element of Gary's story was how he had created a system of expression to survive incarceration without being broken by the system, but he had to do so within the strict confines of prison life. Gary had to make do with the resources that were available and not necessarily what he was used to; for example, he took up tattooing, which he still practices to this day. Gary was able to take the available channels of expression inside prison and use them as an emotional outlet. I was very moved by how Gary took the available process of wood burning and developed his own form of art in practicing photorealistic wood burning.
>
> I wanted to do a project that acknowledged both the usefulness of art as an outlet for incarcerated persons while still reflecting the limited materials available in prison. I chose to do this drawing using just a plain black pen and plain white paper to represent the necessity of simplicity in prison. I focused on using a visual medium because I have no experience with visual art and wanted to use a method that was entirely foreign to me. The process of learning about tattoos and drawing techniques served as a way of representing the reality that many inmates are required to operate outside of their previous experiences. I found the most notable example in that many prisoners who were not genetically or historically connected to Aboriginal culture became connected to First Nations spirituality during their time in prison. I wanted to learn this new method of drawing as a way to represent the ability to find solace in unexpected places.
>
> I chose to depict the silhouette of a prisoner with tattoos to give a nod to the presence of a specific prison culture. I was really interested in learning about the various aspects of prison culture throughout this course, from the relationship between prisoner and corrections officer to the so-called "con-code". I was particularly interested in language of prison tattoos, as they function as a unique, visible language on the bodies of those who spend time incarcerated. I was also moved by how Gary learned how to tattoo in prison and turned that form of expression into a career. I wanted to pay homage to Matsqui's initiation of the Clean Tattoo program, which provides training for inmates, safety precautions such as clean needles to control the spread of infectious diseases, and designated space for tattooing. This program not only seeks to provide a higher standard of safety but also stands to foster a sense of community between inmates and encourages the development of marketable skills. I chose to present the subject without any distinguishing features to represent the lack of identity that many prisoners experience during their incarceration, feeling that they are nothing more than a number to the institution that controls every aspect of their lives.

Stuart explains the meaning of the particular tattoos used in his drawing;

> **Spider web with spider**: This is a ubiquitous prison style tattoo representing being "caught". This could be a reference to the inmate's incarceration, previous involvement with drug trade, previous struggles with addiction or represent the time spent idle in prison.
>
> **Clock with no hands:** This tattoo commonly represents a loss of time. Prisoners often grapple with the sheer volume of time involved in longer prison sentences; this tattoo represents how time seems to not pass at all while incarcerated, or the time "lost" while in prison. It may also represent how the inmate has chosen not to keep track of time during his prison sentence.
>
> **Five dots**: This symbol represents prison life, with the four outer dots depicting the four walls surrounding the central dot, the prisoner. This tattoo was more common in the past, as a lack of sterile materials and designated tattooing locations meant that inmates had to make do with what they had. As a result, prisoners often bore simpler tattoos, done with a "stick-and-poke" method using improvised materials like the ink from ballpoint pens and sewing needles.
>
> **Sacred Heart**: The scared heart is a common icon in Christianity, the presence of this tattoo can represent finding religion as inspiration to survive the process of prison.
>
> **Lighthouse:** Incarceration can inspire inmates to make a new start, or represent a self­ imposed warning for the inmate to stay on the right side of the law. The lighthouse also has the word "Matsqui" on it to represent time spent in this particular institution.
>
> **Tribal dragon with dates**: Tribal tattoos are commonly seen in Canadian prison systems, often illustrating the influence of Aboriginal culture on the inmate. The numbers underneath the tattoo represent the dates of the largest Matsqui riot.
>
> **Dreamcatcher**: Canadian inmates frequently find solace through Aboriginal culture and traditions, even if they are not genetically or historical Aboriginal.
>
> **Teardrop**: This tattoo has a number of different meanings based on the region and personal intention behind it. Such interpretations include: the inmate having killed someone in the past, the inmate seeking to avenge a killed friend or relative, the inmate having a friend in prison or representing his own prison sentence. The teardrop also has different meanings if it is depicted as filled in or not.
>
> **Dates in Scroll**: This tattoo represents the inmate's time in prison, this individual would have been incarcerated in 1987, the blank date would be filled in when the individual is released.
>
> I have also included a number of classic tattoo designs, such as barbed wire on the arm, sparrow, and inspirational words in script. I chose not to include any gang related tattoos to address the misconception that gangs and gang violence are overly prevalent in Canadian penal institutions.

Although there is no project in the Archive documenting  Indigenous body markings these have deeply significant  cultural roots. Along with many other Indigenous societies the Haida Nation’s body markings were associated with clan relationships, genealogy and oral histories. The ethnographic literature from the nineteenth century, although impressed with a colonial narrative that viewed Indigenous societies as disappearing, recognized that these markings embedded cultural knowledge and were more than decorative body painting.[^1]

As a result of missionary pressure to abandon what were deemed uncivilized cultural practices - in this case body painting- but also to participate in expanding trade networks, beginning in the last part of the nineteenth century the Haida shifted from tattooing to the creation of beautifully carved silver bracelets, moving cultural knowledge and identity  to a new artistic format, an example of cultural resilience and adaptation.

![Late 19th century Haida Sea Bear Bracelet.](/assets/2026/03/Stuart_Darrach-Cottick_03.jpg)

>Charles Edenshaw, late 19th century  Sea Bear Bracelet from the 2013 Charles Edenshaw  Exhibition at the  [Vancouver Art Gallery Charles Edenshaw | Vancouver Art Gallery](https://www.vanartgallery.bc.ca/exhibitions/charles-edenshaw/)

Contemporary Haida artists have continued to evolve Haida art forms. Alongside these developments, the revival of ancestral body-marking practices has also become an important part of the resurgence of Haida cultural and artistic identity. This resurgence is shared by other Indigenous peoples in Canada and around the world. A 2014 exhibition at the Museum of Vancouver, titled True Tribal: Contemporary Expressions of Ancestral Tattoo Practices,

>explores 30-plus years of Indigenous tattooing from around the world and the artists who are reconnecting with traditional skin marking practices. The revival of ancestral tattoo designs and motifs, the re-envisioning of meaning and protocols, and the re-fashioning of ancestral application methods is part of Indigenous peoples’ efforts to reclaim their lands, cultures and identities.[^2]

I have previously referred to Maori artist and designer Graham Tipene’s work in changing the public face of Auckland. In his practice of Ta Moko, which he distinguishes from tattooing, he is changing the private spaces, faces and bodies of Maori. 

![GRAHAM TIPENE - Portrait.](/assets/2026/03/Stuart_Darrach-Cottick_04.jpg)

>**GRAHAM TIPENE — Toi Ngapuhi
>
>When people refer to Graham Tipene as a tattoo artist, he usually lets it slide. He understands it’s a sweeping term that makes it easier for the layperson to comprehend. That is until they enter his studio. Then he sets them straight on tā moko.
>>“Tā moko has geneology, history, whakapapa and connection to the land and people,” Tipene explains. “Tattooing is sometimes just a pretty picture and skin. We don’t give people a book for them to choose number 25 in red…”
>Tipene takes his art seriously – and it’s easy to understand why. His designs are both figuratively the face of Auckland and literally on the faces of Auckland.[^3]
