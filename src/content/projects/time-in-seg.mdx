One of the most difficult experiences to describe and understand is the concept of prison time. Those of us within the legal profession, and extending to the community at large, are familiar with the metaphorical concept of ‘the scales of justice’. A statue of Blind Justice holding her scales stands in Arthur Erickson’s Great Hall of the BC Supreme Court Building in Vancouver. A related concept, which for prisoners is anything but metaphorical, is ‘the weight of imprisonment’. In *Prisoners of Isolation: Solitary Confinement in Canada*, (1983) I cited the expert evidence of criminologist Dr. Richard Korn in the *McCann* case:

> Free men spend time. Prisoners do time. Doing time is a specific activity, a calling, an art. Time itself is a force, it has its own action. Offenders are hit with their time and the word for a prison sentence is a jolt. Prison time is almost palpable. It not only has force, it has mass and weight. Too heavy a sentence can suffocate …1

Alisyn Burns in the 2017 Prisoners' Rights seminar created a different set of images and an animation to describe her understanding of the timeline of solitary confinement.

<MediaEmbed url="https://youtu.be/sONwWWGooys" />

 In “Time in Seg” she explains the evolution of her project.

> Originally, I was not going to produce a creative piece for this final project. However, when we reached the discussions on segregation an image formed in my mind that had to be created. First, I drew the image in a quick sketch just to get the idea on paper but then I uploaded the sketch and through layering features was able to turn the idea into a graphic design on my computer. After creating the static picture, I converted the document into another program. This second program allowed me to animate the clock feature. The following write up will explain the inspiration behind the image, and the meanings behind the man, the small clocks, the door and the large clock.
>
> ### Inspiration
> I discussed segregation with ‘Danny’, one of the prisoners at Matsqui prison on our class trip. He did not particularly want to speak about his time in segregation outside of having been in it due to unfair processes within the prison, such as disciplinary hearings. This conversation only left me with more questions.
>
> The description given in Chapter 4 sector 1 under *McCann v The Queen*, 1975 was the first captivating piece. Specifically,
>
> > “The cells measured 11 feet by 6½ feet and consisted of three solid concrete walls and a solid steel door with a 5-inch-square window which could only be opened from outside the cell. Inside the cell, there was no proper bed… One of the expert witnesses described the physical space as "one step above a strip cell . . . a concrete vault in which people are buried."… The light was too bright to permit comfortable sleep and too dim to provide adequate illumination… The prisoners were confined in their concrete vaults for 23½ hours a day.”[^1]
>
> Another quote that provided foundation to my vision is “In the Penthouse, the worst things about prison -- the humiliation and degradation, the frustration, the despair, the loneliness, and the deep sense of antagonism between prisoners and guards -- were intensified.”[^2] Obviously, McCann’s story as discussed in the book Justice Behind the Walls and in our class deeply contributed to this creation.
>
> After completing the sketch I wanted to bring the graphic design to life. In order to do so I drew inspiration from two of the pictures on Justice Behind the Walls website.
>
> Specifically the following two pictures:
>
![Alisyn Burns' animation project title “Time in Seg” background prison photos.](/assets/2024/07/Alisyn_Burns_01-1024x658.jpg)
>
> Between these visuals, and the ones given to me by description I began to prepare my final project.
>
> I wanted to not only capture the physical likeness for the description of segregation in this piece but also the atmosphere. The cell itself is a lonely and humiliating area but then you must add the feelings that go along with such a space. Including the mental loneliness, the frustration in the inability to have any form of control, the humiliation and frustration in being controlled by someone else – the guard. As well as dealing with the sense of unknown. This sense comes from maybe not knowing why you were put in segregation, because you barely stepped a toe out of line, or from the unknown time of getting out, or the unknown amount of time that’s passed. The place, the atmosphere and the emotions all collide, and I would claim, produce an incredibly unsettling, unproductive and punitive experience.
>
> ### The Man
> The focus of our class was mainly investigated the men’s prisons. I purposefully made him faceless because I wanted to provide the allusion that an prisoner can find themselves in the segregation unit. Based on the visit to Matsqui, I imagined this prisoner to have access to regular clothing rather than dressing him in any particular prison garb.,
>
> ### The Small Clocks
> Quite, simply the small clocks represent the 23 hours in a day that prisoners are in the small segregation cell. Each has a different hour of the day to impress the fact that hours pass slowly. Based on the information from this course I had the clocks end at the time of 4 pm because that seemed to be the typical time most prisoners are allowed some time outside of their cells.
>
> ### The Door
> In place of the last clock for the 24th hour I’ve placed a door. This door represents the maximum 1 hour outside of the cell. Outside of the cell, is usually just walking the segregation unit while under strict supervision. On another level, the door also represents the ability to be out of segregation if enough time has passed. In a sense, it is freedom. In another sense, it is more representative of a release to an area with lessened despair, loneliness, and humiliation. Because for most, the exit of segregation is not the exit of the prison, let alone the correctional or justice system.
>
![Alisyn Burns' animation project title “Time in Seg” background sketch.](/assets/2024/07/Alisyn_Burns_02-799x1024.jpg)
>
> ### The Large Clock
> I wanted to make sure I was able to represent more than just hours passing for those in segregation. Prisoners can spend days, even months, in the segregation unit. The large clock allows for the larger passage of time to be represented. With the just one large clock spinning, it shows how unclear the length of time can be. Whether or not the prisoner will be there weeks or months is both depicted by the singular large clock rather than having any form of calendar visual shown. I intended for the large clock to spin at a rather slow pace that repeats seamlessly over and over again. This is how the clock is depicted on my computer, which I showed you at our last class. However, the YouTube video link still has a glitch or two I was unable to resolve, such as it doesn’t continuously repeat.
